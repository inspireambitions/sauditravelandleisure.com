Can a Minor Travel to Dubai Alone?

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Families across the Gulf are asking a focused, practical question: can a minor travel to Dubai alone? In recent years Dubai’s popularity for family visits, education, and short-term stays has grown, and changes to entry procedures for minors have made planning more complex. If you are arranging travel for a child or teen, the stakes are high—airlines, immigration, and receiving families all have a role to play.

Short answer: Yes — a minor can travel to Dubai alone, but strict conditions apply. The United Arab Emirates and most carriers now require clear parental authorization and supporting documents for anyone under 18 traveling without both parents. Airlines also have their own unaccompanied minor procedures and age cutoffs that you must follow. This article explains the legal requirements, airline rules, documentation, and step-by-step planning you need to move from uncertainty to a confident, safe trip.

This post will walk you through the legal framework, airline services, the exact paperwork you should prepare, the practical steps at the airport and on arrival, contingency planning for custody or single-parent cases, and smart travel design so the child’s journey is safe and stress-free. As the KSA Travel Insider, I’ll also connect these rules to planning travel from Saudi Arabia and share resources to help you finalize every detail.

My main message: with careful preparation—correct, notarized consent, coordination with the carrier, and a clear receiving plan—unaccompanied travel to Dubai is manageable. Follow the checklist and processes below to make sure the trip is smooth, compliant, and secure.

Why Rules Have Tightened: Context and Consequences

The policy shift and its purpose

In recent years Gulf authorities increased safeguards for minors entering their airspace and borders to reduce risks associated with human trafficking, custody disputes, and unsupervised child movements. The practical effect is that immigration now scrutinizes unaccompanied minors more thoroughly than before: officials expect documented parental consent, clear arrival plans, and verification that the person receiving the child is authorized.

Airlines and airports are partners in enforcement. They will typically refuse boarding or delay a minor’s exit from the airport if paperwork is incomplete. For families, this means paperwork is not optional—it's a gatekeeper to travel.

Real-world consequences if rules are ignored

If a minor travels without correct documentation the worst-case outcomes include denial of entry, temporary detention at the airport while authorities verify documentation, or deportation. Even when deportation is not enforced, you can expect major delays, cancellation of onward plans, and distress for the child. Preventing those disruptions is a matter of following the procedures closely.

The Legal Framework: UAE Entry Rules for Minors

Age and authorization requirements

The UAE treats travelers under 18 as minors for the purpose of parental authorization. Recent advisories have made it clear: when a person under 18 travels to or through the UAE without both parents present, immigration authorities require a signed, authenticated parental authorization letter that includes details about the guardian or adult who will receive the child in the UAE, plus the child’s itinerary and accommodation address.

This requirement is nationality-agnostic—authorities worldwide apply it consistently to minimize risk. Airlines will typically enforce the requirement at check-in to avoid carrying a passenger who will be refused entry.

What immigration looks for on arrival

Immigration officers will want to see:

  • The child’s passport and valid visa (if required).
  • A parental consent letter that includes contact details for parent(s)/guardian(s) and the person receiving the child in the UAE.
  • Identification documents for the receiving adult (passport copy, UAE ID if applicable).
  • Additional supporting documentation in custody-sensitive cases (court orders, death certificates, or legal guardianship documents).

If documents are missing or unclear, immigration can detain the minor while they contact the parents and verify authorization. That verification will take time and can result in denied entry.

Airlines and Unaccompanied Minor Services

Why airline policies matter

Airlines are the first line of operational enforcement. Their staff check documentation before boarding and manage the minor during the journey. Each carrier sets its own unaccompanied minor (UM) rules — ages covered, service fees, and what is included (escort to immigration, lounge access, cabin supervision). An airline’s policy often determines whether a child can travel at all without an adult.

Typical airline age categories and services

Policies differ, but in practice carriers separate passengers into age brackets such as:

  • Infants/toddlers (0–2): never travel alone.
  • Young children (5–11 or 12): usually require UM service.
  • Teens (12–17): some airlines allow independent travel; others require UM service if traveling to certain destinations, or at parents’ request.

Because airlines change rules frequently and may apply special rules for flights to and from the UAE, always confirm directly with the carrier before booking.

Practical steps with airlines

When booking:

  • Tell the airline the child will be unaccompanied; do not assume a regular ticket is sufficient.
  • Reserve UM service early — some carriers limit the number of UM passengers per flight.
  • Confirm the fee and what it covers (meet-and-assist, in-flight supervision, handover on arrival).
  • Ask whether the carrier will escort a minor through immigration or simply hand them to airport staff for processing.

A final, critical point: some airlines refuse to accept unaccompanied minors for itineraries requiring overnight layovers or when a transfer between terminals is required. Get these transfer rules documented.

Essential Documents: The Non-Negotiable Checklist

Prepare these documents well before travel. Missing any of them can prevent boarding or cause detention on arrival.

  • Child’s passport, valid for the required period for the UAE.
  • Visa (if required for the child’s nationality) or evidence of visa on arrival eligibility.
  • A signed parental authorization letter (see the guidance below for required content and attestation).
  • Copies of parents’ passports (scanned and printed).
  • Proof of relationship if not obvious (birth certificate showing parent names, legal guardianship papers).
  • Contact details and ID for the receiving adult in Dubai (passport copy, Emirates ID if applicable).
  • Airline unaccompanied minor form(s) fully completed.
  • Medical consent or a letter allowing the receiving adult to authorize medical treatment if needed.
  • Where applicable, court custody documents or death certificates to explain single-parent situations.

How to write the parental authorization letter

A practical parental authorization letter should include:

  • Full name and passport number of the minor.
  • Date of birth of the minor.
  • Full contact details (address, phone, email) of both parents or legal guardians.
  • Statement of consent authorizing the minor to travel alone or with a specified adult.
  • Full details of the receiving person in the UAE (name, relationship, passport number, contact details, address where the child will stay).
  • Dates of travel and flight numbers (if known).
  • Medical consent statement and any allergies or chronic conditions.
  • Signatures of parent(s)/guardian(s) and the date.

Although a straightforward template often suffices, authorities and some airlines advise official notarization and legalization. The level of authentication depends on your country of origin and the airline’s internal policy.

Notarization, attestation, and embassy legalization — when to do it

Best practice for cross-border travel: get the parental authorization notarized by a public notary and, when required, authenticated by your country’s ministry of foreign affairs. For extra certainty—particularly in custody or single-parent situations—have the document legalized at the UAE embassy or consulate. When in doubt, ask both the airline and the UAE embassy what level of authentication they expect for the minor’s nationality and your departure country.

Preparing the Minor: Health, Safety, and Practical Skills

Emotional readiness and communication

Children travel best when they know the plan. Explain the itinerary, whom they will meet, and where they will stay. Role-play security and immigration steps so the child knows what to expect. Prepare them for whom to contact if something goes wrong and make sure they have the receiving adult’s phone number stored and printed.

Health and medication management

If the child takes medications, pack ample supplies in carry-on bag with prescriptions and a doctor’s letter. Provide clear written instructions for dosage and any special measures. Ensure vaccinations are up to date for travel and carry copies of immunization records if relevant for the destination.

Technology for safety

Provide a mobile phone with roaming or a local SIM accessible to the receiving adult and parents. Install basic safety apps, have emergency numbers programmed, and ensure the child knows how to contact both parents and the receiving adult.

Planning the Journey — Booking, Check-in, and Day-Of Procedures

Choosing the right flight and carrier

Select an airline with a robust unaccompanied minor service and friendly schedules (no overnight layovers, minimal transfers). Direct flights limit risk. When comparing options, contact each airline’s customer service and confirm UM age cutoffs, fees, and the precise handling steps at departure and arrival.

Booking and completing airline UM requirements

When you book:

  • Note UM service on the child’s reservation and pay any fees.
  • Complete the airline’s unaccompanied minor form and carry printed copies.
  • Provide contact details for the person handing over the child at departure and the person receiving at arrival.
  • Request seats near cabin crew where possible.

Airlines typically require parents to arrive early for an in-person handover. They will need to verify documentation and escort the child through security as far as permitted.

Airport arrival and check-in procedures

Parents should arrive at least two to three hours before an international departure. Expect to:

  • Present all documents at the check-in counter, including the UM form and parental authorization letter.
  • Remain at the airport until the aircraft departs in some cases—airlines sometimes ask parents to wait until the flight has taken off.
  • Ensure the receiving adult in Dubai is aware of arrival time and contact procedures; the airline or airport team will often coordinate handover on arrival.

Arrival and handover in Dubai

On arrival, immigration will complete its checks. After clearance, the minor will be handed to the authorized person specified in the documentation. The receiving adult should present original ID and possibly a printed authorization form. Airlines typically do not release a child to anyone other than the person listed on their paperwork.

Special Cases and Potential Complications

Traveling with one parent, divorced parents, or legal disputes

If a single parent travels with a child, or a child is traveling with one parent while the other parent is not present, the absent parent’s consent may still be required. Immigration is cautious about custody disputes.

If the travel is contested, carry court orders, custody agreements, or any legal documentation proving permission. In many jurisdictions, having a notarized letter from the absent parent plus supporting court documents will smooth the process.

Death of a parent or guardianship issues

If a parent is deceased or absent for other reasons, carry official death certificates or court-appointed guardianship papers. These documents should be properly authenticated and, where possible, translated into English or Arabic.

Schools, exchange programs, and organized travel groups

If the child is traveling for study, summer camp, or a supervised group, get a formal letter from the host institution confirming dates, accommodation, and a responsible contact person. Many airlines and immigration officials accept these institutional confirmations as part of the travel authorization.

From Saudi Arabia: Country-Specific Considerations

Saudi exit requirements and cultural factors

If the child is a Saudi national or departing from Saudi Arabia, be aware of the local procedures around minors leaving the country. Saudi authorities have specific regulations related to guardianship and travel for minors; these can include electronic permissions through national systems and additional verification for female travelers. Because policies may vary by case and change with regulations, verify with local authorities or the airline early in the planning process.

Cross-border itineraries and land crossings

Most unaccompanied minor travel to Dubai is by air. Land border crossings create more complexity due to variable inspection and verification practices. For cross-border plans originating in Saudi Arabia or involving multiple Gulf states, coordinate with the carrier and the receiving authorities in the UAE and consider flying to minimize bureaucratic hurdles.

Practical Templates and Sample Wording (Do’s and Don’ts)

Example phrasing for a parental authorization letter (prose template)

Begin with identification: “I, [Full Name], holding passport number [XXXXX], am the [mother/father/legal guardian] of [Child’s Full Name], born [DOB], passport number [XXXXX]. I hereby authorize my child to travel from [Departure City/Country] to Dubai, United Arab Emirates, on [dates], on flight [flight number if known]. The child will be received upon arrival by [Name of Receiving Adult], passport number [XXXXX], who resides at [address in UAE] and can be reached at [phone/email]. I grant permission for [Receiving Adult] to care for my child and to authorize medical treatment in case of emergency. I attest that I have legal authority to provide this consent. Signed, [Parent Name], Date, Contact Phone.”

Do get it notarized and, when asked, legalized by the UAE Embassy or consulate.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using vague contact information or failing to name the receiving adult explicitly.
  • Submitting unsigned or undated letters.
  • Leaving out the receiving adult’s passport or ID number.
  • Relying on email confirmations alone — bring printed originals.
  • Assuming airline staff will accept incomplete or unverified paperwork at the last minute.

Contingency Planning: If Something Goes Wrong

Who to call and what to do at the airport

If immigration or airline staff detain a minor for verification, the receiving adult and parents should be reachable immediately. Have emergency contact numbers ready: the airline’s UM desk, the receiving airport’s special assistance desk, and the local embassy or consulate.

If the child is detained pending verification, remaining calm and providing notarized documents, insurance information, and contact details will speed resolution. If the incident escalates, contact your embassy for guidance.

Deportation risk: causes and mitigation

Deportation is rare but possible if authorities deem documentation insufficient or suspect illegal activity. The best mitigation is to be proactive: verify documentation requirements with your airline and the UAE embassy weeks before travel, obtain necessary notarization and legalization, and ensure that the receiving adult’s status is clear and verifiable.

Travel insurance and legal assistance

Purchase travel insurance that covers emergency repatriation and legal support for international incidents. For complex custody situations, consult a family lawyer who understands cross-border travel and can advise on the paperwork required to avoid disputes.

Alternatives to Solo Travel for Minors

If the combination of airline policy and immigration requirements seems too complex, consider alternatives:

  • Travel with a trusted adult companion who can serve as the legal escort.
  • Arrange for an airline-appointed escort if the carrier provides a full transit escort service.
  • Use a reputable travel agency that offers supervised child transport services and handles documentation.
  • Delay travel until both parents can accompany, particularly in custody-sensitive situations.

Each option has trade-offs in cost and convenience; evaluate them in light of the child’s age and the length of the trip.

Smart Itinerary Design for Unaccompanied Minors

Minimize transfers and avoid overnight layovers

Whenever possible, book a direct flight. If a transfer is necessary, select an itinerary with a single, short connection in a single terminal and confirm the airline accepts minor transfers.

Schedule arrival during daytime

Daytime arrivals reduce stress for a minor and simplify handover logistics. Immigration lines and airport services are typically more available during daytime hours.

Choose convenient airports and receiving locations

If the receiving adult will pick the child up, choose an airport entrance and meeting point that are well-known and easy to access. Share a simple map and photos of meeting points with the child.

Day-Of-Travel Quick Checklist (list 2 — day-of essentials)

  • Arrive early at the airport (2–3 hours minimum for international).
  • Carry printed originals of parental authorization and airline UM forms.

  • Pack medications with clear instructions and doctor’s note in carry-on.

Case Studies: Practical Scenarios and Recommended Actions

Scenario: A 15-year-old traveling alone from Riyadh to Dubai to stay with relatives

Action steps: Obtain a notarized parental authorization letter naming the relatives and providing their ID and address. Confirm with the airline whether UM service is required for a 15-year-old on this specific route and reserve the service if needed. Provide the child with a printed copy of the relatives’ passport and a mobile phone with their contact info. Ensure the relatives are at the airport arrival gate and prepared to show ID.

Scenario: A minor traveling with one parent (single-parent travel)

Action steps: Carry the single parent’s passport and a court order or a notarized letter from the absent parent if required. Check airline rules and Saudi exit permit requirements if the child is a Saudi national. Bring custody documents and, if necessary, an embassy-attested letter.

Scenario: A 10-year-old traveling on a flight with an overnight layover

Action steps: Verify the airline accepts unaccompanied minors for itineraries with overnight layovers. If the airline does not, arrange an alternative (direct flight or daytime connection) or find an adult chaperone. If the airline allows it, prepare detailed transfer instructions and confirm that the airline will supervise the child during the overnight period.

Final Legal and Safety Reminders

  • Start early—documents, notarization, and embassy legalizations can take time.
  • Double-check airline UM age policies and limitations on transfers and overnight holds.
  • Never assume a digital copy suffices—carry printed originals and backups.
  • For custody, single-parent, or guardianship cases, elevate documentation and legal authentication.
  • Keep communication lines open and test them before travel—phone numbers, WhatsApp accounts, and backup emergency contacts.

Conclusion

Can a minor travel to Dubai alone? Yes—provided you treat the trip as a legal and logistical project: secure the correct parental authorization, satisfy airline unaccompanied minor procedures, and coordinate a clear receiving plan on arrival. The combination of notarized consent, airline coordination, and careful contingency planning turns a high-risk question into a manageable process. Use the two checklists above as your working blueprint and maintain clear communication with the airline and the receiving adult.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is a notarized parental authorization always required for a minor traveling to Dubai alone?
A: Not always required in every case, but it is strongly recommended. UAE immigration often expects a signed parental authorization for anyone under 18 traveling without a parent. Airlines commonly require UM forms and may ask for notarization to avoid problems at check-in. When in doubt, get the letter notarized and, if necessary, legalized by the UAE consulate.

Q: Can a minor travel alone on a connecting itinerary?
A: It depends on the airline. Some carriers refuse to transfer unaccompanied minors, especially if the connection requires a terminal change or overnight stay. Confirm with every carrier involved in the itinerary before booking.

Q: What should I do if the child’s other parent refuses to sign a travel consent?
A: This is a legal matter. Do not attempt to circumvent parental consent. Consult a family lawyer to explore custody documentation that permits travel or to obtain a court order if appropriate. Traveling without proper authorization risks detention and deportation.

Q: How early should I start arranging travel for a minor alone?
A: Start as soon as you know travel dates—ideally several weeks in advance. Notarization, embassy legalization, airline approvals, and any required visas can take time, and early planning prevents last-minute denials at the airport.
