Can a US Permanent Resident Travel to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai remains one of the world’s most visited cities, and demand from travelers across the globe—including long-term U.S. residents—continues to grow. Whether you’re drawn by futuristic skylines, world-class shopping, or quick access to the wider Gulf, understanding entry rules is the first step to a smooth trip.

Short answer: Yes—most U.S. permanent residents can travel to Dubai, but eligibility to enter the United Arab Emirates (UAE) is determined primarily by the passport you hold, not by your U.S. Green Card. Some passport nationalities qualify for visa-free entry or visa on arrival; others must obtain a preapproved visa before traveling, and a U.S. Green Card can sometimes make certain visa-on-arrival options available for specific nationalities. Always confirm current requirements with the airline and official UAE visa tools before you book.

This article explains the rules, the common scenarios Green Card holders face, step-by-step application options, what to expect at arrival, ways to avoid problems, and practical planning advice for combining a Dubai trip with broader travel in the region. Along the way I’ll draw on the reliable travel frameworks we use at Saudi Travel & Leisure to help you plan confidently and reduce friction before you fly.

How UAE Entry Rules Work: Passport First, Residency Second

The Core Principle

UAE entry policies are passport-driven. Immigration looks at the nationality recorded in the passport you present on arrival; your immigration or residency status in any third country—such as a U.S. Green Card—does not replace that nationality in the determination of visa eligibility. In short, the passport in your hands is the deciding factor.

Why a Green Card Still Matters

Although a Green Card is not a substitute for the passport, it can be an important facilitating document in two ways. First, certain nationalities (notably Indian passport holders) with a valid U.S. Green Card or valid U.S. visa may be eligible for a special visa-on-arrival arrangement or a preapproved visa facilitation offered by airlines or UAE authorities. Second, as a practical matter the Green Card helps demonstrate legal residence and ties to the U.S., which is frequently required by airlines or by some visa sponsors in the UAE when processing a prearranged visit permit.

Typical Entry Types

Immigration into Dubai is generally processed under one of the following entry categories:

  • Visa-free / visa on arrival: Passport holders of certain countries (including U.S. passport holders) receive a visit visa upon landing for 30, 90, or multiple-entry terms depending on nationality. This is the simplest route.
  • Prearranged eVisa or embassy visa: Required if your passport nationality is not covered by visa-free or visa-on-arrival rules. This can be obtained via the UAE eVisa system, airline service, hotel sponsorship, or a UAE embassy/consulate.
  • Sponsored visa: If an employer, UAE resident, or UAE-based entity sponsors your visit, they will apply on your behalf via GDRFA or relevant channels.
  • Transit visa: Short-stay permits for stopovers that must be arranged in advance or through certain airlines.

Common Scenarios for U.S. Green Card Holders

1) U.S. Passport Holder Who Also Has a Green Card

If you hold a U.S. passport, you qualify for visa on arrival in the UAE. With a U.S. passport you will generally receive a 30-day or 90-day entry stamp depending on prevailing UAE policy at the time of arrival, often with the option to extend. Your Green Card is not necessary for entry, but it is useful as secondary ID when traveling on long multi-leg itineraries or when returning to the U.S.

2) Non-U.S. Passport Holder With a U.S. Green Card (Example: Indian Passport + Green Card)

If your passport is Indian or another nationality that typically requires a prearranged visa, having a U.S. Green Card may change your options:

  • Indian passport holders with a valid U.S. visa or U.S. Green Card can often obtain a 14-day or 30-day visa on arrival (policies have changed over time and airlines may issue these on certain routings). This convenience is airline- and policy-dependent.
  • If you are not eligible for visa on arrival, you will need a prearranged UAE visa. This can be arranged by airlines for passengers with confirmed tickets, by hotels that offer visa sponsorship for guests, or via a UAE sponsor (friend, family, or private company).

The critical step is to verify whether your passport nationality plus Green Card qualifies for a visa-on-arrival scheme before you travel.

3) Passport From a Country Requiring a Prior Visa

If your passport is from a country that requires a visa in advance regardless of U.S. residency, you will need to apply for an eVisa, secure a sponsored visa, or apply through a UAE mission abroad. The Green Card may speed processing if used as proof of legal U.S. residence, but it will not negate the need for a proper UAE visa.

How to Check Your Specific Eligibility (Actionable Steps)

Before you buy a ticket, follow this decision flow to avoid surprises:

  1. Identify the passport you will use to travel and ensure it’s valid for at least six months from your arrival date.
  2. Visit the official UAE visa service or your airline’s visa tool to check visa-on-arrival or eVisa eligibility for your passport nationality. Airlines often provide a visa-check tool during booking.
  3. If your nationality is not eligible for visa on arrival, choose the best prearranged path: airline-facilitated eVisa, hotel sponsorship, UAE embassy/consulate, or private sponsor.
  4. Gather required documents (passport scans, passport photos, Green Card/visa copy, return ticket, hotel booking or sponsor letter).
  5. Apply early—allow several business days for processing and additional time if embassy authorization is required.

If you prefer an authoritative verification before booking, consult your airline and the UAE embassy resources. Step-By-Step: Applying for a UAE Visa as a Green Card Holder

Use the numbered checklist below as a practical workflow. This is the only list in this article and it’s designed to give you a compact, implementable process for most prearranged visas.

  1. Confirm passport validity: Minimum six months beyond arrival and at least four blank pages recommended.
  2. Decide which passport to use: Travel on your strongest passport for entry preference; if you hold dual nationality, choose the passport with the more favorable UAE policy.
  3. Check eligibility: Use the airline or UAE eVisa portal to see if your passport + Green Card qualifies for visa-on-arrival or a facilitated eVisa.
  4. Choose application route: eVisa via UAE government, “Manage Your Booking” through your airline (if available), hotel sponsorship, or UAE embassy/consulate.
  5. Prepare documents: Digital passport scan, passport-sized photo (white background), copy of U.S. Green Card or U.S. visa, confirmed return ticket, hotel reservation or sponsor letter, and any additional documents requested.
  6. Submit application and pay fees: Use secure payment channels and keep the confirmation receipt and reference number.
  7. Receive and print eVisa or keep a digital copy: Some arrival screenings may require a printed copy; airlines may ask for it at check-in.
  8. On arrival: Present passport used in application, visa copy (if preapproved), and supporting documents if requested. Be ready for random visual screening and to present printed copies if needed.

Documents You Should Always Travel With

Carry these in both printed and digital form:

  • The passport you plan to use for entry (6+ months validity).
  • U.S. Green Card or valid U.S. visa (photocopies and digital photos).
  • Digital and printed copy of your UAE eVisa or sponsor confirmation.
  • Confirmed return ticket showing onward or return travel.
  • Proof of accommodation (hotel confirmation or host details).
  • Travel insurance that covers emergency medical evacuation.
  • Any supporting invitation or sponsorship letters if you are on a sponsored visa.

If you are traveling with a non-U.S. passport that might require extra support documentation, have those ready as well (e.g., employment letter, evidence of funds). Airlines can refuse boarding if required documentation is missing.

Applying Via Different Channels: Pros and Cons

Airline-Facilitated eVisa (e.g., Emirates "Manage Your Booking")

Applying through the airline is often fast and convenient when the flight itinerary is eligible. Airlines can process an eVisa based on your ticket and passport details, and you can proceed without physically submitting your passport.

Pros: Convenient, integrated with your booking, often quick processing, payment handled online. Cons: Only available with some carriers and on certain routings; requires buy-in from the airline; fees and processing times vary.

Hotel or Sponsor Application

Hotels and UAE hosts can apply on your behalf. This is common for travelers whose passports need prior approval but who are staying in hotels that offer sponsorship.

Pros: Practical if you already have a confirmed hotel booking; hotel will usually handle details. Cons: You must receive confirmation before travel; not all hotels offer sponsorship; timeframes depend on sponsor responsiveness.

UAE Embassy / Consulate

Applying via the UAE mission is a traditional route that suits complex or long-stay visas, diplomatic or official travel, and when additional documents are needed.

Pros: Direct official channel, good for complex cases or long-stay visas. Cons: Longer processing times, more rigid procedures, possible requirement to submit originals.

Third-Party Visa Agencies

Many travelers use reputable visa agencies or visa specialists to prepare, submit, and follow up on applications. If you use an agency, verify its legitimacy and read reviews.

Pros: Removes administrative burden, helpful for uncommon passports or complicated itineraries. Cons: Extra fees, reliance on the agency’s accuracy and responsiveness.

At the Airport: What to Expect on Arrival in Dubai

Immigration and Entry Screening

Immigration officers will check your passport and visa status. For prearranged eVisas you should have a confirmation number or a digital/printed visa. Be ready to present:

  • Passport (same one used in the visa application).
  • Visa confirmation or entry stamp.
  • Return or onward ticket.
  • Accommodation proof.
  • Any additional supporting documents listed in your visa approval.

Dubai immigration may conduct random visual screening. If directed to screening, presenting a printed visa copy, return ticket, or sponsor details will accelerate processing. A small charge may be applied if you request copies at the airport.

Customs and Prohibited Items

The UAE enforces strict rules on items that may be acceptable in other countries. Prohibited items include:

  • Narcotics and certain medications that require prior approval—carry a doctor's prescription and authorization if bringing controlled medications.
  • Pornographic materials, and materials that contravene public decency laws.
  • Some e-cigarettes and products containing CBD are restricted.
  • Military-style items, weapon components, body armor and other law-enforcement equipment are forbidden without government approval.

Declare high-value items and be mindful that the UAE enforces strict penalties for offences that are treated leniently elsewhere.

Exit Controls and Overstay Risk

The UAE maintains exit controls. You must depart on the passport you used to enter. Ensure prior visas have been properly cancelled if you work or have previously resided in the UAE. Do not leave with unresolved financial disputes or civil cases; outstanding issues can lead to detention and exit bans. Overstays can attract steep fines, and visa cancellation procedures must be followed for past and expired permits.

Extensions, Changing Status, and Working in the UAE

Extending Your Visit

Short tourist visits are sometimes extendable at immigration discretion. If you plan a longer stay, inquire at a GDRFA office or ask immigration at the airport. Extensions often require a fee and are not guaranteed.

Changing to a Work or Residence Visa

Converting a tourist entry permit to a work or residency visa is not a walk-in process. Employment and residence visas are employer- or sponsor-driven and require formal medical checks, documentation, and approvals. If you intend to work or relocate to the UAE, your employer will guide application steps.

Medical Tests and Residency

A full medical exam is required for residence and work permits, with specific infectious disease screenings performed inside the UAE. Note that positive results for certain conditions can lead to deportation for those seeking residency—tourists are not typically subject to such screening upon arrival.

Practical Travel Advice and Cultural Guidelines

Money and Banking

The UAE dirham (AED) is the currency. ATMs are widespread; major credit cards are accepted almost everywhere. Notify your U.S. bank/cards of travel to avoid fraud blocks. Carry small cash for taxis and small purchases.

Transport and Getting Around

Dubai’s metro, taxis, ride-hailing apps, and well-developed road network make getting around straightforward. If you rent a car, ensure your U.S. driver’s license is valid and check whether an International Driving Permit is recommended based on your license and rental company policies.

Dress and Public Behavior

Dubai is a tolerant and cosmopolitan city, but public modesty is expected in many areas. Dress modestly in public spaces—beachwear on the beach is fine, but avoid overly revealing clothing in shopping malls and public areas. Public displays of affection can attract attention and, in rare cases, legal consequences.

Alcohol and Nightlife

Licensed venues serve alcohol. Drinking in public places or being publicly intoxicated is illegal. Always carry ID and drink responsibly.

Security and Safety

The UAE is generally safe for travelers. However, exercise standard travel caution: keep valuables secure, be aware of local laws, and maintain situational awareness in crowded spaces. Follow official travel advisories if geopolitical issues or regional tensions escalate.

Mistakes Travelers Make—and How to Avoid Them

Mistake: Relying on Green Card Alone

Some travelers assume a U.S. Green Card replaces visa needs; it does not. The passport rules are decisive. Always verify the passport-driven rules well in advance.

Mistake: Booking Nonrefundable Tickets Before Visa Confirmation

If your passport requires prior visa approval, avoid nonrefundable airfares until you have visa confirmation. Airline-facilitated visas sometimes tie to specific ticketing rules.

Mistake: Ignoring Passport Validity and Blank Page Requirements

A passport with less than six months validity can trigger refusal of boarding. Ensure your passport has the recommended number of blank pages.

Mistake: Overlooking Entry/Exit Stamp Consistency

You must exit on the passport you entered with. If you enter the UAE on one passport and attempt to leave with another, you may face complications.

Mistake: Bringing Restricted Medications Without Documentation

Some prescription drugs allowed in other countries are restricted in the UAE. Carry a doctor’s prescription and, if possible, a medical certificate.

Avoid these pitfalls by following the step-by-step checklist earlier, verifying with airlines, and keeping key documents handy.

How to Handle Denials, Delays, or Unexpected Problems

If your visa is refused, or you face issues at airport immigration:

  • Stay calm and cooperative. Hostile behavior escalates matters.
  • Ask to speak to a supervisor or the immigration officer for clarification.
  • Contact your airline immediately. They may assist with ticketing or temporary solutions.
  • Reach out to your embassy or consulate if detained or if you require urgent consular assistance.
  • If the issue is documentation-related (e.g., missing sponsor confirmation), contact the sponsor or hotel to transmit the needed proof electronically.

Practical Itineraries and Timing Tips for First-Time Dubai Visitors

Rather than a rigid day-by-day list, below are prose-based itineraries suited to common trip lengths—these are frameworks you can adapt depending on your interests.

48–72 Hours: Essentials

A short stay should prioritize efficient movement and pre-booked tickets for major attractions. Start with a morning visit to the Burj Khalifa and the Dubai Mall precinct; reserve an afternoon to visit the historic Al Fahidi neighborhood for a perspective on the city’s trading past. A sunset dhow cruise or a dinner desert safari makes great use of evenings and allows you to experience both modern and traditional contrasts.

4–7 Days: Classic Dubai With a Leisurely Pace

Add a day for a day-trip to Abu Dhabi to see the Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque and perhaps visit a museum or cultural district. Reserve time for the Palm Jumeirah and a beach day, allowing for museum or market visits and a relaxed evening in Dubai Marina. If you plan to intersperse business or family time, leave buffer days for any visa or transit contingencies.

Longer Stays and Multi-Country Trips

If you plan to combine the UAE with Saudi Arabia, block travel days to account for flights and possible additional visa processing. When building a broader Gulf itinerary, consider spending 3–4 days in Dubai followed by a direct flight to Riyadh or Jeddah and then moving inland to cultural sites in Saudi Arabia such as AlUla, which requires more time and specific permits for some attractions.

Costs: Typical Visa Fees and Budget Considerations

Visa fees vary by visa type and processing method. Basic guidelines:

  • Visa on arrival for eligible passport holders is usually free or has a nominal fee depending on duration and terms.
  • Prearranged tourist eVisas typically incur government fees plus any airline or agency charges; expect the total to range from a modest administrative fee to several hundred USD for longer-term or multiple-entry permits.
  • Embassy or consular processes can be more expensive and include handling and mailing fees.

When budgeting for your trip, include visa fees, travel insurance, airport transfer costs, accommodation deposits, and a contingency fund to cover unexpected documentation or timing delays.

Medical Insurance and Health Preparedness

Travel insurance that covers medical evacuation and trip interruption is recommended for travel to the Gulf. For longer stays or residency transitions, the UAE requires health screenings. For tourists, ensure your routine vaccinations are current and carry prescriptions with original documentation. If you take controlled medications, verify their legality in the UAE and carry the appropriate medical certificates.

Legal Risks and What to Avoid

The UAE has strict laws on social media conduct, public speech, and behavior. Avoid posting material that could be interpreted as defamatory or insulting toward the state, religion, or individuals. Do not engage in public behavior that could be construed as offensive or indecent. Familiarize yourself with laws on photographs—some locations prohibit photography—and get explicit permission before photographing people.

If you face legal trouble, contact your embassy. The U.S. embassy or consulate can provide a list of local attorneys and explain consular limitations; they cannot get you out of court penalties or pay fines on your behalf.

Final Planning Checklist Before You Fly

Complete these last checks to reduce last-minute friction: passport validity, visa confirmation (or eligibility to get visa on arrival), proof of return or onward travel, accommodation confirmation, travel insurance, and a printed/digital copy of your U.S. Green Card. Confirm pet travel arrangements, declare any restricted items, and be aware of local customs and legal boundaries.

Conclusion

Traveling to Dubai as a U.S. permanent resident is straightforward when you plan around the fundamental rule: the passport you present determines visa eligibility. A U.S. Green Card can offer helpful facilitation for certain passport holders but does not replace nationality-based visa requirements. Use the step-by-step checklist earlier in this article to select the best application route—airline eVisa, hotel sponsorship, embassy processing, or a reputable visa agency—prepare the right documents, and verify entry conditions with your carrier before departure.

FAQ

Can a US Green Card holder get a visa on arrival to Dubai?

Often no—the determining factor is the passport you hold. Some nationalities, such as Indian passport holders with a valid U.S. Green Card or U.S. visa, may qualify for special visa-on-arrival arrangements, but these options are policy- and airline-dependent. Always check the airline and UAE visa tools before you travel.

If my passport requires a prearranged visa, can a hotel or airline sponsor it?

Yes. Many hotels will sponsor guest visas, and some airlines offer eVisa services for passengers with eligible itineraries. Confirm sponsorship services before booking and obtain the visa confirmation in writing.

How long before I fly should I apply for a UAE eVisa?

Apply as soon as you know your travel dates. Processing times vary—some eVisas are issued in a few days while some embassy processes can take longer. If you plan last-minute travel, check airline visa facilitation options which can sometimes speed the process.

I want to visit Dubai and Riyadh on the same trip—what should I do first?

Check visa requirements for both the UAE and Saudi Arabia for the passport you intend to use, then plan your flights to allow sufficient processing time for any required visas.
