The Legal Shift: What Changed and Why It Matters

A New Law, A New Approach

Saudi Arabia replaced the older 2000 framework with a modern law authorizing real estate ownership by non-Saudis in specifically designated zones. The change is strategic: it opens controlled, market-friendly pathways for foreign capital while preserving social and religious safeguards. The law will be implemented through implementing regulations and Council of Ministers resolutions that define the exact geographic boundaries, permitted ownership shares, and operational details.

What Types of Real Estate Rights Are Allowed?

The law does not restrict foreigners to only full ownership. Authorities can set maximum ownership percentages and term limits for certain rights to balance investment with local priorities.

Financing Property in Riyadh: Mortgages and Alternatives

Saudi banks have historically extended some mortgage products to residents, and these offerings are likely to expand with the new legal regime. Non-resident foreign buyers and foreign companies may face stricter lending criteria or rely on international financing.

Taxes, Fees, and Ongoing Costs

Transfer Fees and Taxes

Under the new law, the Real Estate General Authority may levy a transfer fee on disposals by non-Saudis of up to 5% of the property value. Separately, Saudi Arabia imposes existing transactional taxes (typically a 5% real estate transaction tax), which will likely apply in many sales. Budget for combined transaction costs and verify final fees at the time of purchase.

Service Charges, Utilities, and Municipality Fees

Expect standard condominium or community service charges in gated developments or mixed-use projects. Municipal fees and utility connection charges also apply; commercial properties have different fee structures. Factor in insurance, property management costs (if youâll be an absentee owner), and maintenance reserves.
