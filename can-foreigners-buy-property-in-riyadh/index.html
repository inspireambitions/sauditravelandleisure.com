The Legal Framework: What Changed and Why It Matters

The New Legal Landscape

Saudi Arabia replaced its older 2000-era law with a new 2025 Real Estate Law that takes effect once the implementing regulations are issued. The replacement introduces a "designated zones" approach: foreign ownership is permitted in specific geographic zones determined by the Council of Ministers and recommended by the Real Estate General Authority. The policy shift reflects a deliberate move to open targeted parts of the market while retaining controls that protect social and cultural priorities.

This change is important because it removes several former barriers, expands eligibility to a wider class of non-Saudis (including foreign companies and non-profits), and clarifies the types of property rights that can be held. But the freedom is structured: ownership rights, permissible property types, and geographic scope will be defined in the implementing regulations and subsequent ministerial decisions.

For a national perspective on how these reforms fit into broader economic policy and investment programs, consult the national real estate overview.

Why Designated Zones?

Designated zones allow policymakers to balance openness with control. By concentrating foreign ownership in areas where demand is high or where international investment supports development goals, the government can stimulate construction, diversify housing stock, and attract capital while minimizing speculative pressure across the entire market. Expect early zones to include central and newly planned districts in major cities, with a strong likelihood that parts of Riyadh will be prioritized.

Key Provisions You Should Know Now

Several legally binding concepts will shape any purchase:

  • Definition of ânon-Saudiâ: individuals without Saudi citizenship, foreign companies, and foreign non-profits fall within the lawâs definition.
  • Residency distinctions: residents (iqama holders and premium residents) have different rights compared to non-residents; residents may be allowed to own a personal residence outside designated zones under restricted conditions.
  • Full ownership and other rights: foreigners can obtain full title or limited real rights such as long-term leaseholds and usufruct, depending on regulatory specifics.
  • Special rules for Makkah and Madinah: ownership in the holy cities remains tightly controlled and subject to unique conditions.
  • Registration and enforcement: foreign acquisitions must be recorded in the national Real Estate Registry to be legally effective. Violations carry significant penalties, including heavy fines and, in extreme cases, forced sale of property.

Can Foreigners Buy Property in Riyadh Right Now?

The Short Practical Answer

Foreigners will be permitted to buy property in Riyadh, but only in areas designated by the authorities. Resident expatriates can generally access more options than non-resident buyers, and corporate entities have their own pathway for acquisitions tied to business purposes. The exact maps and detailed rules are released through implementing regulations; however, the policy direction is clear: Riyadh will host designated zones where foreign ownership is allowed to encourage investment.

Who Is Eligible to Buy in Riyadh?

Eligibility depends on several criteria:

  • Non-resident individuals: Likely limited to purchases within designated zones only.
  • Resident expatriates: Holders of valid residency documents (iqama), and premium residency holders where applicable, are granted broader rights, including the possibility of owning a personal residence outside designated zones subject to approval.
  • Foreign companies and funds: Permitted to acquire property that supports their business operations or investment mandates. Corporate acquisitions often require registration and compliance with additional financial and regulatory rules.
  • Diplomatic missions and international organizations: Allowed to own premises for official use after obtaining approvals from relevant ministries.

This differentiated approach is designed to keep housing and community needs balanced while allowing foreign capital to participate in urban growth.

Types of Property Foreigners Can Own in Riyadh

Under the new law, ownership will not be limited to whole-title purchases. Expect these legal instruments to be available in practice:

  • Freehold ownership: Full title where allowed in the designated zones.
  • Long-term leaseholds: Multi-decade leases giving effective control without absolute title.
  • Usufruct rights: The right to use and derive benefit from property for a defined period.
  • Easements and other limited rights: For infrastructure, access or shared services.

The choice among these instruments affects financing options, taxes, transferability and how local law protects your interest. The implementing regulations will specify time limits and maximum ownership shares permitted for non-Saudis in any given zone.

Designated Zones: What to Expect for Riyadh

How Zones Are Determined

The Real Estate General Authority recommends zones to the Council of Ministers, which will approve them after economic and social assessments. Criteria will likely include:

  • Strategic development priorities (business districts, mixed-use projects)
  • Capacity of local services and infrastructure
  • Market supply-demand dynamics to avoid inflationary pressures
  • Security and cultural considerations near sensitive sites

In Riyadh, designated zones are expected to include central business districts, major new urban developments, and areas earmarked for international-standard residential and commercial projects. What Zones Mean for Buyers

If a property is in a designated zone, a foreign buyer will have clearer legal paths to acquire real rights. Outside these zones, ownership for non-residents will generally remain restricted, with some exceptions for residents and corporate needs. Every prospective buyer must confirm the zone status of a parcel before moving forward.

Budget for transfer taxes, registration fees, potential foreign transfer levies, and service charges. While the new law authorizes a transfer fee up to a capped rate for foreign disposals, anticipate conventional fees like the standard real estate transaction levy and municipal charges.

Financing, Taxes and Financial Planning

Mortgages and Financing Availability

Domestic banks increasingly offer mortgages to expatriates and foreign companies under specific conditions. Terms depend on residency status, income stability, down payment size and the property type. Foreign buyers should compare:

  • Interest rates and fixed vs variable options
  • Maximum loan-to-value ratios for non-Saudis
  • Prepayment penalties and currency risk management

If you plan to fund a purchase with financing, obtain pre-approval early to strengthen your bargaining position.

Taxes, Transfer Fees and Ongoing Costs

Saudi Arabia does not impose a general personal income tax on employment income for individuals, but real estate transactions carry fees and taxes. Key items to consider:

  • Real estate transaction fee/levy: a standard transaction tax exists on many property sales (buyers should confirm current rates).
  • Transfer fee for foreign disposals: the law allows a transfer fee capped at a stated percentage; expect regulators to finalize the exact mechanism.
  • Municipality and utility connection charges: one-time and recurring costs.
  • Property management and homeowners association fees for gated communities or complexes.

Careful financial planning should include a buffer for unexpected levy assessments or municipal fines.

Registration, Records and Legal Formalities

Registration Process

A sale becomes legally effective once recorded in the national Real Estate Registry. The registry entry establishes legal ownership and protects buyer rights. Registration typically requires:

  • Verified title deed documents
  • Proof of identity and residency status
  • Clearance of any mortgage or lien
  • Payment of applicable fees and taxes

Registration procedures and timelines can vary; allow sufficient lead time, and ensure your local counsel handles submission to the registry to avoid delays.

Residency, Visas and Ownership Implications

Owning property does not automatically confer residency. While premium residency previously provided one pathway to ownership, the new law expands access without requiring a residency program in many cases. However, residency status affects eligibility, post-purchase rights and the ability to live in the property long-term. If property ownership is central to relocation plans, coordinate purchases with visa planning.
