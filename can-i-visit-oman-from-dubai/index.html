Can I Visit Oman From Dubai? Quick Overview

Who Can Cross From Dubai Into Oman?

Oman and the UAE maintain friendly, well-managed land borders. Citizens of GCC states (Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Oman) do not require visas to cross. Many nationalities are entitled to visa-free entry for short stays or are eligible for visa-on-arrival; others use the Royal Oman Police eVisa portal to request an electronic visa before travel.

If you live in the UAE as a resident but hold a non-GCC passport, you can typically receive a visa on arrival at the border for 14 days, provided you present valid UAE residency documentation and a passport with adequate validity. For stays longer than 14 days, an eVisa (up to 30 days) or a different visa class is required. The advice in this article will treat three typical traveler profiles: GCC citizens, UAE residents (non-GCC nationals), and tourists visiting Dubai.

Travel Options From Dubai to Oman

You can enter Oman from Dubai by road, bus (limited services), or air. Road travel is the most popular for short escapes because it provides flexibility and fast access to Muscat, Musandam, or southern routes toward Salalah. Flights between Dubai and Muscat are short and frequent if you prefer air travel, and private bus operators cover certain crossings and routes seasonally.

Visa Rules and How To Secure One

Visa On Arrival vs eVisa: What Applies To You

Oman offers multiple ways to enter depending on nationality and residency:

  • Visa on Arrival: Many UAE residents and nationals of certain countries can obtain a visa at the land border. Typically this provides a 14-day stay, though terms vary by nationality. The border visa is convenient for short trips and is often stamped immediately during immigration clearance.
  • eVisa (Royal Oman Police): The ROP eVisa portal issues multiple visa types — tourist unsponsored, sponsored, multi-entry and GCC resident variants. The tourist eVisa can allow up to 30 days for many nationalities and is the right choice if you plan a longer stay or want certainty before travel. Processing normally takes several business days.
  • Visa-Free Entry: Citizens of over 100 countries are eligible for visa-exempt entry into Oman for stays up to 14 days under certain conditions (passport validity, return ticket, hotel reservation, health insurance). GCC nationals are visa-exempt and can enter freely.

Short practical rule: If you are a UAE resident with a non-GCC passport and plan a short, spontaneous weekend, you can usually rely on visa on arrival. If you plan to remain longer than two weeks, or want the comfort of pre-approval, apply for an eVisa through the ROP portal ahead of departure.

Passport and Residency Validity Requirements

Oman and UAE authorities expect travellers to carry documentation with sufficient validity. General guidelines:

  • Passport: At least six months’ validity remaining is the common requirement for visitors applying for visas or entering Oman.
  • UAE Residency: As a UAE resident, your residency visa should be valid (often at least three months beyond travel date is recommended) if you rely on residency status to obtain border visas.

Before travel, verify your exact passport/residency status relative to the eVisa rules for your nationality. If in doubt, phone the Royal Oman Police eVisa call centre for confirmation.

Fees to Expect

Typical fees you will encounter at the border or online:

  • Visa on arrival fee: Nominal (often a small Omani rial amount, e.g., 5 OMR, but this can change — always confirm current rates).
  • UAE exit fee: AED 35 per person is commonly required at UAE departure formalities.
  • Border motor insurance ("Orange Card"): If your car insurance does not cover Oman, you will pay for shortâterm third-party cover at the border (prices vary — often around AED 100–150 for a minimum period).
  • eVisa processing fees: These depend on visa type and nationality and are paid online.

Step-By-Step Border Crossing Procedure

What Happens At The UAE Exit Post

The practical crossing sequence is straightforward but follows fixed steps:

  1. Approaching the UAE exit point: Have passport, Emirates ID (if resident) and vehicle documents handy.
  2. Pay the UAE exit fee (if applicable); the border staff will collect AED 35 per person as you leave.
  3. Proceed to Omani immigration: Park at the Omani immigration post; all travellers leave the car for stamping.
  4. Obtain visa: If eligible for visa on arrival, present passport and residency documents and pay the fee. If you already hold an eVisa, show the confirmation.
  5. Secure motor insurance for Oman if needed: If your UAE policy excludes Oman, you must buy the short-term third-party cover (orange card) at the border or via kiosks before continuing.
  6. Vehicle inspection: Customs may perform a brief check of the car. Once cleared, you enter Oman territory.

This process is efficient at most crossings during non-peak hours but expect delays during weekends, holidays, or the Khareef season when many tourists travel.

Border Post Practicalities and Opening Hours

Each border post may operate different hours. Major crossings are typically open during daylight and into the evening, but remote posts can close earlier. Before you travel, check the specific crossing’s opening times and any seasonal advisories.

Choosing Your Border Crossing and Route

Major Crossings From UAE Into Oman

There are five frequently used land crossings between the UAE and Oman, each suited to different itineraries. The choice of crossing determines driving time, scenery and availability of onward roads.

Hatta / Al Wajajah (via E44)

  • Best choice from Dubai for Muscat or northern Oman journeys.
  • Closest crossing to Dubai (roughly 1.5–2 hours from central Dubai).
  • Scenic drive through the Hajar Mountains before joining Omani roads toward Sohar and Muscat.
  • Pros: Shortest drive from Dubai, straightforward route. Cons: Busy at weekends and holidays.

Al Darah (Route E11 via Ras Al Khaimah to Musandam)

  • Ideal for visitors heading to Musandam’s Khasab peninsula and northern coastal fjord scenery.
  • Pros: Direct route to Musandam exclave; excellent for dolphin cruises and snorkeling. Cons: Musandam is an exclave; planning ferry/boat trips or local transfers may be needed.

Dibba/Khatmat Milaha (via Fujairah, E99)

  • Serves eastern coastal access, linking to Dibba and coastal towns south toward Sohar.
  • Pros: A coastal route that leads toward Fujairah and the Gulf of Oman shoreline. Cons: Longer if your final destination is Muscat from Dubai.

Mezyad (via Al Ain, Route E40)

  • Works well for southern Oman access, including routes to Dhofar/Salalah.
  • Pros: Best exit for longer drives south. Cons: More remote and a longer initial drive from Dubai.

Khatm Al Shiklah / Meyzed options around Al Ain

  • These crossings suit travelers stopping through Al Ain or heading into central Oman and the Jebel Akhdar / Jebel Shams area.
  • Pros: Good for travelers combining Al Ain sightseeing with Oman entry. Cons: Some crossings are quieter but may require extra local driving.

Rather than summarise each crossing in a table, weigh them by the region you intend to visit: Musandam = Al Darah/Dibba; Muscat and northern Oman = Hatta/Al Wajajah or Khatmat Milaha; Southern Oman = Mezyad.

Drive Times And Distances

Approximate one-way driving times from central Dubai:

  • Dubai to Hatta/Al Wajajah crossing: 90–120 minutes
  • Dubai to Muscat (via Hatta border): 4.5–6 hours total driving time depending on stops
  • Dubai to Khasab (Musandam, via Ras Al Khaimah/Al Darah): 2.5–4 hours depending on route
  • Dubai to Salalah (southern Oman): This is a multi-day drive (1,200+ km) and requires advanced planning.

Vehicle and Insurance Rules

Driving Your Own Car From Dubai To Oman

You can drive your privately owned vehicle from Dubai to Oman, but have these documents ready:

  • Original passport and Emirates ID (for UAE residents)
  • Vehicle registration card (Mulkiya)
  • Valid driving licence (UAE driving licence accepted)
  • Proof of ownership (if applicable)
  • Car insurance covering Oman (orange card or explicit GCC coverage in policy)
  • No Objection Certificate (NOC) if driving a vehicle not registered in your name

If your UAE policy includes Oman coverage, request proof (an “orange card” certificate from your insurer) before departure. Many large UAE insurers include GCC territory coverage as part of comprehensive packages; contact your insurer in advance.

If you do not have cross-border coverage, you can purchase thirdâparty insurance at the border. The border insurance typically covers third-party liability only, not damage to your own vehicle.

Renting a Car in Dubai For Oman Travel

Not all Dubai rental companies permit their vehicles to be taken into Oman. Rental conditions vary and may require:

  • An explicit No Objection Certificate (NOC) from the rental company granting permission to cross the border.
  • Additional daily charges or deposits for cross-border travel.
  • Specific vehicle categories (4x4s for off-road or mountainous travel may be limited).
  • Insurance add-ons to cover Oman territory.

Before booking, confirm the rental company’s policy in writing and obtain the NOC. If you’re planning off-road travel in Oman, rent a high-clearance 4x4 and confirm that the rental agreement permits off-road driving and use in Oman. If the company refuses cross-border travel, do not attempt to cross with a rented vehicle — you risk fines and insurance invalidation.

Driving a Borrowed or Company Car

If you drive a car borrowed from a friend, family member or employer, you must carry a notarised NOC from the owner. The NOC should:

  • Be signed and dated, include owner’s identification details (ID number/Passport), vehicle details (registration number and chassis), and permission statement for you to drive the vehicle into Oman.
  • Be translated to Arabic if possible and, when asked, show a copy of the owner’s ID and registration.

Some crossings may be stricter than others about the format of NOCs, so prepare a clear, concise document and copies of supporting ID to avoid delays.

Documentation Checklist

Use this single, compact checklist before you depart — having all paperwork streamlined at the border removes friction.

  • Passport (minimum six months validity)
  • Emirates ID (for UAE residents)
  • Airline ticket or return ticket (if required by visa rules)
  • Hotel booking confirmation or proof of accommodation
  • Vehicle registration (Mulkiya)
  • Valid driving licence (UAE or international as applicable)
  • Car insurance proof covering Oman (or cash/card to buy border insurance)
  • No Objection Certificate (NOC) when driving a non-owned vehicle
  • eVisa printout or eVisa confirmation (if you applied in advance)
  • Sufficient local currency or card for fees and border purchases
  • Emergency phone numbers and ROP eVisa contact if needed

Practical Driving Advice and Road Rules

Road Conditions And Driving Culture

Roads between the UAE and Oman are generally well maintained. Main highways are smooth and signposted, but mountain roads in the Hajar range are narrower, with sharp bends and occasional landslides after heavy rains. In remote areas, cell reception can be intermittent — download offline maps or carry a physical map if you intend off-grid exploration.

Speed limits are enforced by cameras in many areas; fines can be heavy. Police and customs inspections at borders are routine and respectful — remain calm, follow instructions, and keep documentation ready.

Safety Equipment And Preparation

For mountain or desert travel, prepare basic emergency equipment: water, spare tyre, first aid kit, phone charger and a copy of car documents. Petrol stations are frequent on main routes but sparse on remote roads; fill up before long stretches.

Seasonal Considerations

  • Khareef (Monsoon) Season in Dhofar (Salalah): June–September brings lush landscapes, cloud cover and heavier traffic to Salalah. Road conditions vary — verify weather and road advisories.
  • Summer Heat: Temperatures can be extreme; ensure vehicle air conditioning is reliable and carry extra water.
  • Ramadan: Service hours for restaurants and attractions change; observe local customs and refrain from eating or drinking in public during daylight hours in Ramadan.

Weekend Escape: Muscat In 48 Hours

Day Trip: Musandam Dolphin Cruise (Khasab)

Early morning: Drive north via Ras Al Khaimah to the Al Darah/Dibba crossing to reach Khasab. Mid-morning to early afternoon: Join a dhow cruise for dolphins and snorkeling. Late afternoon: Return to Dubai.

Best for: Nature-focused day trip without the commitment of long-distance driving; requires early start and punctual ferry/cruise bookings.

Day 3–4: Head inland toward Nizwa and Jabal Akhdar or Jebel Shams for mountain scenery and hiking (note off-road segments and permits if required). Day 5–7+: For a deep southern experience, continue to Salalah (long stretches; consider overnight stops) to experience Khareef season or coastal landscapes.

Tickets, Buses and Flights — Alternatives To Driving

Buses

Intercity and cross-border buses have operated on some Dubai-Oman routes, but services can be suspended or limited depending on bilateral transport agreements and seasonal demand. Private operators sometimes run scheduled trips between Dubai and Muscat via Hatta; check current availability before planning. Note that in recent seasons official Dubai–Muscat bus operations were intermittent.

Flights

Flying remains the fastest option. Dubai to Muscat flights take about one hour and are frequent on major carriers. If you prefer to arrive quickly and avoid car paperwork, flying is sensible — then rent a car locally in Oman if you want to drive within the Sultanate.

Ferries and Coastal Transfers

For Musandam travel, consider ferry or dhow options from ports closer to Ras Al Khaimah or local operators in Khasab. If you wish to combine a boat-based experience with your overland plan, align crossing times and bookings in advance.

Customs, Currency and Local Etiquette

Currency and Payments

The Omani rial (OMR) is the local currency. Many businesses accept cards in cities and tourist hubs, but cash is useful for small purchases, parking or remote areas. ATMs are available in Muscat and major towns; rural areas may have limited access.

Alcohol and Prohibited Items

Oman has conservative alcohol laws; alcohol sales are limited to licensed hotels and certain venues. Carry only legally obtained alcohol and avoid public drinking. Import rules can be strict regarding medication — always carry prescriptions and check the legal status of medications before travel. Do not carry cannabis or narcotics; penalties are severe.

Cultural Respect

Dress modestly in public spaces — for men and women; women are commonly expected to cover shoulders and knees in non-resort public areas. During prayer times or at religious sites, follow posted instructions. Be polite and patient with border officials; politeness speeds processing.

Common Problems And How To Avoid Them

I Forgot My Insurance (Orange Card)

If you arrive at the border without Oman coverage on your UAE insurance, purchase the border thirdâparty policy. It covers third-party liability but not your own vehicle’s damage. If you require comprehensive coverage, arrange it with your insurer before departure.

My Rental Company Refuses Cross-Border Travel

Do not cross the border without explicit written permission. If a rental firm refuses, either switch to a firm that allows cross-border travel or fly to Oman and rent there. Crossing without authorization risks insurance invalidation and fines.

NOC Rejected

If a border officer questions an NOC, remain calm and provide supporting identification and vehicle registration. If necessary, contact the vehicle owner to verify permission. As a best practice, bring both a free-form NOC and a notarised signature where possible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get an Omani visa at the land border if I’m a UAE resident with a non-GCC passport?

Yes. Many UAE residents with non-GCC passports can obtain a visa on arrival at the land border for a short stay (commonly up to 14 days). If you plan to stay longer, apply for an eVisa in advance.

What paperwork do I need to drive my car from Dubai to Oman?

Carry your passport, Emirates ID, vehicle registration (mulkiya), driving licence, proof of insurance covering Oman (orange card or insurer confirmation), and an NOC if you’re driving a non-owned car. Bring printed or digital copies of eVisa confirmations if you applied online.

Can I take a rental car from Dubai into Oman?

Only if the rental company permits cross-border travel and issues an NOC. Confirm permission in writing, understand any fees, and ensure the rental insurance covers Oman. If the rental company refuses, do not cross the border with their vehicle.

Which border crossing is best for a quick trip to Muscat from Dubai?

The Hatta / Al Wajajah crossing via E44 is the most practical and fastest route from Dubai to Muscat. It offers a direct drive through the Hajar mountains and is commonly used by travellers doing weekend trips.
