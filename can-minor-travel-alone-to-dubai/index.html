Can Minor Travel Alone to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Families across the Gulf increasingly face the practical question: can a minor travel alone to Dubai? Flights between Saudi Arabia and the UAE remain among the region’s busiest, and more parents are weighing independent travel for older children — whether for education, family reunions, or short holiday trips. Clear rules, careful paperwork and the right airline service make the difference between a stressful crossing and a smooth, confident arrival.

Short answer: Yes — minors can travel alone to Dubai, but it depends on a mix of immigration requirements, airline policies, and the documentation you provide. Some airlines mandate an unaccompanied-minor service for certain age ranges or for flights to Dubai, and UAE immigration may expect a parental consent letter plus proof of parental identity and the receiving adult’s details. Preparing these elements in advance prevents delays and potential refusal at the airport.

This article explains, step by step, what families and guardians must know and do before, during, and after a minor travels alone to Dubai. You will find practical checklists, the paperwork that immigration and airlines commonly require, how to book unaccompanied-minor services, what to expect at departure and arrival airports, and safety and legal precautions that protect the child and the adults involved. My goal is to give you the blueprint for planning a secure, legally compliant solo trip for a minor between Saudi Arabia and Dubai so you can travel with confidence.

The Legal Foundation: Immigration Rules and Consent

Why paperwork matters more than you might expect

Airports and immigration authorities prioritize the protection of children. For minors traveling without both parents present, the concern is simple: how to verify consent and prevent trafficking or irregular migration. Dubai (and the wider UAE) has tightened checks in recent years; airlines and border officers will ask for clear evidence that the minor has permission to travel and that a responsible adult will receive them on arrival.

Who is considered a “minor” for travel purposes?

Different agencies and airlines use slightly different age brackets, so you must confirm specifics with your airline and relevant embassy. Generally, a “minor” means anyone under 18. For airline unaccompanied-minor services, many carriers draw lines (for example, “young passengers” 12–15, “unaccompanied minors” 5–11, and some apply special rules up to 18 for particular routes). Those distinctions determine whether a paid escort service is required.

What immigration typically requires for a minor entering the UAE

When a minor is arriving alone, immigration officers most often ask for:

  • The minor’s valid passport (and visa if required for their nationality).
  • A parental consent letter authorizing travel and naming the adult who will meet the child at arrival.
  • Copies of the parents’ passports and, when relevant, their residency permits.
  • Proof of the receiving adult’s identity and contact information in the UAE.
  • Birth certificate or other proof of relationship when the name differences could cause confusion.

Because rules vary by nationality and circumstances, families should treat this list as a checklist to prepare around rather than an exhaustive legal guarantee.

Special legal considerations for Saudi residents and citizens

Saudi nationals travelling to the UAE typically benefit from visa-free entry, but the requirement for consent letters still applies when a child travels without both parents. Saudi residents who are non-citizens must ensure their residency status and visa arrangements meet both Saudi exit and UAE entry requirements — which can include a parent’s passport copy and a signed travel consent document.

Airline Policies: Booking and Unaccompanied-Minor Services

Why the airline rules are as important as immigration rules

Even if immigration will allow the minor to enter, an airline may refuse to carry an unaccompanied child who does not meet its age guidelines or who lacks the required paperwork. Airlines control the carriage of passengers; their unaccompanied-minor (UM) procedures define how a child will be supervised from check-in to handover at arrival.

Typical airline age rules and common variations

Airlines fall into three broad categories regarding minors:

  • Strict UM Service Required: Some airlines require UM service for minors on certain international routes up to age 18 or another high threshold. For example, select carriers have extended UM requirements on flights specifically into Dubai.
  • Conditional UM Service: Other airlines treat children under a lower age (often under 12 or 16) as eligible for UM services, which parents can request if they want extra assistance.
  • No Formal UM Service Above a Certain Age: A few carriers consider travelers 16 or 17 as adults for flight purposes, permitting solo travel without the UM procedure.

Because the thresholds differ widely — and because fees, booking deadlines and handover rules differ — always check airline policy while booking and call the airline to confirm that they will accept a minor for the intended flight.

How to book and what to expect from UM services

When booking a UM service you should:

  • Notify the airline well in advance; many require booking the UM service before travel and may limit the number of unaccompanied minors on a single flight.
  • Expect a fee that varies by carrier and by whether the route is domestic or international.
  • Bring the signed and completed UM forms the airline provides; multiple printed copies are often requested at check-in.
  • Arrive early: airlines typically ask guardians to show up earlier than normal for in-person handover and document verification.

In-flight care usually includes a cabin crew assigned to look after the minor, seating nearer the crew, and assistance through transit if direct connections are permitted by the airline. Some airlines do not accept UM transits that require an overnight stay.

Preparing the Necessary Documents

The essential documents every traveling minor must carry

Before departure, make sure the child has these core documents securely in hand luggage:

  1. Valid passport (with at least six months validity recommended).
  2. Valid visa for the UAE when required by nationality, or evidence of visa-free eligibility.
  3. Parental consent letter signed by one or both parents (see recommended contents below).
  4. Copies of parents’ passports or national IDs.
  5. Birth certificate or legal proof of guardianship if names differ.
  6. Airline unaccompanied-minor form and booking confirmation.
  7. Contact information and ID copy of the adult receiving the child in Dubai.
  8. Any required medical documents, vaccination certificates or medication authorizations.

(For clarity, an expanded step-by-step checklist with supporting details appears in the next section.)

What to include in a parental consent letter

A consent letter should be clear, specific and signed. Typical contents are:

  • Full name, date of birth and passport number of the minor.
  • Full names, passport or national ID numbers of the parents/guardians.
  • Statement explicitly authorizing the minor to travel alone to the UAE and naming the dates and flight numbers where possible.
  • Full name, passport or ID, contact information, and UAE address of the person who will receive the minor on arrival.
  • A statement authorizing airport/airline staff to assist the minor for the duration of travel.
  • Contact phone numbers and email addresses for parents/guardians and the receiving adult.
  • Notarization and any required legal authentication (see next subsection).

Sample language should be simple and direct and translated into Arabic if requested by authorities.

Notarization and legalization: when it’s required

Some countries and airlines demand notarized consent letters; others expect further legalization through a foreign ministry or an embassy stamp. The safest approach for international travel is to have a consent letter notarized by a local notary public, authenticated by the national foreign ministry if needed, and if applicable, attested by the UAE embassy or consulate.

If you are traveling from Saudi Arabia, check whether your local notary and the Saudi Ministry of Foreign Affairs require additional steps before the document will be accepted by UAE authorities. When in doubt, obtain both notarization and legalization — the extra effort prevents last-minute refusals.

Visa and residency nuances for minors

Visa requirements for the UAE depend on nationality. Some passports receive visas on arrival; others need a pre-arranged visa. Minors who are not citizens of the country they are departing from (for example, a child who is a foreign resident of Saudi Arabia) must carry residency documentation plus any visa required by the UAE. Work with your chosen visa pathway — an e-visa, visa on arrival or sponsored visa — well in advance of travel.

Pre-Departure Checklist: Step-by-Step

  1. Confirm the airline’s unaccompanied-minor policy for the specific route and book the UM service when required. Request seat assignments near the crew and receive the UM confirmation document.
  2. Prepare a signed parental consent letter with all contact details and have it notarized; legalize or apostille if your nationality or the airline asks for it.
  3. Assemble photocopies of both parents’ passports and any legal guardianship documents; include a certified copy of the child’s birth certificate.
  4. Verify visa requirements for the child’s nationality and secure the visa if necessary. Print and carry a hard copy.
  5. Complete and print the airline’s UM form in multiple copies. Keep originals and spare copies in hand luggage.
  6. Notify both departure and arrival airports (and the receiving adult) of the travel time and coordinate the handover plan, including airport meeting point and contingency contacts.
  7. Pack a clear folder with all documents, a small comfort kit (snacks, water bottle empty at security, a change of clothes), and emergency contact cards for the child.
  8. Secure travel insurance that covers minors and includes medical evacuation or emergency contacts; ensure the receiving adult has a copy.

Use this checklist as your operational blueprint — missing any single item can result in the airline refusing carriage or immigration delaying entry.

At the Airport: Check-In, Handover, and Departure

Arriving early and the check-in process

Plan to arrive at least three hours before international departures where a minor is traveling alone. Airlines typically require extra time to verify paperwork and to brief the child about the process. At check-in, staff will:

  • Verify the minor’s passport, visa and consent letter.
  • Collect and process the UM form.
  • Take contact details of the receiving adult at arrival and confirm handover arrangements.
  • Provide a boarding pass and may issue a wristband or identification tag for the minor.

Expect airport staff to be meticulous. Their priority is ensuring clear handoff points and documented responsibility.

Security, boarding and in-flight care

Airport security procedures are identical for minors, but staff will escort unaccompanied minors through boarding. Cabin crew make sure the child is seated near them and introduce themselves to establish rapport. Some airlines brief minors about safety protocols and offer an in-flight escort.

If the flight has connections, confirm whether the airline will handle transit handovers. Some carriers do not transfer unaccompanied minors between airlines or terminals, and joint operations across carriers can complicate transit — especially if an overnight stay is involved.

What the sending adult must do during handover

When handing over the child, the sending adult should:

  • Present the original signed consent letter and a photo ID.
  • Stay at the airport until the aircraft has departed if the airline requests it.
  • Provide any medicines with clear instructions and a medical authorization note if necessary.
  • Ensure the minor understands who will meet them and how to contact that person.

Airline staff will take custody of the minor only after confirming identity and documentation. This is the crucial legal exchange — do not leave before staff confirms the child is accepted on board.

Arrival in Dubai: Immigration, Handover and Aftercare

Arrival procedures and the receiving adult’s responsibilities

Upon touchdown, airline staff often supervise the unmanned minor through immigration and hand them to the designated receiving adult in the arrivals hall. The receiving adult must present their ID and the authorization named in the consent letter, and may be asked to sign a handover receipt.

Receiving adults should arrive at the airport ahead of the plane’s landing time and have the contact number for both the airline’s arrival desk and the departure airport for contingencies. If the receiving adult misses the flight, airline staff will initiate their contingency procedures, which can include returning the minor to a holding area or, in extreme cases, arranging temporary accommodation until an authorized adult arrives.

Common issues and how to avoid them

The most frequent causes of delay or refusal are missing or unsigned consent letters, mismatched names, or insufficient contact details for the receiving adult. Resolve these by:

  • Using the same name formats across all documents (passport names first and last, no nicknames).
  • Ensuring the receiving adult’s contact number is local and active.
  • Having multiple printed copies of every document and a digital backup accessible via email.

If immigration officers suspect insufficient documentation, they may detain the minor temporarily for verification. Avoid this by pre-empting requests and presenting clear, authenticated paperwork.

Transit, Layovers and Connecting Flights

When connections complicate unaccompanied travel

Not all airlines will accept responsibility for unaccompanied minors who must transit through intermediate airports, especially when changing airlines or terminals. Rules typically prohibit UM transits that require overnight stays or passenger transfer through unsecured areas.

When planning a connecting itinerary for a minor:

  • Prefer direct flights whenever possible.
  • If a connection is unavoidable, use the same carrier for both legs or confirm a formal interline agreement and UM transfer protocol.
  • Confirm in writing that ground staff at the transit airport will provide continuous care.

Transit visa and immigration concerns

Some transit paths require the minor to clear immigration (for example, if they must exit the sterile transit area). If that is the case, ensure the child has the necessary visa for the transit country, and confirm that the receiving staff at the transit airport are prepared to supervise the minor.

Special Situations and Edge Cases

Minor traveling with a single parent or a relative

When a child travels with only one parent or with a relative who is not a legal guardian, airlines and immigration typically expect a signed letter from the absent parent authorizing the travel. This letter should include the absent parent’s passport copy and contact details. The more complete and documented the permission, the fewer complications you will face.

Minors of different nationalities or with complex residency status

If the minor’s passport differs from the parents’ passports or if the child and parents have different nationalities or residencies, carry certified proof of guardianship and any court orders that clarify custody. In international family situations, be prepared for extra scrutiny and longer processing times.

Medical needs, special assistance and disability considerations

If the minor requires medication, an inhaler, or a medical device, prepare a medical letter signed by a physician and a treatment plan to present to airline personnel. For special assistance (for example, a child with autism who needs particular support), inform the airline in advance so they can arrange suitable measures and staff briefings.

Practical Tips for a Smooth Solo Trip

Communication and technology

Equip the child with more than one way to contact adults: a prepaid local SIM card on arrival, a phone with roaming enabled, and a printed contact card with names and phone numbers. Teach the child how to identify airline staff and airport security, and practice simple scripts for them to use: “I am traveling to Dubai alone. My receiving adult is [name].”

Dress, behavior and comfort

Choose comfortable clothing and pack a small comfort kit that the child can manage independently. Avoid valuables in carry-on aside from documents, and instruct the child on basic in-flight etiquette and what to do if they become anxious or separated.

Contingency planning

Create a contingency plan and share it with the child and the receiving adult. Include backups: alternate contacts if the primary receiving adult cannot make it, embassy numbers (if relevant), and the airline’s emergency line.

Travel insurance

Purchase travel insurance that explicitly covers minors traveling alone, including medical coverage and trip interruption benefits. Confirm whether the policy covers delayed handovers or emergency guardianship arrangements.

Mistakes Families Make — And How to Avoid Them

Relying on verbal permission only

Do not assume a phone call from a parent will be accepted at immigration. Always provide the proper signed and, when required, notarized authorizations.

Booking without confirming airline policy in writing

Verification over the phone is useful, but request a written confirmation or email that the airline accepts the minor on that flight with the UM service booked.

Ignoring name format consistency

Inconsistencies between passports, birth certificates and consent letters (for example, a middle name omitted in one document) trigger delays. Maintain identical name formats across all documents.

Overlooking time zones and layover times

A short connection might be feasible for an adult but unrealistic for an unaccompanied child. Avoid tight connections and overnight layovers.

Conclusion

Sending a minor to Dubai alone is entirely possible with the correct preparation: verify airline UM policies, secure notarized consent letters with the receiving adult’s details, obtain visas if required, carry verified ID documents and airline forms, and coordinate a precise handover plan. These steps protect the child and the adults involved, and they turn uncertainty into a reliable travel process.

FAQ

1) Does a minor need a visa to enter Dubai?

Visa rules depend on the minor’s nationality. Some passports allow visa-free entry or visas on arrival; others require a pre-issued visa. Check the UAE entry requirements that apply to the child’s passport well before travel and secure a visa if necessary.

2) Can an airline refuse to carry my child even with a consent letter?

Yes. Airlines set their own carriage policies and can refuse travel if a minor does not meet their UM policy, if documentation is incomplete, or if staff capacity for UM care on that flight has been reached. Always confirm UM acceptance and obtain booking confirmation.

3) What should the receiving adult present at Dubai airport?

The receiving adult should present their original ID or passport and be the person named in the consent letter. Immigration or airline staff may ask for additional ID or a printed copy of the testament of authorization; being prompt and having matching documentation prevents delays.

4) Are special documents required if the minor has a different surname than the parents?

When surnames differ, a birth certificate or legal custody documents help prove relationship. A notarized consent letter that explains the discrepancy and includes copies of both parents’ IDs reduces the chance of complications.

For any specific situations — complex custody arrangements, special medical needs, or complicated multi-carrier itineraries — consult with the airline and consult local consular guidance well in advance so your planning is airtight.
