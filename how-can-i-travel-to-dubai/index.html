How Can I Travel to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow

Introduction

Dubai continues to draw millions of visitors each year, with the city’s skyline, beaches, and experiences offering something for every traveler. Whether you’re leaving from Riyadh or Jeddah, moving across the Gulf, or planning your first long-haul trip, understanding the practical steps—visa rules, flights, and local logistics—will turn curiosity into a smooth, confident trip.

Short answer: You can travel to Dubai by air on frequent scheduled flights from across the region and the world, and many nationalities qualify for a visa on arrival or simple pre-arranged e-visas. Make sure your passport has at least six months’ validity, check whether your nationality requires a preapproved visa, book an airline or package that supports your entry permit, and plan the essentials—accommodation, travel insurance, and a local SIM—before departure.

Why Dubai Is So Accessible Right Now

Strong air connectivity

Dubai is one of the world’s most connected cities. Major carriers and budget airlines operate nonstop flights from most Gulf capitals and large international hubs, and the city’s two airports (Dubai International DXB and Dubai World Central DWC) facilitate high-frequency services. For travelers based in Saudi Arabia, daily flights make short trips feasible and weekend getaways convenient.

Modern immigration processes

The UAE’s visa framework is flexible: many nationalities receive visas on arrival, others can obtain e-visas through airline partners or the UAE official portals, and GCC citizens can travel using national ID cards in many cases. Immigration officers conduct routine checks, and a simple pre-arranged visa or an airline-assisted service is often enough for most leisure travelers.

Year-round choice of experiences

With warm winters and bustling indoor options, Dubai is a year-round destination. Whether you’re visiting for architecture, beaches, theme parks, shopping, cultural sites, or business events, the infrastructure makes moving from one experience to the next efficient.

Who Needs a Visa and How To Get One

Basic rules everyone should know

All travelers must present a passport valid for at least six months from the date of entry. Visa rules depend on nationality, purpose, and length of stay. Short tourist visits for many passport holders are handled with a visa on arrival; others require a pre-arranged visa or a visa assistance service offered by airlines. GCC citizens have different entry rules and may not need visas at all.

For a centralized and up-to-date overview of eligibility and visa types, you should consult the official UAE guidance and the authoritative country-specific listings on our regional resource hub.

Visa On Arrival: who typically qualifies

A large number of countries are eligible for a 30-day visa on arrival; certain European countries, the United States, and others qualify for 90-day multi-entry stamps depending on agreements in place. A small but growing set of nationalities (for example, eligible Indian passport holders with a valid US visa or green card) may receive special short-term arrival permissions under recent policy updates.

Keep these practical points in mind: passport must be machine-readable and valid for six months; GCC residence permit holders may need to pre-apply for a visa depending on the policy changes; and if you expect to extend beyond an initial tourist stamp, plan ahead because visa extensions are discretionary and have administrative requirements.

Prearranged and airline-assisted visas

If your nationality requires a prearranged visa, you have several routes:

  • Apply directly through official UAE portals or visa centers.
  • Use an airline-assisted visa service when you book with carriers like Emirates or flydubai, which allow certain passengers to upload documents and receive an electronic visa tied to the ticket.
  • Ask a UAE sponsor (hotel, tour company, or resident family member) to apply on your behalf.

Be prepared to present a scanned passport bio page, a passport photo, proof of hotel booking or tenancy, and your return flight itinerary. Fees and processing times vary, but many tourist visas are issued within a few working days when documentation is complete.

Common visa types and typical requirements

Tourist entry permits are commonly offered as short-term single-entry (30 days) and longer single- or multiple-entry permissions (60 days or more) with explicit extension options for some categories. Transit and short-stay visas (48-hour or 96-hour) are available for travelers using Dubai as a stopover.

When arranging a visa, carefully match your intended travel dates with the visa validity window, and keep a printed copy of your visa confirmation during travel—immigration officers sometimes request a hard copy at screening points.

From Saudi Arabia to Dubai: The Most Practical Routes

Flights: the primary option for most travelers

Flying is the simplest, fastest route from Saudi Arabia and well-supported with multiple daily options from Riyadh, Jeddah, Dammam and other cities. Major carriers include Emirates, flydubai, Saudia, and regional low-cost airlines. Flight times are typically around 1.5 to 3 hours depending on departure city.

If you’re departing from Riyadh or Jeddah you’ll find priority morning and evening flights; if your schedule is flexible, traveling mid-week often produces better fares. For business travelers or short stays, the late-night red-eye arrivals into Dubai can maximize daytime hours.

For specific departure-city planning and local airport information, check our guidance on flights from Riyadh and nonstop services from Jeddah.

Overland travel and GCC travel status

GCC citizens enjoy simplified border formalities and often may cross into the UAE with national ID or passport; visa requirements differ for non-GCC nationals. Overland crossings require valid travel documents and, in most cases, a pre-planned route and vehicle documentation. If you’re considering driving from Saudi Arabia, verify the latest entry rules, insurance requirements, and whether temporary import permits are needed.

Sea travel and yacht access

A specialized option for private yacht owners and charterers exists at Dubai’s marinas, and regular ferry and cruise services operate seasonally to and from the region. These services are niche and require advance booking and customs clearance.

Step-By-Step: How To Arrange Your Entry (Practical Checklist)

  1. Confirm passport validity (minimum six months).
  2. Check whether your nationality requires a prearranged visa or qualifies for visa on arrival; if needed, apply via the airline or the UAE portal, and save a printed copy.
  3. Book your flight with an airline that supports visa issuance if you need that assistance.
  4. Reserve accommodation and obtain a booking confirmation (often requested for visa applications).
  5. Purchase travel insurance covering health and trip interruption.
  6. Prepare any medical documentation for controlled medications and check MOHAP guidelines if you carry prescription drugs.
  7. Plan airport transfers: book an airport taxi, arrange ride-hailing, or confirm car rental logistics.
  8. Pack adaptors, local currency (AED), and a copy of contact details for your country’s embassy or consulate.

This ordered checklist covers the high-impact tasks that prevent last-minute problems: visas, documented bookings, and health and insurance preparedness.

Documents You Should Carry (Quick Reference)

  • Passport (original) with at least six months’ validity.
  • Printed visa confirmation or evidence of visa on arrival eligibility.
  • Hotel booking confirmation or tenancy agreement if staying with someone.
  • Return or onward flight ticket.
  • Travel insurance policy number with emergency medical coverage.
  • Prescriptions and medical permits for any controlled medicines.
  • Emergency contact numbers and a copy of your passport stored separately (electronic and paper).

Where To Stay: Neighborhoods and Why They Matter

Downtown Dubai and the Burj Khalifa area

If your priority is iconic views, close access to major attractions like the Dubai Mall and the Dubai Fountain, and proximity to upmarket dining, Downtown is a high-value option. Expect higher rates but walkable access to several tourist venues and straightforward Metro connections.

Dubai Marina and Jumeirah Beach Residences

For beachfront vibes and a long promenade for evening walks, Dubai Marina is ideal. It offers a mix of high-rise hotels, serviced apartments, and dining along the water. This neighborhood is suited to travelers looking for relaxed seaside evenings while still being close to major malls and theme parks via short drives.

Al Barsha and Business Bay

Al Barsha (near Mall of the Emirates) and Business Bay are practical mid-range choices with good hotel variety, family-friendly accommodation, and reasonable access to Dubai’s road network. Both areas cater well to business travelers mixing leisure activities.

Historic districts: Al Fahidi and Deira

For travelers who prioritize culture, markets (souks), and a more traditional feel, Al Fahidi and Deira offer narrow lanes, museums, and budget-friendly hotels. These neighborhoods give a sense of daily Emirati urban life and easy abra (water taxi) rides.

Choosing a neighborhood should align with the reason for your trip: sightseeing and central convenience, beachfront leisure, family amenities, or budget-minded exploration.

Getting Around Dubai

Public transit: Metro, trams, and buses

Dubai’s public transport network is modern, efficient, and cost-effective. The Metro covers key corridors including stops near Tourist-centric areas. Trams serve the Marina and Jumeirah neighborhoods, while buses fill in the gaps. Buy a Nol card (rechargeable fare card) to ride across modes.

Using public transit is the fastest way to avoid traffic during peak hours—especially the Metro for Downtown-to-Marina links.

Taxis and ride-hailing

Taxis are plentiful and metered; ride-hailing apps like Careem and Uber also operate and often provide English-language support and fare estimates. For fixed-route journeys and airport transfers, taxis may be the most straightforward option, particularly during late evening hours.

Driving and rentals

Renting a car gives flexibility for day trips, desert excursions, and reaching outlying parks. Remember to factor in Salik toll charges, parking fees, and the sometimes heavy traffic approaching popular attractions. International driving permits are not always required for GCC residents, but outside visitors should confirm requirements with the rental company.

Safe mobility tips

Avoid driving during peak event arrivals if possible, use parking apps to find spaces in busy districts, and prefer the Metro or taxi for single-day, inner-city hops to save time.

Money and Costs: What to Expect

Currency and payment culture

The Dubai currency is the dirham (AED). Card payments are widely accepted, and many venues prefer card or contactless transactions. Small cash denominated in AED is useful for taxis, local markets, and street vendors.

Typical price points

While Dubai has a reputation for luxury, it also accommodates mid-range and budget options. Expect higher prices in Downtown and beachfront hotels, while Deira and neighborhood hotels in Al Barsha offer more economical choices. Street food and casual dining can be very affordable compared to upscale restaurants.

Budgeting for different traveler profiles

A shoestring traveler can manage with smart neighborhood picks and public transport; couples looking for boutique or beachfront experiences will have a moderate to high budget, while luxury seekers should anticipate premium pricing for hotels, dining, and experiences.

Laws, Culture, and Local Expectations

Respectful dress and behavior

Dubai is cosmopolitan, but public modesty is expected in certain contexts. Swimwear is appropriate for beaches and pools but not in public malls or business districts. Dress conservatively when visiting traditional neighborhoods or religious sites.

Alcohol and public conduct

Alcohol is available in licensed venues to adults over 21 (check local rules). Public intoxication, offensive acts, and aggressive behavior can lead to fines or detention. Use licensed venues and avoid public drinking.

Prohibited items and legal risks

The UAE has strict laws on drugs and certain materials; possession of illegal substances carries severe penalties. Medications that contain controlled substances may require prior permits—always check with the UAE health authorities and carry a doctor’s prescription.

Electronic devices and content

Be mindful that some forms of written or visual content that are legal elsewhere may be restricted in the UAE. Avoid importing materials that contravene local moral or religious standards.

Understanding these legal contours keeps your trip smooth; when in doubt, err on the side of caution and check with official guidance.

Health, Insurance, and Medical Preparations

Travel insurance is essential

Medical costs can be high, and travel insurance that covers medical evacuation and COVID-related care is highly recommended. Verify coverage levels for inpatient care, evacuation, and pre-existing conditions.

Medication rules

Controlled medications often require documentation or permits prior to travel. Bring a doctor’s prescription and, when possible, a medical certificate. For complex treatments, contact the UAE Ministry of Health and Prevention for prior authorization.

Hospitals and emergency response

Dubai has modern hospitals and clinics with English-speaking staff. Keep local emergency numbers handy and the contact details for your embassy or consulate should you require consular assistance.

Food, Dining and Practical Etiquette

Dining diversity

Dubai’s food scene spans street shawarma stands to Michelin-starred restaurants. Try local flavors at traditional Emirati restaurants and sample the wide range of international cuisines available.

Tipping and service

Tipping is appreciated but not mandatory; many restaurants include a service charge. For taxis and small services, leaving a small amount is common practice.

Halal options and alcohol

Most food is halal by default in many restaurants, and alcohol is sold only in licensed venues. If you’ll be attending events or eating in hotel restaurants, check policies and opening hours in advance.

Day Trips and Nearby Excursions

Abu Dhabi and cultural landmarks

Abu Dhabi is a short drive from Dubai and offers major cultural attractions—from modern museums to national landmarks. For practical day-trip planning and timed-entry venues, review local transit options and book in advance where necessary.

Desert experiences and coastal escapes

Desert safaris, dune bashing, and overnight camps remain popular. Book operators with strong safety records and clear equipment and pickup arrangements. For calmer experiences, coastal towns and nearby emirates provide quieter beaches and alternative scenery.

Short international hops

Dubai is a hub for regional travel. If you plan to extend into neighboring Gulf countries or beyond, factor in visa rules, border controls, and connecting flights from DXB or DWC.

Sample Itineraries (Adaptable)

Three-day urban highlights (first-time visitor)

Day 1: Morning arrival and check-in; afternoon at the Dubai Mall and Burj Khalifa (book timed tickets); evening fountain show and dinner nearby.
Day 2: Marina promenade and boat abra ride; Jumeirah Beach for sunset; Atlantis Palm visit or Aquaventure if traveling with family.
Day 3: Explore Al Fahidi district, Dubai Museum, and souks in Deira; late afternoon shopping or a short desert excursion before departure.

Five-day mixed-pace trip

Days 1–2: Core city attractions (Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall, Downtown, Marina).
Day 3: Abu Dhabi day trip focused on cultural landmarks and the national museum.
Day 4: Desert safari with overnight glamping or morning hot-air balloon and an afternoon at a beach club.
Day 5: Souk exploration, artisanal markets, and a relaxed departure.

Family-friendly 4-day plan

Focus on parks, waterparks, and family attractions: Dubai Aquarium, theme parks at Dubai Parks and Resorts, beach time, and a kid-friendly desert camp. Choose accommodations with family rooms and kitchen facilities.

Adapt these frameworks to travel pace and interests; book high-demand attractions in advance to avoid disappointment.

Money-Saving Strategies and Smart Bookings

Advanced planning and flexible dates unlock savings on flights and hotels. Use mid-week flights for lower fares, consider apartment rentals for families, and buy attraction combination passes for bundled savings. If you’re traveling from Saudi Arabia, monitor both direct carrier promotions and regional low-cost options that run frequent specials.

For airline-linked visa services or combined hotel+visa packages, check whether your ticket qualifies for streamlined visa issuance through the carrier when booking.

Safety and Security: Practical Advice

Dubai is generally safe for tourists, but standard travel prudence applies. Keep digital copies of important documents, avoid leaving valuables in plain view in rented cars, and check local travel advisories before departure. Obtain contact information for your embassy or consulate and be aware of the UAE’s exit controls and legal processes for outstanding civil or criminal cases.

Sustainability and Responsible Travel Notes

Dubai’s tourism sector is developing sustainability programs. When choosing tour operators, look for companies with environmentally responsible practices for desert safaris and cultural tours. Respect local heritage sites by sticking to marked paths and avoiding graffiti or other damaging behaviors that can harm archaeological or natural sites.

Booking Tips and Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Don’t assume visa rules are unchanged: confirm just before booking.
  • Don’t rely solely on hotel staff to resolve visa issues—use official or airline-assisted channels.
  • Don’t pack prohibited medications without checking permit requirements.
  • Avoid last-minute bookings for Burj Khalifa or major attractions during high season—slots fill up quickly.
  • If driving, confirm cross-border insurance and vehicle permits in advance.

Avoiding these pitfalls will save time and reduce stress at immigration and arrival.

Practical Packing Advice

Pack lightweight clothing for daytime, with a few modest options for cultural visits. Carry a small medical kit, adaptors for UAE sockets (Type G), and a refillable water bottle. If you plan desert activities, include a hat, sunscreen, and comfortable walking shoes.

When To Visit: Weather and Crowd Considerations

Dubai’s high season generally runs from late October through April, offering pleasant daytime temperatures and lively outdoor programming. Summer months are hot and humid; however, indoor attractions remain fully operational and often less crowded. For lower hotel rates and quieter attractions, consider shoulder-season travel.

FAQ

Do I need a visa before I fly to Dubai?

That depends on your nationality. Many passport holders receive a visa on arrival, while others must obtain a prearranged visa through an airline, visa center, or UAE portal. Always verify your status well before booking and retain a printed visa confirmation when traveling.

How long should my passport be valid for Dubai travel?

Your passport should be valid for at least six months from the date of entry into the UAE. This is a strict rule at immigration.

What’s the best way to get from Dubai International Airport to the city center?

The Dubai Metro, taxis, and ride-hailing apps all serve DXB effectively. The Metro is cost-effective and avoids traffic, while taxis and ride-hailing offer door-to-door convenience—especially with luggage or at night.

Are there any medications that require paperwork to bring into Dubai?

Yes. Some prescription and controlled medications require a permit or declaration. Bring the original prescription and a medical certificate, and check the UAE health authority’s rules for any restricted substances.
