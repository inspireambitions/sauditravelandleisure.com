How Is Life in Riyadh for Expats

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Introduction

Riyadh is changing fast. Over the last decade the city has expanded economically, culturally, and physically—attracting professionals, families, and adventurers from around the world. For anyone asking "how is life in Riyadh for expats," the honest, practical answer depends on where you start from and what you want to get out of life here: career growth, a family-friendly routine, or a base to explore the Arabian Peninsula.

Short answer: Life in Riyadh is structured, safe, and opportunity-rich, but it requires cultural adjustment and careful planning. Expats benefit from strong salaries and modern services, yet must adapt to local norms, limited alcohol and nightlife options, and a car-centric city layout. With the right expectations and preparation, Riyadh becomes a comfortable, stimulating place to live.

This article will walk you through daily life, legal and logistical realities, social and cultural dynamics, neighborhood choices, schooling and healthcare, ways to make friends and build community, and practical blueprints for moving, settling, and thriving. What Expats Should Expect on Arrival

First Days: Paperwork, Phones, and Orientation

The first 48–72 hours in Riyadh will feel administrative. Your employer or relocation specialist will usually handle most formalities, but knowing the sequence helps you stay in control. Expect to register for a residency permit (iqama) quickly: it is the single document that unlocks banking, phone contracts, a driver’s license transfer, and many household services. Carry multiple passport photos, certified copies of your employment contract, and academic credentials; translations into Arabic are sometimes required.

Before you step on a plane, review your employer’s relocation support. Many companies provide a "fixer" who shepherds new arrivals through the municipal processes. If your company does not offer this, allow additional time for appointments and paperwork during your first week.

Immediate Practicalities: SIM Cards, Banking, and Transport

Set up a local SIM card within the first day or two; Saudi telecom providers offer good coverage and affordable data packages. To open a bank account you will need your passport, iqama (or pending iqama confirmation), and proof of address. Local banks integrate seamlessly with mobile payment systems, which you will quickly adopt for everyday life.

Driving is the dominant mode of transport in Riyadh. If your employer supplies a car and driver, you will have immediate mobility; otherwise, plan to buy or long-term rent a vehicle within the first weeks. Ride-hailing apps are widely used for short-term needs.

Housing and Where Expats Live

Compounds, Villas, and Apartments: Know Your Options

Housing in Riyadh falls into familiar categories: gated expat compounds, stand-alone villas, apartment complexes, and serviced residences. Each has trade-offs in price, privacy, community, and proximity to international schools and business districts.

Living inside a compound offers immediate community and amenities—pools, gyms, playgrounds, housekeeping and security. Compounds are convenient for families and new arrivals who want a soft landing. Villas provide more space and a local experience, often preferred by longer-term residents. Apartments are cost-efficient and better for singles or couples without children.

Many families opt to live near international schools; neighborhoods close to these schools tend to generate steady demand and waiting lists.

Neighborhoods to Consider (Detailed Choices, Pros and Cons)

Al Olaya and Olaya/Kingdom: Central, high-rise living with access to corporate offices, shopping, and restaurants. Ideal for professionals who want short commutes to business hubs. Expect higher rents.

Diplomatic Quarter (DQ): Quiet, green, and secure. The DQ is popular with diplomats and families seeking walkable streets and community events. It’s slightly removed from core commercial districts but offers a calm, high-quality environment.

Al Nakheel and Riyadh North: Family-friendly with several international schools nearby and easy access to malls and services. Villas and larger homes are common.

Peripheral Compounds: Compounds farther from the center offer larger living spaces at lower cost but add commute time. Good option if your employer provides private transport.

Cost of Living and Compensation Expectations

Salaries, Benefits, and Negotiating Your Package

One of the defining features for many expats is the compensation package. Many employers offer tax-free base salaries alongside benefits such as housing allowance, education allowance for children, annual leave tickets home, health insurance, and relocation support. When negotiating, treat the package as a whole: a lower base salary with a strong allowances and housing provision can be preferable to a higher base without benefits.

Be explicit about schooling and healthcare expectations during recruitment. If you have children, secure clarity on school fees—these often form a substantial portion of the employer’s benefits.

Day-to-Day Costs: Food, Utilities, and Domestic Help

Groceries are affordable relative to many Western cities; you can purchase international ingredients and fresh produce across modern supermarkets as well as at specialty souks. Dining out spans the spectrum from inexpensive local eateries to high-end international restaurants. Petrol is inexpensive compared with global prices, which influences the decision to drive rather than rely on transit.

Domestic helpers (nannies, housekeepers, drivers) are commonly hired and can be cost-effective for families. Always follow legal hiring channels: helpers must be sponsored and registered; informal hiring without correct documentation exposes both parties to legal risk.

Work Culture and Professional Life

Office Etiquette and Hierarchies

Riyadh’s work environment places emphasis on hierarchy, respect, and personal relationships. Decision-making can be more centralized than many Western workplaces. Patience and careful relationship-building are highly valued. Expect formal greetings, a degree of ceremony in introductions, and a slower pace for major approvals.

A successful expat professional adapts communication style: be respectful and clear, look for consensus, and be mindful of scheduling around prayer times and public holidays. During Ramadan, business hours commonly change and the pace of the workday may shift.

Sectors with Demand and Career Paths

Riyadh’s economic diversification under Vision 2030 has created demand across finance, construction, engineering, healthcare, education, technology, and renewable energy. Professionals with niche skills in project management, fintech, and specialized engineering are particularly sought-after. Short- to mid-term contracts and consultancy roles are common for expertise-driven placements.

Legal, Residency, and Immigration Essentials

Visas, Work Permits, and the Iqama

Most foreign workers enter on a work visa sponsored by their employer. After arrival, the iqama is issued; it is your residence permit and must be carried at all times. The iqama contains vital information, and you must keep it valid—renewals are employer-driven.

Understand your rights and obligations under the Saudi labor law and confirm whether your employer provides an employment contract in writing. Contracts should specify salary, allowances, probation periods, notice periods, and end-of-service benefits.

Driving Licenses, Pets, and Import Rules

If you plan to drive, convert your license where possible or obtain a Saudi license following local procedures. Bringing pets into the Kingdom requires careful preparation: vaccinations, permits, and quarantine rules apply. Check airline and customs rules well in advance.

Be mindful of strict regulations regarding medications and prohibited goods. Certain medications require a prescribing letter (translated into Arabic) to pass customs checks. Never assume that medication available at home is legal to import without documentation.

Cultural Norms, Dress, and Everyday Etiquette

Public Behavior and Gender Interactions

Saudi social norms are conservative by global standards. Public displays of affection are not acceptable; modest dress is expected. Women's dress codes in public call for modest clothing—many women choose loose-fitting garments and long sleeves; some local women wear the abaya. The environment has evolved, and enforcement varies by area and context. Within private compounds or approved family sections, expectations relax.

Gender segregation still exists in many public settings—designated family and single sections in restaurants, separate queues in certain government offices, and gender-specific events. Observe signage, follow local cues, and ask politely if in doubt.

Religious Observances and Their Daily Impact

Prayer times punctuate the day. Most businesses close briefly during prayer windows. Ramadan brings altered schedules: daylight hours are quieter and dining is private until sunset. Non-Muslims should be respectful: avoid eating or drinking in public during fasting hours in Ramadan and follow local decorum.

Building Community: Making Friends and Social Life

Expat Networks, Local Connections, and Social Clubs

Expats often meet through schools, workplaces, compounds, and community organizations. International clubs, embassy events, and interest-based meet-ups provide reliable ways to expand your social circle. For business networking and social integration, professional associations and community events hosted by international schools are effective.

Making local Saudi friends takes time. Family and clan relationships are central to social life, and invitations to a Saudi home are meaningful and should be honored with respect and punctuality. Approach relationships with curiosity, patience, and cultural sensitivity.

Leisure, Sports, and Nightlife Within Cultural Limits

Riyadh offers a growing array of leisure choices: gyms, private club events, golf courses, desert outings, museums, and shopping centers that double as social hubs. The city's entertainment scene is expanding—concerts, festivals, and cultural events are being hosted more frequently. Remember that late evenings and weekend family outings often center on malls and private social venues rather than bars or clubs.

For out-of-city escapes, Riyadh is a gateway to desert camping, dune driving, and stargazing. For curated heritage excursions and itineraries, consider planning visits to northern archaeological sites and desert heritage locations like AlUla; our desert heritage itineraries section has practical suggestions for planning multi-day trips.

Families and Children: Schools, Activities, and Childcare

Choosing the Right School

Most expat families enroll their children in international schools where instruction is in English and curricula range from British and American to International Baccalaureate. Schools often have waiting lists and high fees; secure placement early and confirm tuition packages and transportation options when negotiating your contract.

Extracurricular activities—sports, music, and scouting—are plentiful within schools and private clubs. For preschool-age children, many compounds offer playgroups and on-site daycare.

Healthcare, Pediatrics, and Insurance

Riyadh’s healthcare infrastructure includes modern hospitals and private clinics with English-speaking staff. Expats typically rely on private health insurance, either provided by their employer or purchased independently. Verify the level of coverage before travel and consider supplementary packages for dental, optical, and repatriation coverage.

Hospitals and clinics often offer pediatric services, but continuity of specialist care may require referrals and careful scheduling. The city’s private medical sector is robust; for more specialised treatment, there are internationally accredited hospitals with expatriate-friendly services.

Practical Blueprints: Moving and Settling In (Step-by-Step)

Below is a focused pre-departure and first-month checklist to convert planning into action.

  • Passport, work visa, and printed employment contract; arrange translations if needed.
  • Medical check-up and complete immunisations; obtain prescriptions with translated doctor’s letter for controlled medications.
  • Arrange temporary accommodation for arrival; confirm iqama process and expected timelines with employer.
  • Pack climate-appropriate clothing, formal wear for work, and modest attire for public settings; bring copies of important documents.
  • Plan banking: decide whether to open a local account immediately; bring proof of address and employer letter for verification.
  • Prepare school documents and transcripts if relocating with children; apply early to international schools.

This checklist will help you structure the critical decisions and avoid common early mistakes.

Transportation: Cars, Drivers, and Public Transit

Buying vs Leasing vs Company Car

Most expats choose to drive. If your package includes a company car, evaluate fuel allowances and maintenance coverage. Buying a car is straightforward with local dealerships and financing. For families, four-wheel drive vehicles are sensible for desert trips and offer greater safety on highways.

If you don’t want to drive, hiring a private driver is a common and practical solution for daily school runs and errands. Drivers can also double as a local guide for navigating the city.

The Riyadh Metro and Future Mobility

The Riyadh Metro is transforming urban mobility; as segments open and operations scale up, expect public transit to become a more viable alternative for commuting in central districts. Until fully implemented, ride-hailing apps and private vehicles remain the dominant options.

Food, Shopping, and Daily Life Rhythms

Where to Shop and How to Find Familiar Foods

Modern supermarkets offer international brands and fresh produce. Specialty stores and souks supply ingredients from South Asia, East Asia, and the Mediterranean. For home comforts, online shopping and delivery services have matured, easing access to hard-to-find items.

Malls provide climate-controlled spaces for shopping and socialising, with a wide food-court selection and restaurants that cater to diverse tastes. For traditional markets and local crafts, plan visits to historic souks and local bazaars outside of the shopping centers.

Eating Out and Food Culture

Riyadh’s dining scene is diverse—Middle Eastern staples like kabsa and shawarma, regional Gulf cuisine, and international restaurants. Family sections in restaurants are common; be mindful when seating yourself. Alcohol is prohibited across the Kingdom; social life and dining adapt around this rule.

Safety, Law, and Risk Management

Personal Safety and Legal Awareness

Riyadh is generally very safe with low violent crime. The primary risks are legal rather than violent: breaches of public conduct laws or importation of prohibited items can lead to severe penalties. Understand local regulations before you act. Keep emergency numbers and embassy contacts handy. If you travel outside major cities, especially for work in remote areas, coordinate with your employer for security arrangements.

Common Legal Pitfalls to Avoid

Avoid public displays considered indecent, do not distribute religious materials in public, and obey strict controls on alcohol and drug possession. Respect traffic laws—speeding and reckless driving can attract fines and legal issues. When hiring domestic workers, always register and sponsor them legally.

Weekend Getaways and Regional Travel

Short Trips From Riyadh

Riyadh’s central location makes it an excellent base for weekend escapes. Desert camping, rock climbing at nearby escarpments, and heritage site visits provide variety. Integration Strategies: How to Feel at Home Faster

Language, Cultural Learning, and Volunteering

Learning basic Arabic phrases quickly opens doors and shows respect. Enroll in a language course, use conversational apps, and practice with local colleagues—small efforts go a long way. Participate in cultural events and community volunteering to meet both Saudis and long-term residents. Building a routine—regular exercise class, family gatherings, or a weekend club—creates anchors that speed up the feeling of belonging.

Mental Health and Finding Support

Relocating can trigger loneliness and culture shock. Many compounds and international schools host counseling services, and private therapists are available. Establish a local support network and maintain regular contact with friends and family back home. Structured social activities, hobbies, and consistent routines help stabilize mental well-being.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Mistakes New Expats Make

One common misstep is underestimating time for bureaucratic processes—expect delays and have contingency plans. Another is ignoring cultural norms; small missteps can escalate if handled poorly. Avoid hiring informal domestic help without proper sponsorship and documentation. Finally, don’t assume every workplace follows Western norms—clarify expectations early.

Practical Corrections

Plan for paperwork delays by scheduling buffer time before important dates. Respect local customs and ask if unsure rather than guessing. Use formal hiring channels for staff and carefully review all contractual terms before signing.

Living Long Term: Residency, Buying Property, and Retirement

Long-Term Residency Options

Saudi Arabia is modernizing immigration frameworks, including longer residency pathways for investors and skilled professionals. Long-term residency programs and investor visas are evolving—monitor official announcements and speak with immigration advisors for up-to-date guidance.

Property Ownership and Investment

Foreign property ownership rules in Saudi Arabia are changing gradually. For most expats, long-term leases remain the standard. If you’re considering property investment, consult legal professionals and verify property rights, title transfers, and local tax implications.

FAQ

Is Riyadh safe for expats?

Yes. Riyadh is one of the safest major cities in the Middle East, with low rates of violent crime and strong security infrastructure. The primary risks are legal—avoid prohibited behaviors and follow local laws.

Do I need to live in a compound as an expat?

No. Many expats live in compounds, villas, or apartments. Compounds ease the transition by offering community amenities and security, but they are not mandatory. Choice depends on family needs, school locations, and employer support.

How do I find international schools and secure a place?

Apply early. International schools are in demand and often have waiting lists. Use your employer’s relocation support, contact schools directly for application timelines, and prepare transcripts and immunisation records in advance.

Can I drink alcohol in Riyadh?

No. Alcohol is prohibited in Saudi Arabia. Even small infractions involving alcohol can have serious legal consequences. Respect local laws and follow guidance from your employer and compound rules.
