How Long Can You Visit Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

The Gulf has seen a dramatic rise in travel over the last decade, and Dubai remains one of the region’s busiest magnets for tourism, business, and stopovers. For many travelers the first practical question is simple: how long can you visit Dubai? The answer depends on nationality, the type of entry permit you use, and how you plan to manage extensions or repeat entries.

Short answer: For many nationalities Dubai offers visa-free or visa-on-arrival stays of 30 or 90 days; prearranged tourist visas can permit 30, 60 or 90 days per entry and are extendable in some cases; there are also multi-entry options that allow repeated visits over months or years with limits on how long you can be in the country on each visit. Exact durations and extension rules are controlled by UAE immigration and differ by passport, sponsor type, and visa product.

This article explains, in practical detail, the varieties of entry permits that determine how long you can legitimately stay in Dubai, how to obtain or extend them, how to calculate your permitted days, and how to plan an itinerary that fits those restrictions. The Foundation: How Dubai Determines Allowed Stay

Immigration Categories and Who Controls the Clock

UAE immigration does not use a single universal rule for every nationality. Instead, how long you can visit Dubai is determined by:

  • Your nationality and passport type. Many Western passports receive 30- or 90-day visas on arrival; other passports require a prearranged visa.
  • The visa product you enter on (visa on arrival, prearranged tourist visa, transit visa, residence permit).
  • Whether your visa is single-entry, multiple-entry, or long-term multi-entry.
  • Whether you apply to extend a permitted stay while in the UAE or apply for a new visa from outside the country.

Understanding these categories is the first step. Below we walk through the main entry types and the typical stay limits they permit.

Overview: Common Stay Durations You’ll See

To frame the choices before we dive deeper: you will most often encounter these stay durations when planning travel to Dubai—

  • 48-hour and 96-hour transit stamps for very short stopovers.
  • 30-day tourist visas (single or multiple entry), commonly issued on arrival or prearranged.
  • 60-day tourist visas for longer stays (single or multiple entry).
  • 90-day stamps for certain passport holders (often multiple-entry within a six-month validity).
  • 180-day entitlements in specific circumstances for a narrow set of passport holders or diplomatic categories.
  • Multi-year multiple-entry tourist visas that allow repeated stays (for example, five-year permits that allow stays of up to 90 days per visit for eligible applicants).

These are the practical building blocks; the rest of the article explains who gets which, how to secure extensions, and the planning frameworks that keep your trip lawful.

Visa On Arrival and Visa-Free Entry

Who Gets A Visa On Arrival?

Dubai offers visa-on-arrival (VOA) or visa-free entry to citizens of many countries. For these travelers the passport is stamped at the airport and the allowable stay is fixed by the stamp—commonly 30 days for many nationalities, 90 days for a defined list including citizens of several European countries, the United States, Canada, Australia, and others. There are also a handful of passports (e.g., Mexico historically) that can receive longer allowances in specific visa categories.

If you qualify for a visa on arrival, your permitted stay and whether that stamp is single or multiple entry will be printed on the passport at immigration. For many visitors the VOA is the simplest option: no pre-arrival paperwork and a fast transition through immigration.

Practical Steps When Arriving With a VOA

When you arrive at the immigration desk in Dubai:

  • Present your passport (valid for at least six months), onward or return ticket, and proof of accommodation if asked.
  • Keep a printed copy of your entry ticket and reservation; some arrivals are asked to show confirmation.
  • Note the date of entry in your passport stamp. This is the day immigration counts from.
  • If your passport is eligible for a 30- or 90-day stamp, confirm whether that stay is continuous or counted across multiple entries (the stamp text will show validity).

Short stays handled with visa-on-arrival are straightforward, but if you intend to linger beyond your authorized days, read on for how to obtain extensions and longer options.

Prearranged Tourist Visas: Airline, Hotel, and Agency Routes

How Prearranged Visas Work

If your nationality is not eligible for visa-on-arrival, you must obtain a prearranged visa. These visas are often provided through UAE-based airlines, tour operators, or hotels and must be approved before you travel. Major carriers and branded hotels can issue these permits as part of a ticket or a confirmed reservation.

Popular prearranged options include:

  • Short-term tourist visas valid for 30 days (single or multiple entry).
  • Long-term tourist visas valid for 60 days (single or multiple entry).
  • Short transit visas of 48 or 96 hours for stopovers.

Airlines such as Emirates, Etihad, and Flydubai provide services to apply for and obtain these permits when you book a ticket with them, and licensed hotels or travel agents can secure visas on behalf of guests.

Documents and Conditions You’ll Need

Typical documents and conditions for a prearranged tourist visa include:

  • A passport copy (with at least six months validity).
  • Passport-sized photo.
  • Confirmed return or onward ticket.
  • Confirmed hotel reservation or tenancy agreement (if staying with family).
  • Proof of relationship documents when applying for family member visas.
  • For some long-term or 5-year multi-entry products, additional proof may be required (bank statements, insurance, etc.).

If you plan to arrange a visa through an airline, the ticket must generally be on that carrier and may need to be booked on a qualifying fare type. If you are applying via a hotel or travel agency, confirm the agency’s license and that they will provide the entry permit before departure.

Visa Types at a Glance (One List)

  • 48-hour / 96-hour transit visas: valid for short stopovers; non-extendable.
  • 30-day tourism visas: available on arrival or prearranged; often extendable once.
  • 60-day tourism visas: prearranged for longer visits; commonly extendable.
  • 90-day visas and 90-day multiple-entry stamps: issued to certain passport holders; may be valid as multiple-entry within six months.
  • Long-term multiple-entry tourist visas (e.g., five-year tourist visas): allow repeated entries with limits per visit (for example, 90 days per visit) and may require financial proof and insurance.

Long-Term and Multi-Entry Tourist Visas

The Multi-Year Tourist Visa Option

The UAE has introduced multi-year tourist visas in recent years that allow multiple entries over a set number of years (for example, five years). These products are attractive for frequent visitors, family members, and digital nomads who want flexibility. Typical conditions include:

  • A maximum number of days allowed per visit (commonly 90 days per entry, sometimes extendable).
  • Proof of financial means and health insurance may be required.
  • The visa allows re-entry multiple times while the visa’s overall validity remains in force.

If you expect to travel to Dubai repeatedly within a multi-year window, this can be the most convenient legal option and avoids repeated reapplications.

Pros and Cons Compared to Single-Entry Stays

A multi-year visa reduces paperwork and allows flexible repeat visits, but it can be costlier upfront and often requires demonstrating funds or ongoing travel plans. If your travel is irregular and short, visa-on-arrival or 30-day tourist visas may be cheaper and simpler. If you plan extended stays or seasonal living in Dubai, a long-term multiple-entry tourist visa can be the better financial and practical choice.

Transit Visas and Stopovers

Fast Stopovers (48 or 96 Hours)

Dubai and the UAE are set up for rapid transit visas that accommodate connecting passengers who want a short stopover. These visas are strictly time-limited and usually non-extendable. Airlines commonly offer these for transiting passengers who have a layover in Dubai and want to leave the airport for a day or two.

If you have a longer stopover in route to another destination, a transit visa is the legal way to exit the airport and see the city for a short period. Always ensure your flight itinerary and ticket class meet the airline’s eligibility rules for transit visas.

Planning a Longer Stay From a Transit

If a transit becomes the seed for a longer adventure, you must secure the correct tourist visa before your permitted transit period expires. Do not assume immigration will allow you to convert a transit stamp into a tourist stay without formal application; these conversions generally require formal permits.

Extending Your Stay: Legal Options and Practical Steps

How Extensions Work

Depending on your visa type, many tourist visas can be extended once while in the UAE—commonly for an additional 30 days, subject to fees and approval. Visa-on-arrival stamps for certain nationalities (like some 90-day stamps) may not be extendable and instead require exiting and re-entering or applying for a new permit from outside the country.

To apply for an extension while in Dubai you typically:

  • Visit an immigration service center or an authorized visa service.
  • Provide your passport and original entry permit.
  • Pay the required extension fee.
  • Present reasons for extension (tourism, medical, family) if requested.

Some travelers also use airlines’ local offices or visa agents to help process extensions. If you are near the end of your permitted stay and cannot secure an extension, plan to depart before overstaying.

Step-by-Step: How to Apply or Extend Your Dubai Stay (Second List)

  1. Check the stamp in your passport and note the expiry date; calculate how many days are left.
  2. Determine whether your visa type is eligible for extension (consult immigration or an authorized visa service).
  3. Gather required documents: passport copy, original passport, proof of onward travel, and payment method for fees.
  4. Submit the application through an immigration center, airport office, or authorized service provider.
  5. If approved, confirm the new expiry date and keep a printed copy of the permit in your possession.

(Use this checklist early—processing times and requirements can change, so start extension procedures at least a week before expiry to avoid complications.)

Overstay Rules, Fines, and Consequences

Overstaying in Dubai has financial and practical consequences. The UAE imposes overstay fines calculated per day beyond your permitted stay. The fines can accumulate quickly and may require payment before you are allowed to leave the country, and repeated or long overstays could affect future visa applications or ability to re-enter. Immigration record checks are strict, and you must clear fines and legal issues before re-entry into the UAE in the future.

If you find yourself in a position of accidental overstay, address it immediately through immigration channels. Don’t rely on leaving quietly—attempting to depart without resolving overstay can result in being detained at the airport and paying higher penalties.

Calculating Your Allowed Stay: The 90-in-180 Rule and Variations

Understanding Rolling and Fixed Periods

A common area of confusion is how multiple entries add up, especially under “90 days in 180 days” rules that some countries apply for foreigners. In the UAE context, certain 90-day visas are issued as multiple-entry within a six-month validity; in other words, the visa itself has a validity period and a maximum aggregate stay within that period.

To calculate stays carefully:

  • Read the stamp: Does the stamp or entry permit say "90 days stay" or "90 days within 180 days validity"? The wording matters.
  • For visas that say "valid for 6 months with 90 days stay," you can enter multiple times within the six months but cannot exceed 90 days total during that validity unless the visa explicitly allows 90 days per entry.
  • Keep a running tally of days spent in the UAE during the permit's validity. Use a calendar or a visa calculator tool.

When in doubt, consult immigration or a visa agent to clarify whether the 90 days is per visit or aggregate.

Example Calculation (Generic, Not a Case Study)

If you hold a visa valid for six months with a "90-day stay" allowance, you might enter for 30 days, leave, and later return for 60 more days—reaching 90 days total. If you attempt to remain for a 91st day without extension or a new permit, you would be overstaying.

Avoid relying on assumptions—record every entry and exit date to avoid surprises.

Residency, Work, and Converting a Tourist Visit

From Visitor to Resident: The Legal Route

A tourist visa is not a direct path to residency or employment. If you plan to live and work in Dubai, you need the appropriate residence permit, typically sponsored by an employer, family member, or property owner (in specified schemes). Employers generally apply for work and residence permits on behalf of employees, and family residency requires documented relationships and financial eligibility.

If you arrive on a tourist visa and are offered employment, your employer must follow the correct legal process to sponsor a work visa and convert your status while you are in the UAE or have you exit and re-enter on the new permit—procedures vary and are strictly regulated.

Digital Nomad and Remote Work Options

Dubai has introduced long-term permits for remote workers and investors that allow stays beyond typical tourist timelines; these are distinct visa categories with their own criteria (proof of remote employment, income thresholds, professional credentials). If you are considering long-term remote work from Dubai, explore the dedicated visa products designed for that purpose rather than trying to extend a tourist stay repeatedly.

Practical Itineraries: How Long to Stay Based on Trip Goals

Short Stopover (1–3 Days)

A one- to three-day stopover is ideal for seeing Dubai’s highlights: the Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall, a short desert activity, and a historic walk in Al Fahidi. If you are using a transit visa, plan a streamlined schedule centered on a single cluster of sights.

Standard City Break (4–7 Days)

Four to seven days gives you time for city attractions, a desert excursion, a dhow dinner along Dubai Creek, and some relaxation on Jumeirah Beach. This is the most common stay length favored by tourists on a 30-day visa or 90-day visa on arrival seeking a thorough sample of Dubai without extended logistics.

Extended Stay (30–60 Days)

A 30- to 60-day stay suits travelers who want to explore Dubai’s neighborhoods in depth, do multiple day trips in the UAE, or combine leisure with remote work. For this length you’ll usually secure a 30- or 60-day tourist visa in advance or extend a shorter stamp if allowed.

Seasonal Living (90 Days and Beyond)

If you plan to spend several months in Dubai on a rotating basis, a 90-day visa (if eligible) or a long-term multiple-entry visa is more appropriate. For stays approaching six months per visit, evaluate long-stay visas or residency options—relying on repeated tourist visa renewals can become expensive and legally cumbersome.

Money, Insurance, and Financial Proof

Certain visa types and multi-year permits require proof of funds, bank statements, and internationally valid travel medical insurance. Even for visa-on-arrival entries, carrying travel medical insurance is prudent as private healthcare in the UAE is costly without coverage.

If you apply for extended or long-term tourist permits, prepare 6 months of bank statements, a confirmed insurance policy, and other requested documents to strengthen the application.

Border Practices and Airport Procedures

Dubai immigration occasionally performs random visual screening of visitors, asking travelers to present hard copies of their visas or confirmation pages. Printing a copy of your entry permit, hotel reservations, and ticket can expedite the process if selected for screening. If you do not have a printed copy, authorities may provide a printed copy for a small fee at the airport.

Also remember passport validity: your passport should be valid for at least six months from the expected date of arrival. Non-standard travel documents may trigger additional checks or denial of entry.

Calculating Cost: Visa Fees and Typical Expenses

Visa fees vary by type, length, and whether the permit is single or multiple entry. Prearranged visas obtained through airlines often include the processing fee in the visa service charge, while extensions have separate fees at immigration offices. Multi-year tourist visas carry higher upfront costs but may be cost-effective for frequent visitors.

Beyond visa fees, budget for:

  • Return or onward flights as proof required on arrival.
  • Health insurance if required for your visa product.
  • Hotel or tenancy proof when applying via a sponsor or hotel.
  • Extension fees if you plan to lengthen your stay.

For travelers from Saudi Arabia and neighboring GCC countries, low-cost regional flights and frequent promotions often reduce air costs, but visa strategy remains the main determinant of how long you can stay.

  • The option to book open-jaw tickets that permit entry through one hub and exit through another to optimize visa timings.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Many travelers unintentionally complicate their Dubai visits by making predictable mistakes:

  • Ignoring the passport-visa conditions and overstaying inadvertently.
  • Booking a return or onward ticket that doesn’t align with extension requests or visa validity, causing immigration to deny entry.
  • Relying on verbal assurances from travel agents without a printed permit before departure.
  • Failing to secure the correct visa product for work or long-term stays and attempting to convert a tourist visa into employment status without legal sponsorship.

Avoid these problems by reading the fine print on your permit, keeping clear timelines of entry and exit dates, and obtaining written confirmations for visas applied through third parties.

Legal, Cultural, and Practical Considerations During Your Stay

While Dubai is a globally familiar city, staying mindful of local laws is essential. Dress codes in public areas, public conduct, and regulations concerning alcohol and public displays vary from other international cities, and enforcement is strict. Familiarize yourself with relevant local rules before you arrive, and carry digital and physical copies of key documents (passport, visa, insurance).

Tools and Resources to Keep Your Stay Legal and Smooth

Practical tools include:

  • A visa calculator to track the number of days used under a multi-entry permit.
  • A calendar or spreadsheet recording entry and exit dates for clarity.
  • A clear folder or scanned backups of your permit and hotel reservations in case airline or immigration officers request proof.
  • Reliable local service providers or official immigration channels for extensions; if in doubt, go directly to immigration.

Sample Itinerary Blueprints Based on Allowed Stays

A Weekend Layover (2–3 Days)

A direct flight from Riyadh or Jeddah lands you in Dubai early—use day one for iconic city experiences, day two for a desert safari or coastal relaxation, and day three to explore cultural neighborhoods before departure. A transit or short-term 48–96 hour permit often covers this.

A One-Week Immersion (5–7 Days)

Book a 30-day tourist visa if you want flexibility. Schedule urban highlights, a day in Abu Dhabi, and one 24-hour desert-camp experience. Consider a day trip to the more traditional parts of the UAE and a dhow cruise for cultural balance.

A Two-Month Exploration (45–60 Days)

Secure a 60-day tourist visa or plan to extend a 30-day permit early. Rotate weeks between Dubai neighborhoods, explore the northern emirates, and use Abu Dhabi as a two- or three-day city break. FAQ

Do I need a visa before I travel to Dubai?

It depends on your nationality. Many travelers receive a visa on arrival for 30 or 90 days; others must obtain a prearranged visa through airlines, hotels, or travel agents. Check eligibility and the specific visa product that applies to your passport well before departure.

Can I extend my tourist visa while in Dubai?

Some tourist visas can be extended once while in the UAE, commonly for an additional 30 days. Visa-on-arrival entries for certain passports may not be extendable. Start extension procedures through immigration or an authorized service at least a week before your visa expires.

What happens if I overstay my permitted visit?

Overstaying carries daily fines and can complicate future travel or entry to the UAE. If you anticipate an accidental overstay, contact immigration immediately to resolve fines and legal requirements before attempting to depart.

Is it possible to turn a tourist visit into long-term residency?

A tourist visa alone is not a direct route to residency. To live and work in Dubai you’ll need the appropriate residence permit, generally sponsored by an employer, family member, or qualifying investor. If you receive an employment offer while on a tourist visa, your employer typically handles the work/residence permit application.
