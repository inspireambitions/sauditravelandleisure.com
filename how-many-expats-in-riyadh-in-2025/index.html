Introduction

Saudi Arabia is attracting more international workers and families than at almost any point in its modern history. Rapid urban development, Vision 2030 projects and major corporate relocations are reshaping who lives in the capital and how Riyadh functions as a global city.

Short answer: Current estimates put the number of expatriates living in Riyadh at roughly 4.0–4.3 million people, representing about half of the city’s population in early 2025. The exact figure varies by source and methodology, but the range reflects consistent findings from municipal forecasts and private-sector reports.

This article explains where that number comes from, why estimates differ, which nationalities are most common, how expat life in Riyadh is organized on the ground, and what the trends mean for housing, work and travel planning. You’ll finish with a clear framework to evaluate statistics directly, practical planning steps if you’re moving to or visiting Riyadh, and the policy context that will affect expat life over the next decade. For travelers and expatriates who want to move beyond headlines and actually plan with confidence, this is the blueprint you need.

Understanding the Baseline: Population and Expat Definitions

What counts as an "expat" in Riyadh?

Numbers in media and research often use different definitions. For our purposes in this article, “expat” refers to non-Saudi residents who live in Riyadh for employment, family, education, or long-term residence, and who are registered under a visa or residency system (including iqama or premium residency) at the time of counting. Estimates sometimes include temporary workers, domestic staff, and foreign students; other measures exclude short-term visitors and unregistered migrants. Always check the fine print of a statistic to understand the coverage.

Why Riyadh is treated differently from national totals

Riyadh is Saudi Arabia’s political, administrative and business center. It draws a distinct mix of expats—senior corporate staff, government-related professionals, technical specialists, contractors, and service workers—that differs from coastal or holy cities. National counts (the total number of foreign residents in the kingdom) are useful context, but city-level estimates reflect internal migration, corporate relocations, construction cycles and major infrastructure projects concentrated in Riyadh.

Key datasets and their limitations

The big-name sources you’ll see quoted include national censuses, private consultancies (real estate and economic consultancies), municipal planning departments, and international organizations. Each has trade-offs:

  • National census: thorough but infrequent and often reported at the provincial rather than city-block level.
  • Private reports: timely and focused on housing or business demand, but use sampling and modeling that produce ranges rather than single-point precision.
  • Municipal projections: useful for planning and infrastructure but can lag when economic shocks occur.
  • Administrative records (visa/iqama): accurate for documented residents but may exclude temporary or irregular populations.

I’ll walk through how these datasets combine to produce the current estimate of expats in Riyadh.

Data Snapshot: Where the 4.0–4.3 Million Range Comes From

City population baseline

Multiple recent sources place Riyadh’s total population between 7.0 million (earlier 2022 references) and approximately 7.95 million in 2025, depending on whether remote suburbs and satellite communities are counted. For the purpose of the expat calculation, we use a working city population of roughly 7.9–8.0 million in 2025 as a baseline. That figure aligns with municipal projections and independent demographic analysts.

Expat share estimates

Private-sector studies—most prominently the Knight Frank population and housing forecasts—have reported that expatriates make up approximately 50–54% of Riyadh’s population in recent years. Applying that proportion to a 7.9–8.0 million city population produces an expat total in the 4.0–4.3 million range.

Cross-check with national trends

Saudi Arabia’s nationwide share of foreign residents was reported at around 41–44% in recent large-scale enumerations. Riyadh’s higher expat percentage reflects the capital’s economic role, concentrated corporate headquarters, and building projects that demand both skilled and unskilled foreign labor. When national totals and Riyadh’s share are reconciled, the mid-city estimate of about 4 million expats remains consistent.

Why sources disagree — common causes of variance

Estimates differ for several practical reasons. First, whether the count includes short-term contractors and domestic workers affects totals substantially. Second, projections issued before or after major recruitment drives for construction or events (for example, large-scale building programs, new airports, or corporate relocations) will diverge. Third, the rate at which authorities register arrivals and renew iqamas changes the administrative count. Finally, private models may include anticipated inward migration tied to specific projects; municipal counts tend to be more conservative.

Nationality Mix and Occupational Profile in Riyadh

Which nationalities are most common in Riyadh?

The broader Kingdom’s expatriate composition gives a reliable sense of Riyadh’s mix: South Asian nationals (Bangladeshis, Indians, Pakistanis, Nepalese), Yemenis, Egyptians, Sudanese and Filipinos feature heavily. The precise distribution in Riyadh skews toward professionals and managers from a wider international set—Western and GCC nationals are more prevalent among corporate and diplomatic roles—while the large South Asian and Middle Eastern contingents supply both skilled technical labor and service-sector roles. Expect the following character of representation:

  • South Asian labor force (construction, services, technical roles)
  • Egyptian and Sudanese professionals (education, healthcare, engineering)
  • Filipinos in sectors like healthcare, hospitality and domestic work
  • Western and Gulf expatriates concentrated in multinational corporations, diplomacy and high-skill professions

While national-level counts are available for the Kingdom, city-level breakdowns are less granular publicly. However, applying national proportions to Riyadh with sectoral adjustments produces a realistic portrait for planning.

Occupational sectors with large expat presence

Expatriates in Riyadh are heavily represented across several sectors:

  • Construction and infrastructure: large numbers of manual and technical workers supporting development projects.
  • Healthcare and education: doctors, nurses, teachers from regionally diverse backgrounds.
  • Oil, energy and industrial services: specialized international staff and contractors.
  • Financial services, ICT and corporate headquarters: a growing cadre of high-skill foreign workers tied to Vision 2030 initiatives.
  • Domestic and personal services: a substantial share of female expatriates from Southeast Asia and parts of Africa.

These sectoral patterns drive demand in housing, schooling, leisure and transport.

How Expats Live in Riyadh: Neighborhoods, Housing and Daily Life

Where expats cluster and why

Riyadh contains a variety of residential typologies, from high-rise apartments in central business districts to villa compounds in suburban neighborhoods. Expatriate settlement patterns are shaped by employment sector, family status, and income:

  • Single or young professionals often live in central or newly developed districts with ready access to offices and leisure.
  • Families prefer gated compounds or villa neighborhoods that offer international schools and community amenities.
  • Contract workers frequently live in purpose-built accommodations or grouped housing near major construction zones.

These patterns impact transport flows and the demand for consumer services.

Housing market pressures and affordability

Riyadh’s housing market has expanded rapidly as the capital modernizes. Demand from long-term expatriates seeking home ownership, plus substantial recruitment for projects, has tightened the market in desirable neighborhoods. Developers are responding with a mix of affordable apartments, mid-range villas and high-end gated communities. For newcomers, understanding the lease market—typically annual contracts with security deposit requirements—and negotiating utilities and maintenance responsibilities is essential.

Social life and community structures

Expat communities in Riyadh are not monolithic. Many national communities organize cultural associations, religious centers (private spaces for various denominations), and social networks that support newcomers. The Diplomatic Quarter, international schools and multinationals act as social hubs. Knowing where a particular community gathers will help incoming expats settle faster and find culturally familiar services.

Visas, Residency and Legal Status: What Counts in the Numbers

The iqama system and registration effects

Most long-term foreign residents are registered under the iqama system—an employer-sponsored residency permit that shows legal status, work authorization and identity details. Administrative registers of iqamas are a primary source for official counts, but numbers fluctuate with renewals and transfers.

Premium residency and the changing landscape

The introduction of Premium Residency (the private residency program) has created a small but growing cohort of expats with more permanent-seeming status and the right to own property. While premium residents remain a minority among expats overall, they contribute to shifting demographics, especially in higher-income brackets and entrepreneurial activity.

Undocumented and short-term categories

Some populations—undocumented migrants or short-term contractors—may not be fully captured in iqama-based statistics. This is a typical issue in many countries with large migrant workforces and explains part of the variance between administrative and sample-based estimates.

Practical Framework: How to Evaluate Expat Population Claims

For readers who want to verify numbers themselves or assess new claims, use this three-step framework:

  1. Identify the source and date: who produced the number and when.
  2. Check the coverage: does the number include temporary workers, domestic staff, and unregistered migrants?
  3. Cross-validate with at least two other sources: municipal data, private consultancy reports, and national census or ministry figures.

What the Numbers Mean for Travelers, Expats and Planners

Impacts on travel and tourism

A large and growing expatriate population changes the visitor experience. International food, entertainment and retail offerings expand, international flights and hotels increase capacity, and cultural diversity becomes more visible in city life. For travelers, this means more familiar services but also busier peak seasons and greater demand for international-standard accommodations.

Employment implications

Employers in Riyadh have a larger talent pool to hire from, and many international companies are establishing regional operations in the city. That increases opportunities for foreign professionals but also contributes to competition for housing and schooling.

Infrastructure and services

Rapid population growth—particularly if concentrated in certain districts—creates pressure on transport, utilities and health services. The municipal response in building rapid transit, new hospitals and additional schooling capacity will shape where future expat clusters form.

Neighborhoods and Practical Local Advice (Narrative)

Central business and mixed-use districts

The heart of Riyadh contains modern business towers, shopping centers and high-density apartment blocks. These neighborhoods are convenient for professionals working in finance, technology and multinational companies. Expect lively retail and dining options; apartments may be compact but well-serviced.

Gated compounds and family suburbs

Families often select compounds for the sense of community, security and access to international schooling. Suburbs with villa clusters provide larger homes and private gardens. These areas typically include private transport arrangements and community amenities like pools and gyms.

Peripheral and project-adjacent districts

Large development projects and construction sites create pockets of high worker accommodation demand. Wages, commuting patterns and neighborhood services reflect the concentration of construction and logistics activity.

Where to find culturally familiar services

Neighborhoods near international schools and diplomatic institutions tend to host a greater density of international restaurants, specialty grocery stores and services. Locate these clusters when planning housing—especially in your first weeks—to access immediate support networks.

Policy Context and Future Outlook

Vision 2030 and demand drivers

Vision 2030 is reshaping Riyadh’s workforce composition. As the city attracts more regional headquarters and international investment, demand for both skilled expats and supporting service workers will rise. Forecasts by real estate consultancies predict steady population growth tied directly to planned mega-projects and corporate relocations.

Projected growth and housing demand

Analysts project continued expansion of Riyadh’s population through the late 2020s. Some forecasts point to Riyadh reaching 9–9.6 million by 2030, with expat numbers growing in absolute terms even if policy initiatives aim to increase Saudi employment in selected sectors. This will intensify housing demand and create opportunities for new residential development.

Saudization and labor-market dynamics

Policy measures designed to increase Saudi participation in private-sector employment (Saudization) are likely to change occupational composition over time. While some roles will transition, many sectors—construction, hospitality and domestic work—will continue to rely heavily on expatriate labor for the foreseeable future.

Data Use Cases: For Researchers and Decision Makers

Estimating demand for services

Public planners and private firms can use expat population ranges to model demand for housing, schools and healthcare. Apply conservative, moderate, and aggressive growth scenarios to stress-test investments. For most practical decisions, planning with a mid-range estimate (e.g., 4.1 million expats) and sensitivity bands +/- 10% is sensible.

Corporate relocation and HR planning

Companies relocating staff into Riyadh should budget for housing market volatility, school admission cycles and the administrative lead time for iqama and visa processing. A two- to three-month lead time for administrative clearances and localized onboarding reduces surprises.

Travel operators and hospitality

Travel and hospitality providers should position inventory and services around peak expat movement—end-of-contract rotations, school start dates and major development milestones—to capture demand spikes.

Common Mistakes When Interpreting Expat Numbers (and How to Avoid Them)

Misreading population statistics leads to poor planning. Here are typical errors and how to avoid them:

  • Treating a single report as definitive: always triangulate with at least two sources.
  • Ignoring scope differences: clarify whether the figure includes temporary contract workers, domestic staff or only iqama holders.
  • Over-emphasizing short-term spikes: hiring for a specific project can inflate local counts temporarily; plan for both peak and base usage.
  • Confusing city boundaries with metropolitan areas: know whether the statistic refers to the municipal district or the greater metropolitan region.

Applying the verification framework above will prevent most misinterpretations.

FAQ

1) Is the expat number in Riyadh growing or shrinking?

Expat numbers in Riyadh have been growing in absolute terms due to major projects, corporate relocations and housing demand, even as policy efforts aim to increase national employment in some sectors. Short-term contractions can occur during economic slowdowns or policy shifts, but the medium-term trend tied to Vision 2030 projects points to continued growth.

2) How accurate are city-level expat estimates?

Accuracy depends on the source. Administrative records (iqama registries) are accurate for documented residents but may lag in capturing rapid inflows or short-term workers. Private forecasts are timely but model-dependent. The best practice is to compare administrative counts, municipal projections and reputable private studies to form a range.

3) Where do most expats in Riyadh live?

Settlement varies by income and family status. Professionals often live in central and newly developed districts, families favour gated compounds or villa suburbs near international schools, and contract workers live in project-adjacent or group accommodations. Neighborhood choice is driven by commute, schooling and available amenities.

4) How should I use these numbers when planning a move or trip?

Use the expat range as a demand indicator for housing, healthcare and schooling. Apply the short-step blueprint: secure visa support, research neighborhoods, budget for housing, arrange health coverage, and connect with local communities.
