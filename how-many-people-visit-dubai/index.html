How Many People Visit Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai’s skyline and global events make headlines around the world, but the raw numbers tell the clearest story of its tourism rebound and ongoing momentum. In recent years Dubai has transformed from a fast-growing regional hub into one of the planet’s busiest visitor magnets—driven by investment in attractions, open visa policies, and a relentless calendar of events that draw both leisure and business travelers.

Short answer: In 2024 Dubai received approximately 18.72 million international visitors, up from about 17.15 million in 2023. This steady growth reflects a fast recovery from the 2020 pandemic slump and positions Dubai among the world’s most visited cities.

This article explains those headline figures and what they mean for travelers, tourism businesses, and anyone planning a trip to the UAE. I’ll break down year-to-year trends, monthly and seasonal patterns, where visitors come from, how much they spend, and the practical implications for planning a smart visit. Along the way I’ll share proven frameworks and step-by-step planning advice from the Saudi Travel & Leisure perspective so you can plan with confidence and make choices that match your travel style.

My main message: understanding visitor volumes is about more than curiosity—those numbers shape pricing, peak seasons, event planning, transport capacity, and how you should book. Read on for the data, the travel blueprint, and the practical decisions to make before you go.

The Big Picture: Visitor Numbers Over Time

Rapid growth, a pandemic pause, and a strong recovery

Dubai’s visitor trajectory over the last decade is a clear arc: steady growth through the late 2010s, a dramatic downturn in 2020, and a robust rebound beginning in 2021 that accelerated through 2023 and 2024. After seeing roughly 16.73 million international visitors in 2019, Dubai’s international arrivals dropped to about 5.51 million in 2020 because of the global pandemic. Recovery began in 2021 with 7.28 million visitors, then accelerated to 14.36 million in 2022, 17.15 million in 2023, and reached about 18.72 million in 2024.

This pattern matters because it shows not just resilience, but also structural changes: Dubai’s tourism strategy (events, attractions, visa facilitation) and airport connectivity have scaled rapidly and are now supporting record passenger numbers.

Airport traffic and the hub effect

Dubai International Airport is a central reason for the city’s high visitor throughput. Passenger volumes tell a related story: after a dip to 18.2 million passengers in 2020, the airport rebuilt traffic to 29.1 million in 2021, 66.1 million in 2022, approximately 87 million in 2023, and more than 92 million in 2024. Because DXB is both a destination and a global transit hub, the airport’s recovery amplifies inbound tourism—both stopover travelers and those whose primary destination is Dubai.

The economic dimension: visitor spending

Visitor numbers are one metric; spending is another. International visitors spent around $179.8 billion (close to AED 660 billion) in 2024, showing that Dubai has regained not only volume but also high-value visitation. This spending supports hotels, retail, entertainment, and F&B sectors, which explains why Dubai continues to invest heavily in new attractions and events.

Who Visits Dubai? Source Markets and Visitor Profiles

Major source markets in plain terms

Understanding where visitors come from explains peak demand windows, language needs, culinary offerings, and direct flight routes. In 2023 and 2024, the largest source markets included India, Oman, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, Russia, and the United States.

To summarize the most relevant national contributors in a compact form:

  • India led with over 2.2 million visitors in 2023.
  • Nearby GCC nations (Oman, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait) contributed high volumes because of short travel times and frequent flights.
  • Europe (especially the UK and Western Europe) and CIS/Eastern Europe were significant contributors, especially in cooler months.
  • South Asia, Africa, and North America also represent important segments for leisure and business travel.

Why these markets matter for travel planning

If you’re planning a visit, knowing the source market dynamics helps you anticipate crowd patterns. For example, strong Western European visitation peaks during Dubai’s winter season (November–March), while GCC and South Asian travelers may visit year-round or during religious and national holidays. If you prefer quieter periods, plan around the flows from your primary visitor base; if you want cultural events and festivals, time your trip for months with heavy inbound traffic.

Month-by-Month and Seasonal Patterns

When crowding, prices, and temperatures intersect

Dubai is a year-round destination but the experience differs dramatically by season. The most comfortable weather—and the busiest tourist months—are between November and March. Expect outdoor event programming, higher hotel occupancy, and premium pricing during this window.

Summer months (June–September) are extremely hot, which reduces outdoor tourism but increases indoor-and-luxury experiences: shopping malls, indoor theme parks, and resort pools remain busy. Many savvy travelers take advantage of lower rates in summer if they’re comfortable with heat or want quieter sightseeing.

Typical monthly flow and what it implies

Most months have consistent arrivals, but there are spikes tied to international events, school holidays, and festival calendars. Dubai has built a calendar of attractions—shopping festivals, global exhibitions, sports events, and new attraction openings—that shift demand dramatically for short windows. When planning, consult event calendars and book hotels early for periods tied to major events.

Attractions and Demand Drivers

What draws nearly 19 million people a year?

Dubai’s visitor numbers are driven by a combination of factors:

  • Major landmarks and attractions (e.g., observation decks, the city’s retail hubs, large indoor attractions).
  • Mega-events and exhibitions that attract global crowds.
  • Competitive visa facilitation and a liberal entry policy for many nationalities.
  • Exceptional connectivity through direct flights and the hub functionality of DXB.
  • An expanding roster of family-focused and experiential attractions that increase length of stay and per-person spend.

These are not abstract benefits; they directly influence booking windows, hotel pricing, and transport congestion. If you’re booking a trip around a specific attraction or event, plan and reserve at least 3–4 months in advance during peak season.

Attractions to include on an itinerary without relying on lists

When building your Dubai itinerary, mix iconic experiences with local culture. Start with the skyline and its observation experiences for a birds-eye orientation, then spend time at the older trading neighborhoods and coastal creek areas to feel the city’s maritime heritage. Balance a desert experience to see the emirate’s natural landscape and add one or two indoor mega-attractions for hot days. Accommodation, Hotels, and Occupancy

Supply versus demand

Dubai’s hospitality supply expanded quickly to meet demand: by 2024 the city had well over 150,000 hotel rooms and more than 800 properties open to guests. Average occupancy rebounded to mid-70% in 2023 and 2024, indicating healthy demand across the city’s full range of hotel classes.

Where supply meets demand affects your choices: luxury properties often sell out during peak months and event weeks, while mid-range and family-oriented hotels increase rates based on school holiday calendars. Visitor Duration and Behavioral Changes

How long do people stay, and what they do

Average stay lengths vary by segment and travel purpose. Pre-pandemic averages were around a week, but in certain post-pandemic windows visitors have lengthened stays due to combined work-leisure travel or multi-destination itineraries. In 2023–2024 there were clear signs that visitors were staying longer on average and spending more per trip.

Why this matters: longer stays increase per-person spend on local experiences, and they change how you plan your itinerary. If you’re allocating time for both the city and a nearby emirate, plan logistics for internal transfers and choose accommodation that minimizes commute time to your primary activities.

Travel Logistics: Visas, Flights, and On-the-Ground Transport

Entry and visa essentials

Dubai maintains relatively liberal entry policies for many nationalities, including visa-on-arrival options and e-visa facilities. Visa rules change occasionally, so verify your entry requirements well before booking. For travelers coming from the Kingdom and other nearby markets, frequent flight schedules make last-minute planning possible, but check visa requirements if you plan a longer stay or intend to work remotely while in Dubai.

Flights and airport considerations

Dubai’s airport network offers direct connections globally and multiple daily flights to and from major regional hubs. For travelers based in the Kingdom, there are numerous daily services between Saudi cities and Dubai; flying mid-week often yields better fares and less crowded terminals. If you aim to minimize transfer time, consider flights that arrive early morning or late evening to maximize daylight hours for activities.

Getting around the city

Dubai’s public transport—metro, trams, buses—works well for many tourist corridors and is especially reliable in Downtown, Business Bay, and along Sheikh Zayed Road. Taxis and ride-hailing services provide door-to-door convenience, and car hire is practical if you are comfortable with local driving conditions. Peak Season Strategies: How to Travel Smarter

Booking timeline and rate management

High-demand periods require an early-booking mindset. In the winter months and around major events, book flights and hotels at least two to four months ahead to secure reasonable rates. For special events, reserve even earlier.

Budget-conscious travelers can benefit from off-season travel in summer when room rates are lower—but plan indoor-heavy activities and consider accommodation with great recreational facilities. For itinerary flexibility, look for hotel bookings with free cancellation and multiple flight options.

Timing for family travel, events, and business

Family travel frequently peaks during school holidays and public holidays. For business travelers, weeks with high conference activity are predictably more expensive. If you’re visiting for leisure, align your dates with the experiences you care most about—beach and pool time require warmer months, while outdoor festivals and desert excursions are most pleasant in cooler months.

Practical Budgeting: How Visitor Numbers Affect Cost

How to forecast expenses

Visitor volumes translate directly into price pressure. When arrival numbers are high, expect higher hotel rates, surcharges for airport transfers, and premium pricing for popular attractions. Use a simple budgeting formula: estimate accommodation as 40–50% of trip cost during peak season, allocate 20–30% for food and experiences, and reserve 10–15% for internal transport and incidentals. Adjust this distribution depending on whether you prefer luxury stays or a more budget-friendly approach.

Itineraries and Sample Stays (Practical Approach, Not a Checklist)

Short stay: 2–3 days

Prioritize orientation: a skyline viewpoint early, a walk through a restored heritage quarter to understand local culture, and a late-afternoon desert experience. Keep logistics tight—stay in a central district to minimize travel time and choose attractions that offer pre-booking.

Medium stay: 5–7 days

Add a day for a major indoor attraction and one full day dedicated to a nearby emirate or coastal experience. Include at least one relaxed evening for dining and an event or show. If you’re splitting time between city and beach, factor in commute times and whether you’ll use public transport or a rental car.

Extended stay: 10+ days

A longer stay allows deeper exploration—multiple neighborhoods, museums, cultural districts, and day trips to neighboring emirates. Common Visitor Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Mistake: Underestimating seasonal crowds

Many travelers underestimate how popular certain weeks are. Avoid disappointment by checking event calendars and booking critical experiences (observation decks, concerts, desert safaris) before arrival.

Mistake: Expecting the same pace year-round

Dubai’s tempo shifts by season. If you want a relaxed experience, avoid the festival and exhibition weeks even if rates are attractive; those weeks are busy and require extra time for simple transfers.

Mistake: Ignoring transport time between experiences

The city is larger in travel time than many visitors expect. Cluster activities by neighborhood—don’t schedule attractions on opposite ends of the emirate in a single afternoon.

A Travel-Planning Framework From Saudi Travel & Leisure

Planning a Dubai trip with confidence means combining data-driven timing with cultural awareness and logistical readiness. Use this four-part decision framework:

  1. Define priorities (weather, events, budget, length of stay).
  2. Map neighborhoods to priorities (Downtown for iconic views, creek districts for heritage, Jumeirah for beaches).
  3. Book core inventory early (flights + first two hotel nights + marquee attraction).
  4. Flex around secondary experiences (dining, smaller tours) and keep a time buffer for transport.

Cultural Notes and Etiquette

Dubai is cosmopolitan and accommodating, but it’s still part of a conservative region. Dress modestly in certain public areas, respect local customs during religious periods, and observe signage at cultural sites. Public displays of affection and loud behavior in public areas are frowned upon—follow the lead of locals and you’ll avoid uncomfortable situations.

When interacting with local businesses and vendors, polite negotiation and maintaining respect get better outcomes and enrich the travel experience. If you plan to travel on to Saudi Arabia or vice versa, familiarizing yourself with cross-border etiquette makes multi-destination trips smoother.

Comparing Dubai With Regional Alternatives

Why choose Dubai versus other Gulf capitals?

Dubai combines scale, variety, and connectivity. If you want the biggest possible variety of attractions and year-round flight options, Dubai leads; but other regional destinations (Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, Jeddah, AlUla) offer distinct cultural and historical experiences that complement a Dubai visit.

For example, if your trip plan includes a cultural deep-dive into the Arabian Peninsula, consider pairing Dubai with a few days in Abu Dhabi for cultural sites—and then a separate trip to Saudi regions for heritage-rich experiences. Safety, Health, and Practical Advice

Dubai is safe by global city standards. Standard precautions apply: keep valuables secure, be mindful in busy tourist areas, and carry identification. Health infrastructure is strong; travel insurance that covers medical evacuation and emergency care is advisable.

Because visitor volumes fluctuate widely during peak months, plan for longer waits at attractions and pre-book where possible. If you have mobility concerns, request accessible services in advance—these tend to be in higher demand when visitor numbers spike.

The Impact of Events and New Attractions on Visitor Volumes

Dubai’s continual investment in attractions—new museums, theme parks, and sports facilities—drives incremental increases in visitor numbers and lengthens the stay of many tourists. Each large opening or worldwide event shifts both short-term demand spikes and long-term perception of the city as a destination.

Event-driven spikes are predictable if you watch development timelines and major event calendars. For travel professionals and frequent visitors, monitoring these calendars is essential for booking strategy and price forecasting.

Conclusion

Dubai’s visitor numbers—about 18.72 million international arrivals in 2024—reflect a city that has rebuilt rapidly and continues to expand its tourism capacity and global reach. The raw totals matter because they shape pricing, crowding, event programming, and the travel choices you should make as a visitor. FAQ

How many people visited Dubai in 2024?

Approximately 18.72 million international visitors visited Dubai in 2024, representing continued recovery and growth following the pandemic years.

When is the best time to visit to avoid crowds but enjoy pleasant weather?

October through April offers the best weather but also the highest crowds. If you prefer fewer crowds and lower prices, consider shoulder months or summer stays while planning more indoor activities.

Do visitor numbers affect how I should book activities?

Yes. During months with high visitor volumes (winter and major event weeks), book flights and hotels early and reserve major attractions in advance to avoid sold-out experiences and high last-minute prices.

Are there easy day-trip options from Dubai to neighboring emirates or Saudi Arabia?

Yes. Abu Dhabi is a practical day-trip option for cultural sites, while combining Dubai with visits to Saudi Arabia requires separate planning for visas and transport.
