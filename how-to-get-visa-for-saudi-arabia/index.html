Which Visa Do You Need?

Tourist Visa (eVisa)

The tourist eVisa is the entry point for most international visitors who plan to travel for leisure, sightseeing, events, Umrah outside Hajj season, or family visits that are not long-term. It is commonly issued as a one-year multiple-entry visa allowing stays up to 90 days per visit for eligible nationalities; in some cases single-entry short-stay visas are issued. The eVisa application is completed online, generally includes basic travel health insurance, and removes the need to visit an embassy for many applicants.

What the eVisa allows: sightseeing, attending cultural events, short family visits, Umrah (not Hajj), and tourism-related activities. What it does not allow: working, studying, or long-term residency without switching to the appropriate visa category.

Visa on Arrival

Citizens of certain countries can obtain a visa on arrival at major Saudi airports and land crossings. Visa-on-arrival remains a useful option if you are eligible and traveling spontaneously, but applying for the eVisa in advance is usually faster and avoids long airport queues and potential delays. Visa-on-arrival eligibility is tied to passport nationality and can change; when in doubt, apply before you travel.

Business Visas

Business visitors attending meetings, conferences, or corporate negotiations typically enter on a business visa. These often require a Saudi host (company or organization) to apply on your behalf through official channels. Business visas can be single-entry or multiple-entry with varying validity periods and permitted stay durations. Short-term professional activities (presentations, training sessions, contract negotiations) are normally covered; direct employment is not.

Employment (Work) Visas and Residence (Iqama)

If you plan to work in Saudi Arabia, your Saudi employer will apply for a work visa and then for a residence permit (iqama) once you arrive and complete required procedures. The work visa allows entry for a medical exam and processing that leads to the iqama if all checks are passed. The iqama is the legal document that grants long-term work and residency rights. Work visas have strict sponsor and documentation rules: authenticated diplomas, employment contracts, MOFA approvals, and more.

Family Visit, Companion, and Personal Visit Visas

Family visit visas allow Saudi residents or citizens to host first-degree relatives for short stays. Companion and personal visit visas are other short stay categories used for accompanying travelers or invited guests. These visas are typically processed by the host in Saudi Arabia.

Student, Hajj, Umrah, and Transit Visas

Students accepted by Saudi institutions will receive student visas tied to their school. Hajj visas are issued through licensed organizers during the Hajj season with strict quotas and health requirements. Separate Umrah eVisas exist for pilgrims outside Hajj, and the tourist eVisa often includes Umrah privileges except during the Hajj season. Transit visas short-term options for travelers with extended layovers are also available under specific circumstances.

When to Contact an Embassy or Consulate

If your nationality is ineligible for the eVisa or visa-on-arrival, if you require a diplomatic or special-purpose visa, or if your situation is complicated (criminal records, passport irregularities, unusual name formats), contact your nearest Saudi embassy or consulate early. Embassy applications remain the route for many non-tourist categories and for travelers who cannot complete the eVisa process online.

How the Saudi eVisa Works (Practical Overview)

Eligibility and Who Benefits Most

The eVisa is designed for citizens of eligible countries and is especially helpful for independent travelers, small groups, and families traveling for leisure. It reduces paperwork, removes embassy visits for many applicants, and combines health insurance for the trip. Diplomats and people with special mission needs will use different procedures.

Typical Validity and Stay Limits

A standard tourist eVisa is valid for one year with multiple entries and allows a stay of up to 90 days per visit in most cases. Some applicants may receive single-entry visas allowing shorter stays (e.g., 30 days) depending on reciprocity rules and nationality. Always check your exact visa stamp or eVisa approval message for the validity and permitted duration.

Fees and Processing Time

Processing time can vary from same-day decisions to several business days. Fees vary by nationality and visa type and include the government charge plus processing or service provider fees if you use a third-party service. Expect faster processing if your application is complete and payment clears promptly.

Insurance and Health Requirements

Many eVisas include mandatory short-term travel health insurance for the duration of the trip. Certain visasâparticularly Hajjârequire additional health certificates or vaccinations (e.g., meningitis). For general tourism, there is no routine vaccination requirement for entry, but it's wise to be up to date on routine immunizations and to check any current public health notices.

Step-by-Step: How To Apply for a Tourist eVisa

Below is the clear, practical sequence to prepare and submit a successful eVisa application.

  • Passport (valid for at least six months from entry date), a recent passport-style photo, payment method (credit/debit card), accommodation details, and travel dates.
  • Optional but recommended: scanned copies of return ticket, travel itinerary, and contact information for your host or tour operator.
  1. Choose the correct visa type and gather documents. Confirm that you are applying for a tourist eVisa and that your nationality is eligible. If not, identify the correct embassy route.
  2. Visit the official online portal or approved application channels and start the form. Fill out every field accurately, matching exactly whatâs on your passport.
  3. Upload your passport photo and any required documents. Make certain the photo meets the requirementsâno glasses, plain background, good lighting.
  4. Pay the fee and submit the application. Save any confirmation number and the email you receive.
  5. Track your application if the portal provides tracking. Processing times vary; many approvals come within a few business days.
  6. Receive eVisa approval electronically. Carry a printed copy and a digital screenshot of the eVisa on your phone for arrival.
  7. On arrival, present your passport and eVisa confirmation at immigration. Keep copies of accommodation bookings and return tickets in case immigration officers request them.

Extra tips for a smooth application: use a desktop browser (some mobile browsers cause upload issues), scan documents at high resolution, and keep copies of everything. If you have a non-Latin alphabet name, ensure transliteration on passport and application are identical.

Applying Through an Embassy or Consulate

Some nationalities must apply at an embassy or consulate. Embassy applications sometimes require in-person appointments, notarized documents, and longer lead times. If your situation is complexâworking, studying, or relocatingâcoordinate with your sponsor or the Saudi consulate early. Embassies also handle special services like birth and death certificates, family legal matters, and official authentication of documents for immigration purposes.

Visas for Work and Long-Term Residence: The Sponsor System

The Sponsor (Kafeel) and MOFA Procedures

Most work visas require a Saudi employer to act as sponsor and submit a request to the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MOFA). Once the employer clears the initial approval and a visa issuance number is granted, the foreign worker receives a visa to enter and complete in-country steps for the iqama (residence permit).

Documents and Medical Exams

Workers typically present authenticated diplomas or professional certificates, employment contracts, a clean criminal record certificate, and undergo medical examinations both before departure and upon arrival. The employer coordinates many steps, but you as the applicant must ensure your documents are certified and translated if necessary.

From Work Visa to Iqama

After arrival, the employer completes the medical check, biometric enrollment, and applies for the iqama. Once issued, the iqama becomes your legal ID in Saudi and replaces the temporary visitor visa. It is essential for opening bank accounts, obtaining a driverâs license, and everyday life.

Arrival, Immigration, and Practical Rules

What to Expect at Immigration

Present your passport and eVisa (or entry stamp) to the immigration officer. Officers may ask for proof of accommodation, return ticket, or sufficient funds. Electronic gates process many travelers; when in doubt, join the regular immigration lines and have your documents ready.

Customs and Declarations

Declare regulated items as required. Saudi Arabia has strict rules on controlled substances, pornography, and other proscribed materials. Importing alcohol, pork products, or items that conflict with local laws is not permitted. Always check customs restrictions before you travel.

Currency and Payments

Saudi Riyal (SAR) is the official currency. Credit cards are widely accepted in cities; carry some local cash for smaller towns or informal markets. ATMs are available in airports and cities.

Health and Medical Care

Carry travel health insurance details and prescriptions in their original packaging. Medical care in larger cities is modern and widely available; in remote areas, services are more limited. The eVisa often includes short-term insurance but check what it covers and supplement it if necessary for medical evacuation or extended care.

Respecting Local Laws and Customs

Saudi Arabia has specific rules related to public behavior, dress, gender separation in some spaces, and religious observance. Be mindful of modest dress in public, especially in government buildings and religious sites. Alcohol is prohibited and public displays of affection can draw attention. Following local norms will make your stay more comfortable and reduce the risk of fines or complications.

Extending Your Stay or Changing Visa Type

If you need to extend your stay, the options depend on your visa type. Tourist eVisas are generally not extendable beyond their permitted stays; overstaying can result in fines and deportation. For work or residency changes, your sponsor facilitates amendments through official channels. If you anticipate a longer stay, plan to apply for the correct long-term visa before arrival or coordinate with your sponsor early after arrival.

Overstays, Fines, and Legal Consequences

Saudi authorities enforce visa rules strictly. Overstaying can lead to fines, deportation, and restrictions on future travel to the Kingdom. For non-citizens living in Saudi under sponsorship, itâs essential to keep iqama and exit/re-entry visas current to avoid penalties. If you realize you will overstay, contact immigration authorities or your sponsor immediately to find lawful remedies.

Special Cases: Family, Minors, and Newborns

Traveling with children: ensure that passports and visas cover minors (children need individual passports in many countries). For newborns born to expatriate parents in Saudi, there are procedures to register births and obtain documentation; these situations involve residence visa rules and possibly embassy services. If you are a first-degree relative visiting a Saudi citizen or resident, the host typically applies for the family visit visa.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q1: How long does the tourist eVisa take to be approved? Most eVisa decisions are issued within several business days if your application is complete, though exact processing times are not guaranteed and can vary by nationality and season. Submit your application well in advance of travel to avoid last-minute stress.

Q2: Can I perform Umrah on a tourist visa? Yes, the tourist eVisa generally includes Umrah access outside the Hajj season. Hajj itself requires a dedicated Hajj visa issued through licensed operators and within specified seasonal windows.

Q3: What happens if I lose my passport while in Saudi Arabia? Report a lost passport immediately to local police, your embassy or consulate, and your sponsor or hotel. Your embassy will assist with emergency travel documents and guidance on how to proceed.

Q4: Can I switch from a tourist visa to a work visa while in Saudi Arabia? Switching to a work visa usually requires a sponsor and processing through official channels. Many work visa procedures begin with the Saudi employer applying on your behalf; depending on circumstances, you may need to leave and re-enter under the new visa. Coordinate closely with your employer and the relevant authorities.
