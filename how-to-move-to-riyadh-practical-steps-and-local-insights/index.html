How to Move to Riyadh: Practical Steps and Local Insights

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Introduction

Riyadh is rapidly reshaping itself as a global hub for business, culture, and modern living. With new jobs, expanding infrastructure, and a growing international community, thousands of professionals and families now consider Riyadh a serious relocation option. If you are asking "how to move to Riyadh," this guide gives the clear, actionable roadmap you need—legal steps, logistics, cultural preparation, and on-the-ground tips from a local expert voice.

Short answer: The most reliable route to move to Riyadh is to secure employment or a valid sponsor, complete the visa and document attestation process, and then finalize housing and practical arrangements before arrival. Your employer will typically sponsor your work visa and handle the residence permit (iqama) process once you enter the Kingdom, but you must prepare key documents, medical checks, and a realistic timeline to avoid avoidable delays.

This article will walk you step-by-step from the decision to relocate through your first six months in Riyadh. You’ll find legal checklists, a timeline you can follow, housing and schooling frameworks, cultural and safety briefings, and pragmatic tips that seasoned expats wish they’d known before moving. My goal as the KSA Travel Insider is to give you the blueprint for a confident relocation that balances cultural respect with modern comfort. Why Choose Riyadh Now?

A Changing Economic Landscape

Riyadh is the epicenter of Saudi Arabia’s economic transformation under Vision 2030. Sectors such as technology, finance, renewable energy, entertainment, and tourism are expanding—creating opportunities for professionals across disciplines. Salaries are often tax-free for expats, and major employers frequently include housing, schooling allowances, and private health insurance as part of the package.

Modern Amenities with Deep Heritage

Riyadh blends fast-paced urban development—skyscrapers, international restaurants, and leisure venues—with historic quarters and cultural institutions. Safety, Infrastructure, and Family Life

The Kingdom generally ranks highly for public safety, and Riyadh offers high-quality hospitals, international schools, and secure residential options. Weekend structure (Friday–Saturday) and prayer times shape daily rhythms, so understanding local scheduling is a practical first step in planning your life here.

Understanding The Legal Routes To Move

Visa Types and Which One You Need

There are several legal pathways for moving to Saudi Arabia; choosing the right one determines your timeline and obligations.

  • Employment Visa (most common): Your Saudi employer applies for a work visa on your behalf. After arrival, the employer processes your residency permit (iqama). This route is the standard path for those relocating for a job.
  • Family Visit / Dependent Visa: If your spouse or parent is a Saudi resident with an iqama, you may enter on a family visa, though employment rights remain restricted without a proper work visa.
  • Student Visa: For those who enroll in a recognized Saudi educational institution.
  • Premium Residency (Privileged Iqama): A paid residency option for high-net-worth individuals offering broader rights, though it does not lead automatically to citizenship.
  • Tourist and Business Visas: These do not permit work or long-term residency, but they can be useful for reconnaissance visits before committing to a move.

Your employer will typically manage the employment visa process, but understanding each step keeps you in control.

The Work Visa & Iqama Process — Step By Step

The typical flow for an employment move is:

  1. Job offer and signed contract with a Saudi sponsor/employer.
  2. Employer applies for a work visa through the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and issues an electronic visa invitation.
  3. You submit documents to the Saudi consulate or embassy in your home country for visa issuance; this can include medical checkups and attested educational certificates.
  4. You arrive in Saudi Arabia on the issued visa. Within 90 days, the employer completes medical tests locally and initiates iqama issuance.
  5. Once the iqama is issued, you become a legal resident with access to most local services; your employer will complete residence registration, bank introductions, and insurance setup.

Expect the entire pre-arrival and iqama issuance to span several weeks to a few months depending on your nationality, job sector, and the employer’s efficiency.

Documents That Must Be Prepared and Attested

Document authentication is a critical and often underestimated task. Prepare for attestation and translation where required. The essential documents usually include:

  • Valid passport (six months minimum validity)
  • Signed employment contract
  • Educational degrees and professional certificates (authenticated by home country authorities and sometimes the Saudi consulate)
  • Police clearance certificate (may be required for certain roles)
  • Medical records and vaccination certificates
  • Birth/marriage certificates, if bringing dependents

Because document requirements change, always confirm your employer’s checklist and use professional attestation services if needed. For a concise list of what to gather before you go, see the Essential Documents list below.

Essential Documents (List 1 — Use this as your packing checklist)

  • Passport (original + photocopies)
  • Signed employment contract
  • Degree certificates (attested)

Practical Timeline: From Offer to Settled (90-Day Moving Timeline — List 2)

  • 90–60 Days Before Departure: Accept offer; confirm visa sponsorship; start attesting documents; notify current landlord/employer.
  • 60–30 Days Before Departure: Schedule medical checks required by employer; finalize school applications; obtain police clearance; arrange pet transport decisions.
  • 30–14 Days Before Departure: Book flights; obtain travel insurance; finalize international shipping; register for any employer onboarding portals.
  • 14–0 Days Before Departure: Confirm airport pickup with employer; prepare a travel-ready essentials kit; scan and email important documents to your sponsor and trusted family.
  • Arrival to 30 Days: Undergo local medical tests; apply for iqama; open a local bank account with employer assistance; secure accommodation.
  • 30–90 Days: Receive iqama; register for local services (SIM, utilities); enroll children in school; get driving license converted or obtain a Saudi license.

Treat this timeline as a practical framework. Each move has its own variations, but keeping the sequence clear prevents last-minute bottlenecks.

Finding Work and Negotiating Your Contract

Job Search Strategies That Work in Riyadh

Network, use industry recruiters, and target companies with established expat hiring practices. Online job portals for the Gulf market, professional LinkedIn outreach, and international recruitment firms focusing on the Middle East are productive channels. When you receive a verbal offer, request a written contract before proceeding with the visa application.

Key Contract Clauses to Negotiate

A relocation-friendly contract should clearly specify items such as:

  • Salary and currency (SAR is standard)
  • Housing allowance or company-provided accommodation
  • Health insurance coverage for dependents
  • Schooling allowance for children
  • Flight allowances and repatriation clauses
  • Probation period and notice terms

Do not assume entitlements; get them in writing. Having housing included or a housing allowance can save months of searching and significant out-of-pocket expense.

Housing and Neighborhoods in Riyadh

Compound Living vs. Local Housing

Many expats prefer gated compounds for the amenities and social comfort they provide—swimming pools, gyms, and mixed-community facilities that feel familiar to Western lifestyles. Compounds offer practical advantages for families and those who value a more relaxed dress code within the compound. However, compounds can be pricier and sometimes have long waitlists.

Outside compounds, you’ll find apartment buildings and private villas across Riyadh’s districts. Each area has a different feel: Olaya and Al Olaya are central and business-oriented, while northern neighborhoods like Al Malaz and Al Nakheel offer family-friendly villas. Work location often dictates the most practical neighborhood.

For guidance on city-specific neighborhoods and practical living tips, refer to our broader Saudi resources, which include housing articles and neighborhood breakdowns (broader Saudi Arabia coverage).

Typical Rent and What to Expect

Expect rental patterns similar to these ranges (monthly, approximate):

  • 1-bedroom apartment (city center): 5,000–6,500 SAR
  • 1-bedroom outside center: 2,500–5,000 SAR
  • 3-bedroom apartment (city center): 8,300–12,500 SAR
  • 3-bedroom outside center: 5,800–10,000 SAR

Villas with private pools are widely available at various price points; employers sometimes include villas in relocation packages for families. Factor in utilities—air conditioning costs can be high in summer months.

How to Search and What Questions to Ask

When viewing a property, assess internet availability, AC efficiency, proximity to groceries, schools and mosques, and noise levels during Friday prayers. Ask about furnished vs. unfurnished status, included maintenance, contract length, and deposit terms. Visit at prayer times to understand how the neighborhood rhythm feels live.

Healthcare, Health Insurance, and Medical Prep

Health Insurance Requirements

Most employers include private health insurance. Verify policy limits, exclusions, and whether dependents are covered. Check for coverage of chronic conditions, maternity, dental, and international evacuation.

Health Preparations Before You Travel

Get up-to-date vaccinations (tetanus, influenza, MMR, hepatitis A/B recommended depending on history), bring prescriptions with a doctor’s letter and translations, and have a plan for routine medications. Riyadh’s major hospitals are well-equipped, and English is commonly understood in medical settings.

Education and Schooling for Families

International School Options and Enrollment Tips

Riyadh hosts many international schools offering British, American, IB and other curricula. These schools can have long waiting lists—apply as early as you can once your move is confirmed. Expect tuition to be a significant budget item; negotiate schooling allowances if possible.

Public School Considerations

Public schools are primarily Arabic-language and segregated by gender. Most expatriate families choose private international schools for continuity and language matters.

Transport and Driving in Riyadh

Getting Around

Riyadh is a car-first city. Public transport options are improving—Riyadh Metro is a major development—but many residents rely on private cars, ride-hailing apps, and taxis. Traffic can be heavy during peak commuting hours.

Driving License and Vehicles

If you plan to drive, bring an International Driving Permit (IDP). Some nationalities can convert their licenses once resident; others must take local driving tests. Driving rules are similar to many countries: stay aware of speed limits, keep your vehicle documentation (istimara), and ensure regular vehicle inspection (fahs).

If you work with a company, they may provide a driver or a company car as part of the package.

Culture, Etiquette, and Daily Life

Basic Cultural Rules to Respect

Dress modestly in public—women typically wear abayas in many public settings and men should avoid shorts in public contexts. Avoid public displays of affection, be mindful of gender-specific seating areas in some restaurants, and respect prayer times, during which many shops briefly close.

Avoid political or religious commentary that could be perceived as offensive toward the monarchy, Islam, or public policy. Respectful curiosity and a willingness to learn are highly valued.

Ramadan, Public Holidays, and Rituals

During Ramadan, daytime public eating is restricted; follow local norms and be discreet. The rhythm of life changes on religious holidays—plan travel and service appointments accordingly.

Social Life: Making Friends and Building Community

Join expat groups, company social events, and community activities at schools. While forming deep friendships with Saudi nationals may take time due to cultural structures emphasizing family life, professional and social connectors like language classes, cultural events, and business associations are effective bridges.

Women Living in Riyadh

Saudi social policy has evolved; women now drive and participate in the workforce in growing numbers. However, norms vary across neighborhoods and settings. Women should be aware of dress expectations, gendered spaces in some venues, and local etiquette. Many women work, socialize, and have active lives—your experience will depend on your sector, company culture, and chosen neighborhood.

Money, Banking, and Taxes

Saudi Arabia uses the Saudi Riyal (SAR). Major global banks operate in Riyadh. Employers usually provide a letter of introduction to open a local account. Take care to understand salary disbursement timelines and bank fees. There is no personal income tax for most expats, but a 15% VAT on purchases is in force—budget accordingly.

Moving Logistics: Shipping, Pets, and Customs

Shipping Household Goods

Decide between sea freight (cost-effective, slower) and air freight (fast, expensive). Some employers include a shipment allowance; confirm whether your accommodation is furnished. Use experienced international movers and request itemized customs clearance guidance.

Pets

Import rules for dogs are strict; many dog breeds are restricted and dogs are limited to working breeds with breed-specific rules. For other pets, import permits, vaccination records, and quarantine requirements may apply. Confirm with your relocation company early on.

Customs and Restricted Items

Saudi customs enforce strict controls on items such as alcohol, certain medications, and media considered offensive. Bring prescriptions with medical letters and translations. Avoid shipping banned items altogether.

Practical Tech & Service Setup After Arrival

Within the first month after arrival, prioritize obtaining a local SIM card, setting up internet, and opening a bank account. Employers often help with these introductions. Use e-services (Absher and others) where appropriate once you have your iqama.

Leisure, Travel, and Weekend Options

Riyadh is centrally located for exploring the Kingdom’s cultural and natural attractions. Weekend escapes to desert landscapes, historical sites, and nearby cities are common. For cultural excursions beyond the capital—ancient rock formations and well-preserved sites—you’ll find inspiring itineraries and practical tips on culture-focused travel resources (cultural sites beyond Riyadh).

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

  • Relying on incomplete paperwork: Double-check attestation and translation requirements with both your employer and the Saudi consulate.
  • Underestimating timing: Visa and iqama issuance can take longer than expected—start early.
  • Accepting vague contract terms: Get housing, health insurance, and schooling clauses in writing.
  • Ignoring cultural norms: Even small breaches can create social friction; learn basic etiquette and key Arabic phrases.
  • Not planning schooling early: International school waitlists fill quickly—apply early.

How to Make the First Six Months Successful

Begin with a structured settling-in routine: prioritize iqama, bank account, health insurance, and housing. Build a local social calendar—language classes, sports clubs, and company events accelerate integration. Keep a flexible mindset; adapting to new rhythms—prayer times, business hours, and different social norms—is part of successful relocation.

Preparing Financially: Budgeting For Your Move

Account for one-time moving costs (shipping, flights, visa fees), short-term accommodation (compounds can be costly), deposits and rent advance, school fees, and initial furniture or household items if unfurnished. Maintain a contingency fund for unexpected medical or administrative expenses.

Language: How Much Arabic Do You Need?

English is widely used in business, healthcare, and many services, but basic Arabic will transform your daily life. Learn key phrases for markets, taxis, and social niceties. Formal classes or apps paired with local practice will speed progress and deepen cultural connections.

For structured language learning options before you move, explore local and online Arabic courses tailored for relocation preparation—these offer practical vocabulary and scenarios useful from day one.

Workplace Culture and Professional Etiquette

Business culture in Riyadh blends formal respect with a growing international professional standard. Meetings often begin with polite conversation; building trust is key. Dress conservatively in many professional settings and follow the lead of your Saudi colleagues on formalities. Accept that hierarchical structures may be more pronounced in some organizations.

Long-Term Residency and Citizenship

Long-term residency routes like the Premium Residency Program offer broader rights for eligible applicants, but full citizenship remains rare. If long-term residency is your goal, discuss options early with your employer and understand the legal differences between temporary work residency and premium residency.

Final Checklist Before You Go

  • Confirm visa type and sponsorship details.
  • Complete and attest all educational and civil documents.
  • Secure a flight and confirm airport pickup.
  • Arrange temporary housing for arrival (hotel or short-term rental).
  • Prepare a travel essentials bag with documents, prescriptions, and local-currency cash.
  • Enroll children in schools and organize pet transport or alternatives.

FAQ

What is the single most important step to get right when planning a move to Riyadh?

Securing a valid sponsor or employment contract is the decisive step. The employer or sponsor initiates the visa and iqama process, so ensure your contract clearly defines salary, housing, health insurance, and schooling allowances before you commit.

How long does it usually take to receive an iqama after arriving?

Timelines vary, but most employers complete local medical checks and submit residency paperwork within weeks of arrival. In many cases, expect the iqama issuance to take anywhere from a few weeks up to 2–3 months depending on documentation and administrative processing.

Can my family accompany me immediately when I move for work?

Yes—dependents can be added, but they require their own dependent visas and attested documents. Schools and family medical cover should be arranged as early as possible; many expat families delay dependents’ arrival until housing and schooling are confirmed.

Is it necessary to live inside an expat compound?

No. Many expats live in private apartments or villas without being in a compound. Compounds offer lifestyle conveniences and social networks that can be helpful initially—especially for families—but they are not mandatory. Your choice should balance cost, commute time, and social preferences.
