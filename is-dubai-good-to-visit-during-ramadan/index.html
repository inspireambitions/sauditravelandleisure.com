What Is Ramadan â A Quick Practical Overview

The Basics of the Month

Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar. Fasting (sawm) from dawn (suhoor) until sunset (iftar) is the central observance, but the month also emphasizes prayer, community, charity, and self-discipline. Because the lunar calendar is about 11 days shorter than the solar year, Ramadan shifts earlier each year â so the exact Gregorian dates vary.

How Ramadan Affects Daily Life

For residents and businesses, Ramadan brings predictable changes:

  • Eating and drinking in public during daylight hours is socially sensitive and often illegal in some jurisdictions, though hotels typically serve non-fasting guests discreetly.
  • Work hours may be shortened and some government offices operate on adjusted schedules.
  • Restaurants that normally close during the day may stay shut or operate in private areas; many open after sunset for lavish iftar spreads.
  • Evening streets, malls, and mosques become focal points for social life; late-night options expand, and cultural programming intensifies.

Understanding these shifts is the first step to planning a respectful and enjoyable trip to Dubai during Ramadan.

After sunset, food markets and pop-up stalls proliferate. Areas around Al Fahidi, Al Seef, and parts of old Dubai often host lively evening markets where you can sample regional specialties. Licensed hotels and bars will generally continue to serve alcohol to non-Muslim guests, but some restrictions may apply and venues may operate on different schedules. Always carry ID and be discrete.
