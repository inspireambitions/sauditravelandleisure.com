Practical Logistics and Money-Saving Tips

Transport: Taxis, Car Rentals, and Buses

Taxis are ubiquitous but can be costly for frequent cross-city travel unless you negotiate or use metered services when available. Renting a car gives the greatest flexibility for day trips outside Muscat, with well-maintained roads and predictable driving etiquette. Public buses exist but are less convenient for tourists on short stays.

When renting a car, book in advance through reputable providers and inspect the vehicle and insurance terms. If you plan a single-day organized trip (for example, to the Daymaniyat Islands) you can avoid car rental and rely on tour operators.

Money and Payments

Omani Rial is the local currency. Credit cards are widely accepted in hotels and upmarket restaurants, but carry cash for small purchases, souqs, and tipping. ATMs are available at the airport and around the city. For short stays, pre-purchasing an eSIM can save time and avoid airport queues.

Budget Expectations

Muscat is not the cheapest capital in the region, but it is far from the most expensive. Expect higher prices at tourist-favored restaurants and luxury hotels; local eateries and mid-range options offer excellent value. Prioritize your spending: choose either more nights in comfortable accommodation and fewer expensive excursions, or lower-cost lodging with more day trips.

Safety and Health

Muscat is a safe city for travelers; usual precautions apply. Carry sun protection, drink bottled or treated water where recommended, and be cautious of flash floods in wadis after storms. For outdoor activities, carry first-aid basics and let someone know your plan. If you have health concerns, confirm the availability of medical facilities at your accommodation or in nearby towns.

Cultural Etiquette and Dress

Dressing Respectfully

Modesty matters in Oman. For most public spaces, women should cover shoulders and knees; when visiting mosques, women must cover their hair and wear clothing that covers arms and ankles. Men should avoid tank tops and short shorts in public. On resort beaches, swimwear at private hotel pools is acceptable, but public beaches frequented by families call for more conservative swimwear choices or cover-ups.

Photography and Privacy

Always ask before photographing people, particularly women and children. Many Omanis value privacy; polite, clear verbal permission is the right approach. In public spaces like markets, photography of goods and architecture is generally fine, but personal photography still benefits from asking.

Is Muscat safe for solo travelers?

Yes. Muscat is considered safe for solo travelers, including solo women. Standard precautions applyâavoid poorly lit, isolated areas at night and respect local dress and cultural norms. How many days are enough to see the highlights of Muscat?

Three to four full days cover Muscatâs main city highlights and one major day trip (for example, the Daymaniyat Islands or a wadi). If you plan multiple day trips or want to explore southern regions like Salalah, allocate a week or more.

Can I visit Muscat during Ramadan?

Non-Muslim visitors can be in Oman during Ramadan, but you must follow local rules: no eating, drinking, or smoking in public during daylight hours. Many restaurants are closed or serve discreetly during the day, and cultural sites may have altered hours. Dress modestly and be respectful of the fasting period.

Yes. English is widely spoken in tourism hubs, hotels, and restaurants. Learning a few Arabic greetings and phrases enhances interactions and is appreciated by locals.
