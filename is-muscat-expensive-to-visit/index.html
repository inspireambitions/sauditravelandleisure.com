Is Muscat Expensive to Visit?

Updated October 24, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Muscat, Oman’s coastal capital, has been steadily attracting travelers who want warm seas, dramatic mountains, and a calm, cultured city that feels distinctly different from louder Gulf hubs. As regional tourism grows, practical questions come up first: what will you actually spend, and can you control the cost without sacrificing experiences?

Short answer: Muscat is moderately priced — neither a bargain backpacker haven nor among the ultra-expensive capitals of the world. With intentional choices you can visit Muscat on a tight budget, enjoy a comfortable mid-range trip, or indulge in luxury experiences. This article breaks down the real costs across accommodation, transport, food, activities, and planning logistics, then gives the step-by-step blueprints to plan a Muscat trip that matches your priorities and wallet.

The purpose of this post is to turn ambiguity into clear decisions. You’ll get accurate daily-cost ranges, season-by-season cost implications, comparison context across the Gulf, and actionable frameworks for saving or splurging strategically. These are the same practical, on-the-ground planning systems we use at Saudi Travel & Leisure to help readers transform curiosity into a flawlessly executed trip.

How To Read This Cost Analysis

What "expensive" means in the context of Muscat

When travelers ask "Is Muscat expensive to visit?", they usually mean one of three things: the cost of accommodation, the price of local food and transport, or the expense of activities. Muscat’s price profile is shaped by a strong local currency (Omani rial), a high service standard in hospitality, and a geography that makes some experiences inherently more costly (boat trips, guided nature tours, and coastal diving). In short, Muscat sits in the middle of the spectrum for the Gulf: pricier than many Asian budget destinations, but much more accessible than ultra-luxury capitals.

Currency and conversion context

Card payments are widely accepted in Muscat; ATMs are plentiful in the city but less common in remote areas. International cards work reliably, though a small cash cushion is wise for markets, small cafés and day trips.

The Big Picture: Daily Budget Ranges for Muscat

Typical daily cost categories

A realistic planning approach treats Muscat costs as the sum of five categories: accommodation, food & drink, local transport, activities & attractions, and incidentals (SIM, tips, entrance fees). Below are workable daily ranges per person that reflect the choices travelers typically make.

  • Budget travel (shared dorms or cheap guesthouses, mostly public transport, cooking or inexpensive local meals, free attractions): around USD 40–70 per day (â OMR 15–27).
  • Mid-range travel (3-star hotels or private guesthouses, some taxis or rental car days, a mix of local restaurants and café meals, paid excursions): around USD 100–180 per day (â OMR 38–70).
  • Luxury travel (4–5 star resorts, private transfers, guided tours, boat charters, fine dining): from USD 250+ per day (â OMR 96+).

These bands are guides — your real spending will depend on seasonality, group size, and how many paid activities you include.

Why Muscat’s cost profile is flexible

Muscat offers strong economies of scale for self-drivers and camp-style adventures in Oman’s wider landscape, yet the city itself is service-oriented: hotels, international restaurants, and organized tours are available at all levels. That mix creates flexibility: you can anchor in Muscat and use day-trips for high-impact, cost-controlled experiences.

Accommodation: Where You’ll Spend Most (Or Not)

Accommodation types and realistic pricing

Hotels and guesthouses set the tone for your daily budget because lodging often accounts for 30–50% of costs on short trips.

  • Hostels / Budget Guesthouses: OMR 8–15 per night (USD 20–40) for a simple private room or shared dorm. These are limited but available near central neighborhoods.
  • Mid-range Hotels / Boutique Guesthouses: OMR 20–50 (USD 52–130). This is the sweet spot for comfort, breakfast included, and central locations near Muttrah, Shatti Al Qurum or Ruwi.
  • 4–5 Star Hotels & Resorts: OMR 60–200+ (USD 155–520+). Beachfront resorts, especially those offering all-inclusive packages or private beach access, sit at the top end.

Muscat’s hotels often include generous breakfasts, which reduces daily food costs. Booking early (especially for high season) secures better rates; last-minute luxury deals do appear, but for mid-range travelers early booking is still the safer money move.

Neighborhoods explained for value and convenience

Where you stay affects both price and logistics. Muttrah (the old harbor) is excellent for souqs and museums; Shatti Al Qurum has a coastal promenade and more modern hotels; Al Khuwair and Ruwi are business districts with economical hotels and easy transport. Choosing a central neighborhood can reduce taxi costs and allow you to walk to major attractions.

Practical savings tactics for lodging

Consider splitting nights: stay mid-range in Muscat for 2–3 nights to experience the city, then move to a budget guesthouse or campsite if you’re extending into Oman’s hinterlands. Use the booking horizon — mid-range properties often release non-refundable promotional rates if your dates are set.

Food & Drink: Affordable, Diverse, and Locally Delicious

Typical meal pricing

Omani cuisine and regional restaurants are where great value hides.

  • Street/Local eateries: OMR 1–3 (USD 2.5–8) for sandwiches, shawarma-style wraps, and local rice plates.
  • Mid-range restaurants and cafés: OMR 4–10 (USD 10–26) per person for mains and a drink.
  • Upscale dining / hotel restaurants: OMR 15–40+ (USD 40–104+) per person.

Breakfasts are frequently included in hotel rates; supermarket prepared foods are affordable and perfect for light lunches or picnic-style dinners.

Alcohol and special-case costs

Alcohol is available in licensed hotel restaurants and a few bars; expect prices higher than comparable cities due to import costs and licensing. If you plan to drink, budget extra. For most travelers who stick to non-alcoholic options or local cafés, food costs remain very reasonable.

Ways to save on meals without missing out

Eat where locals eat—small roadside cafés and marketplaces serve excellent, low-cost meals. Shopping at modern supermarkets for a few prepared meals or breakfast basics is smart if you’re mixing hotel breakfasts and day trips. Muscat’s food scene rewards curiosity; try a local fish-market plate or Omani halwa for a memorable and inexpensive bite.

Local Transport: Taxis, Rental Cars, and Public Options

Taxi and car-hire realities

Taxis are plentiful in Muscat, but fares can add up if you use them frequently. Metered taxis operate in city centers; negotiate or confirm the use of the meter for out-of-town trips. Ride-hailing apps are available in Muscat and offer predictable fares.

A rental car is the most common choice for travelers who want to explore beyond Muscat. Rental cost depends on car type and insurance options. Expect to pay more for 4x4s and less for sedans; fuel is comparatively cheap across Oman, which makes driving an economical choice for multi-day itineraries. If your plan is only intra-city, taxis and apps are more cost-efficient.

Public buses and alternatives

Muscat has improved public transport options in recent years, including intercity buses that are cost-effective for longer transfers. For shorter trips within the city, minibus services and shared taxis can save money but require time and local navigation.

Cost comparisons: car hire vs local transport

If you plan to take multiple day trips outside Muscat, a rental car often lowers per-day transport costs. For a short stay in Muscat with concentrated city sightseeing, taxis plus an occasional guided tour ticket will be cheaper and simpler.

Activities & Attractions: Where Costs Concentrate

Free and low-cost highlights

Muscat has many wonderful experiences that are low- or no-cost: Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque (free entry for visitors at specified hours), coastal promenades, public beaches, and walks through Muttrah Souq. These provide major cultural value without a heavy price tag.

Paid experiences and what they cost

  • Dolphin-watching boat trips: OMR 10–30 (USD 26–78) depending on operator and duration.
  • Snorkeling and diving day trips (Daymaniyat Islands): OMR 25–80 (USD 65–208) — excursions to marine reserves are one of the largest discretionary spends.
  • Guided historical tours and museum entry fees: typically OMR 1–5 (USD 2.5–13).
  • Private guided adventures (off-road, desert camps): OMR 50–200+ per activity depending on group size and inclusions.

These paid activities are often high-value: east-of-city snorkeling or private mountain guides provide uniquely Omani experiences that aren’t available elsewhere.

How to decide where to spend

Allocate more budget to two or three “headline” experiences you care about (e.g., Daymaniyat snorkeling and a half-day boat trip), then prioritize low-cost cultural exploration for the rest of your time. This mix delivers both signature memories and controlled spending.

Seasonal Effects on Cost and Value

High season vs low season

Muscat’s high season runs roughly from October to April when temperatures are pleasant. Expect hotel prices and tourist activity to be higher in winter months; book well in advance for holiday periods and school vacations. During the intense summer months (May–September), hotel rates can drop and there are more deals, but extreme heat limits some outdoor activities.

Events, Ramadan and special circumstances

During Ramadan, the rhythm of the city changes: daytime dining options for non-fasting visitors will be more limited, and many businesses alter hours. Pricing for accommodation may be more favorable during Ramadan, but cultural respect and adjusted schedules are essential. Major events, festivals and school holidays will push accommodation prices up, so check the calendar when locking in dates.

Step-By-Step Budget Blueprint: Plan Your Muscat Trip With Confidence

Below is a concise checklist that will guide you from initial planning through on-the-ground budgeting. This is the only list in the post and is designed to be an actionable, sequential tool you can follow.

  • Decide your travel style and choose three headline experiences (e.g., snorkeling, mountain hike, cultural walking tour). Set a firm budget for those.
  • Lock in flights and check visa rules early; these can be significant single costs depending on origin.
  • Book at least the first two nights’ accommodation centrally; compare refundable vs non-refundable rates for savings.
  • If exploring beyond Muscat, calculate rental car costs by day and fuel usage versus taxi/transfer rates for specific routes.
  • Pre-book paid activities where possible to secure better rates and availability; prioritize one high-impact paid activity and fill the rest with free/low-cost options.
  • Prepare a daily spending cap by category: lodging, food, transport, activities, incidentals. Reallocate mid-trip as needed.
  • Download offline maps, buy a local SIM for data, and pick payment methods (cards + small cash reserve) to avoid ATM fees.

Follow this sequence and you’ll have a transparent, controllable budget that supports both flexibility and memorable experiences.

Sample 3-, 5- and 7-Day Budgets (Practical Scenarios)

3-Day Budget: City Concentration — Low to Mid Range

A compact itinerary focused on Muscat core attractions and one paid activity.

  • Accommodation: 2 nights in a mid-range hotel (OMR 40–60 total per night shared).
  • Food: mix of hotel breakfast, cafés and a local restaurant (OMR 6–12 per meal).
  • Transport: taxis and one day-hire for coastal exploration (OMR 10–25 per day).
  • Activity: half-day dolphin trip or museum + souq time (OMR 10–25).

Estimated total (per person): USD 200–400 depending on choice of hotel and activities, with potential to go lower if choosing budget hotels and exclusive use of public transport.

5-Day Budget: Balanced City and Coastal Experience — Mid-Range

A balanced plan allowing for a full-day marine excursion and a half-day mountain trip.

  • Accommodation: 4 nights mid-range (OMR 25–45 per night).
  • Food: breakfasts included or supermarket/market lunches with one evening at a pricier seafood restaurant.
  • Transport: mix of rental car days and taxis for city nights.
  • Activities: Daymaniyat snorkeling or boat, Grand Mosque visit, Muttrah Souq, short mountain drive.

Estimated total (per person): USD 500–900. This mid-range scenario gives room to pay for two paid excursions while keeping most other days inexpensive.

7+ Day Budget: Extended Exploration — Flexible

Longer trips allow cost smoothing. Rent a car for multiple days and split nights between Muscat and a coastal or mountain base.

  • Accommodation: varied (mix of guesthouses and one nicer beachfront stay).
  • Food: more supermarket meals and local restaurants.
  • Transport: rental car for several days; fuel is affordable.
  • Activities: two-to-three paid excursions plus free cultural exploration.

Estimated total (per person): USD 800–1,800 depending on number of private excursions and overall accommodation choices.

Smart Money Moves and Mistakes to Avoid

Proven ways to reduce cost without losing experience

  • Prioritize one or two paid, high-value experiences and keep the rest low-cost. A snorkeling day and a cultural guided walk will often deliver more satisfaction than three mid-tier paid activities.
  • Use hotels with breakfast included. That single upgrade often offsets a significant portion of daily food spend.
  • If planning multiple day trips, renting a car (with good insurance) typically beats repeated private transfers.
  • Book early for high season and look for weekday discounts on tours.

Common budgeting mistakes

  • Booking expensive city-center hotels for the entire trip when part of your journey will be in remote coastlines or mountains.
  • Underestimating activity costs for marine excursions and specialized guided trips.
  • Ignoring the impact of holiday weeks and festivals on accommodation rates.

Logistics: Visas, SIMs, Safety and Cultural Costs

Visas and arrival considerations

Visa policies vary by nationality. Plan this early: some nationalities require e-visas or pay fees upon arrival, and visa duration can affect total cost. Allow a small contingency in your budget for visa fees, printed documentation, or mandatory travel insurance if required.

SIM cards and connectivity

Local SIM packages with reasonable data are available at the airport and in city centers. Expect to pay a modest one-time fee for decent data plans. Many travelers now prefer eSIMs for convenience and often competitive pricing.

Safety and tipping norms

Muscat is safe for travelers. Tipping is appreciated but not always obligatory—round up bills or tip porters and guides modestly. Budget a small daily incidental for tips and small services.

Cultural expectations that affect cost

Certain activities — like private guides for cultural tours, or dress-appropriate entrances to mosques — may require small fees or purchases (e.g., renting an abaya or headscarf). Factor these into day-one planning to avoid unexpected spending.

Where Muscat Delivers High Value

What your money buys in Muscat

Muscat’s price vs experience equation is excellent in several areas: the quality of food for cost, well-maintained public spaces, and authentic cultural attractions like Muttrah Souq and the Grand Mosque. Marine life excursions are high-impact experiences that justify higher per-day spending because they offer access to protected islands and reefs.

When to splurge and when to save

Splurge on guided marine or mountain experiences where specialized guides and equipment enhance safety and access. Save on city transit and meals by choosing local eateries and walking where possible. This approach preserves budget for standout experiences without constant compromise.

Practical Day-By-Day Micro-Planning Blueprint

Day 1–2: Arrive and acclimatize

Check into a centrally located hotel, walk the Corniche, visit Muttrah Souq and catch a sunset. Use this time to pick up an Omani SIM and confirm timings for major attractions like the Grand Mosque.

Day 3: Marine focus

Book an early-morning dolphin-watching trip or a snorkeling day trip to the Daymaniyat Islands. Expect the largest single-day expense here — plan accordingly and bring snorkeling gear if you have it to reduce rental fees.

Day 4: Cultural depth

Reserve a guided walking tour of old Muscat and the Royal Opera House area, then visit a museum. These low-cost days often deliver surprising cultural richness.

Day 5+: Regional day trips

Depending on your interests, pick a mountain drive to Wadi Shab or a coastal exploration. If you’re renting a car, schedule multi-day drives to amortize rental fees.

Use the budgeting checklist earlier to track day-by-day spending and adjust quickly. This micro-planning approach keeps flexibility while avoiding costly last-minute decisions.

Final Practical Advice Before You Travel

  • Always check the precise cancellation and refund rules for hotels and tours, especially if traveling in shoulder season.
  • If you plan to rent a car, choose the right insurance package and check mileage restrictions. Fuel cost is typically low, but some rentals include daily mileage caps.
  • Carry a compact first-aid kit and necessary medications; pharmacy availability is good, but brand options vary.
  • Keep an eye on weather forecasts: Muscat’s winter is perfect for outdoor activity, while summer requires a more indoor-leaning itinerary.

FAQ

1) Can I visit Muscat on a tight budget?

Yes. By choosing budget guesthouses, relying on public transport or limited taxi use, eating where locals eat, and prioritizing free attractions, a visitor can comfortably spend in the lower daily range outlined earlier. Self-driven day-trips are cost-effective if you split rental costs across a group.

2) How much should I budget for activities like snorkeling or dolphin tours?

Expect to allocate your largest activity budget to marine excursions. Snorkeling or dive trips to protected islands typically cost significantly more than museum entries; set aside one to two high-cost days if marine life is a priority.

3) Is it cheaper to rent a car or use taxis?

If you plan multiple day trips outside Muscat, rent a car — fuel is affordable and flexibility is valuable. For city-only stays of a few days, taxis and ride-hailing services are often cheaper and easier.

4) When is the best time to get the most value for money?

Travel in shoulder seasons (early autumn or late spring) can yield lower accommodation prices than peak winter while still offering good weather. Summer months may yield the best hotel deals but limit outdoor options due to heat.
