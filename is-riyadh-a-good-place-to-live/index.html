Transport and Getting Around

The car-first city

Riyadh is a car city. For most residents, owning or leasing a car is the most practical way to commute, run errands, and manage family schedules. The road network is extensive but congested during peak hours; driving styles are assertive and require defensive driving. If youâre not comfortable driving, hiring a personal driver is common among families.

Public transit and the future

Riyadh is building an expansive public transport system, including a metro network that will transform intra-city travel when fully operational. Until then, ride-hailing apps, taxis, and private buses are common. Many families rely on school buses and compound shuttles.

Walking, cycling and the pedestrian experience

Sidewalks are limited in many districts and the heat makes walking uncomfortable during much of the year. Newer developments and the Diplomatic Quarter are more pedestrian-friendly, but overall, realistic expectations assume car dependence.

Cost of Living: Budgeting for Riyadh

Key cost drivers

Major costs include housing, international school fees, private healthcare (if not covered), domestic help and transportation. Eating out and lifestyle expenses vary widely depending on choices, with luxury options mirroring any global city while everyday groceries can be very affordable.

Example budgeting approach

Plan housing as your single largest expense. If your employer offers a housing allowance, understand whether it covers rent fully or partially. Factor in school fees early if you have children. Domestic help and drivers, if used, add to monthly costs but can significantly affect quality of life.

Money management practicalities

Banking is modern and efficient; opening an account requires an iqama (residency permit). Cash is still common for small purchases. Currency is the Saudi riyal (SAR). Many expats use international banking services for savings and transfers, but local banks suffice for daily needs.

Cultural Expectations and Everyday Etiquette

Dress and public behaviour

Public dress codes are conservative. For many expatriates in international areas and compounds, expectations are relaxed; in public spaces, modest dress is expected. Respect for local customs extends to interactions and public conduct.

Religious sensitivity and public life

Religion informs public schedules and community norms. Respect prayer times, be mindful during Ramadan (no eating or drinking in public during daytime), and avoid behaviour that could be seen as disrespectful to religious practices.

Building respectful local relationships

Making long-term Saudi friends takes time and sensitivity. Business and formal relationships develop faster than intimate friendships. Engage respectfully, accept invitations, and show interest in local culture; this fosters meaningful ties over time.

Recently introduced programs have expanded options for certain skilled professionals and investors, but the employer-sponsored iqama remains the most common path.

2. Are there communities or clubs for newcomers to meet people?

Yes. International schools, compound social programs, embassy events, professional associations and expat networking groups provide dependable ways to meet people. Many newcomers find that joining community sports clubs, volunteering or attending cultural events speeds integration.

3. What are the realistic transport options if I donât want to drive?

Ride-hailing services and taxis are widely available. Compounds often provide shuttle services and you can hire drivers. The Riyadh Metro, once fully operational, will significantly improve public transit options inside the city.

4. How should families approach schooling applications?

Start early. Research curricula and visit campuses, if possible. Apply as soon as a move becomes likely and prepare for waiting lists. Consider temporary schooling solutions while waiting for a permanent placement and review school fee structures and transport options before committing.
