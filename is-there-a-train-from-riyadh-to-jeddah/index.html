Is There a Train From Riyadh to Jeddah?

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Introduction

Saudi Arabia is investing heavily in transport infrastructure as tourism and domestic travel surge. Between ambitious high-speed rail projects and the popular Haramain route along the Red Sea coast, travelers frequently ask whether they can take a train between the Kingdom’s two most prominent cities: Riyadh and Jeddah.

Short answer: No — as of now there is no direct passenger train service that connects Riyadh and Jeddah end-to-end. Rail travel in Saudi Arabia is modern and expanding, with world-class high-speed lines serving parts of the country, but a direct Riyadh–Jeddah passenger line is still in planning and development phases rather than regular operation.

This article explains exactly why a direct train does not yet exist, what rail services are available today, how long-term projects could change travel between Riyadh and Jeddah, and practical alternatives you can use right now. Throughout, you’ll find actionable planning frameworks, step-by-step travel options, cultural and logistical tips, and links to trusted resources to turn curiosity into a confident, well-planned trip across the Kingdom.

The Current Saudi Rail Landscape: Foundation for Understanding

A snapshot of major passenger services today

Saudi Arabia’s rail network is evolving quickly. The two passenger services travelers encounter most often are the high-speed Haramain line along the western corridor and a handful of conventional passenger routes that link major urban centers in the east and north. The Haramain high-speed rail is the flagship for intercity passenger comfort and speed on the Red Sea side; it has altered how pilgrims and leisure travelers move between Jeddah, Makkah, and Madinah.

The big-picture fact to keep in mind: the Kingdom’s network is not yet a fully integrated national passenger grid. Instead, it currently functions as a set of high-quality corridors focused on strategic corridors and ports of pilgrimage.

Who operates passenger rail and why it matters for Riyadh–Jeddah service

Passenger rail services in Saudi Arabia are delivered through government-backed entities and large infrastructure programs tied to Vision 2030. Operational emphasis has been on routes that serve religious travel corridors and existing high-traffic corridors. New projects and private-sector participation will determine when and how a direct Riyadh–Jeddah link becomes reality.

How Haramain changed travel on the west coast

The Haramain high-speed service demonstrates what modern rail can do for Saudi travel: speeds up to 300 km/h, frequent departures, and comfortable onboard services. It transformed movement along the western corridor, linking the Red Sea cities with fast, predictable travel times that many travelers now prefer over driving for convenience and safety. That model shows what a dedicated Riyadh–Jeddah line could accomplish — but it doesn’t yet answer the practical question travelers have today.

The Direct Answer: Why There Is No Regular Train From Riyadh to Jeddah Today

Technical and geographic challenges

Riyadh sits deep in the central plateau of the Arabian Peninsula, while Jeddah is on the Red Sea coast. Constructing a direct high-speed or conventional line across this geography requires major investment, route planning to serve intermediate population centers, and coordination with national logistics strategies. The Kingdom has prioritized certain corridors that deliver immediate economic returns and support the Hajj/Umrah pilgrim flows. While a Riyadh–Jeddah high-speed railway has been proposed and discussed publicly, project timelines and contracts have seen revisions and delays — common in projects of this scale.

Priorities and phased development under national plans

Saudi transport investment has focused on phased rollouts: port improvements, the Haramain corridor, and a stronger logistics backbone for freight. Decision-makers weigh whether building a direct passenger corridor is the highest priority versus expanding airports, highways, or freight rail. Until a binding contract is signed, land surveyed, and construction underway, travelers can expect to rely on airplanes, long-distance buses, and private driving for Riyadh–Jeddah travel.

Clarifying rumor versus reality

There are frequent headlines and announcements about planned high-speed links across the Kingdom. These projects are real in policy terms, but the practical traveler outcome is this: at the time you are reading this, regular, scheduled passenger trains that will take you directly from Riyadh to Jeddah do not operate. Plans can accelerate, and we track those changes for travelers. What Rail Travel Is Available Now — How It Helps (and How It Doesn’t)

Haramain: the high-speed corridor on the west coast

Haramain connects Jeddah and Makkah and extends to Madinah, serving pilgrims and residents with fast, clean service. For anyone whose itinerary involves these holy cities or coastal stops near Jeddah, Haramain is the best rail product in the Kingdom today.

What Haramain does for the traveler: it offers a predictable, fast way to move between the Red Sea urban centers and pilgrimage sites. It can also make connecting journeys more comfortable if you structure your trip to take advantage of the coastal rail instead of attempting long road stretches.

If your trip is Riyadh-centric but you plan to visit the west coast or the holy cities, use Haramain strategically and integrate it into a multi-mode itinerary. Our advice on visiting Makkah and navigating rules for visitors is essential if you plan to include religious sites: key advice for travel around Makkah.

Conventional passenger lines and regional services

Outside the Haramain corridor, conventional passenger lines operate in different regions, mostly serving economic and freight needs. Passenger services have historically run between eastern cities and Riyadh, and while they are reliable for some regional movements, they don’t create a direct rail option for Riyadh–Jeddah.

Freight and non-regular special services

Sometimes rail infrastructure is used for special movements or freight operations that touch segments of the potential Riyadh–Jeddah route. Those are not available for public passenger travel; don’t plan to rely on freight trains or special charter movements as a substitute for scheduled passenger service.

Practical Alternatives Right Now: How To Travel Between Riyadh and Jeddah Efficiently

When a direct train is not available, the smart traveler evaluates alternatives across four criteria: time, cost, comfort, and cultural experience. Below is a concise comparison followed by detailed itineraries and planning steps.

  • Flight: fastest and often competitively priced; multiple daily flights; 1.5–2 hours flight time.
  • Drive: flexible, scenic in places, 8–10+ hours depending on stops and route; best for those who want to explore along the way.
  • Bus: long-distance coach services that are economical, comfortable, and safe — expect 10–12+ hours.
  • Multimodal: combine train segments (where available) with road or air to optimize cost or experience.

Flight: The default for most travelers

Flights between Riyadh and Jeddah are frequent, competitively priced, and the quickest way to travel. Major national and regional carriers operate multiple daily services. For many travelers — business visitors, families on holiday, and those transiting to international flights — flying is the practical choice. Airport transfers are convenient: Riyadh’s King Khalid International Airport and Jeddah’s King Abdulaziz International Airport both offer taxis, ride-hailing, and car rental with clear signage in English and Arabic.

Booking tip: look for mid-week flights for lower fares, and compare fare bundles that include baggage if you plan to travel with larger luggage.

Driving: For flexibility and discovery

Driving between Riyadh and Jeddah gives you control over stops and the chance to visit towns along the way. Typical driving time is around 9–10 hours non-stop, but sensible travelers plan at least one overnight stop to rest. Route options include passing through Taif and then descending into the Red Sea plains — if you choose this route, factor in elevation changes and brief detours for cultural and scenic stops.

Safety and comfort considerations: pick a modern, air-conditioned vehicle, check tire and coolant levels before long desert legs, and avoid night driving in unfamiliar stretches. Book hotels in advance in towns where you plan to stop, particularly during peak seasons and holidays.

If you’d like to build a multi-day road itinerary that mixes city stops and cultural experiences, our regional travel pages include tips for stops worth considering, including Taif, which is a traditional mountain retreat near the Red Sea foothills: planning a stop in Taif.

Bus: Cost-effective and comfortable long-distance travel

SAPTCO and other long-distance coach services operate routes between major Saudi cities. Coaches are typically modern, with air conditioning and reclining seats, and they follow safety standards and rest stops. Travel time by coach is longer than by car because of stops and regulated driving shifts, but buses are ideal for budget-conscious travelers who prefer a seat and predictable schedules.

Booking tip: reserve a seat online where available or buy tickets through official sales points. If you’re traveling during Eid, Hajj, or other national holidays, book well in advance.

Multimodal strategies: creative ways to use rail where it exists

If you prefer to experience Saudi rail even while moving between Riyadh and Jeddah, you can incorporate rail on portions of a longer journey. For example, if your trip includes Madinah or Makkah passages, use Haramain between Jeddah and Madinah or Jeddah and Makkah, and then continue to or from Riyadh by flight or road. This hybrid approach blends the comfort and novelty of rail with the speed of air travel or the flexibility of driving. Designing Your Trip: Detailed Itineraries and Planning Steps

Below are practical itineraries tailored to different traveler priorities: speed, budget, scenic interest, and cultural emphasis. Each itinerary includes recommended bookings, what to expect on the road or air, and cultural or regulatory notes.

Itinerary A — Fastest: Fly and maximize time in each city

Planning steps:

  1. Book early-morning flight to maximize your arrival day.
  2. Arrange airport transfer or pre-book car sharing; Jeddah’s airport has efficient options.
  3. If your trip includes religious sites, verify entry requirements and timing in advance.
  4. Reserve a centrally located hotel near the Corniche for quick access to major attractions.

Why this works: minimal travel time means more time to explore Jeddah’s historic Al-Balad, the Corniche, and the city’s evolving culinary scene.

Itinerary B — Budget: Coach or mixed transport with a stopover

Plan summary: overnight coach from Riyadh to Jeddah, 2–3 days in Jeddah, return by low-cost flight.

Planning steps:

  1. Purchase bus tickets in advance to secure preferred departure times and seats.
  2. Choose a daytime coach if you prefer scenery and more frequent stops; nighttime coaches can be efficient but consider sleep quality.
  3. Book a budget carrier for the return leg to reduce round-trip cost and save time.

Why this works: buses are pocket-friendly and reliable; pairing with a return flight creates a balanced budget/time trade-off.

Itinerary C — Scenic and cultural: Road trip with curated stops

Plan summary: 2–3 day drive with an overnight in Taif or a desert camp, arrive in Jeddah refreshed.

Planning steps:

  1. Map a route and select a comfortable driving pace with scheduled rest stops every 2–3 hours.
  2. Book accommodation in advance for your stopping point — Taif is a popular and convenient choice.
  3. Include cultural or natural stops such as mountain lookouts or local markets, and observe local customs when visiting smaller towns.

Why this works: driving lets you experience Saudi Arabia’s interior landscapes, local hospitality, and regional towns beyond the main highways.

Planning steps:

  1. Fly into Jeddah and spend a day exploring.
  2. Book Haramain tickets in advance for a comfortable rail ride to Makkah or Madinah.
  3. If your schedule permits, connect back to Riyadh via a short flight from Jeddah or Madinah.

Why this works: combines the best of air and rail, letting you experience formal Saudi rail travel while minimizing travel time between major hubs.

Booking, Tickets, and Practical Logistics: Step-by-Step

Step 1 — Decide your priority: speed, cost, experience

The simplest planning schema is to ask: Do I want the quickest route (flight), the most flexible route (car), the most economical route (bus), or a mix that includes rail for experience? Your answer determines the booking channel and timing.

Step 2 — Book tickets and reserve accommodations

  • Flights: compare airlines and fare bundles; use flight-alert tools to track price drops.
  • Buses: buy tickets through official carriers or authorized resellers; check departure terminals.
  • Cars: if self-driving, reserve a reliable rental with GPS and full insurance.
  • Trains: if using Haramain for a leg of your journey, reserve seats early during busy seasons.

Step-by-step booking tip: For multi-leg trips, align arrival and departure times so you aren’t forced into a rushed connection — allow buffer time for traffic, check-in, and prayer times.

Step 3 — Prepare documents and respect local rules

Carry your passport, residency or visa documentation as required, and keep vaccination or health documentation handy if relevant. Non-Muslim travelers should be aware of access restrictions around Makkah. Our guidance for visitors to holy cities outlines the behavior and access norms you should follow: respectful travel around Makkah.

Step 4 — Pack and plan for the climate and culture

Saudi climate varies with elevation and season. Riyadh can be extremely hot in summer while winter evenings are cooler; Jeddah’s coastal humidity requires lightweight breathable clothing. Respect local dress norms, particularly in conservative urban areas and religious sites. Pack a basic first-aid kit, chargers, adapter plugs, and a reusable water bottle.

Step 5 — On the day: checkpoints and arrival

Allow extra time for security checks at airports and bus stations. If driving, follow official highway rules, and use service stations for rest and fuel. On arrival in Jeddah, plan for local transport taking into account traffic and the Corniche’s spread-out attractions.

Cultural Etiquette and Practical Tips for Traveling Between Riyadh and Jeddah

Respectful conduct and dress

Saudi Arabia has modernized public norms, but respectful dress and behavior remain important. For men: collared shirts and long trousers are safe in all but the most relaxed beach areas. For women: modest, non-revealing clothing and a headscarf are advised in conservative areas, and many public places will expect modest attire. In cities like Jeddah you may find more varied dress, particularly in private resorts and international hotels.

Prayer times, public life rhythms, and schedules

Prayer times shape day-to-day rhythms. Expect shops and services to adjust their hours around prayer times and Friday congregational prayer. Plan your travel legs with prayer breaks in mind — arrangements at major stations and airports usually accommodate prayer facilities.

Navigating language and local help

Arabic is the official language, but English is widely used in airports, hotels, and by taxi/ride-hailing drivers. Travel apps and offline maps are valuable if you plan to drive or explore neighborhoods off the main tourist circuit. When in doubt, ask hotel staff or official information desks for the safest route and timing.

Looking Ahead: The Riyadh–Jeddah Railway Prospects

Planned high-speed corridors and timelines

A direct Riyadh–Jeddah high-speed railway has been proposed as part of national rail expansion plans to enhance connectivity between major economic centers. Such a line would significantly shrink travel time between the capital and the Red Sea coast and would be a major step in integrating the Kingdom’s passenger network. However, major projects move through planning, approvals, procurement, and construction phases; while the high-level commitment is clear, completion dates depend on contractual and funding milestones.

What a direct line would mean for travelers and tourism

A functioning Riyadh–Jeddah passenger line would create new possibilities: same-day business travel between the two hubs, new domestic tourism circuits combining heritage and coastal experiences, and a more balanced distribution of traffic between air and rail. It would also encourage multi-city rail passes and potentially lower carbon travel footprints for the Kingdom.

How to Choose If You Should Wait for a Train or Travel Now

Deciding whether to wait for a future rail service or travel today depends on three practical questions: timeline, flexibility, and purpose.

  • Timeline: Are you traveling within the next 6–12 months? If so, the train is unlikely to be operational. For trips beyond a few years, watch project timelines closely.
  • Flexibility: If you need precise arrival times and short travel windows (business meetings, connecting flights), flying is a more dependable choice.
  • Purpose: If your trip is about exploration and experiencing regional culture, driving or bus travel with planned stops can be part of the experience.

If your schedule is flexible and your goal is to experience new transport options, plan a trip that mixes existing rail like Haramain with air or road travel so you can enjoy both aspects now.

Safety, Costs, and Practical Money Advice

Safety considerations

Saudi highways are generally well-maintained, with fuel stations and rest services along major routes. Exercise caution during night driving, carry emergency contact numbers, and make sure your phone has local connectivity. Public transport — buses and trains — maintain safety standards, but always use official carriers and confirmed booking channels.

Budget expectations

  • Flights: budget carriers can be very competitive; watch for baggage fees.
  • Bus: least expensive with good value on longer legs.
  • Car rental: variable depending on vehicle size and insurance.
  • Trains (Haramain): prices are reasonable for the speed and convenience but expect to pay a premium during pilgrim seasons.

Currency and payments

Card payments are widely accepted in cities and commercial centers, but carry some cash for small purchases, local markets, and fuel stops. Notify your bank about travel plans to avoid card blocks.

Conclusion

There is no regular passenger train that takes you directly from Riyadh to Jeddah at this moment. In the meantime, travelers should use an evidence-based approach: fly for speed, take the bus for economy, drive for flexibility, and weave Haramain rail into itineraries that touch the western corridor.

FAQ

Is it possible to travel by train part of the way between Riyadh and Jeddah?

Yes — you can use rail services where they exist on portions of a longer itinerary, particularly the Haramain line on the west coast. However, there is no single continuous passenger rail service that connects Riyadh and Jeddah today, so you will need to combine modes (air or road) to complete the trip.

How long would a future Riyadh–Jeddah high-speed train take if built?

Estimated journey times for proposed high-speed lines are commonly projected to be comparable to flights when accounting for total travel time to and from stations (i.e., a few hours door-to-door), but specific journey times depend on route alignment and train speed and will be confirmed only once project contracts and technical plans are released.

Are there any restrictions I should know about when traveling between Riyadh and Jeddah?

Standard travel documentation (passport, visa/residency permit) is required. If your journey includes visits to Makkah, be aware that non-Muslim visitors cannot enter the holy city; ensure you understand access rules before planning. Also consider prayer times and local public holidays which can affect service schedules.
