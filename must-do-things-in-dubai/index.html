Must Do Things in Dubai

Updated September 12, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai is a place that excites every kind of traveler: striking modern architecture, lively waterfronts, traditional markets, desert silence, and dining that spans the globe. If you’re planning a visit and want to make the most of limited time, this post answers the single question you came here to solve: what to do first, how to prioritize, and how to build a trip that balances iconic highlights with meaningful cultural moments.

Short answer: Prioritize a mix of skyline experiences (Burj Khalifa, The View at The Palm), waterfront neighborhoods (Dubai Marina, Bluewaters Island), a day in Old Dubai (the souks, Al Fahidi, Al Seef), and at least one desert experience — but organize those highlights into a realistic itinerary and cover practical steps like visas, transport, and cultural etiquette before you leave. Use that structure to move from sightseeing to authentic experiences, and apply the same planning templates we use at Saudi Travel & Leisure to ensure your trip is efficient, culturally respectful, and unforgettable. For essential entry requirements and visitor rules, review this concise set of essential travel guidelines for the Kingdom as a model for how to prepare documentation and expectations when traveling in the Gulf.

In the sections below I’ll give you the full picture: what to see and why it matters, how to plan each part of your trip, where to eat and sleep, sensible budgets, logistics and safety, plus sample itineraries you can adapt depending on whether you have 48 hours or a week. The main message is simple: Dubai’s biggest sights are spectacular, but the most memorable visits combine those icons with local rhythms and thoughtful planning.

Why Dubai Deserves a Strategic Visit

The City’s Dual Appeal

Dubai’s allure comes from two complementary qualities: deliberate spectacle and accessible experiences. The skyline is deliberately showy — tallest towers, record-setting attractions — but the city also offers low-effort pleasures: promenades, public beaches, and cafés where you can watch the city go by. That means planning matters: without a clear framework you’ll either waste time in queues or miss the quieter cultural corners that give context to the glitz.

What “Must Do” Actually Means Here

“Must do” is not only about ticking off photo ops. It’s about assembling a trip where each major sight connects to the next with minimal friction. See the Burj Khalifa to understand Dubai’s modern ambitions, then visit the souks to feel the city’s commercial roots. Finish with a desert experience to ground the narrative in landscape and tradition. That three-act structure — skyline, old town, desert — is the organizing principle I use when helping travelers craft a high-value short trip.

How to Plan Before You Go

Timing: Best Months and When to Book

Dubai’s peak season runs from November through March when temperatures are pleasant. Expect higher prices and larger crowds in December and early January. If you travel between April and September you’ll save on accommodation and may secure last-minute bookings, but be prepared for very hot daytime conditions.

Booking windows matter more than you think for marquee attractions. For the Burj Khalifa “At The Top” or Ain Dubai (the observation wheel on Bluewaters), secure tickets at least a week in advance during high season and several weeks for sunset times. Hotels at desirable areas like Downtown Dubai, Dubai Marina, or Palm Jumeirah should be reserved as early as possible during festivals and holidays.

Essential Steps Before Departure

  1. Confirm visa and entry requirements. Many nationalities receive visa on arrival; others require e-visas. Use your airline and official government sources to verify.
  2. Arrange mobile connectivity (eSIMs are widely used), travel insurance, and currency exchange. Plan a small local data budget — navigation and ride apps are essential.
  3. Reserve timed-entry attractions for priority slots (Burj Khalifa, The View at The Palm, aquarium experiences).
  4. Book at least one guided cultural or food experience in Old Dubai to gain access and context that self-guided walks often miss.
  5. Review local customs and dress expectations to avoid uncomfortable situations in certain neighborhoods and religious sites.
  6. Build transport time into your schedule: Dubai is spread out and travel can take longer than map distances suggest.

Those six steps create a reliable blueprint I recommend to anyone traveling in the Gulf — and if you want consolidated checklists for logistics, see the collection of practical travel advice, logistics, and planning I use when advising readers.

Quick Budget Framework

Dubai can be done on a wide range of budgets if you plan deliberately. Expect mid-range travelers to budget per-day amounts for accommodation, food, transport, and a couple of paid attractions. Tickets for major attractions vary; allocate more for special experiences (premium Burj Khalifa access, private yacht charters, exclusive desert camps). If you want to reduce costs, use public beaches and free shows (Dubai Fountain), pick shareable dining options in food halls, and prioritize one paid highlight per day.

Getting Around: Transport Options and Tricks

Public Transport vs Taxis vs Driving

The Dubai Metro is efficient for Downtown, Airport, and some major corridors; it’s air-conditioned and affordable. Taxis and ride-hailing apps are ubiquitous and reasonably priced for short distances — a practical choice if you’re traveling with luggage or at odd hours. Renting a car gives flexibility for day trips (Abu Dhabi, Hatta), but be prepared for busy parking and unfamiliar rules if you’re new to Gulf driving.

What matters: plan journeys by time, not distance. Cross-city rides can take longer than you think when traffic is heavy. Build buffer time for evening plans around major attractions.

Navigation Apps and Payment Methods

Use local ride apps or international ride-hailing services. Cash is still accepted, but most places prefer card or contactless payment. For small purchases and taxis, having some AED on hand is useful.

Where To Stay: Choosing Your Base

Neighborhoods With Different Vibes

Downtown Dubai: Central to Burj Khalifa and Dubai Mall, ideal for first-time visitors who want walkable access to major icons. Great for evening fountain shows and nearby dining.

Dubai Marina & JBR: Waterfront lifestyle, many cafés and restaurants, evening promenade energy. The Marina is practical for beach access and boat cruises.

Palm Jumeirah: Exclusive resorts and private beaches. Choose this if you want a resort-style stay with easy access to Atlantis and sea views.

Al Fahidi / Al Seef / Deira: For Old Dubai access, these neighborhoods put you close to souks and cultural attractions. Accommodation options vary from heritage guesthouses to modern hotels.

For recommendations on hotels and neighborhood breakdowns, explore this hub for hotels, resorts, and accommodation guides to match your priorities with the right area.

Practical Accommodation Tips

  • If your trip’s about sightseeing and nightlife, staying in Downtown or Marina minimizes travel time.
  • For a relaxed beach-focused break, choose Palm Jumeirah or Jumeirah Beach Residence (JBR).
  • If your priority is cultural immersion and local markets, pick an accommodation in Old Dubai; you can still reach Downtown within 20–30 minutes by taxi.

The Must-Do Experiences — What to See and Why

Below are the core experiences I consider essential. For each, I explain what makes it worthwhile and practical tips to maximize your visit.

Burj Khalifa — See Dubai from the Highest Angles

Why go: The Burj Khalifa is the architectural symbol of modern Dubai. The observation decks give a perspective on the city’s sprawl, desert edge, and coastline that photographs can’t fully capture.

Tips for visiting: Book timed tickets and aim for sunset if you want daytime-to-night transition views. If you can, upgrade to higher-level access for less crowded viewing and lounge amenities. Expect security screening and lines; arrive 30 minutes early.

Dubai Mall & The Dubai Fountain — A City Stage

Why go: Dubai Mall is more than shopping — it’s a public stage with aquarium views, indoor waterfall art, and direct access to the fountain performance. The Dubai Fountain, timed to music, is free and can be viewed from multiple vantage points.

How to experience: Watch the fountain from the promenade or reserve a table at a restaurant with a terrace view. Combine this with an evening at the Burj Khalifa for theatrical urban transitions.

Dubai Marina & Ain Dubai — Waterfront Glamour

Why go: Marina’s skyline lights up at night and the Ain Dubai (observation wheel) offers sweeping panoramic views of the marina, Palm Jumeirah, and the Gulf.

Practical note: Book Ain Dubai tickets in advance and choose a ride time close to sunset for the best light. For the marina itself, a dinner dhow cruise or an evening walk on the Marina Walk is one of the most pleasant ways to spend an evening.

Palm Jumeirah & The View at The Palm — A Different High Point

Why go: Palm Jumeirah is Dubai’s human-made island icon. The View at The Palm is an alternative vantage to the Burj that frames the Palm’s geometry and the crescent hotels.

Tip: If you want fewer crowds and unobstructed photos, the Palm Tower observation deck offers a different composition compared to Burj Khalifa’s vertical sweep.

Old Dubai: Souks, Al Fahidi, and Al Seef — Commerce and Memory

Why go: Old Dubai is where you feel continuity between past and present. The Gold Souk, Spice Souk, and Textile Souk are places to observe trade rituals. Al Fahidi Heritage Quarter and neighboring Al Seef sit along Dubai Creek and show historic trading life, wind towers, and courtyard architecture.

How to experience respectfully: Take a guided walking tour or a curated food walk that introduces spice merchants and textile sellers. This context makes bargaining and browsing more rewarding.

For organized cultural explorations and food-focused walks that add layers to your visit, look for guided options that focus on heritage zones; these often include access and introductions not available to casual visitors.

Desert Experience — Sustainability and Authentic Encounters

Why go: The desert is the other “must” — a landscape that contrasts Dubai’s urban spectacle. Choose your desert experience intentionally: dune-bashing may be exciting, but sustainable and conservation-minded operators offer wildlife-aware drives, vintage vehicle tours, and authentic cultural interaction in protected reserves.

How to choose: Favor operators who run reserves ethically and emphasize cultural exchange over entertainment spectacles. An overnight stay in a well-managed desert camp or a luxury glamping site gives a different depth than a single evening safari.

Hot Air Balloon and Sunrise Views — A Quiet High

Why go: Sunrise hot air balloon rides over the desert offer a silent, panoramic perspective and an opportunity to see native flora and fauna in daylight.

Booking note: These tours run early and often sell out. Book in advance and expect a full early-morning schedule that includes transfers and a post-flight breakfast.

Themed Attractions: Ski Dubai, Atlantis, Miracle Garden

Why go: These are “experience” attractions that showcase Dubai’s ability to create controlled environments: skiing in a mall, waterparks on artificial islands, or vast seasonal floral displays.

When to pick them: Choose one or two based on who you’re traveling with (families often prioritize Atlantis and Aquaventure) and whether you value a novelty experience vs. cultural exploration.

Dhow Cruise — A Gentle Evening

Why go: A dhow dinner cruise on Dubai Creek or Dubai Marina is an elegant way to combine food, views, and calm water reflections. It’s a slower-paced counterpoint to the high-energy attractions.

Markets, Food Halls, and Food Tours — Taste the City

Why go: Dubai’s cuisine spans Arab, South Asian, Persian, Levantine, and global influences. Food walking tours through Old Dubai and the creek neighborhoods introduce you to street specialties and the stories behind them.

If you enjoy structured culinary discovery, a curated food walk will let you sample authentic plates and learn how different communities shaped Dubai’s food scene. For deeper exploration, check curated recommendations and local dining roundups in the hub for restaurants, cafés, and culinary guides.

Food and Drink: Where To Eat and What To Try

Flavors To Seek Out

Dubai’s dining palette reflects its migrant population and trading history. Seek out Emirati staples like mandi and local rice-spiced dishes, Gulf seafood, Persian and Levantine mezze, and South Asian kebabs. Don’t miss street-level Arabic sweets and the array of coffee styles — from Arabic coffee (gahwa) to specialty café culture.

How to Eat Respectfully

Modest dress in certain traditional neighborhoods and during visits to religious sites is recommended. If you’re invited into private homes or local gatherings, modesty and punctuality are appreciated, and it’s polite to accept at least a small amount of offered food.

Food Experiences to Prioritize

  • A guided souk food walk to sample flatbreads, sweets, and spice mixes.
  • A waterfront seafood dinner for Gulf specialties.
  • A modern Emirati tasting menu at one of the city’s thoughtfully curated restaurants to gain context about national dishes and their modern reinvention.

Culture, Etiquette and Practical Behavior

Respectful Dress and Behavior

Dubai is cosmopolitan and tolerant, but public modesty is still the baseline. In public malls and government buildings, dress that covers shoulders and knees is standard. Swimwear is acceptable on beaches and pool areas; avoid it in public walkways away from the beach. Public displays of affection should be discreet.

For more in-depth notes about customs and expectations relevant to regional travel, consult curated articles on local customs and etiquette which provide practical, respectful guidelines that travelers appreciate.

Religious Sites and Visits

Non-Muslim visitors can sometimes visit notable mosques if the site allows tourism; always check dress requirements and visiting hours in advance. Be quiet, follow signage, and accept guidance from staff.

Safety, Health and Legal Basics

General Safety

Dubai is considered a low-crime city; normal travel cautions apply: safeguard valuables, be cautious with luggage in crowded places, and use registered taxis or ride apps. Drinking laws are strict: alcohol is permitted in licensed venues but public intoxication and drinking outside licensed premises are illegal.

For full clarity on entry, restrictions, and legal basics — and as a model for preparing travel documents when visiting Gulf countries — consult the official travel guidelines which I recommend reading before any regional trip.

Health and Medical Care

Medical care in Dubai is high-quality and modern; travel insurance that covers medical evacuation is recommended. Pharmacies are widespread and many treatments are available over the counter; however, certain medications that are common elsewhere may be restricted. Verify prescriptions before you travel.

Responsible Travel and Local Impact

Choose Operators Mindfully

For desert tours, wildlife experiences, and coastal activities, verify that operators follow conservation-minded practices. This reduces environmental impact and supports sustainable livelihoods.

Cultural Exchange Over Consumption

Balance flashy experiences with smaller interactions — a conversation with a market vendor, a visit to a local arts gallery, or a meal in a neighborhood restaurant will deepen your understanding beyond postcard views.

Sample Itineraries — Buildable and Realistic

Below are narrative-style itineraries you can adapt to different trip lengths. These are prose-first templates — use them as frameworks rather than strict schedules.

48-Hour Snapshot

Day One: Start at Downtown Dubai — visit Burj Khalifa mid-morning, then explore Dubai Mall and the aquarium. Break mid-afternoon at your hotel. Return at sunset for the Dubai Fountain and a terrace dinner with a view. End the night with a walk along the waterfront near the Dubai Opera.

Day Two: Morning: take a guided Old Dubai walk to the souks and Al Fahidi. Lunch at a traditional restaurant by the creek. Afternoon: transfer to Dubai Marina for a stroll and optional short boat cruise. Evening: finish with a sunset desert experience if you can spare the 4–6 hours.

If you’re limited to a 48-hour stay, pick either Marina/Bluewaters or Palm Jumeirah as your late-afternoon plan rather than trying to do both.

4-Day Balanced Visit

Day One: Downtown hubs and Burj Khalifa + Dubai Fountain.

Day Two: Old Dubai (Al Fahidi, souks), cultural afternoon, and a food walking tour.

Day Three: Morning at The View at The Palm and Atlantis; afternoon relaxing on Jumeirah Beach; evening Marina dhow cruise.

Day Four: Full day desert option — choose a conservation-friendly operator for dune time, cultural camp experience, or overnight glamping.

One-Week Deep Experience

Use the first few days to cover marquee attractions, then slow down: include a hot air balloon sunrise, a dedicated museum or gallery visit, time for a day trip to Abu Dhabi if you wish, and two full evenings at neighborhoods that serve different energies (JBR for beach life, Global Village or Miracle Garden seasonally).

Money, Tickets and Where to Save

Smart Booking Order

  1. Lock in flights and hotels.
  2. Book timed attractions (Burj Khalifa, Ain Dubai, popular restaurants).
  3. Reserve desert experiences and special tours (balloon rides, glamping) early.
  4. Leave flexible time for spontaneous activities.

Ways to Save

  • Use public beaches and free fountain shows.
  • Book attraction tickets for off-peak times.
  • Choose combined attraction passes when they match your interests.
  • Dine in neighborhood cafés rather than hotel restaurants for daily meals.

One Practical Planning Checklist

  1. Verify passport validity and visa needs; prepare printed and digital copies of confirmations.
  2. Book marquee attractions and your desert experience in advance.
  3. Arrange local connectivity and confirm airport transfer plans.
  4. Pack for varied conditions: light clothing for daytime, a light layer for cooler nights, modest pieces for cultural areas, and swimwear for beaches.
  5. Purchase travel insurance with medical coverage.
  6. Note emergency numbers and the address of your embassy.

(That checklist is intended to be a short, high-impact list to help you finish planning efficiently.)

Common Planning Mistakes and How To Avoid Them

  • Underestimating travel time: schedule buffer time for transfers and evening plans.
  • Over-packing attractions into a single day: pick one major paid attraction per day and pair it with low-effort activities.
  • Ignoring cultural norms: read local etiquette notes before you arrive to prevent uncomfortable interactions.
  • Booking last-minute for popular attractions during peak season: tickets and prime slots sell out.

Day Trips and Nearby Extensions

Abu Dhabi in a Day

Abu Dhabi is a 90–120 minute drive from Dubai, depending on traffic. Key reasons to visit include the Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque, Louvre Abu Dhabi, and cultural sites like Qasr Al Watan. If you’re interested in a day trip, prioritize one or two major sites and consider a guided transfer to optimize time.

Hatta and the Mountains

Hatta offers a contrasting landscape — mountains, freshwater kayaking, and heritage villages. It’s a 90-minute drive from Dubai and makes for a refreshed day outside of the city.

Responsible Souvenir Shopping and Bargaining

When buying in souks, expect to haggle and enjoy the rhythm of bargaining. For ethically produced crafts or specialty items, ask about provenance. When purchasing gold or jewelry, verify weights and certificates.

Final Practical Reminders

  • Carry a small water bottle, sunscreen, and sun protection — the Middle East sun is powerful.
  • Keep a photocopy of passport and tickets accessible online and offline.
  • If you plan to drink alcohol, confirm the venue license and your hotel’s serving policies.

Conclusion

Dubai rewards planning as much as curiosity. The right preparation — clear priorities, timed attraction bookings, and cultural awareness — converts a trip from a sequence of photo ops into a layered experience that blends skyline spectacle with local life and desert landscapes. Use the framework above to choose one headline attraction each day, add context through a cultural or culinary activity, and secure logistics early.

FAQ

1) How many days do I need in Dubai to cover the “must do” experiences?

With careful planning, you can cover the headline experiences in 3–4 days: Burj Khalifa and Dubai Mall; Old Dubai and souks; a marina or Palm Jumeirah afternoon; and one desert experience. A week lets you add deeper cultural visits, a day trip, or slower-paced beach time.

2) Is Dubai family-friendly?

Yes. Many attractions are family-oriented: Atlantis Aquaventure, Dubai Aquarium, Miracle Garden (seasonal), and safe public beaches. Choose accommodations with family amenities and plan quieter afternoons to avoid midday heat.

3) What’s the best way to see Old Dubai without getting overwhelmed?

Book a guided walking tour or a curated food walk. A local guide provides context, teaches bargaining cues, and introduces specialized vendors you wouldn’t find on your own.

4) Are tipping and bargaining expected?

Tipping is appreciated in restaurants and for guides; a small 10% tip in casual settings is common. Bargaining is part of the souk experience but is not used in fixed-price retail shops and malls.

Travel thoughtfully, plan deliberately, and let each major sight connect to a quieter moment of cultural discovery — that’s how you turn Dubai’s must-dos into a memorable trip.
