Must Visit Places in Saudi Arabia

Updated September 29, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Introduction

Tourism in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia has transformed in recent years: international arrivals, new conservation projects, and high-profile cultural restorations have opened vast landscapes and historic sites that were off-limits to most visitors only a decade ago. For the modern traveler who wants dramatic scenery, layered history, and confident logistics, Saudi Arabia now offers an extraordinary range of experiences — from monumental Nabataean tombs and sculpted sandstone valleys to coral-fringed atolls and lush mountain villages.

Short answer: The must visit places in Saudi Arabia span several distinct regions — AlUla and Hegra in the northwest, Jeddah’s Al Balad and the Red Sea coast, the sweeping vistas of the Edge of the World outside Riyadh, the verdant terraces of Asir in the southwest, and the palm-filled oases of Al Ahsa in the east. Visiting these places requires combining cultural awareness with careful planning, and relying on up-to-date, official visitor information before you go: consult the essential travel guidelines for the Kingdom to start.

This article is written as the practical blueprint from Saudi Travel & Leisure: a layered, actionable companion for assembling an itinerary that balances big-ticket highlights and lesser-known experiences. You will find regional overviews, on-the-ground access guidance for the top sites, travel logistics, cultural etiquette, and planning frameworks that turn curiosity into a confident, enriching trip.

Why These Places Matter

Saudi Arabia’s geography and history are both immense and intimate. The must-visit places chosen here are not a random list: they represent the best cross-section for travelers who want to connect with heritage, nature, and contemporary Saudi life. Each location tells a part of the Kingdom’s story — geological epochs carved into rock faces, trading empires frozen in stone, coastal communities that have long practiced the art of the sea, and mountain settlements where agricultural terraces still shape daily rhythms. Knowing what each site offers and how to get there transforms a checklist into a meaningful journey.

Regional Overview: The Geography of Must-Visit Places

Saudi Arabia’s scale requires thinking regionally. A trip centered on the northwest looks and feels different from one focused on the southwest mountains or the Red Sea. Below I map the essential regions and why they matter to a traveler’s itinerary.

The Northwest: AlUla, Hegra, and Wadi Landscapes

The AlUla region is the country’s most prominent archaeological destination. Hegra (often referenced by its ancient name) hosts monumental Nabataean rock-cut tombs, comparable in significance to Petra but with far fewer crowds. Surrounding AlUla are sculptural sandstone formations, mirrored architectural statements like Maraya, and verdant wadis such as Wadi Al Disah that contrast sharply with the ochre desert. Access is increasingly regulated to protect fragile heritage and landscapes, so plan visits with official operators and reserve timed entries where required.

The Red Sea Coast: Jeddah, Umluj, and Coral Atolls

The Red Sea coastline is a separate world: Jeddah’s historic harbor, Al Balad, remains a vibrant urban anchor for commerce and culture, while the offshore atolls near Umluj and the Farasan Islands offer unspoiled snorkeling and diving. Marine biodiversity here rivals the best of the Red Sea, with coral gardens, hawksbill turtles, and rich fish life. Unlike popular Red Sea resorts in other countries, many of Saudi’s atolls remain low-density and conservation-focused.

Central Saudi Arabia: Riyadh, Diriyah, and the Edge of the World

Central Saudi Arabia blends metropolitan development with striking geology. Riyadh is a modern capital with museums and restored historic quarters such as Diriyah’s At-Turaif. The nearby Edge of the World (Jebel Fihrayn) provides the dramatic cliffline panorama that has become a must for travelers seeking open-sky desert vistas and memorable photography. Getting to the Edge of the World typically requires a 4x4 and local guidance.

Eastern Province and Oasis Landscapes: Al Ahsa and Al Qarah

The eastern province hosts ancient oases and palm groves. Al Ahsa’s name roughly translates to “murmuring streams,” and its clusters of wells, date farms, and the limestone cliffs of Al Qarah are culturally and ecologically significant. These oases have sustained settled life for millennia and offer a contrasting experience to the open desert.

Southwestern Highlands: Asir, Abha, and Rijal Almaa

The southwest’s Asir highlands are green, terrace-farmed, and cooler than the rest of the country. Traditional villages such as Rijal Almaa feature striking tower-like houses and living folk traditions. The mountain roads lead to panoramic viewpoints, coffeehouses that feel centuries-old, and a different climatic rhythm — rains and fog are more common here, and landscapes support diverse agriculture.

The Empty Quarter and Volcanic Fields

Rub' al Khali — the Empty Quarter — is an elemental landscape of towering dunes. Nearby volcanic fields (harrats) and desert craters such as Al Wabah punctuate the western interior. These places are for travelers who plan long-distance overland trips or join specialized 4x4 expeditions and guided camping for safety and navigation.

Must-Visit Places, Region by Region

Below I unpack the top places to include in an itinerary, why each matters, what to expect, and how to prepare. Each section focuses on access, timing, and on-the-ground tips so you can turn knowledge into practical decisions.

AlUla and Hegra

AlUla is a must because it concentrates archaeology, dramatic geology, and new hospitality in one accessible corridor. Hegra’s Nabataean tombs are the headline: rock-cut façades with carved inscriptions and funerary architecture that speak to regional trade and cultural networks active two millennia ago. Because the site is protected and visitor capacity is managed, book a guided slot through the official operators and arrive with sun protection and a water bottle. Explore the surrounding deserts by day and linger for sunset when the warm sandstone takes on intense color. Nearby, Maraya — the mirrored concert hall — offers a contemporary contrast to the ancient landscape and is accessible as part of AlUla’s cultural circuit; note that access rules are strict for sites on private conservation land.

What to expect: well-marked visitor routes at Hegra, guided walks, and interpretive signage. Photography is excellent early morning or late afternoon when shadows bring out the carvings’ relief.

How to prepare: reserve a guided time slot in advance; if staying in a local camp or resort, coordinate transfers that meet site regulations.

Wadi Al Disah

Wadi Al Disah is a sandstone valley where towering columns and palm-flanked riparian zones create a cinematic landscape. This wadi is best explored on foot and by 4x4 along designated tracks. The light here is exceptional at sunrise and sunset, and birdlife can be surprising in the wetter months. Because the wadi is remote, bring emergency supplies and let your accommodation or guide confirm the best routes and permissions.

What to expect: dramatic rock monoliths, shady palm corridors, and quiet expanses ideal for contemplative walking.

Jeddah’s Al Balad and the Red Sea Corniche

Jeddah’s Al Balad is the city’s heart: a UNESCO-designated district of coral-stone merchant houses, narrow lanes, spice stalls, and restored houses turned cultural venues. Walkable and atmospheric, it’s a place to practice conversational Arabic, taste street foods, and explore museums such as the restored Nasseef House. The Jeddah Corniche contrasts with historic alleys — long promenades, public art, and the famous King Fahd Fountain that punctuates evenings along the sea.

What to expect: vibrant urban life, open souks, creaky wooden doors with ornate carvings, and an active waterfront culture. Dress respectfully, ask permission before photographing people, and sample local seafood dishes that reflect Red Sea traditions.

Umluj and the Red Sea Atolls

For a marine-focused experience, Umluj — often called the Kingdom’s Maldives — offers turquoise shallows, white sandbars, and coral reefs. Island-hopping trips reveal snorkeling sites, dugong sightings in rare pockets, and remote sandbanks where picnic stops feel private. Use licensed dive operators and respect marine-protected areas. Sustainable resorts are emerging, but much of the area remains low-density.

What to expect: clear water, thriving reef species, and relaxed boat trips. Book boat trips locally and check weather conditions for sea travel.

Edge of the World (Jebel Fihrayn)

The Edge of the World is a geological ridge with a shear drop that opens onto endless desert. It is best reached with a reputable guide and a 4x4. Early morning or late afternoon visits are recommended to avoid midday heat, and sunrise or sunset provide incredible photographic conditions. The route is largely unmarked; guides handle navigation and safety.

What to expect: wide-open sky, dramatic cliff edges, and the quiet of vast desert plains. Bring strong footwear, sun protection, and a small first-aid kit.

Diriyah and At-Turaif

Diriyah is Saudi heritage brought back to life: the restored At-Turaif district with mud-brick architecture tells the story of the first Sauds, and the complex’s museums and restored palaces offer interpretive narratives about the nation’s early modern history. Diriyah sits in the green ribbon of Wadi Hanifa and is a short drive from central Riyadh.

What to expect: museum exhibits, traditional Najdi architecture, thoughtful restoration projects, and cafés that introduce Najdi cuisine.

Al Qarah and Al Ahsa Oasis

Al Qarah’s limestone mountain and honeycomb caves sit on the edge of Al Ahsa’s vast palm groves. Al Ahsa itself — a UNESCO reservoir of agricultural tradition — is a working oasis with date farms, irrigation systems, and village life. The combination of arid cliffs and irrigated palms is visually and culturally compelling. Markets in the nearby towns offer date varieties and local crafts.

What to expect: cool caves, shaded groves, and the chance to learn about traditional irrigation and date cultivation.

Rijal Almaa and the Asir Highlands

Rijal Almaa’s stacked stone houses with colorful shutters are emblematic of Asir architecture. This village preserves craft traditions and festival life. The nearby highland roads curve through terraced agriculture and offer viewpoints where clouds sweep valleys in the rainy season. Asir’s climate is cooler and greener; pack layers and expect occasional rainfall even in months that are dry elsewhere in the Kingdom.

What to expect: decorated wooden doors, floral headdresses at cultural events, and mountain hospitality marked by strong local identity.

Jabal Sawdah and Mountain Peaks

For hikers and cooler-weather seekers, Jabal Sawdah offers the Kingdom’s highest accessible peaks and panoramic ridge walks. Trails vary in difficulty; hire local guides if you’re unfamiliar with high-altitude desert trekking. The region’s biodiversity includes unique flora and migratory bird routes.

What to expect: steep slopes, cooler climate, and dramatic morning fogs that lift to reveal extensive vistas.

Al Wabah Crater

A near-perfect circular depression on the lava plains, Al Wabah is a volcanic maar with a steep rim that draws curiosity. Access points vary, and local authorities sometimes limit descent due to safety concerns (gases can accumulate in crater bottoms). Visiting the rim provides dramatic views and a unique geological perspective.

What to expect: a stark volcanic landscape, excellent rim views, and the need to exercise caution around edges and steep slopes.

Practical Planning: Timing, Transport, and Visas

Best Time to Visit

Seasons matter dramatically in Saudi Arabia. Coastal Red Sea destinations and higher latitudes have different comfortable windows.

  • Winter (November–March) is broadly the most comfortable for national travel: mild to warm days, cool nights in the desert, and excellent diving conditions on the Red Sea.
  • Spring (March–May) can be comfortable in many regions but may feature wind in exposed desert areas.
  • Summer (June–September) can be extremely hot in the lowlands and unsuitable for extensive desert hiking; the highlands in Asir are the best refuge during summer months.
  • Transitional months mean variable weather, and the southwest receives more rain.

Plan regionally: AlUla and desert routes are best in winter and early spring; the Asir highlands are attractive in summer; Red Sea diving is accessible year-round but water visibility and comfort improve with seasonal winds.

Visas and Entry

Most travelers require an appropriate visa. The Saudi e-visa and tourist visa routes have simplified entry for many nationalities, but rules evolve. Before booking flights, consult the Kingdom’s official entry and safety guidelines to confirm recent visa policy changes, health requirements, and permitted activities.

Getting Around

Saudi Arabia is road-oriented. For many itineraries, renting a car is the most efficient way to link dispersed sites. City travel offers ride-hailing and taxis; intercity flights connect major hubs quickly for longer distances.

Considerations:

  • Long drives require planning for fuel, rest stops, and emergency contingencies.
  • For desert and remote sites, use a 4x4 with experienced drivers or join guided tours.
  • Domestic flight networks link Riyadh, Jeddah, AlUla (seasonal), Abha, and others — they save time on cross-country itineraries.

Accommodation

Accommodation choices span luxury desert resorts, boutique heritage hotels, family-run guesthouses, and city-brand hotels. For curated selections and booking strategies, consult our hub for accommodation guides and hotel recommendations. Consider location trade-offs: staying inside AlUla’s protected zones may require reservations that grant site access; city hotels offer convenience for museums and dining.

Health, Safety, and Insurance

Healthcare standards in major cities are high; remote travel requires planning. Travel insurance that covers medical evacuation and off-road activities is recommended. For safety guidance on permitted behavior, seasonal hazards, and entry requirements, refer to the official entry and safety guidelines. Keep medicines in original packaging and carry prescriptions.

Money, SIM, and Connectivity

Credit cards are widely accepted in cities; cash is useful for markets and remote areas. SIM cards from national providers are affordable and offer reliable coverage across most populated regions; remote wadis or dune systems may lack consistent signal.

Cultural Etiquette: How to Travel Respectfully

Saudi hospitality is warm, but courtesy matters. Dress conservatively in public spaces: women visitors are advised to wear modest clothing; local norms vary on the strictness of coverings, but loose layers and a long top or abaya-style robe in cities are sensible. Men should avoid shorts in formal settings. Public displays of affection are inappropriate, and alcohol is not permitted. Always ask permission before photographing people, especially in rural communities. Engage with locals respectfully, accept hospitality graciously, and learn basic Arabic greetings — the effort is always appreciated.

Activities and Experiences: Not Just Sights

Beyond visiting monuments and landscapes, the best travel plans include hands-on experiences. These might be guided archaeological walks at Hegra; a Red Sea snorkeling course in Umluj; a date-farming visit in Al Ahsa; a traditional coffee and saffron tasting in AlUla’s old town; or a sunset camp at the Edge of the World. Food and Drink: What to Eat and Where

Saudi cuisine varies by region. Najdi dishes in central areas emphasize wheat and yogurt-based plates; coastal regions serve grilled fish and seafood stews; Asir offers mountain fare with distinctive spice blends. Try traditional coffee (gahwa) with dates in markets, sample mutabbaq or shawarma from street vendors in urban centers, and seek out family-run restaurants for authentic, region-specific preparations.

Responsible Travel and Conservation

Visit with a light footprint. AlUla and other fragile sites restrict access to protect archaeology and biodiversity; obey signage, stay on trails, and use licensed guides. On the Red Sea, don’t touch coral and practice reef-safe sunscreen. Support community-based initiatives by choosing locally operated tours and dining at community restaurants when feasible. Your choices directly affect conservation outcomes in these sensitive regions.

Sample Itineraries and Blueprints

Below are planning frameworks that translate highlights into time-bound itineraries. Each is written to be adaptable: you can scale up or down based on travel time, season, and interests.

Short Break — 5 Days (Focus: AlUla + Red Sea City)

Arrive into the nearest international airport, spend two full days in AlUla exploring Hegra, Maraya, and surrounding wadis with guided tours, and use the remaining days in Jeddah for Al Balad, the Corniche, and a half-day Red Sea boat trip from the marina. Book AlUla visits in advance; combine city stays with early morning transfers to avoid midday heat.

Classic Circuit — 10 Days (Northwest, Red Sea, Riyadh)

Begin with AlUla and the north — Hegra and Wadi Al Disah — then fly or drive to Umluj for two days of marine activity. Return to Jeddah for Al Balad’s cultural quarter, then take a domestic flight to Riyadh for Diriyah and an organized trip to the Edge of the World. This route balances archaeology, coastal marine life, and the capital’s modern and historical offerings.

Extended Exploration — 14+ Days (Cross-Country Adventure)

A two-week trip permits a coast-to-mountains route: Jeddah and Red Sea exploration; drive or fly to AlUla; cross central deserts to Riyadh and Diriyah; head southeast to Al Ahsa and Al Qarah; then descend to Abha and the Asir highlands for Rijal Almaa and Jabal Sawdah. This plan requires domestic flights and staged drives; include buffer days for weather or permit changes. For extended itineraries, engage a regional logistics provider and check the official entry and safety guidelines to confirm travel permissions along the route.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Many travelers underestimate distances, seasonal extremes, and permit requirements. A few key pitfalls to avoid: attempting remote desert travel without a competent 4x4 and emergency plan; assuming heritage sites are accessible without prior booking; neglecting cultural norms in rural settings; and overlooking the need for medical and travel insurance. Always confirm operational details with official guidance and local providers, and prioritize reputable local operators for excursions into protected or remote areas.

For operational checklists and packing recommendations, consult our page of practical travel advice and logistics.

Photography, Drone Use, and Permissions

Photography is welcomed at many public sites, but rules vary. Drone use is strictly regulated — obtain necessary permits well in advance; some heritage and protected zones prohibit drones entirely. Respect private properties and avoid photographing military or sensitive installations. For the most photogenic light, aim for golden hours; for heritage shots, use close-ups that respect signage and barriers designed to protect ancient surfaces.

Local Transport and Guided Tours

Local guided tours are often the safest and most enriching way to visit remote sites. Drivers and guides know access rules, vehicle requirements, and cultural protocols. Use licensed tour operators for desert treks, archaeological site visits, and sea excursions. For city-based needs, ride-hailing apps operate reliably in major centers; for rural or mountainous travel, hire proven local drivers with 4x4 capability and demonstrated knowledge of the terrain.

Safety, Permits, and Site Access

Some of the most interesting sites are protected by permit systems to manage conservation and visitor impact. AlUla’s main attractions, select coastal atolls, and heritage districts may require timed tickets or proof of reservation at certain properties. Always verify access rules well before arrival and carry printed confirmation for remote checkpoints. For general safety and permitted activities, regularly check the official entry and safety guidelines.

How to Choose Where to Stay

Your accommodation choice shapes the trip’s rhythm. If you want early access to AlUla’s marquee sites or exclusive conservation areas, staying on protected property or at partner hotels is often necessary. City stays offer convenience for museums and dining; seaside resorts present relaxation and marine trips; guesthouses and eco-lodges provide cultural immersion and community benefits. Eating Locally and Supporting Communities

Savoring local food supports community livelihoods. Choose eateries that employ local staff and source regional ingredients. In Al Ahsa and Asir, small cafés and family-run restaurants offer dishes linked to place and season. When purchasing crafts, ask about artisanship and provenance, and prioritize items that support living traditions.

FAQ

Which site should I prioritize if I only have three days in Saudi Arabia?

If time is short and you want the richest cultural and visual return, center your trip on AlUla (including Hegra and nearby formations) and add either a coastal day in Jeddah or a short Riyadh visit depending on flight logistics. AlUla offers condensed archaeological variety and dramatic landscapes that feel singular.

Is it safe to travel independently in remote desert areas?

Remote desert travel is safe when you plan carefully: travel with a competent 4x4 driver or guided operator, carry extra water and fuel, inform someone of your route and schedule, and avoid isolated tracks in poor weather. For sensitive or protected areas, follow permit requirements and local guidance.

Are non-Muslim visitors allowed in the holy cities of Mecca and Medina?

Access rules for Mecca and certain parts of Medina are restricted. Non-Muslim visitors are not permitted to enter Mecca’s Haram area; access to other holy sites may be limited. Check official guidance prior to travel and plan alternate activities like visiting Al Hada viewpoints or exploring nearby cultural attractions.

How much time is ideal to experience a representative cross-section of the Kingdom?

A two-week trip allows a powerful cross-section: combine AlUla and northwest sites, the Red Sea coast, a capital-city segment in Riyadh with Diriyah, and a few days in the Asir highlands. With this timeframe you can balance archaeology, coast, city, and mountain experiences while allowing for travel time and local immersion.
