Budget Travel in Saudi Arabia: The Complete Hostel Guide for 2026

Updated March 29, 2026 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Hostels in Saudi Arabia have transformed the budget travel experience. Five years ago, finding affordable group accommodation felt nearly impossible. Today, Riyadh and Jeddah host thriving hostel communities catering to backpackers, young professionals, and solo travellers. I've spent the last three years working across the Gulf region, and I've watched this shift firsthand.

The Kingdom opened tourist visas to international visitors in 2019. This single change created demand for budget-friendly lodging options. Hostels filled that gap quickly. From my conversations with forty-plus international colleagues relocating to the region, nearly half chose hostels during their first month to ease transition costs.

The Saudi Hostel Scene Today

Riyadh leads the hostel movement with fourteen established properties. Jeddah follows with nine facilities. Secondary cities like Al Khobar and Dammam are catching up. These aren't cramped dormitories. Most Saudi hostels combine modern amenities with cultural sensitivity, respecting local customs whilst maintaining backpacker-friendly spaces.

I stayed at three Riyadh hostels whilst researching employment patterns for Gulf-based recruitment. Each offered high-speed wifi, communal kitchens, and prayer spaces. This balance matters. Hostels here understand that guests need both connectivity and cultural respect.

Pricing remains genuinely affordable. A dorm bed costs between 80-150 SAR nightly (21-40 USD). Private rooms run 200-350 SAR (53-93 USD). These rates underscore why hostels attract solo travellers stretching limited budgets across extended journeys.

What Makes Saudi Hostels Different

Prayer schedules shape hostel culture here. Most facilities provide quiet zones during salah times. Common areas buzz with activity afterwards. I noticed this rhythm immediately during my first stay. It creates natural downtime for reading, writing, or planning.

Single travellers benefit most. Female travellers find dedicated women-only floors standard practice. Male backpackers mix easily with the growing community of digital nomads working remotely from common spaces.

Alcohol policies differ from Western hostels. Officially, the Kingdom prohibits alcohol. Most hostels respect this rigorously. If nightlife matters to your trip, adjust expectations. The social scene centres on coffee culture, late dinners, and desert adventures rather than bar crawls.

Food costs remain impressively low. A restaurant meal runs 20-45 SAR (5-12 USD). Self-catering through hostel kitchens cuts costs further. I prepared meals five times weekly during my research stays and spent roughly 200 SAR (53 USD) for an entire week.

Daily Budget Breakdown

Here's what I actually spent during two weeks across Riyadh and Jeddah:

Accommodation: Average 110 SAR nightly = 770 SAR weekly (205 USD weekly). I rotated between budget and mid-range dorms to test facilities.

Food: Self-catering four days, eating out three days. Weekly total: 280 SAR (75 USD). Shopping at local souks yields fresh produce cheaper than any Western country. One memorable lunch at a local restaurant cost 25 SAR (7 USD) for grilled chicken, rice, and fresh juice.

Transport: Uber runs 8-15 SAR per ride (2-4 USD). I moved between three hostels using shared rides. Weekly transport: 140 SAR (37 USD).

Activities: Museum entries, desert tours, and cultural sites averaged 50-100 SAR each (13-27 USD). I explored five different attractions across two weeks for 600 SAR total (160 USD).

Miscellaneous: Phone credit, toiletries, snacks: 100 SAR weekly (27 USD).

Total weekly spend: 1,890 SAR or roughly 500 USD.

Breaking this down further, daily costs averaged 270 SAR (72 USD) including accommodation. Remove accommodation and daily expenses for food, transport, and activities dropped to 160 SAR (43 USD).

Finding the Right Hostel

Location matters enormously. I prioritised hostels within walking distance of metro stations. Riyadh's metro system, opened in 2020, transformed accessibility. No metro in Jeddah means Uber dependency, which increases transport budgets by forty percent.

Research reviews across multiple platforms. Google, Agoda, and Booking.com each host different reviewer demographics. I cross-referenced thirty reviews before booking. Consistent themes emerged: cleanliness, staff helpfulness, and community atmosphere ranked highest.

Ask about long-stay discounts before booking. Weekly rates often drop fifteen percent. Monthly arrangements sometimes drop prices further. During my research, I negotiated a weekly rate in Jeddah and saved 140 SAR total (37 USD).

Verify wifi speed directly. Remote workers pack hostels here. Old infrastructure means some properties still offer weak connections. I tested wifi at seven facilities. Three delivered consistent speeds above 30 Mbps. The others struggled with peak-hour performance.

Hostels as Social Hubs

The community aspect transforms budget travel. I attended three hostel-organised activities: a group dinner, a desert sunrise trip, and a cultural walking tour. Each cost nothing or required small contributions. These experiences connected me with travellers from Canada, Germany, Australia, and three other countries.

Women travellers report strong peer support networks. Several told me about forming friendships that lasted beyond their trips. One Canadian engineer extended her stay because hostel friendships developed into collaborative projects and weekend adventures.

Male solo travellers experience natural community building too. I observed mixed-gender common spaces where conversations flowed naturally. Shared kitchen duty, group meals, and planned activities created genuine connections between strangers.

Practical Tips for Budget Hostelling

Pack light. Storage space limits in dorms force minimalism. I travelled with one backpack and never needed more. Excess luggage requires paid storage.

Bring earplugs and eye masks. Shared dormitories mean lights, noise, and movement at unpredictable hours. Both items cost less than five SAR but improve sleep quality dramatically.

Eat breakfast at hostels when included. This meal often represents the best value of the day. One hostel's included breakfast featured fresh fruit, bread, cheese, and strong coffee. Buying these separately costs 60 SAR. Included pricing: zero.

Join hostel activities even if you're initially reluctant. I initially planned solo exploration. Group activities connected me with people and revealed local insights I'd never discovered alone.

Download offline maps before arrival. Mobile data speeds slow during peak hours. Having maps prepared eliminates dependency on real-time connectivity for navigation.

The Bigger Picture

Saudi Arabia's hostel scene reflects broader economic shifts. Tourism revenues now matter. Quality affordable accommodation drives visitor numbers. Government initiatives support young traveller markets. I watched this development directly through conversations with hospitality directors. Several mentioned ten-year projections showing hostel capacity doubling.

This growth matters professionally too. Travel opportunities build cultural competence essential for Gulf-region careers. Working across multiple nationalities requires understanding diverse perspectives. Budget travel forces daily interaction with people unlike you. These experiences sharpen exactly the skills Gulf employers value most.

Final Thoughts

Budget travel in Saudi Arabia has matured substantially. Hostels no longer mean minimal comfort. Modern facilities, community-focused design, and affordable pricing make backpacking genuinely viable. Whether you're stretching savings, exploring before relocating, or building intercultural experience, Saudi hostels deliver excellent value.

I'd return tomorrow. The combination of affordability, cultural richness, and genuine hospitality from both hostel staff and local communities creates something genuinely special.
