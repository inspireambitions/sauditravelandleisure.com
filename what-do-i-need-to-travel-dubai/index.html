What Do I Need To Travel Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Short answer: You need a passport with at least six months’ validity, the correct visa or entry permit for your nationality, printed and digital copies of travel and accommodation confirmations, travel insurance that covers medical evacuation, and basic preparations for local laws and customs. Beyond paperwork, prepare for heat, strong sun protection, and respect for public conduct rules that differ from Western countries.

This article responds directly to the most common planning question for first-time visitors: what practical documents, health preparations, legal considerations and on-the-ground arrangements ensure a smooth trip to Dubai. I’ll walk you from the moment you start planning through arrival, everyday life in the city, and departure—giving you the exact checklist, step-by-step processes, and local knowledge a confident traveler needs.

As the lead voice for Saudi Travel & Leisure, my aim is to deliver a balanced travel blueprint that blends inspirational context with highly practical steps so you can travel to Dubai informed, legal, and comfortable.

Understanding Entry Rules: Visa, Passport and Basic Eligibility

Passport Validity And Type

Immigration in the UAE requires that visitors present a machine-readable passport valid for at least six months beyond the date of entry. Handwritten passports, emergency 12-page passports, or passports marked with an “X” are typically not accepted. Always check the expiry date and renew well before booking if you are close to the six-month threshold.

Visa Options — How To Choose

Your visa path depends on your nationality, travel purpose, itinerary, and whether you have connecting flights or residency in a GCC country. There are three common routes:

  • Visa on arrival: Citizens of many countries receive a free visit visa on arrival (commonly 30, 90, or 180 days depending on nationality). This is straightforward for short leisure visits.
  • Pre-arranged e-visa: Some nationalities must secure a visa before travel via the UAE’s immigration system or through an airline’s visa service (Emirates, Etihad) or VFS. Pre-arranged visas include transit visas (48-hour, 96-hour) and multiple single- and multi-entry tourist visas.
  • Sponsor visa: Visits organized through a UAE resident, hotel, or a tour operator can be sponsored and arranged in advance.

Each option has pros and cons. Visa on arrival is convenient but limited in duration and not extendable in all cases. Pre-arranged visas reduce the risk of delays at the airport but require paperwork and fees in advance. Sponsor visas allow longer stays and additional support but require a reliable host.

Nationality-Specific Notes

Certain nationalities enjoy longer visa-on-arrival or special visa routes. Indian nationals, for example, have multiple entry options when holding certain residence permits or valid US visas. If you are unsure of your eligibility, check with your airline and the UAE immigration portal or consult your embassy.

Tickets, Proof Of Accommodation And Return Requirements

Immigration may ask to see a round-trip ticket and proof of accommodation. Keep printed and digital copies of your confirmed flights and hotel bookings in a folder and on your phone. If you will be visiting multiple emirates or staying with friends/family, have contact details and a copy of tenancy agreements or invitations as appropriate.

Overstay, Exit Controls, And Legal Risks

UAE immigration enforces overstays and can impose heavy fines and travel restrictions. Be aware that the UAE can impose exit controls or bans for unsettled legal or financial cases, including credit or civil disputes. Before you travel, clear outstanding debts or legal issues that might affect your ability to depart. This is especially important for visitors transferring or returning to the UAE in the future.

Pre-Travel Checklist (A Practical Quick-Scan)

To keep this section compact and actionable, here is a single, focused checklist you can tick off before you leave. Keep both printed and digital copies of these items.

  • Passport (valid 6+ months) and photocopies (digital & paper)
  • Appropriate visa or documentation proving visa-on-arrival eligibility
  • Flight itinerary and hotel reservations
  • Travel insurance with medical coverage and evacuation
  • Prescription medicines plus doctor’s note for controlled substances
  • International Driving Permit if you plan to rent a car
  • Credit/debit cards with travel notices set, and some AED cash
  • Emergency contact details and local embassy/consulate information
  • Downloaded maps, airline and hotel apps, and a UAE eSIM or local SIM plan

Treat this checklist as your baseline. For families, business travelers, or those with special medical needs, expand the list with additional prescriptions, childcare items, and printed medical records.

Booking Flights and Airports: What To Expect

Which Airport Will You Use?

Dubai has multiple airports serving different needs. Most international travelers arrive at Dubai International (DXB), the city’s main hub with extensive international connectivity and excellent ground transport links. Dubai World Central (DWC), south of the city, handles growing long-haul and low-cost carrier traffic; it’s further from the city center and may add transfer time. Some travelers also use nearby Sharjah (SHJ) for budget flights, but cross-emirate transfers can be longer in traffic.

If you fly into Abu Dhabi, be prepared for a one-hour transfer to Dubai by taxi or shuttle. When choosing flights, compare total door-to-door time and transit convenience.

Arrival Formalities And Immigration

Dubai airports are efficient, but queues can form at peak times. Keep your passport, arrival card (if required), and visa documents at hand. Immigration occasionally conducts random visual screening and may request a printed visa or confirmation page—carry a printed copy to avoid delays or fees for printing at the airport.

Customs procedures are strict on certain items (see the customs section), and you may be asked about the purpose and length of your stay.

Health And Vaccinations

Recommended Vaccinations And Health Preparations

There are no mandatory vaccines for routine tourism to Dubai unless you are arriving from a country with yellow fever; in that case, a yellow fever certificate is required. However, standard travel vaccinations and boosters are recommended: hepatitis A, routine childhood vaccines (MMR), tetanus/diphtheria, and for some travelers hepatitis B or rabies depending on planned activities. Annual influenza and COVID-19 vaccination recommendations remain sensible for international travel.

If you take prescription medications, bring enough for the whole stay plus a few days’ extra in case of delays and pack a doctor’s letter and the original prescription. Confirm whether your medicines are permitted in the UAE and whether any require prior approval.

Dealing With Heat And Hydration

Dubai’s climate is arid and, outside the winter months, intensely hot. Sun protection—high-SPF sunscreen, wide-brim hats, sunglasses and lightweight long sleeves—reduces the risk of sunburn and heat exhaustion. Stay hydrated and avoid peak sun hours on outdoor tours.

Travel Insurance

Travel insurance is strongly recommended; ensure medical coverage includes hospital care and medical evacuation. Some health systems treat tourists at private facilities with high costs—insurance prevents surprise bills.

Customs, Prohibited Items, And Legal Considerations

Dubai enforces strict rules on what you can bring into the country. Violations can lead to fines, detention, or deportation.

Items That Are Strictly Prohibited Or Controlled

Firearms, weapon parts, body armor, functional or inert ammunition, and certain defense equipment are prohibited without explicit permission. Items that may appear innocuous elsewhere can be illegal in the UAE:

  • Pornographic material and some religious missionary literature
  • E-cigarettes and certain vaping products may be tightly regulated or prohibited in some emirates—check specifics
  • Narcotics and recreational drugs of any kind—possession, trafficking and use carry severe penalties, including long prison terms
  • Products containing THC/CBD or other cannabinoids are prohibited
  • Some medicines available over the counter in other countries require prescriptions and documentation in the UAE

If you must carry controlled prescription medication, carry the prescription and a doctor’s note and confirm the drug is permitted in the UAE.

Electronic Devices And Privacy

Bring laptops and phones—these are allowed—but be cautious about content that could be offensive under UAE law (explicit images, certain political content). While photographing public places is generally fine, always obtain permission before photographing local people, especially women, and avoid photographing military installations, police stations, and certain government buildings.

Declare High-Value Items And Currency

Declare large amounts of cash at customs; travelers carrying AED 60,000 or more (or equivalent) may need to declare it. Keep proof of purchase for expensive electronics and jewelry if you bring them with you.

Getting Around Dubai

Public Transport

Dubai’s metro is modern and efficient, with two main lines serving central tourist and business districts. Trains are clean and air-conditioned; during rush hours they can be crowded. Metro trips are economical and often faster than road transport during peak traffic. Consider a Nol card (the local transport card) for seamless metro, tram and bus payments.

Taxis And Ride-Hailing

Taxis in Dubai are plentiful and metered; the government regulates fares. Uber and Careem operate widely and may be slightly more expensive than regular taxis during peak times. For the best value, use standard taxis for short trips and ride-hailing for longer or fixed-price journeys if you prefer booking in advance.

Avoid unmetered private drivers and always confirm the approximate fare or ensure the meter is used.

Renting A Car And Driving

If you plan to explore beyond central Dubai—coastal roads, Sharjah, Ras Al Khaimah, or cross-border trips—renting a car gives flexibility. You will need:

  • A valid national driver’s license; many nationalities also require an International Driving Permit (IDP)
  • A credit card for security deposit
  • Awareness of local driving laws: UAE enforces strict speed limits, heavy fines for mobile phone use while driving, and stringent penalties for driving under the influence

Traffic can be heavy during commute windows; plan extra time for airport transfers and cross-city travel.

Inter-Emirate Travel And Border Considerations

Travel between emirates is straightforward for tourists on valid visas, but be mindful of travel times and tolls. If you plan to cross into neighboring countries by land (e.g., Oman), check border rules and permits.

Accommodation: Where To Stay And What To Expect

Dubai offers a wide accommodation range—luxury resorts, business hotels, mid-range chains, and serviced apartments.

Choose By Experience

Choose your base according to the experience you want:

  • Dubai Mall / Downtown: central, near Burj Khalifa, good for city sightseeing and shopping
  • Dubai Marina / Jumeirah Beach: beachside hotels and nightlife, Palm Jumeirah access
  • Old Dubai / Deira: traditional markets, lower-cost options, and access to the creek and souks
  • Al Barsha: convenient for Mall of the Emirates and central transit links

Booking platforms often list cancellation policies clearly; ensure you have refundable reservations or flexible booking if plans may shift.

Hotel Policies

Hotels in Dubai are modern and international, but some properties have dress codes for public areas or restaurants. Alcohol is usually available within licensed hotel venues but not in public areas, and hotels may ask for ID for guests purchasing alcohol.

Money, Costs, And Tipping

Currency And Payments

The currency is the UAE dirham (AED). Credit and debit cards (Visa and Mastercard) are widely accepted; American Express acceptance is more limited. ATMs are plentiful; however, notify your bank before departure to avoid card blocks and check foreign withdrawal fees.

Typical Costs

Dubai ranges from budget-friendly to ultra-luxury. Street food and local eateries can be economical, while upscale restaurants and attractions (high-end hotels, helicopter tours, fine dining) can be costly. Taxis are relatively affordable for short trips, but hire costs for airport transfers, private tours, and desert safaris will vary.

Tipping

Tipping is customary but not always mandatory: small tips for cafe or restaurant service (5-10%), rounding up taxi fares, and modest tips for bellhops and housekeeping are common.

Cultural Etiquette And Laws You Should Respect

Understanding and respecting local norms will prevent awkward situations or legal trouble.

Dress And Public Behavior

Dubai is cosmopolitan, but modest dress in public spaces—especially in government buildings, mosques, and shopping malls—is appreciated. Avoid overly revealing clothing when away from the beach or pool. Public displays of affection are frowned upon; holding hands or a light kiss is tolerated in many areas, but passionate displays can attract attention or legal action in rare cases.

Alcohol And Nightlife

Drinking is permitted in licensed venues, and many hotels have bars and restaurants serving alcohol. Public intoxication or drinking in public spaces is illegal. Non-Muslim residents can obtain licenses to buy alcohol at licensed stores; tourists should purchase alcohol only through licensed venues or duty-free shops.

Religious Observance And Ramadan

During Ramadan, avoid eating, drinking or smoking in public during daylight hours as a courtesy—many restaurants operate behind screens or offer takeout. Respect prayer times and observe noise and behavior norms.

Photographing People And Religious Sites

Always ask permission before photographing individuals, particularly women and families. When visiting mosques, follow dress and behavior rules; many mosques require modest clothing and cover for women, and some restrict non-Muslim visitor access to specific times and areas.

Digital Preparations: Connectivity And Apps

SIM Cards, eSIMs, And Data

Buying a local SIM or eSIM at the airport or in the city is straightforward. Du and Etisalat provide tourist SIMs with data and voice packages, and airport kiosks often offer free small tourist starter packs with limited data. Activate roaming if preferred, but local plans are generally more cost-effective and reliable.

Download key apps before you travel: ride-hailing (Careem, Uber), maps (offline maps), translation apps, your airline, and accommodation apps. Many attractions and restaurants accept contactless QR menus and mobile payments; ensure your phone is ready.

Security And Privacy

Use strong passwords and avoid connecting to unsecured public Wi-Fi for sensitive tasks like banking. Consider a VPN for added privacy.

Planning Activities And Managing Expectations

Popular Experiences—How To Book Smart

Dubai’s top experiences—dune safaris, Burj Khalifa visits, water parks, and luxury dining—are in high demand during peak season. Book tickets in advance for timed-entry attractions and choose early morning or late afternoon slots for outdoor activities to avoid the heat.

When choosing between options—guided tour vs. private driver, economy safari vs. premium desert experience—consider comfort, time, and cost. Private options provide flexibility; group tours are easier and often less expensive.

Day Trips And Regional Travel

Abu Dhabi is an easy day trip from Dubai for the Grand Mosque and cultural sites. If you want to expand beyond the UAE to the wider Gulf, plan travel logistics and visa requirements carefully. Safety, Crime, And Security Considerations

General Safety

Dubai is relatively safe compared with many global cities. However, exercise normal travel awareness: secure valuables, watch for pickpockets in crowded places, and be cautious when withdrawing cash at ATMs.

Terrorism And Regional Concerns

The Gulf region has varying security concerns; travelers should exercise situational awareness and follow travel advisories from their governments. Avoid demonstrations and large gatherings that could attract police or security responses.

Exit Bans And Financial Disputes

A particularly important consideration for travelers: unpaid debts or unresolved legal cases in the UAE can cause an exit ban. Always maintain awareness of any local legal exposure, particularly if conducting business or entering into financial arrangements while in the country.

Practical Packing And What To Bring

Rather than a long bulleted list, here’s a compact shopping-and-packing narrative that reduces overpacking while hitting essentials.

Start with a versatile wardrobe of lightweight, breathable fabrics; include one smart outfit for nicer restaurants or events. Pack a swimsuit and a light cover-up for beaches and hotel pools; when visiting mosques or traditional areas, have a scarf or long-sleeved shirt handy. Shoes: comfortable walking shoes plus sandals for the beach. Include sunscreen, lip balm, and a reliable reusable water bottle. For gadgets, bring travel adapters for Type G sockets, a power bank, and copies of travel documents on your phone and in paper form. Include a small medical kit and copies of prescriptions. If you expect to buy luxury items, leave a little luggage space for purchases and keep receipts in case you are questioned at customs.

Common Mistakes And How To Avoid Them

Travelers often trip over a few recurring issues. Avoid these mistakes:

  • Relying only on visa-on-arrival without checking specific nationality rules. Always confirm your specific eligibility well before travel.
  • Forgetting the six-month passport rule—renew well in advance.
  • Underestimating travel times because of traffic—plan conservatively for airport transfers and inter-emirate drives.
  • Bringing prohibited medications or items without documentation—check legality and bring prescriptions.
  • Assuming universal acceptance of American Express—carry Visa/Mastercard or sufficient cash.

Address these proactively: review official immigration guidance, secure travel insurance, notify your bank, and carry printed backups of your important documents.

Step-By-Step: A Practical Pre-Departure Plan

Follow this practical sequencing to organize your trip without surprises:

  1. Confirm passport validity and visa eligibility; apply for any required visa or pre-clearance.
  2. Book flights and accommodation with flexible cancellation if possible.
  3. Obtain travel insurance and check required vaccinations or medication.
  4. Notify your bank and prepare payment methods; convert some currency or plan ATM withdrawals.
  5. Download essential local apps and arrange local connectivity (eSIM or SIM).
  6. Pack appropriately for climate and cultural norms; include printed document copies.
  7. Before departure, check updated travel advisories and airport transfer options; retain local emergency numbers.

This step-by-step approach prevents last-minute scrambling and ensures you are legally prepared and practically equipped.

On Arrival: First 24 Hours Checklist

Upon landing, follow this short sequence:

  • Have passport, visa confirmation, and flight itinerary ready for immigration.
  • Collect any printed visa or arrival stamp and keep a copy.
  • Withdraw a small amount of AED or use a reputable taxi to your accommodation.
  • Check into your hotel and register your presence; hotels often ask for a passport copy.
  • If staying longer or planning to drive, confirm car rental documents and local driving rules.
  • Purchase a local SIM/eSIM if needed and set up mobile payments or apps for transport.

These actions set the tone for a low-stress stay and reduce the risk of trouble early in the trip.

Responsible Travel Considerations

Dubai’s environment and society benefit when visitors act responsibly. Avoid littering in public places, respect local wildlife and coastal areas, and support reputable local operators for experiences like desert safaris—this promotes conservation and fair labor practices. When shopping, consider buying from local artisans and markets to support small businesses.

Sample Stay Frameworks: How To Structure Your Time (Templates, Not Stories)

Below are neutral planning templates—use them to adapt an itinerary to your pace and priorities.

  • Short visit (2–3 days): Concentrate on Downtown, Dubai Marina and a single desert safari. Prioritize pre-booked attractions such as the observation deck and a late-afternoon desert experience.
  • Moderate stay (4–6 days): Add a cultural morning in Old Dubai, a day trip to Abu Dhabi, and a leisure day for beach or mall activities.
  • Extended stay (7+ days): Include excursions to Ras Al Khaimah or Hatta, more in-depth cultural programs, and time for relaxed meals and shopping without rushing.

Treat these frameworks as structural templates to personalize according to mobility, budget, and interests.

Final Practical Notes

  • Always double-check the latest rules before you travel; UAE immigration and public health guidance can change.
  • Keep electronic and paper backups of all documents.
  • When in doubt about legal restrictions or large purchases ask your embassy or hotel for local guidance.
  • Respect local laws and customs; doing so will keep your trip safe and enjoyable.

Conclusion

Planning a trip to Dubai is less about a long list of unknowns and more about following a clear blueprint: verify passport validity, confirm visa eligibility, secure travel insurance, be mindful of health and customs rules, and prepare for the city’s climate and cultural expectations. With these foundations in place you can focus on the experiences Dubai offers—its modern skyline, desert landscapes, and vibrant cultural scenes—confidently and respectfully.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a visa before I fly to Dubai?

It depends on your nationality. Many passport holders qualify for a visa on arrival while others require a pre-arranged e-visa or sponsor. Check your country’s eligibility well before travel and apply for any required visa through your airline, a visa service, or UAE immigration channels.

How long must my passport be valid to enter Dubai?

Your passport should be valid for at least six months beyond your planned date of entry. Machine-readable passports are required; handwritten passports are typically not accepted.

Are there health checks or vaccinations required to enter?

There are no routine mandatory vaccinations for general tourists, aside from yellow fever if you are coming from a country with yellow fever risk. Recommended vaccinations include hepatitis A and routine immunizations. Carry prescriptions and a doctor’s note if you bring controlled medicines.

What should I avoid bringing to Dubai?

Avoid drugs and any medication that is illegal in the UAE without proper documentation; pornographic material; certain vaping products that may be restricted; and firearms or anything that can be considered a weapon. If in doubt, check UAE customs and airline guidance before packing.
