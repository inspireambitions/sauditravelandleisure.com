What Do I Need to Travel to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai is one of the world’s busiest travel crossroads, welcoming millions of visitors each year for business, leisure, and family visits. For travelers coming from Saudi Arabia—and around the globe—knowing exactly what you need before you go reduces stress at the airport, speeds you through immigration, and helps you enjoy the city from your first hour on the ground.

Short answer: You need a valid passport with at least six months’ validity, the correct visa or entry permit (if required for your nationality), proof of onward travel, accommodation confirmation, and compliance with UAE customs and medication rules. Beyond documents, prepare for local laws and cultural expectations so your trip is smooth and safe.

Understanding Entry Basics

Passport Validity and Type

For most nationalities, the UAE requires your passport to be valid for at least six months from the date of arrival. That rule is enforced at check-in and at immigration. Diplomatic, service, or emergency passports can have different rules—confirm with your carrier and consulate well before departure.

If you’re traveling on a passport that has limited pages, renew it to avoid being denied boarding. The UAE also does not accept certain travel documents (for example, passports marked with an “X” by some issuing countries have restrictions). Make renewal a top priority if your travel is within six months of passport expiry.

Visas and Entry Permits: Who Needs What

Visa rules vary by nationality and by the type of passport you hold. Many Western passports and some others are eligible for a visa on arrival, while other nationalities require a prearranged visa. There are also transit visas (48 and 96 hours), tourist visas (30 to 60 days), and multiple-entry options.

To determine your specific visa pathway, consult the official UAE information and the aviation carrier you’re using. For a clear overview of national visa eligibility and the various types available, see the resource on UAE entry requirements and visa options. If you plan to enter on a short-term tourist visa and extend later, understand that extensions are discretionary and often require a visit to the immigration office.

Proof of Onward Travel and Accommodation

Immigration officers commonly expect to see a return or onward ticket and a confirmed place to stay for the duration of your visit. Even if your nationality qualifies you for visa-on-arrival, having electronic or printed confirmations removes ambiguity and reduces the chance of questioning at the border.

If you’ll be staying with a friend or relative who is a UAE resident, having a printed invitation or a copy of their tenancy/ID information is wise. If a hotel is sponsoring your visa application, ensure the hotel provides a formal confirmation you can present at immigration if requested.

Complete Documents Checklist

Below is the condensed, airport-ready checklist you should have in your carry-on. Keep digital copies on your phone and one printed set in case of electronic device failure.

  • Passport (valid for at least six months)  
  • Visa or evidence of visa-on-arrival eligibility (if required)  
  • Return or onward airline ticket  
  • Confirmed hotel reservation or host invitation/tenancy proof  
  • Proof of travel insurance covering medical evacuation and repatriation  
  • Prescriptions and doctor’s letters for any controlled medications  
  • Copies of vaccination certificates if arriving from yellow fever risk areas  
  • National ID or residency permit if traveling from or through Saudi Arabia

Treat this list as non-negotiable: missing one of these items is the most common reason travelers are denied boarding or delayed at immigration.

Health, Vaccinations, and Medical Requirements

Routine Health Preparations

There are no general COVID-19 entry requirements currently, but travel health guidance can change. Make sure routine vaccinations are up to date and check whether any destination-specific vaccines are recommended or required based on transit points.

If you are traveling from a country with yellow fever risk, you will need proof of yellow fever vaccination. Other standard advice applies: bring basic travel medicine (rehydration salts, analgesics, antiseptics) and a small first-aid kit.

Medications and Controlled Substances

The UAE has strict controls on certain medications, including many that are over-the-counter in other countries. If you bring prescription or controlled medication:

  • Carry it in original packaging with a copy of the prescription.  
  • Obtain a signed doctor’s letter explaining the medical need and the drug names (generic names are helpful).  
  • For medications classified under UAE controlled substances, secure pre-approval through the UAE Ministry of Health before travel—failure to do so can lead to seizure and criminal charges.

Always verify your medications against UAE lists because what’s legal at home can be illegal there.

Flights, Transfers, and Transit Rules

Choosing Your Route: Direct Flights Vs. Stopovers

Dubai is served by numerous international carriers with direct flights from major Saudi airports—Riyadh, Jeddah, Dammam, and Al Khobar—making direct travel fast and abundant. If you are planning a stopover, check transit visa requirements and whether you need to clear immigration during your connection.

If your flight itinerary is through another country, confirm transit rules for that country as well as for the UAE.

Immigration Procedures on Arrival

Dubai International and Al Maktoum International operate smooth, high-volume immigration processing systems, but peak times and special events can lengthen queues. Expect immigration officers to check:

  • Your passport and visa (or eligibility for visa on arrival).  
  • Your reason for travel.  
  • Where you will stay and evidence of onward travel.  
  • Proof of sufficient funds if asked.

Officers can ask for additional documents; having printed confirmations and a single folder with originals and copies makes inspection less stressful.

Random Screening and Document Copies

Dubai immigration occasionally conducts random visual screening. Carry a printed version of your visa confirmation page just in case you’re selected for screening and asked to present a hard copy. There may be a small administrative charge if you need the immigration office to print a copy for you.

Customs, Prohibited Items, and Cultural Restrictions

What You Cannot Bring

UAE customs is strict about items that contradict public morality, religious sensitivities, and national security. Prohibited items often include pornography, certain political materials, and religious pamphlets intended for proselytizing. Firearms, ammunition—even a single bullet—are forbidden without explicit government permission. Items that are legal in other countries—some medications, e-cigarettes with specific ingredients, and CBD products—may be prohibited.

If you carry electronic cigarettes or vaping devices, check the most recent UAE rules and the airline’s policy.

Food, Gifts, and Personal Items

Bringing pork products, laced or illicit substances, or materials that would be illegal under UAE law is a quick path to detention. Homemade gifts with alcohol content or items that carry explicit imagery should be avoided. When in doubt, leave it at home or check with UAE customs regulators before travel.

Drones and Photography Equipment

Drones are regulated in the UAE and need permits for recreational or commercial use. If you plan to bring a drone, obtain permission in advance and register it where required. Photography in public places is generally fine, but exercise discretion around government buildings, military installations, and private property. Respect requests from people who ask not to be photographed.

Money, Currency, and Payments

The UAE dirham (AED) is used everywhere. Major credit cards are widely accepted, but small vendors, taxis, and markets may prefer cash. Notify your bank of travel dates and locations to reduce the risk of transaction holds. Carry a small amount of AED on arrival for visas, taxi fares, and incidentals; airport exchange counters or official ATMs are convenient.

ATMs are plentiful, but use machines inside banks or reputable malls to reduce scam risk. For budgeting, Dubai can be as affordable or as expensive as you choose—plan daily spending and keep an emergency fund for unexpected expenses.

Accommodation and Booking Tips

Book accommodation in reputable hotels that can issue confirmation documents required for visa processing or immigration checks. If you’re using short-term rentals, keep a printed booking confirmation and the host’s contact information. Some visa applications and immigration checks still prefer formal hotel confirmation over short-term rental receipts, so choose accordingly.

If your trip is timed around major events (e.g., trade shows, sporting events, New Year), book months in advance—prices and availability shift quickly.

Transportation Within Dubai and to Neighboring Emirates

Public Transport, Taxis, and Ride-Hailing

Dubai’s public transport system is efficient: the Metro and tram cover major districts, and buses reach further afield. Taxis and ride-hailing services operate 24/7 and offer a safe, efficient way to move across the city, with English widely spoken among drivers.

For journeys to Abu Dhabi or other emirates, intercity bus services and taxis are available, and driving between emirates is straightforward. If you plan to drive yourself, ensure you have an International Driving Permit if your license is non-GCC and check with rental companies about additional insurance and age requirements. For cross-emirate travel tips and attractions in nearby Abu Dhabi, see useful planning information on traveling to neighboring emirates.

Driving From Saudi Arabia or Other GCC Countries

Driving from Saudi cities such as Dammam or Al Khobar to the UAE is a popular route for residents in the Eastern Province. Cross-border road travel requires proper vehicle documentation, insurance that covers the UAE, and passports with valid entry permission. If you plan a border crossing, prepare for customs inspections and ensure visas or entry permissions are in place for all passengers. For practical departure details from the Eastern Province and cross-border planning, review regional guidance from Al Khobar travel resources.

Legal and Cultural Considerations

Laws That Affect Tourists

The UAE has laws that reflect local cultural norms. Public behavior that might be overlooked elsewhere—public displays of affection, drunkenness in public, offensive language—can lead to fines, detention, or deportation. Dress modestly in public spaces (beachwear is fine at beaches; not in shopping malls or public transport). Alcohol is regulated: you can consume it in licensed venues and hotels but not in public. Avoid any actions that could be seen as insulting to religion, the royal family, or public institutions.

Social Etiquette and Respectful Behavior

Respect local customs: address people formally when in doubt, remove shoes when invited into a private home, and use your right hand for giving or receiving items where cultural norms recommend it. Hospitality is a core value here—respond with courtesy and patience, and you’ll enjoy warm local interactions.

LGBTQ Travelers and Special Legal Considerations

The UAE’s laws regarding sexual behavior are conservative. LGBTQ travelers should exercise extra discretion in public and avoid behavior that could be interpreted as public indecency. Check up-to-date travel advisories for personal safety guidance.

Working, Studying, and Long-Term Stays

If you plan to work or study in the UAE, do not attempt to do so on a tourist or visit visa. Work and residence permits require medical tests and formal sponsorship; medical testing for residence includes checks for certain communicable diseases. Employment on a visit visa can result in fines, detention, and deportation. If your goal is long-term relocation, begin the visa and sponsorship process well before your intended travel.

For Saudis and GCC citizens interested in residency or longer stays, consult the official channels and consider professional visa assistance to ensure compliance.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

  1. Leaving passport renewal to the last minute. Start renewal processes at least three months before travel if your passport is close to six months’ validity.  
  2. Assuming medications are allowed without verification—always check lists and secure pre-approvals for controlled drugs.  
  3. Forgetting printed copies of visa confirmations and hotel bookings—some immigration checks still require hard copies.  
  4. Underestimating customs restrictions on items like e-cigarettes, CBD products, or certain gifts.  
  5. Traveling without travel insurance that includes medical evacuation—medical costs and repatriation without proper coverage can be extremely costly.

Pre-Travel Timeline: What To Do and When

  1. At least 90 days before travel: Check passport validity; renew if needed. Schedule time for any necessary medical appointments and secure prescriptions.  
  2. 60–30 days before travel: Confirm visa requirements for your nationality and apply if needed. Book flights and accommodation and secure travel insurance. Consult any employer or sponsor documentation if traveling for work.  
  3. 14–7 days before travel: Print or save all confirmations (flight itinerary, visa, hotel), carry physical prescriptions, and verify mobile roaming or eSIM plans. Notify banks of travel.  
  4. 48–24 hours before travel: Reconfirm flights, check baggage allowances, and prepare a travel folder with originals and color copies.

This timeline reduces the chance of last-minute complications and helps you travel with confidence.

Special Situations: Minors, Pregnant Travelers, and Legal Complications

Traveling with children often requires additional documents: notarized consent letters if they travel with one parent or guardians who have a different surname, birth certificates, or custody documents. UAE and some airlines may request proof of guardianship or parental consent. Pregnant travelers should consult their airline about medical clearance and consider travel insurance that covers pregnancy-related events.

If you have outstanding legal or financial issues in the UAE (or even in another country that might affect exit permissions), resolve them before travel. UAE authorities can impose travel bans that prevent departure; several countries have reported travelers being detained at departure for outstanding debts or legal matters. If you have any concern, contact the relevant embassy or legal counsel.

Technology, Connectivity, and Communication

Dubai offers excellent mobile and broadband coverage. Consider buying a local SIM or eSIM for data-heavy travel, or take advantage of airport kiosks and online providers. Many apps simplify life in Dubai: ride-hailing, food delivery, payment apps, and public transport maps. Have a secure cloud backup for scanned travel documents and an offline copy of essential confirmations.

Emergencies and Consular Support

Know the address and contact details of your country’s embassy or consulate in the UAE. In emergencies—lost passport, arrest, severe medical events—contact your embassy as they provide crucial assistance and guidance. For U.S. citizens, the U.S. Consulate in Dubai is a primary contact point; other nationalities should check their government’s travel pages.

Practical Final-Mile Tips for a Smooth Arrival

When you land in Dubai:

  • Have your passport, visa confirmation (or proof of eligibility), and accommodation printed and accessible.  
  • Complete arrival cards or health forms if requested—some ports may have online or kiosk-based options.  
  • Keep a small amount of AED for taxis and incidental fees.  
  • Use official taxi stands or book ride-hailing services from inside the terminal to avoid unregulated drivers.  
  • If you plan to visit Abu Dhabi or nearby emirates, keep extra time for inter-emirate travel and check requirements for any attractions that may have separate entry rules.

For in-depth regional travel connections and city-specific advice for neighboring Saudi departure points, review our content on traveling via Riyadh or departing from Jeddah.

Avoiding Overstay and Legal Penalties

UAE overstays can lead to stiff fines, detention, and deportation. Keep careful track of your permitted stay and apply for extensions only where allowed. If your visit spans multiple trips to the UAE, use the official visa calculator where possible and maintain clear records of entry and exit stamps. If you have doubts about your visa standing or need to extend a stay, contact General Directorate of Residency and Foreigners Affairs or the relevant immigration authority promptly.

Sustainable and Responsible Travel in the UAE

Dubai is investing in sustainability and expects visitors to respect the environment. Simple actions—reducing single-use plastics, minimizing water usage, and following local rules at natural sites—make a real difference. If you enjoy outdoor activities, choose licensed operators and respect conservation rules in sensitive areas.

Closing Practical Notes

Travel to Dubai is straightforward when you prepare methodically: secure a valid passport, confirm your visa path, carry proof of onward travel and accommodation, comply with medication and customs rules, and understand local laws and etiquette. For travelers from Saudi Arabia, frequent direct connections and shared cultural familiarity make Dubai a convenient, high-value short trip or a gateway to broader Gulf travel.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a visa before flying to Dubai?

It depends on your nationality. Many passports qualify for visa on arrival; others require a prearranged visa. Check your specific status well before travel and, if required, apply through the airline or the UAE visa channels. For general national eligibility and visa types, see the UAE entry overview at useful UAE visa information.

What is the passport validity requirement for Dubai?

Your passport should be valid for at least six months from the date of arrival in the UAE. Renew early if you’re within that window.

Can I travel to Dubai with prescription medication?

Yes, but you must carry medication in its original packaging, bring a doctor’s letter and prescription, and for controlled substances get advance approval from UAE health authorities. When in doubt, seek pre-approval.

What documents do minors need when traveling to Dubai?

Minors traveling with one parent or different surnames may need notarized consent letters, birth certificates, or custody documents. Airlines and immigration may insist on additional documentation—arrange these at least a few weeks before travel.
