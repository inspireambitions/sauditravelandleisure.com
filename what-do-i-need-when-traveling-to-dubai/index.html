What Do I Need When Traveling to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai is one of the world’s fastest-growing tourist hubs, welcoming millions of visitors each year with a mix of modern luxury, accessible adventure, and a clear set of entry rules. Whether you’re flying from Riyadh, crossing over from Doha, or arriving from farther afield, preparation is the difference between a smooth, confident trip and avoidable headaches at the airport or on the ground.

Short answer: You need a valid passport (six months minimum), the correct visa or visa-on-arrival eligibility, travel insurance, key documents printed and stored digitally, and a practical packing list that respects local culture and climate. Beyond paperwork, plan for connectivity, local transport, and an awareness of UAE laws that differ significantly from many Western countries.

This article will explain every practical requirement for visiting Dubai: entry rules and visas, essential documents, money and payment tips, health and safety rules, local laws and etiquette, packing recommendations, transport options, and planning frameworks to turn your curiosity into a seamless trip. As the KSA Travel Insider & Cultural Guide for Saudi Travel & Leisure, I’ll combine insider logistical guidance with culturally grounded advice so you arrive informed, respectful, and free to enjoy Dubai’s remarkable experiences.

Entry Requirements and Visas

Passport Validity and Machine-Readable Passports

The first and non-negotiable requirement is passport validity. Your passport must be valid for at least six months from your intended date of entry to the UAE. Machine-readable passports are required; handwritten or damaged passports may be refused. If you hold multiple passports or residency permits, use the passport appropriate for your nationality and the visa issued.

Who Gets a Visa on Arrival

The UAE offers visa-on-arrival facilities to many nationalities. Depending on your passport, you may be eligible for a 30-day or 90-day stamp on arrival. Airlines and immigration have become stricter about passport presentation and prior visa cancellation histories—if you previously held a UAE visa that hasn’t been properly cancelled, you can be refused boarding.

For up-to-the-minute visa categories and country lists, check official visa information before booking; if you need a prearranged visa, begin the application well before travel. For general entry and visa procedures for the UAE, see our discussion of visa and entry rules for the UAE.

Prearranged and Transit Visas

If your nationality requires a visa in advance, apply through an approved service or the airline that offers visa facilitation for passengers (some carriers provide this service when you book). Transit visas (48-hour or 96-hour) are available for passengers with specific ticketing conditions. If you plan to enter the UAE on a multi-carrier itinerary, read the fine print—the online visa services frequently require an itinerary entirely on a single airline.

If you’re traveling from Saudi Arabia or planning multi-country Gulf travel, regional visa rules and multi-entry options can make planning easier; consult our regional insights on traveling from Saudi Arabia and broader Gulf travel guidance for connecting trips.

Visa Extensions and Cancellations

Short tourist visas can be extended once through immigration offices under certain conditions; however, extensions require your passport and supporting documents. If you have previously used or been issued a UAE visa that wasn’t cancelled properly, you may face entry or departure complications. Always verify status for older visas and cancellation history before travel.

Special Rules for GCC Residents and Diplomatic Passports

GCC residents may have specific entry conditions—some will still need prearranged visas depending on nationality and residency status. Official and diplomatic passports may have different processes and, in some cases, require preapproval. If you’re traveling on behalf of an organization or government, check with your employer and the UAE embassy.

Immigration Behavior and Exit Controls

UAE immigration maintains strict rules: you must exit on the same passport you entered with, and exit bans can be placed for outstanding legal or financial disputes. Travelers have been prevented from leaving due to unresolved local cases. If you have any concerns about prior legal matters in the UAE, check with local authorities ahead of departure.

Essential Documents: What To Carry (Checklist)

  • Passport with at least six months’ validity
  • Printed and digital copy of visa or visa confirmation
  • Return or onward ticket
  • Hotel reservations or address of stay
  • Travel insurance policy document and emergency contact
  • Driver’s license and international driving permit (if renting a car)
  • Prescription medications with doctor’s letter (if necessary)
  • Photocopy of passport ID page (stored separately)
  • Emergency contacts and embassy details

Treat this list as the minimum paperwork to have physically and as encrypted digital copies on your phone and cloud storage. Keep one printed set in your luggage and another in your carry-on. For Saudis and other GCC travelers, also carry residency or national ID documents that can help if local checks occur.

Health, Vaccinations, and Medical Preparations

Vaccinations and Routine Care

There are no universal mandatory vaccinations for short tourist visits to Dubai, but ensure routine vaccinations are up to date. If you are taking prescription medication, bring the original containers and, where possible, a doctor’s letter describing the condition and medication dosage. Some medications that are commonplace elsewhere are controlled in the UAE; check ahead to avoid confiscation.

Travel Insurance

Travel insurance is essential. Look for policies covering medical evacuation, hospitalization, and repatriation. If you rely on government-provided healthcare in your home country, do not assume the same coverage applies abroad. Have policy numbers and emergency assistance phone numbers readily available.

COVID-19 and Other Infectious Disease Updates

As rules evolve, verify any testing or vaccination requirements close to travel dates. As of now, Dubai maintains no blanket COVID entry restrictions for most travelers, but individual airlines and transit hubs may require checks. Keep an eye on official UAE updates and your airline communications.

Laws, Cultural Expectations, and Safety

Alcohol, Public Behavior, and Substance Laws

Alcohol is legal in licensed venues—hotels, bars, and licensed restaurants—but public drinking and drunkenness are illegal and can lead to fines, jail, and deportation. Possession or use of illegal drugs, including what may be legal at home (certain CBD or marijuana products), carries very severe penalties. E-cigarettes and vaping devices face restrictions in some emirates—declare or check local rules before traveling with them.

Public displays of affection should be modest—holding hands is generally acceptable for couples, but kissing and more intimate gestures in public can draw legal attention, especially in conservative neighborhoods. If you’re visiting during religious observances, adhere to stricter local norms.

Dress and Public Decorum

Dubai is modern and tolerant, but modest dressing in public places (shopping malls, mosques, government offices) avoids uncomfortable interactions. Swimwear is appropriate only for beaches, water parks, and hotel pools. For mosque visits, women will need to cover hair, shoulders and legs; men should wear long trousers and avoid sleeveless tops.

Photography and Respect for Privacy

Always ask before photographing people, particularly women or families, and be cautious around government buildings, airports, and military installations where photography is prohibited. Drones are tightly regulated and require permits.

Legal Risks and Exit Bans

An important safety note: outstanding civil or criminal complaints, including unpaid bills or credit disputes, can block departure. Keep copies of receipts and resolve disputes before attempting to leave the country. If you face an official action, contact your embassy for guidance.

For travelers from Saudi Arabia or the Gulf, be conscious that local financial regulations and cultural expectations sometimes make casual lending and shared financial arrangements risky; always secure written agreements.

Money, Payments, and Budgeting

Dubai uses the UAE dirham (AED). Major credit cards—Visa and Mastercard—are widely accepted; American Express is accepted in some places but less commonly. Carry some cash for small vendors, taxis that don’t accept cards, and tips.

ATMs are ubiquitous; however, notify your bank before traveling to avoid card blocks. Currency exchange at the airport is convenient but often less favorable than in central bank branches or major malls.

Tipping is customary but not compulsory—rounding up taxi fares, leaving a 10–15% tip at restaurants when service isn’t included, and small tips for hotel staff are standard.

If you’re traveling from Saudi Arabia or neighboring Gulf cities, consider whether to exchange currency in your home country or withdraw on arrival: both approaches are common, but carrying a small amount of dirhams on arrival saves immediate transfers.

Getting There: Airports and Arrival Procedures

Dubai Airports and Which One to Use

Dubai International Airport (DXB) is the main international gateway with three terminals serving different airlines and hubs. Dubai World Central (DWC) is used for many low-cost carriers and some international services. Check your ticket for the correct arrival terminal.

Upon arrival, be prepared to present your passport, visa or eligibility for visa on arrival, and onward ticket if requested. Random visual screenings and requests for hard-copy visas are possible; carry a printed copy of your visa confirmation to avoid paying for copies at the counter.

Customs and Baggage

Customs rules are strict about prohibited items—pornography, certain religious materials intended for proselytizing, certain medications, and weapons are strictly banned. Declare cash over certain thresholds. If you plan to bring antiques, protected species products, or large quantities of commercial goods, secure permits in advance.

If you’re traveling from Saudi Arabia and bringing traditional gifts or food, be aware of restrictions on certain agricultural items.

Connectivity and SIM Cards

A local SIM or eSIM is a high-value purchase for most travelers. Major UAE telecoms offer tourist SIMs at the airport with data and call packages. eSIMs are increasingly common and convenient—buy before departure if your device supports it.

If you prefer to use your home roaming plan, check international rates; in many cases a local SIM or eSIM is the most economical option. For multi-country Gulf trips, regional roaming packages or an international eSIM may be best—see regional options before deciding.

Local Transport: Taxis, Metro, Car Rental, and Ride Apps

Taxis and Ride-Hailing

Taxis are abundant and regulated. You will find airport taxis and street taxis; use metered rides and ask the driver to follow the meter. Ride-hailing apps operate widely, offering transparent pricing and an easy alternative. For short hops, taxis are efficient; for longer trips during peak traffic, the metro can be faster.

Dubai Metro, Tram and Public Transit

The Dubai Metro is clean, efficient, and extends to major hubs including the Dubai Mall/Burj Khalifa and Dubai Marina. Men and women have designated carriages during peak hours; be mindful of signage. The tram services certain beachfront and Marina areas and connects to the metro.

Driving and Car Rental

Driving in Dubai requires a valid driver’s license; visitors can use an international driving permit alongside their national license. Roads are well-maintained but traffic can be heavy during commuting hours. GPS-based navigation is essential. If you intend to rent a car for desert trips, ensure your rental insurance covers off-pavement driving and check local regulations about desert driving.

If you plan to cross borders into Oman or elsewhere, notify the rental company—cross-border policies vary.

Airport Transfers and Private Drivers

Private airport transfers and chauffeur services offer convenience and help with luggage and navigation—useful for first-time visitors or those on tight schedules. When booking tours or desert safaris, arrange reputable operators and confirm all included services.

Accommodation: Choosing Where to Stay

Dubai’s neighborhoods each offer different strengths:

  • Downtown Dubai: Central, near Burj Khalifa and Dubai Mall—best for first-time visitors who want downtown attractions.
  • Dubai Marina and Jumeirah Beach Residence: Coastal vibe, restaurants, nightlife, and yacht marinas.
  • Jumeirah: Upmarket beachfront hotels and resorts with proximity to iconic hotels.
  • Deira and Bur Dubai (Old Dubai): Budget-friendly, near traditional souks and cultural neighborhoods.
  • Palm Jumeirah: Iconic island with luxury resorts—quieter and more resort-style.
  • Al Barsha and Business Bay: Practical mid-range stays with good transport links.

Consider your priorities: proximity to attractions, beach access, family-friendly amenities, or quiet comfort. If you’re traveling from within the Gulf, compare ferry or car options where applicable, but note Dubai’s primary access is by air.

What to Pack: Essentials and Clothing (Packing Checklist)

  • Passport, visa confirmations, and travel insurance
  • Printed hotel reservation and return ticket
  • Lightweight, breathable clothing and a modest-covering layer for public places
  • Swimwear for beaches and hotel pools (use only in those areas)
  • Comfortable walking shoes and closed shoes for desert excursions
  • Sunglasses, hat, and high-SPF sunscreen
  • Universal power adapter and phone charger; portable power bank
  • Local currency for immediate needs and a credit card with international capability
  • Small personal first-aid kit and prescription medications with a doctor’s note
  • Reusable water bottle (stay hydrated) and a small foldable daypack

This packing checklist balances respect for local norms with practical needs for Dubai’s climate and activities. If you plan desert camping, include closed-toe shoes, a light jacket for cooler nights, and a flashlight.

Cultural Navigation: Etiquette and Practical Advice

Visiting Mosques and Religious Sites

Mosques require modest dress and respectful behavior. Non-Muslim visitors are welcome at certain mosques with guided tours; respect dress codes and photography restrictions. Women should carry a shawl to cover hair when needed.

Ramadan Considerations

During Ramadan, eating, drinking (including water) and smoking in public during daylight hours are not permitted for non-observers in many public spaces. Many restaurants offer discreet dining areas or adjusted hours. Dress and public behavior should be especially respectful during Ramadan, and alcohol sales may be restricted or moved to licensed venues.

Dining, Alcohol, and Nightlife

Licensed restaurants and hotels serve alcohol. Avoid consuming alcohol in public areas. Dining etiquette is relaxed but be mindful of local sensitivities about displays of affection and public behavior.

Business and Communication

If you’re combining business with leisure, standard business etiquette—conservative dress, punctuality, professional behavior—applies. Exchange business cards politely and expect formal initial meetings.

Planning Frameworks: Itinerary Templates and Booking Strategy

Planning Your Time: Efficient Itineraries

Create a travel plan based on neighborhoods to minimize transit time. For example, cluster Downtown attractions (Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall, Dubai Fountain) into one day, Marina/JBR and Palm into another, and allocate an evening for a desert experience. If you have limited days, prioritize based on experience type—city, beach, desert, or culture.

Booking Strategy

Book high-demand attractions (observation decks, desert safaris, popular restaurants) in advance to secure times and avoid sold-out days. Use refundable hotel rates when possible if your schedule may shift.

Safety and Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Dubai is safe by global city standards, but common traveler mistakes include:

  • Traveling with prohibited medications without prescriptions
  • Drinking in public or appearing intoxicated
  • Photographing restricted government or private areas
  • Underestimating traffic and transit times during workweeks
  • Overlooking visa stamps or past visa cancellation requirements

If you encounter legal troubles, contact your embassy. For visitors from Saudi Arabia and other countries, ensure your travel documents and financial obligations are in order to avoid exit complications. For embassy resources and emergency contact guidance, consider storing your embassy’s contact details and local emergency numbers.

Practical Tips for Specific Travelers

Families and Children

Choose family-friendly hotels with pools and children’s services. Bring necessary medical records and copies of travel documents for minors. Strollers and child seats are widely available by request with taxis and car rental services—confirm with providers in advance.

Solo Travelers and Women

Dubai is generally welcoming to solo travelers. Female visitors should adopt the same prudence as in any major city—avoid isolated areas late at night, and follow local dress and behavior norms to minimize unwanted attention.

Business Travelers

Allow time for traffic in business schedules and book meetings in the morning when possible. Business districts can be spread out; choose accommodation near your main meeting area.

Cross-Border Travel: From Saudi Arabia and the Region

Many travelers combine Dubai with visits to Riyadh, Jeddah, or other Gulf capitals. Flights between major Gulf cities are frequent, and low-cost carriers offer affordable options. If traveling from Saudi Arabia, check visa reciprocity and specific entry rules for GCC residents. Emergency Contacts and Consular Support

Save emergency numbers and your embassy or consulate contacts before arrival. If you are a citizen of a country with a consulate in Dubai, include their phone numbers and location in your travel file. Keep copies of insurance and important contact details accessible to a trusted person at home.

Conclusion

What you need when traveling to Dubai is straightforward when you prepare: a valid passport and the correct visa or visa-on-arrival status, travel insurance, carefully organized travel documents, modest and climate-appropriate clothing, and clarity on local laws and customs. A little preparation—printing reservations, packing a modest layer, securing local connectivity, and understanding transport—creates confidence and space to enjoy Dubai’s architecture, beaches, markets, and desert experiences.

FAQ

Do I need a visa before I travel to Dubai?

Some nationalities require a prearranged visa; many are eligible for visa on arrival (30- or 90-day stamps). Always verify the visa rules for your passport well before booking and check whether your airline provides visa-facilitation services. For general UAE visa and entry rules, see our overview of visa and entry rules for the UAE.

How much cash should I bring to Dubai?

Carry enough dirhams for immediate expenses (taxis from the airport, small purchases), then use ATMs or credit cards for larger payments. Credit cards are widely accepted; notify your bank before travel to avoid card blocks.

Can I drink alcohol in Dubai?

Alcohol is permitted only in licensed venues such as hotels and certain restaurants. Public drinking and public intoxication are illegal. Always drink responsibly and follow venue rules.

Is Dubai safe for solo travelers?

Yes—Dubai is considered safe for solo travelers, including women. Standard urban precautions apply: avoid isolated areas late at night, respect local customs, and keep emergency contacts handy.
