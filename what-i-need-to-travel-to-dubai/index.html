What I Need to Travel to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai remains one of the world’s most visited cities: in recent years it has welcomed well over 16 million international overnight visitors, drawing travelers with its modern skyline, desert landscapes, and fast-moving business scene. If you’re asking “what I need to travel to Dubai,” you’re asking a practical question that determines whether your trip will be smooth or full of unexpected delays.

Short answer: You need a valid passport (with at least six months’ validity), the correct visa or entry permission for your nationality, proof of onward travel, basic travel insurance, and awareness of the UAE’s customs and laws. Beyond documents, plan for health preparations, a clear arrival strategy, and basic cultural knowledge so you travel confidently and avoid common pitfalls.

This post will walk you step-by-step through every document, process, and decision you’ll face before departure and on arrival. You’ll find practical checklists, a step-by-step visa framework, travel logistics, common mistakes to avoid, and guidance tailored for travelers coming from neighboring Gulf countries, including Saudi Arabia. The goal is to leave you equipped to plan, book, and execute your Dubai visit with confidence and cultural respect.

Do I Need a Visa?

Who Can Get a Visa On Arrival?

Visa rules depend primarily on your nationality. Many passport holders receive a visa on arrival at Dubai International (DXB) or Dubai World Central (DWC). Visitors from a wide range of countries are eligible for a 30-, 90-, or multiple-entry stamp on arrival. If your passport is not eligible, you must apply for a pre-arranged visa before travel.

When To Apply Before You Go

If your passport requires a pre-arranged visa, do this early. Pre-arranged tourist visas can be single- or multiple-entry and range from short transits (48- and 96-hour transit permits) to 30- and 60-day tourist visas. Processing and document requirements vary by nationality and by the route through which you apply (airline, embassy, visa service provider). For step-by-step instructions on applying, see the visa section below.

For travelers based in neighboring countries, such as Saudi Arabia, residency status or existing visas (like a valid U.S. or Schengen visa) can sometimes change your eligibility — check current policies before you buy tickets. Our regional resources explain what to check depending on where you’re departing from and how long you plan to stay; read more about travel in the region to align plans with local rules.

Passport Validity, Blank Pages, and Return Tickets

Dubai requires a passport with at least six months’ validity from the date of entry. You should also have at least one blank page for entry and exit stamps. Immigration officers commonly ask for proof of onward travel or a return ticket, so carry your booking confirmation on arrival.

Special Cases: Minors, Dual Nationals, and Diplomatic Passports

Traveling with children requires extra paperwork in some situations. Minors (under 17) traveling with one parent or a guardian of a different surname may be asked to show a birth certificate or a parental consent letter. Travelers using diplomatic or official passports often face different rules and should verify requirements early.

Dual nationals should be aware the UAE recognizes the passport with which they enter; the choice of passport can affect visa treatment and consular service access.

Passport, Documents & Legal Essentials

Documents You Must Carry

Carry original documents, not just digital copies. Immigration and local authorities prefer to see physical documents at entry or when dealing with authorities. Keep photocopies separate from originals and store scanned copies in secure cloud storage.

Travel Insurance and Medical Coverage

Travel insurance that covers medical evacuation and hospitalization is strongly recommended. While Dubai’s healthcare is high quality, treatment costs can be expensive without insurance. Ensure your policy covers COVID-19-related treatment and repatriation if needed.

Medication and Controlled Substances

Some medicines available over the counter elsewhere are controlled in the UAE. If you need prescription medications, carry the original prescription and, when possible, a doctor’s letter describing the condition and medication. For certain controlled drugs, you must obtain a permit from the UAE health authorities prior to travel. Check the UAE health authority requirements well before departure.

Important Legal Considerations

The UAE has strict laws on drug possession, public behavior, and digital content. Items considered harmless in some countries—certain medications, e-cigarettes, or unlicensed religious materials—may be restricted. Avoid carrying items that could be misinterpreted. If in doubt, verify the item with your embassy or the UAE authorities.

Flights, Airports, and Arrival Procedures

Choosing an Airport: DXB vs DWC

Dubai International Airport (DXB) is the main hub, close to the city center and well connected by Metro, taxis, and buses. Dubai World Central (DWC) serves many low-cost and cargo flights and sits farther from central Dubai; plan ground transport carefully if you land at DWC.

Immigration & Biometric Screening

Immigration procedures are generally efficient, but you may encounter biometric screening or random checks at arrival. Dubai authorities occasionally require visitors to present a printed copy of their visa confirmation at visual screening counters—keep a hard copy handy to avoid delays. Some airlines also participate in facial-recognition biometric boarding; registration is optional.

Customs and Prohibited Items

Declare items when required. The UAE restricts drugs, certain medications, e-cigarette liquids containing prohibited substances, adult material, and some religious items. Weapons, ammunition (even decorative), and body armor are prohibited without formal permission. Transporting items that could be classified as law enforcement or military equipment is strictly forbidden and carries severe penalties.

Currency and Departure Fees

You’ll use the UAE dirham (AED). For travelers leaving the UAE by land, a small departure fee may apply in local currency. Keep some AED for taxis or small purchases on arrival; card acceptance is widespread, but small vendors or metro kiosks may prefer cash.

Health, Vaccinations & Medications

Recommended Vaccinations

No specific vaccinations are mandatory for casual tourists to Dubai, except for yellow fever when arriving from a yellow-fever-risk country. However, some routine and recommended immunizations include hepatitis A, hepatitis B, polio boosters where applicable, influenza, and routine childhood vaccinations. If you plan extended stays or work in healthcare, additional requirements apply.

Mosquito-Borne and Local Diseases

Dengue can occur in parts of the UAE. Use insect repellent, wear long clothing during dawn and dusk if you’re outdoors, and use mosquito nets in areas where mosquitoes are common. Travelers with pre-existing conditions should consult a travel clinic for personalized advice.

Controlled Medications and How To Carry Them

If your medication is on the controlled list, apply for the UAE import permit before travel or carry documentation that clearly states the prescription, dosage, and duration of medication. Pharmacies in Dubai are well-stocked, but brand availability may vary.

Money, Costs & Practical Spending Tips

Currency and Payments

The dirham (AED) is pegged to the US dollar; cash and cards are widely accepted. Major hotels, restaurants, supermarkets, and malls accept international cards. Contactless payments and mobile wallets are common.

Tipping and Service Expectations

Tipping is appreciated but not mandatory. In restaurants, some bills include a service charge—check the receipt. For taxi drivers, small change is customary. For porters, hotel staff, and guides, modest tips are a good way to show appreciation for service.

Typical Prices

Expect a mix: luxury hotels and fine dining can be expensive, whereas street food and local eateries are affordable. Local transport offers excellent value compared to taxis and private drivers.

Getting Around Dubai

Public Transport: Metro, Trams, Buses, and Abras

Dubai’s Metro is fast, modern, and often the easiest way to move between major hubs. Taxis and ride-hailing apps (Uber and local services) are plentiful and reasonably priced. For short, scenic crossings of Dubai Creek, traditional abras are low-cost and part of the city experience.

Driving in Dubai

If you plan to rent a car, an international driving permit (IDP) is useful but not always required if your national license is in English. Traffic laws are strictly enforced—speeding fines and alcohol-related offenses carry heavy penalties. Parking regulations are enforced at nearly every location, so use official parking apps or lots.

Crossing From Saudi Arabia

Land borders between Saudi Arabia and the UAE are commonly used by GCC residents and certain nationalities. Rules for entry differ from air travel—some GCC residence permit holders must secure a UAE visa before arrival. If you’re driving from Saudi cities like Riyadh, plan for border controls, vehicle insurance coverage, and any COVID-era or health checks that may be active at the time.

For travelers flying from Saudi airports, direct flights to Dubai are frequent and convenient; consider flight times, baggage allowances, and immigration paperwork to optimize connections.

Accommodation & Booking Realities

Hotel Registration and ID

Hotels in Dubai register guests and may require a passport and visa details at check-in. Keep photocopies of your passport and visa to leave at the hotel if requested, but never surrender original travel documents unless official forms require it. Short-term rentals are popular; ensure the host or property complies with local regulations.

Refund and Cancellation Policies

High season and major events can sell out quickly. Check cancellation policies carefully, especially around the Dubai Shopping Festival, Expo anniversaries, or religious holidays. Prepaid non-refundable fares and rooms are often cheaper but less flexible.

Dress Code, Etiquette & Cultural Rules

What to Wear Where

Dubai is cosmopolitan, but conservative dress is appreciated in public spaces outside beaches, pools, and private hotel areas. Covering shoulders and knees in malls, government buildings, and neighborhoods around mosques is wise. Swimwear is appropriate at beaches and hotel pools but not in public streets or shopping centers.

Social Behavior and Public Displays of Affection

Public displays of affection (PDA) beyond holding hands are discouraged and can lead to complaints or police involvement. Keep interactions respectful, particularly near conservative neighborhoods and religious sites.

Drinking Alcohol and Nightlife

Alcohol is available in licensed hotels, restaurants, and private venues to guests over 21. Public intoxication, unruly behavior, and drunk driving are criminal offenses. Purchase of alcohol from licensed shops requires registering details with UAE authorities or buying via authorized channels, depending on your residency status.

Safety, Laws, and Things That Can Get You in Trouble

Dubai is generally safe, but strict laws and active enforcement mean minor missteps can have major consequences. Never carry illegal drugs, avoid disrespectful content about religion or the state on social media, and don’t photograph people (particularly women) without permission. Be aware of financial obligations: outstanding debts, unpaid loans, or legal disputes can result in exit bans until resolved.

If you need help, consulates and embassies provide guidance; keep their contact details with you. For tourists from Saudi Arabia or other GCC countries, local consular contact details may differ.

Practical Itineraries & Seasonal Timing

Best Time to Visit

The milder months between October and April are peak tourist season with comfortable daytime temperatures. If your trip is flexible, plan for shoulder seasons to avoid crowds and secure better rates. Summers are hot but offer lower accommodation prices and excellent indoor entertainment options.

Observing Ramadan

During Ramadan, public eating, drinking, and smoking during daylight hours is discouraged in public spaces out of respect. Many restaurants offer covered or private dining; nightlife hours shift. Cultural sensitivity is important—plan activities and dining accordingly.

Combining Dubai with Other Emirates

A multi-emirate trip is straightforward. Abu Dhabi is a common addition for cultural sites and national museums, while Ras Al Khaimah and Fujairah offer mountain and beach escapes. If your itinerary includes multiple emirates, confirm whether your visa allows easy re-entry and travel between emirates.

Step-by-Step: Applying For The Right Visa

  1. Determine your eligibility: Check whether your nationality is eligible for visa on arrival, a 90-day stamp, or requires a pre-arranged visa. If you hold a valid residence permit or visa from certain countries (for example, a U.S. or UK visa), you may qualify for simplified entry options.  
  2. Choose application route: Apply through the airline if available, an authorized visa service, a UAE embassy/consulate in your country, or via the airline’s online visa portal. Some airlines allow visa applications when you book flights with them.  
  3. Gather documents: Typical documents include a passport copy, passport-size photo, confirmed return ticket, hotel booking or invitation, and proof of funds as needed. Certain nationalities must submit extra documents.  
  4. Submit application and pay fees: Visa fees differ by type (transit, 30-day, 60-day, single or multiple entry). Keep proof of payment and the confirmation reference.  
  5. Receive and print visa: If you require a printed confirmation at arrival screening, have a hard copy available. Some visas are electronic but carrying a printed permit helps with random checks.  
  6. Keep track of validity and extension rules: Tourist visas can often be extended through official channels but verify the extension process and required supporting documents if you plan to stay longer.

This step-by-step approach minimizes surprises. For the most current rules by nationality, consult official sources and airline visa services before booking.

Pre-Departure Checklist

  • Passport valid for at least six months and photocopies stored separately.  
  • Visa confirmation or proof of visa eligibility (print a copy if recommended).  
  • Confirmed return or onward ticket.  
  • Travel insurance covering medical emergencies and repatriation.  
  • Any required medical permits for prescription or controlled medication.  
  • Local currency (some AED) for immediate expenses on arrival.  
  • Emergency contact numbers: your embassy, local emergency services, and in-country contact person.  
  • Accommodation confirmation and clear directions for arrival transfer to your hotel.  
  • Driver’s license or IDP if renting a car.  
  • Familiarity with local laws regarding dress, behavior, and restricted items.

(Keep a digital copy of all documents in a secure cloud folder as a secondary backup.)

Common Mistakes and How To Avoid Them

One of the most common errors is arriving with insufficient passport validity. Another frequent issue is carrying medications without prescriptions or the correct permits and then facing delays or confiscation. Travelers also underestimate the importance of printed visa confirmations during the immigration visual screening process; airlines and immigration sometimes request a hard copy. Overstaying your visa or misunderstanding extension rules can lead to fines or travel bans—track dates carefully and act early if you need to extend.

Avoid cultural missteps by dressing appropriately in public spaces outside beaches and private hotel areas, and by limiting public displays of affection. Finally, don’t assume that social media posts are exempt from local laws—content that would be normally acceptable at home may be penalized in the UAE.

From Saudi: Overland and Air Travel Considerations

If you’re traveling from Saudi Arabia, Dubai is one of the most connected destinations by air and road. Direct flights operate frequently between major Saudi cities and Dubai; flying is fast and cost-effective. Overland travel is possible via the Al Batha or other border crossings, but prepare for border checks, vehicle paperwork, and cross-border insurance.

For Saudi residents holding Saudi ID but not Saudi passports, or GCC residents with residence permits, verify whether a UAE visa must be arranged before arrival. Recent policy changes have, at times, required certain GCC residency holders to secure visas ahead of travel. When driving from Saudi cities like Riyadh, account for the long-distance, rest stops, and the requirement for correct vehicle insurance and documentation.

Packing Tips and Tech Essentials

Pack for comfort and reliability. Lightweight clothing for daytime, a light jacket for air-conditioned interiors, and conservative evening options for traditional neighborhoods will cover most situations. For tech, bring an unlocked phone (or plan to buy a local SIM on arrival), a portable charger, and voltage-compatible adapters (UAE voltage is 230V, 50Hz). Keep copies of flight confirmations, hotel bookings, and visa documents offline as well as in the cloud.

If you plan desert activities, include sunscreen, a hat, sturdy shoes, and a reusable water bottle. If you’ll be driving between emirates, bring printed directions and ensure your car insurance covers inter-emirate travel.

Final Legal and Safety Reminders

Before you travel, check travel advisories and confirm that no legal or financial matters could impose an exit ban. Pay outstanding debts and confirm there are no legal disputes pending. Respect local laws regarding online speech, photography, and social conduct. Register your trip details with your embassy if your country provides that option; it makes it easier for consular assistance in emergencies.

Conclusion

Planning a trip to Dubai is straightforward when you address the essentials early: verify your passport validity, confirm whether you need a pre-arranged visa, gather required health and medication documentation, and prepare for arrival procedures at Dubai’s airports. Pair those practical steps with cultural awareness—modest dress in public spaces, respect for local customs, and cautious social behavior—and your visit will be comfortable, lawful, and rewarding.

FAQ

Q: Do I need a visa to travel to Dubai?
A: It depends on your nationality. Many nationalities receive a visa on arrival, while others must obtain a pre-arranged visa. Check your country’s visa rules well before you buy tickets.

Q: How long must my passport be valid to enter Dubai?
A: Your passport should be valid for at least six months beyond your date of entry.

Q: Can I bring prescription medication to Dubai?
A: Yes, but carry the original prescription and a doctor’s letter. Some medications require a prior permit from UAE health authorities—verify before travel.

Q: Is it safe to travel to Dubai during Ramadan?
A: Yes. Travel is common during Ramadan, but you should follow cultural norms such as avoiding eating or drinking in public during daylight hours and dressing conservatively in public spaces.
