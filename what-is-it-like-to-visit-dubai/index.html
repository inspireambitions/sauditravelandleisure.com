What Is It Like to Visit Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai arrives like a promise: gleaming glass towers, palm-lined promenades, and an energy that insists anything is possible. For first-time visitors the city can feel exhilarating and bewildering at once—luxury and tradition sit side by side, and the choices for how to spend a day are endless. Whether you’re drawn by architecture, beaches, food, or a curiosity about life in the Gulf, knowing what to expect will transform confusion into confidence.

Short answer: Visiting Dubai is a study in contrasts shaped by deliberate modernity and longstanding tradition. Expect polished infrastructure, world-class hospitality, a wide range of price points and experiences, and clear local customs you should respect. With practical planning, you can move smoothly between its high-rise spectacles and quieter cultural corners.

This article answers what it’s like to visit Dubai from every angle that matters to a traveler: sensory first impressions, practical planning, neighborhood choices, transport, must-do experiences, cultural etiquette, budgeting, accessibility, and sample itineraries that help you turn curiosity into a smooth, memorable trip. My goal is to give you an actionable blueprint—rooted in local realities and the travel frameworks trusted by Saudi Travel & Leisure—so you can plan with confidence and curiosity.

The main message: Dubai rewards visitors who prepare thoughtfully—understand seasons, respect customs, book key activities in advance, and choose a base that matches your interests—and the result is a holiday that blends spectacle with meaningful cultural encounters.

First Impressions: The Senses of Dubai

The Visual Sweep

When you land in Dubai the skyline announces itself: soaring towers with dramatic silhouettes, irrigation of lights along water, and engineered coastlines like Palm Jumeirah. Downtown Dubai centers around the Burj Khalifa, whose needle-like presence and surrounding plaza create a magnet for visitors. The Marina presents a different visual language—residential skyscrapers framing yachts and a long waterfront promenade. Meanwhile, Old Dubai—Al Fahidi, Bur Dubai and Deira—offers timbered wind-tower architecture, narrow lanes, and spice-scented souks. The contrast is immediate and intentional.

Sound and Movement

Dubai is a city of purposeful motion. Mornings hum with construction and commuter flow, afternoons slow into beach and mall rhythms, and evenings fill with dining and social life. You’ll hear multiple languages everywhere—Arabic, English, South Asian languages, Tagalog and more—reflecting the city’s expatriate mosaic. Public spaces and malls often play soft background music; souks are alive with bargaining and friendly banter.

Taste and Smell

The food scene is one of Dubai’s most democratic features. Walk from a Michelin-starred dining room to a corner shawarma stand and you’ll taste the range of the city’s palate. Scent-markers often shift abruptly: the incense and oud of perfumery shops near the souks, charcoal and grilled spices from street kitchens, and the cleaner, neutral aromas inside luxury hotels and malls.

The Feel of Service

Server attention and hospitality in Dubai are professional and polished. From valet parking to concierge recommendations, service culture is a core part of the experience. Prices and levels of formality vary, but you should expect helpful staff and efficient systems at hotels, attractions, and restaurants.

Practicalities: Before You Go

Visas, Entry, and Paperwork

Visa requirements depend on nationality. Many passports receive a visa-on-arrival for short stays; others require an e-visa applied for in advance. Check the UAE’s official entry rules well before travel. Carry a printed copy of any pre-approved visa and keep your passport accessible. If you plan to cross into other emirates or make a day trip to the capital, allow enough passport validity and carry required documents.

For travelers planning broader exploration across the Gulf, consult resources that explain entry rules and transport connections to neighboring countries; these will help you build multi-city itineraries that include both Dubai and other regional capitals like Riyadh.

Best Time to Visit

Dubai’s climate is unambiguous: scorching summers and comfortable winters. The most visitor-friendly months run from late October through April, when daytime temperatures sit between the high teens and low thirties Celsius (mid-60s to mid-80s Fahrenheit). Peak visitor crowds coincide with December and January holidays, festivals, and events.

If you want warm water and lively beach culture without intense heat, aim for November, March, or April. For lower hotel rates and sparser attractions, consider shoulder months, but be prepared for very hot afternoons in late spring and summer.

Health, Medication, and Insurance

Tap water in Dubai is generally safe for visitors, but many prefer bottled water. If you take prescription medication, always carry the prescription and put medications in original packaging. Check whether your medications are permitted in the UAE—some common international prescriptions and supplements are restricted. Travel insurance that covers medical evacuation is recommended, particularly for desert activities and adventure sports.

Currency, Cards, and Tipping

The UAE dirham (AED) is widely accepted and pegged to stable international rates. Cards are ubiquitous—you don’t need to carry large amounts of cash. ATMs are plentiful in malls and transit hubs. Tipping is customary but not mandatory: rounding up taxi fares, 10–15% in casual dining, and small tips for hotel porters and housekeeping are common practices.

Essential Apps and Documents

  • Use reliable ride-hailing apps (Careem and Uber operate widely) and the official public transport app for the Metro and buses.
  • Keep digital copies of your passport, visa, travel insurance, and hotel bookings in a secure cloud folder.
  • Download maps that work offline, and carry one small amount of local currency for short fares and tips.

Where to Stay: Neighborhoods and Choosing Your Base

Choosing the right neighborhood transforms your visit. Dubai is spread out; traffic can be heavy during peak hours, so staying near the activities you prioritize is wise.

Downtown Dubai

Downtown is the obvious choice for first-timers who want the Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall, and the Dubai Fountain within walking distance. It’s polished, tourist-oriented and filled with high-end hotels. Expect higher rates and crowds but close proximity to major sights.

Dubai Marina and Jumeirah Beach Residence (JBR)

If you want a waterfront vibe, the Marina and JBR offer promenades, beach access, cafés and evening energy. It’s walkable in stretches and good for dining and nightlife. Choose this area if you want a blend of beach and city living with scenic evening walks.

Palm Jumeirah

Palm Jumeirah is island living turned luxury. Resorts and beach clubs dominate—excellent for resort-focused holidays and those who want water-based relaxation and photo-worthy vistas. Expect higher resort prices and a calmer nighttime scene outside hotel complexes.

Al Seef and Al Fahidi / Old Dubai

For cultural texture, base yourself near Al Seef, Al Fahidi Historical Neighbourhood, or Bur Dubai. These areas offer authentic architecture, traditional hotels (boutique or heritage), and easy access to creekside souks. Streets are narrower and less modern, but rich with atmosphere.

DIFC and Business Bay

DIFC blends business with upscale dining and art galleries, while Business Bay is centrally located near Downtown. Both are convenient for dining, museums, and corporate travelers who prefer modern hotels with strong culinary scenes.

Jumeirah

The Jumeirah area (not to be confused with JBR) includes many private villas, mid-range hotels and beach access. It’s quieter and more residential—good for families and longer stays.

When you select a neighborhood, factor in traffic patterns and Metro access. If you plan to move across the city frequently, staying near a Metro station or having quick taxi access can save travel time.

Getting Around: Transport Options and Tips

Metro, Tram, and Public Transport

Dubai Metro is modern, air-conditioned, and connects major nodes like Dubai Mall (Burj Khalifa/Dubai Mall station), Mall of the Emirates, and Dubai Marina (via the tram). It’s an affordable, fast way to dodge traffic during peak hours. Trams and public buses expand accessibility in certain zones. For tourists, a prepaid NOL card simplifies journeys across modes.

Taxis, Ride-Hailing, and Careem

Taxis are plentiful and metered; choose licensed taxis with visible identification. Ride-hailing apps like Careem and Uber operate widely and are convenient for set fares and visibility. App-based services often include car classes (economy to premium), and drivers are familiar with tourist spots and efficient routes.

Driving and Parking

Driving gives freedom but requires confidence—traffic can be heavy and local driving styles are brisk. If you rent a car, ensure your license is valid in the UAE, understand parking costs, and factor tolls (Salik) into budgets. For short city stays, driving is often more hassle than it’s worth unless you plan excursions outside Dubai.

Inter-Emirate Travel

Abu Dhabi is roughly 1–1.5 hours by car from Dubai and makes a frequent day trip for visitors. Plan ahead: major attractions like the Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque and Louvre Abu Dhabi are spread across the city and may require reservations. For broader Gulf travel and regional itineraries consider rail or short flights where available.

What to Do: Experiences That Shape a Visit

Dubai’s appeal lies in the diversity of experience: skyscraper spectacle, desert calm, culinary discovery, and cultural curiosity. Below are the experiences that most commonly define visitor impressions.

Iconic Modern Attractions

Visiting the Burj Khalifa observation decks is a must for many. Book in advance for golden-hour slots if skyline views are essential. Dubai Mall offers more than shopping—an aquarium, ice rink, and scheduled fountain displays that are tourist favorites. The Museum of the Future provides a designed experience exploring technology and imagination, and the Dubai Frame offers a symbolic bridge between old and new Dubai.

Ain Dubai, the world’s tallest observation wheel, offers different city panoramas, and Palm Jumeirah is worth exploring if you want striking coastline views and resort experiences.

Cultural and Historical Encounters

Old Dubai is where you find the city’s origin story: the Abra boat crossings on Dubai Creek, the spice and gold souks, and the Al Fahidi district with wind towers and cultural centers. The Etihad Museum and Dubai Museum explain modern UAE history; visiting a functioning mosque like Jumeirah Mosque during a guided tour adds context to the city’s religious life. These experiences ground Dubai’s glitz in tangible heritage.

Desert, Mountain and Coastal Nature

A desert safari—dune driving, camel rides, and an evening barbecue—gives a taste of traditional desert life and wider Emirati landscapes. For a more active escape, Hatta, with its Hatta Dam and mountain trails, offers kayaking and hiking under a cooler microclimate. Dubai also boasts beaches that range from public stretches to private beach clubs; choose according to whether you want community energy or resort exclusivity.

Food and Dining

The city’s dining scene is a global buffet. Emirati dishes like machboos and luqaimat appear alongside high-end international tasting menus and neighborhood comfort food. Brunch culture is strong (often a weekend midday event), and business lunches can offer exceptional value at notable restaurants. For safe and sophisticated exploration, book popular restaurants in advance.

Nightlife and Events

Dubai’s nightlife centers on hotel bars, sophisticated lounges and designated entertainment districts; many venues enforce dress codes and age restrictions. Expect late dining and lively weekend scenes. Annual events—such as design weeks, film festivals, and shopping festivals—deliver seasonal energy, so check event calendars before booking.

Art, Design, and Local Creativity

Alserkal Avenue is the city’s creative heart for galleries, small theatres, and experimental spaces. The Dubai Design District hosts exhibitions and shows that reflect the city’s growing cultural ambitions. Allocate time to wander these neighborhoods to see the creative undercurrents that balance Dubai’s commercial sheen.

Cultural Etiquette and Local Laws

Understanding local customs and laws is essential for respectful and trouble-free travel.

Dress and Modesty

Dubai is comparatively liberal, but modesty is valued. In malls and public spaces, clothing that covers shoulders and knees is respectful. Swimwear is acceptable at beaches and pools but cover up when leaving those areas; scarf and long sleeves are appropriate when visiting religious sites. For women visiting certain mosques, headscarves may be required.

Public Behavior

Public displays of affection beyond holding hands are discouraged and can attract legal attention. Loud or disruptive behavior, obscene gestures and public intoxication are offenses that visitors should avoid. Use discretion in public spaces.

Alcohol and Controlled Substances

Alcohol is served in licensed venues (hotels, restaurants, dedicated bars) and can be purchased from licensed retailers with appropriate permits. Drinking in public places, being intoxicated in public, or driving under the influence are serious offenses. Controlled substances have stringent penalties; even small amounts of recreational drugs and some internationally common prescription substances may be prohibited, so verify legality and carry prescriptions.

Photography

Avoid photographing government buildings, military installations and certain public facilities. Respect people’s privacy—never photograph people, especially women and children, without explicit permission. When in doubt, ask.

Sensitive Social Issues

LGBTQ+ rights remain restricted in the UAE; public expressions of same-sex relationships can be legally complex. Be discreet and avoid drawing attention to personal relationships in public settings. If you travel onward from Dubai to neighboring countries, research local laws since legal regimes vary.

Ramadan

Visiting during Ramadan requires heightened cultural sensitivity. Non-Muslims are not required to fast, but eating, drinking and smoking in public during daylight hours are considered disrespectful. Many restaurants offer screened dining areas and adjusted hours; public entertainment and nightlife may be reduced. Embrace the quieter daytime pace and the beauty of evening iftar and communal meals.

Budgeting: How Much Will You Spend?

Dubai caters to a broad spectrum of budgets—backpackers, families, and luxury travelers all find their niche. Below I outline practical expectations so you can allocate funds realistically.

  • Economy traveler: With smart choices—budget hotels or apartment rentals, public transport, street food and free/low-cost activities—you can manage modest daily expenses for around a lower mid-range budget. Expect to pay for one or two major paid attractions.
  • Mid-range traveler: Comfortable hotels, a mix of taxis and metro, dining at a variety of restaurants and a couple of paid experiences (Burj Khalifa, desert safari) will increase daily costs but remain reasonable if you book in advance.
  • Luxury traveler: Dubai’s high-end hotels, exclusive dining, private tours, and premium experiences push daily costs higher. Many travelers combine a few extravagances with otherwise moderate spending to balance costs.

Booking peak experiences and restaurants in advance saves money and stress. For shopping, research whether the item’s price advantage compensates for duty-free allowances back home. Return flight costs and travel insurance should be included in any trip budget.

Accessibility and Safety

Accessibility

Dubai has made significant investments in accessibility. Major malls, hotels, and public transport incorporate ramps, elevators, tactile paving and accessible restrooms. Nonetheless, older neighborhoods can be uneven and less accessible. If mobility needs are central to your trip, contact your hotel and attractions in advance to confirm accommodations.

Safety and Health

Dubai is generally safe; violent crime against tourists is low. Common-sense precautions—watching belongings, avoiding poorly lit areas late at night, and protecting valuables—are sensible. Heat-related illness is the primary environmental risk: stay hydrated, seek shade and avoid strenuous daytime activity in summer months.

Sample Itineraries: Turn Ideas into Days

Below are flexible itineraries shaped to different trip lengths. Each is prose-based and aimed at helping you prioritize without locking you into a rigid schedule.

Three-Day Snapshot

Spend your first day in Downtown Dubai with an early visit to the Burj Khalifa observation deck and an afternoon at Dubai Mall to see the aquarium and fountain in the evening. Reserve dinner at a restaurant overlooking the fountain. On day two, split the day between Old Dubai and the modern marina: abra across the creek, explore the spice and gold souks in the morning, then head to Dubai Marina in the afternoon for a waterfront walk and a sunset dinner. Use the third day for a desert safari—arrive in the late afternoon for dune driving and evening entertainment—or choose a slower beach day at JBR if you prefer a water-based relaxation day.

Five-Day Mix

Begin with Downtown and Old Dubai as above. Add a day focused on Palm Jumeirah and Marina: visit Atlantis for its marine attractions, and have lunch with views of the Atlantis skyline. Dedicate a day to cultural institutions and design: the Museum of the Future, Alserkal Avenue galleries, and DIFC’s art scene. Conclude with Hatta or a nature day—kayaking in Hatta Dam or a mountain hike—allowing a quieter finish to the trip.

Seven-Day Exploratory Trip

With a week you can move at a relaxed pace: combine the five-day plan, add an excursion to Abu Dhabi for a day (Sheikh Zayed Mosque and Louvre), and reserve a free day for spontaneity—shopping, spa time, or a food tour that follows local recommendations. Use longer stays to dip deeper into neighborhoods, experience brunch culture, and test a few of Dubai’s diverse culinary offerings.

Practical Booking and Reservation Strategy

Dubai moves fast during peak seasons. Key attractions and sought-after restaurants often sell out—book tickets and table reservations as soon as your dates are firm. For high-traffic activities like Burj Khalifa sunset slots, rooftop pool access, or exclusive beach clubs, confirm booking windows and cancellation policies. For multi-site trips, map each day geographically to avoid inefficient back-and-forth travel and to minimize time lost to traffic.

For cross-border travelers, if you plan to add Saudi Arabia to your itinerary, consult resources that explain entry processes and seasonal rhythms to align both countries’ logistics and get the most of your Gulf travel.

Sustainability and Responsible Travel

Dubai is investing in sustainability—water refill stations, public transport improvements, and policy shifts in hospitality. As a traveler you can contribute by minimizing single-use plastics, choosing responsible tour operators for desert activities, and using public transit where practical. Respecting local habitats in coastal and desert regions preserves the ecosystems that make these excursions special.

Packing, Apps, and a Minimal Checklist

Below is a short, practical checklist of essentials to prepare before travel.

  • Passport, visa confirmation and travel insurance (digital and printed copies)
  • Lightweight clothing that can be layered, a scarf for mosque visits, swimwear and a light jacket for air-conditioned interiors
  • Refillable water bottle, sunscreen, sunglasses and a compact first-aid kit
  • Credit card and a small amount of dirhams for incidental tipping or small purchases
  • Essential apps: metro/public transit app, Careem/Uber, hotel app, and a local map app that supports offline maps

Planning Cross-Gulf Trips and Comparing Destinations

Many visitors use Dubai as a hub for exploring the wider Gulf. Abu Dhabi is a manageable day trip with cultural attractions and open spaces; consider inter-emirate travel times when planning. If your trip extends to Saudi Arabia, build in extra days for domestic travel and cultural orientation—Riyadh and other Saudi destinations require a different pacing and deeper familiarity with local customs. For travelers developing an extended Gulf itinerary, regional travel resources help reconcile visa rules, seasonal differences, and travel rhythms across the Gulf.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

A few repeated missteps undermine otherwise great trips: trying to see everything in a few days, underestimating travel time across the city, not booking dining or attraction slots in advance, and misjudging dress and behavior in public. Counter these by prioritizing experiences, selecting a home base that reduces transit, reserving key activities early, and carrying a light layer or scarf for respectful dress.

Conclusion

Dubai is a layered destination: show-stopping modernity, deliberate service culture, and pockets of authentic history coexist. The best trips combine well-paced planning with openness to local life—book the attractions that matter, choose the right neighborhood for your priorities, respect cultural norms, and leave room for unexpected discoveries. Use the practical frameworks above to move from curiosity to a trip plan that maximizes enjoyment and minimizes friction.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Dubai expensive for first-time visitors?

Dubai offers a spectrum of price points. Luxury hotels and fine-dining are expensive, but you can balance costs with mid-range hotels, efficient public transport, smart dining choices and free or low-cost attractions like public beaches and walking promenades.

Do I need to worry about local laws and customs?

Yes—familiarize yourself with local rules on public conduct, photography, alcohol consumption and drug laws. Observing modest dress in public spaces and avoiding public displays of affection will help you avoid misunderstandings.

What is the best way to get from Dubai to Abu Dhabi?

Driving or using a private transfer is the most straightforward option and takes about 1–1.5 hours depending on traffic. There are also intercity buses and organized day tours that handle logistics and tickets for major sites in Abu Dhabi.

Can I combine a trip to Dubai with travel to Saudi Arabia?

Yes, many travelers plan multi-country Gulf trips. Allow time for separate entry requirements, and align travel plans with seasonal differences and cultural expectations.
