What to Do in Saudi Arabia: Where to Go and How to Plan

Updated September 29, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Introduction

Saudi Arabia is rapidly reshaping its image for international travelers. Over the last few years the Kingdom has opened new regions, invested in world-class visitor experiences, and made leisure travel simpler with eVisas and streamlined entry processes. If you want to move beyond the headlines and plan a trip that balances culture, adventure, and practical logistics, this article is written to be your trusted blueprint.

Short answer: Saudi Arabia offers a mix of archaeological wonder, dramatic desert landscapes, Red Sea coasts, and vibrant urban culture. From exploring Nabataean tombs at Hegra and the mirrored Maraya concert hall in AlUla, to diving pristine reefs off the Red Sea and hiking verdant Asir mountains, the country delivers distinct experiences by region and season. Below you’ll find the planning frameworks, region-by-region must-dos, transport and accommodation strategies, cultural guidance, and realistic sample itineraries to turn curiosity into a confident, well-executed trip — starting with reviewing the essential travel guidelines that govern entry and behavior in the Kingdom: essential travel guidelines for visitors to Saudi Arabia.

As the KSA Travel Insider & Cultural Guide for Saudi Travel & Leisure, my aim here is to combine inspiring storytelling with practical steps so you can plan a trip that’s as seamless as it is memorable. Read on for region-specific activities, when to go, how to move between sites, and planning checklists so your time in the Kingdom is well spent and culturally respectful.

Planning and Practicalities: Start Here

Visas, entry rules, and timing

Saudi Arabia’s tourist eVisa system currently covers citizens of many countries and remains the easiest route for most leisure travelers. Apply in advance and confirm visa validity, entry points, and the specific documents you’ll need — and review the latest health and security updates before booking. For an up-to-date primer on entry, permitted behavior, and documents to carry, consult the site’s essential travel guidelines for visitors to Saudi Arabia. This resource explains what to expect at immigration, how the eVisa works, and any restrictions tied to particular sites, including access to religious areas reserved for Muslims.

Short trips can be organized around city hubs, but if you plan to visit remote nature reserves or archaeological sites, allow additional time for permits and guided access. Many of AlUla’s marquee attractions and nature reserves require booking timed entry or guided tours; failing to reserve in advance can mean missing the experience entirely.

Health, insurance, and safety basics

Saudi healthcare in major cities is modern and reliable; remote locations have limited medical services. Carry international travel insurance that covers medical evacuation if you’ll be trekking in remote canyons or diving. Vaccinations should be up to date and, depending on origin, proof of certain vaccinations may be requested for specific activities. For an official checklist and safety reminders related to travel protocols, see our curated essential travel guidelines for visitors to Saudi Arabia.

Road safety is a top concern: long-distance drives can be monotonous and weather can change quickly in the desert. If you’re driving yourself, ensure your vehicle is well-serviced, keep water and extra fuel, and share your route with hosts or local guides when heading into the Empty Quarter or isolated wadis.

Money, connectivity, and language

Credit cards are widely accepted in cities and tourist centers; smaller villages and market stalls may prefer cash. ATMs are common, but when traveling to remote regions, take a cash buffer. Buy a local SIM at the airport for reliable data and maps — Saudi telecom coverage is strong in urban centers and main highways but can be patchy in deep desert canyons.

English is widely used in hotels, tour operations, and signage in tourist areas, but learning a few Arabic phrases will open doors and enrich your interactions. Carry photocopies of your passport and eVisa both digitally and physically.

How to Use This Article: A Three-Tiered Framework

Before the regional chapters, let me give you a planning framework I use with readers and clients: pick a tempo, anchor it to a hub, and layer curiosity.

  1. Tempo: Decide if you want a slow cultural deep-dive (one region, longer stays), a balanced sampler (two to three hubs with day trips), or an active adventure (internal flights, multi-site logistics).
  2. Hub: Choose one or two main bases (Riyadh, Jeddah, AlUla, Abha, or Tabuk) and plan day trips from there. Hubs cut transit time and let you unpack once.
  3. Layer curiosity: Build in one surprise — a sunrise balloon flight, a night under the desert stars, or a dive off a remote atoll — to anchor memories.

This approach keeps logistics manageable while letting you experience the Kingdom’s variety without constant packing and moving.

Regions and What to Do

Riyadh and Central Najd: Urban history and desert drama

Riyadh, the capital, is both a modern metropolis and a gateway to Najdi history. Start at At-Turaif in Diriyah — the restored mud-brick district that marks the birthplace of the Al Saud dynasty — and pair it with the National Museum for historical context. For an otherworldly geological escape, schedule a 4x4 excursion to the Edge of the World (Jebel Fihrayn) to watch a desert sunset from dramatic cliffs.

Culturally, Riyadh’s cafés and galleries are an invitation to the city’s contemporary arts scene. Food options span Najdi home cooking to restaurants that reinterpret Saudi ingredients with modern technique. If you’re in Riyadh on a weekend, check cultural calendars: the city hosts festivals, performances at Maraya-like venues, and seasonal markets.

Practical note: the Edge of the World is best visited with a reputable guide or organized tour due to unmarked trails; local companies provide transfers, snacks, and safety briefings.

AlUla and Hegra: Living history in sandstone

AlUla is the place many visitors name first when asked what to do in Saudi Arabia. The archaeological significance of Hegra — the Kingdom’s first UNESCO World Heritage Site — combined with Maraya’s mirrored architecture and desert activities makes AlUla a full multi-day program. Dawn hot-air balloons, evening stargazing camps, trail hikes through sculpted canyons, and guided tours through the Nabataean tombs create a layered experience.

Visits to Hegra require timed entries and often a guide; many sites on private lands require proof of reservation or accommodation packages. Book well in advance for limited offerings like sunrise balloons and private reserve access. Conservation rules and permitted behaviors are strictly enforced, so follow the local briefings your guide provides.

Jeddah and the Red Sea Coast: Coral reefs, souks, and coastal culture

Jeddah’s Al-Balad (Old Jeddah) is a sensory richly historic district with coral-stone architecture, carved wooden windows, and vibrant souks. It’s a living port city that showcases Saudi Arabia’s centuries-long maritime connections. Stroll the market lanes, sip coffee overlooking the old harbor, and allow time for a sunset walk along the Corniche.

Beyond the city, the Red Sea is a world-class diving and snorkeling destination. Regions like Umluj and lesser-known atolls host abundant coral reefs, whale sharks, and rich marine biodiversity. If underwater life is a priority, plan a dedicated coastal stint with dive operators who specialize in Red Sea sites; sustainable operators emphasize reef-friendly practices and limit diver numbers at fragile sites.

Cultural visit: the Floating Mosque (Al-Rahmah) is particularly atmospheric at sunset when the sea and lights combine for dramatic photographs. Respect prayer times and dress norms near religious structures.

Asir, Abha, and the southwest highlands: Mountains, villages, and seasonal color

The Asir region around Abha offers a surprising shift from sand to green terraces and juniper-dotted peaks. Villages like Rijal Almaa showcase vivid traditional architecture — the multicolored "gingerbread" houses and museums that narrate local heritage. Hiking routes, elevated viewpoints, and cooler temperatures make this region a summer refuge.

If you travel here, engage local cultural centers and seasonal festivals where traditional dress and floral headdresses enliven public life. Roads can be narrow and windy; allow extra drive time and, when possible, hire a local driver familiar with mountain routes.

Eastern Province and Al Ahsa: Oasis agriculture and living springs

Al Ahsa Oasis, with its mud forts, palm groves, and labyrinthine oases, is an excellent place for leisurely exploration. The limestone caves and Al Qarah mountain provide dramatic panoramas and interesting rock formations. This region pairs well with culinary-focused itineraries since many local dishes are tied to date varieties and oasis agriculture.

Northern Tabuk and Wadi Al Disah: Towering sandstone and coastal atolls

Tabuk province blends coastal atolls and monumental sandstone formations like Wadi Al Disah. The area is ideal for multi-sport travelers who want desert trekking, canyon exploration, and then a few days on quiet Red Sea beaches. Logistics often require private transfers or internal flights, especially when hopping between remote natural sites.

Experiences by Interest: What to Do, Categorized

History and archaeology

Visiting Hegra and Diriyah are priority experiences for anyone interested in history. Look for guided interpretation programs that contextualize the Nabataean world, the incense trade routes, and the socio-cultural evolution of the Arabian Peninsula. Museums and restored heritage houses provide close-up access to artifacts and architectural technique.

Adventure and desert activities

From dune-bashing and overnight desert camps to via ferrata climbs and canyon swings in AlUla, adventure experiences are professionally run and safety-regulated. If you’re chasing sunset vistas or want to sleep under the stars, choose operators with strong safety records and small group sizes to reduce impact on fragile desert landscapes.

Marine and diving

The Red Sea’s coral ecosystems rival many of the world’s best dive sites. Whether you’re a snorkeler or advanced diver, local operators run liveaboard trips, day dive boats, and island-hopping excursions. Prioritize operators committed to reef conservation and inquire about reef-safe sunscreen and environmental briefings.

Cultural and culinary experiences

Saudi cuisine varies by region: Najdi staples in the central region, coastal fish dishes on the Red Sea, and mountain specialties in Asir. Food-focused experiences — markets, cooking classes, and family-style meals — are especially rewarding for understanding daily life and hospitality customs. Where to Stay: From Luxury Camps to Guesthouses

Accommodation in Saudi Arabia spans luxury resorts, desert camps, boutique hotels in restored quarters, and practical city-center options. In AlUla, for instance, exclusive properties may be the only practical way to access certain private reserves at specific times; conversely, in Jeddah or Riyadh you have broad hotel choice and can use the city as a base for day trips.

To explore vetted options by type and region, visit our round-up of [where to stay resources] that focus on hotels, resorts, and region-specific advice: where to stay resources. When booking remote stays, confirm transfer details and any required proof of reservation to access protected areas.

Getting Around: Internal Flights, Trains, and Driving

Saudi Arabia’s transport network has matured fast. Major domestic airlines connect primary hubs; the Haramain High-Speed Rail links Jeddah, Mecca, and Medina with efficient weekday schedules. Renting a car is the best option for regional exploration, but distances are long and desert navigation requires a hardy vehicle and local knowledge.

If you plan to drive, carry a physical map and local emergency contacts. For multi-hub itineraries, combine short internal flights with rental cars for day trips. For city travel, ride-hailing apps and local taxis are convenient and safe. Culture and Etiquette: How to Travel Respectfully

Respect for local customs is essential. Dress codes are relaxed for tourists in many places but remain conservative compared to western norms. Public displays of affection are not appropriate, and modest clothing is expected in public spaces. In religious contexts and holy cities, dress and access rules are stricter; non-Muslims cannot enter Mecca and are limited in access around certain mosque areas. For deeper context on traditions, customs, and regional etiquette, consult our cultural hub: traditions, customs, and etiquette resources.

A few behavior notes that save awkwardness: step back during prayer calls if you’re in a public space, accept hospitality with both hands if offered food or coffee, and clarify photography permissions before shooting portraits of people or inside private courtyards.

Responsible Travel: Conservation and Cultural Respect

The Kingdom is investing in conservation and heritage protection. Many new visitor sites operate under strict carrying-capacity rules to protect archaeological and ecological resources. Follow guides’ instructions within reserves, avoid stray walking paths that cause erosion, and never remove artifacts or natural materials.

When buying souvenirs, favor locally made crafts and producers who can explain their techniques. This supports communities and keeps traditional crafts alive.

Sample Itineraries: Practical Blueprints

Below are realistic itineraries to suit different tempos and interests. Each is written as a narrative plan to help you visualize daily flow, logistics, and time budgeting.

Short city-and-coast sampler (5–7 days)

Begin in Jeddah. Spend two days exploring Al-Balad’s alleys, the Corniche, and local galleries. Allocate a day for a Red Sea snorkeling or introductory dive trip to a nearby atoll. Fly to Riyadh for a full day at At-Turaif and the National Museum, then a half-day excursion to the Edge of the World. This itinerary balances urban culture with one major natural highlight and keeps internal travel times short.

Classic cultural and desert rhythm (8–11 days)

Fly into Riyadh, soak up heritage sites, then take an internal flight to AlUla. Spend three nights in AlUla to experience Hegra, Maraya, and desert adventures like canyon swings and stargazing. From AlUla, fly to Jeddah for two days on the coast, finishing with a day of diving or a boat trip to adjacent islands. This plan allows time to appreciate archaeological sites with relaxed travel pacing.

Adventure and nature deep-dive (10–14+ days)

Base in Tabuk for access to Wadi Al Disah and coastal atolls, add days for liveaboard diving on the Red Sea, then fly to Asir for mountain hiking and culture in Rijal Almaa. This ambitious route requires internal flights and careful logistical planning but rewards with varied ecosystems and outdoor activities.

When to Go: Seasonal Framework

  • Winter (November–February): Best for most of the country — cool days, clear skies, ideal for desert trekking and archaeological visits.
  • Spring (March–May): Wildflower season in certain highlands; comfortable coastal weather. AlUla festivals often run this period.
  • Summer (June–August): Hot across most of the country; mountains in Asir are pleasant and a good retreat.
  • Shoulder months (September, October): Increasingly pleasant temperatures and fewer crowds; excellent for diving.

If you need a short reference for regional timing and weather, see the small seasonal guide below:

  • Northern and central deserts: November–March for comfortable daytime temperatures.
  • Red Sea coast: October–May for diving and calm seas.
  • Asir highlands: June–September for cooler mountain temperatures.

Pre-Trip Checklist

To transform planning into progress, complete the tasks below before you travel:

  1. Secure your eVisa and print/email a copy of the approval.
  2. Purchase travel insurance that covers adventure activities and medical evacuation.
  3. Reserve timed-entry experiences and any private-reserve accommodations, especially for AlUla.
  4. Book domestic flights and confirm transfer times; allow buffer between arrival and booked tours.
  5. Arrange local SIM or international roaming and download offline maps for remote drives.
  6. Pack region-appropriate clothing, reef-safe sunscreen, a first-aid kit, and extra water/charger for long road segments.

Costs and Budgeting

Expect a wide price range depending on travel style. City hotels and local restaurants can be moderate; specialist experiences like hot-air balloons, private desert camps, or boutique eco-lodges can be expensive. For remote areas, factor in transfer fees and mandatory guide costs. Budget-minded travelers can still enjoy markets, public museums, and self-guided city walks, but allow for a handful of splurges to access private reserves or unique experiences.

Safety, Scams, and Common Mistakes

Saudi Arabia is safe for tourists, but common travel mistakes include underestimating distances, not rehydrating sufficiently in arid climates, and arriving at remote sites without confirming opening times. Book tours with licensed operators and ensure guides are insured. Avoid unmarked side roads in desert regions and check weather alerts before long drives.

Bringing It Together: How to Structure Your Trip

Start with one primary hub and one secondary hub for a week-long trip. For longer itineraries, add a third hub or a coastal liveaboard to diversify experiences without excessive internal transfers. Use internal flights to cover large distances and reserve site-specific tours ahead of time. Prioritize one marquee experience per region (Hegra in AlUla; Edge of the World near Riyadh; diving off the Red Sea for marine life) and fill the rest of your days with slower cultural immersion and culinary discoveries.

Accessibility and Family Travel

Many major sites and city attractions are accessible for families and travelers with reduced mobility; however, remote dunes, cliff viewpoints, and older heritage structures may have limited accessibility. When traveling with children or older relatives, choose accommodations with transfer services and shorter excursion times. For family-friendly cultural programming, inquire about guided museum tours that are tailored for interactive learning.

Samples of Reasonable Daily Budgets (Per Person)

Budget travelers can focus on guesthouses, local dining, and public museums. Mid-range travelers opt for a mix of boutique hotels, guided tours, and occasional domestic flights. Luxury travelers may include private guides, exclusive lodges, and VIP access. Always factor in conservation fees, tour surcharges, and tip culture for guides.

Responsible Photography and Drone Use

Photography is popular, but request permission before photographing people. Drone use is subject to strict regulation; check for local restrictions and required permits, especially near heritage sites and protected reserves. Unpermitted drone flights can result in fines or equipment confiscation.

Conclusion

Saudi Arabia rewards travelers who plan with intention: understanding local customs, reserving protected-site access ahead of time, and balancing marquee experiences with neighborhood-level exploration. Whether you are drawn to the carved tombs of Hegra, the mirrored wonder of Maraya, the coral gardens of the Red Sea, or the cool peaks of Asir, the Kingdom offers distinct regions that pair easily when you plan around hubs and seasonal timing.

Frequently Asked Questions

1) Is Saudi Arabia safe for solo travelers?

Yes — many parts of Saudi Arabia are very safe for solo travelers when standard precautions are followed. Urban centers and major tourist sites have strong security and hospitality services; remote travel should be done with licensed guides or reputable operators, especially in desert and mountainous areas.

2) What is the best base for visiting AlUla and Hegra?

AlUla itself is the best base. Stay multiple nights to allow for timed entries, sunrise/sunset experiences, and conservation-led activities. Some special attractions require proof of accommodation, so pre-booking is essential. For practical resources on accommodations, see our where to stay resources.

3) Can non-Muslims visit Mecca and Medina?

Non-Muslims are not permitted to enter the holy city of Mecca. Access to certain mosque areas in Medina is restricted as well. Respect and awareness of these rules are essential; our traditions, customs, and etiquette resources explain these distinctions and how to plan respectful itineraries.

4) How far in advance should I book tours and accommodations?

For popular, limited-access experiences (AlUla, some Red Sea dive seasons, and private desert camps), book as early as possible — ideally 2–4 months before travel. For city hotels and standard attractions, 4–8 weeks is often sufficient. Check the essential travel guidelines for visitors to Saudi Arabia for specifics on timed-entry sites and permit rules.
