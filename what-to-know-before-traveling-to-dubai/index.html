What to Know Before Traveling to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai is one of the most visited cities in the Middle East, welcoming well over 14 million overnight visitors in recent years and continuing to expand its draw with new attractions, festivals, and business opportunities. For many travelers the city’s combination of polished modernity, plentiful services, and year-round sunshine is irresistible — but arriving unprepared can turn a dazzling trip into a stressful one.

Short answer: Know the basics of visas and entry rules, time your visit for comfortable weather, pack smart for both heat and heavy air-conditioning, learn core local customs (especially around dress and public behavior), and plan transport and reservations in advance to beat crowds and traffic. With those foundations covered, you’ll move through Dubai with confidence and get to focus on the experiences that matter most.

This article will walk you through everything you need to know before traveling to Dubai: legal and practical essentials, local customs to respect, transport and neighborhood strategies, smart ways to visit major attractions, health and safety guidance, money-saving tactics, and ready-to-use itinerary blueprints. Practical Preparations Before You Go

Visas, entry rules, and passport requirements

Entry requirements vary by nationality. Many passports receive a visa on arrival, but others require an electronic visa or pre-approval. Confirm your status well before departure and keep digital and printed copies of any approvals. Ensure your passport has at least six months’ validity from your arrival date; airline and immigration officials commonly enforce this.

If you’ll be transiting through Dubai but leaving the airport, verify whether your nationality needs a transit visa. For extended stays, work permits, or long-term visits you’ll need to follow UAE immigration and sponsorship rules — these are stricter and require local employer or host involvement.

Health, medications, and travel insurance

Dubai’s healthcare is high-quality but can be costly without insurance. Buy comprehensive travel insurance that covers medical evacuation, hospital stays, and lost belongings. If you take prescription medications, bring the original prescription and a doctor’s letter describing your treatment. Certain controlled substances are banned or tightly controlled in the UAE — check the official lists before packing and obtain any necessary import permits for medications.

Vaccinations are not typically required for short tourist visits, but ensure routine immunizations are up-to-date. If you have major medical needs or mobility requirements, contact your hotel and the attraction operators in advance to confirm accessibility accommodations.

Money, cards, and tipping

The UAE dirham (AED) is the local currency and is pegged to the US dollar. Cards are accepted almost everywhere, and digital payments have high penetration. It’s best to use the local currency for card transactions when prompted — dynamic currency conversion often costs more. ATMs are plentiful at malls and airports.

Tipping is appreciated but not mandatory. Service charges sometimes appear on bills (hotels and upscale restaurants will often add 10–15%), but small cash tips for drivers, porters, and restaurant staff remain common practice.

Timing Your Visit: Weather, crowds, and seasons

Best months to visit and what to expect

Dubai has a desert climate: hot summers and mild winters. The most comfortable window for most visitors runs from late October through April, when daytime temperatures are pleasant for outdoor exploration and events. Peak season falls in winter months and coincides with festivals and hotel demand, so expect higher prices and busier attractions.

Summer months (June–September) are extremely hot, often exceeding 40°C (104°F). If you travel during this period you’ll spend more time indoors — Dubai is expertly climate-controlled, and many attractions remain open — but outdoor plans will be limited to early mornings or late evenings.

Events and crowd planning

Major events like the Dubai Shopping Festival, culinary weeks, and large sporting fixtures draw local and international crowds. If a particular festival or exhibition is on your wish list, book well in advance and expect busy transit during event days. Conversely, low-season travel can unlock hotel bargains and quieter attractions if you’re comfortable with the heat.

Where to Stay: Neighborhoods and strategic decisions

Choosing a neighborhood by itinerary

Dubai stretches along a long urban corridor. Choosing the right neighborhood will save time and reduce stress in a city where traffic can be heavy.

  • If your priority is malls and landmark attractions (Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall), stay in or near Downtown Dubai for walking access to those sites.
  • For beaches and a relaxed promenade vibe, Jumeirah Beach Residence (JBR), The Walk, and Palm Jumeirah are ideal.
  • For waterfront dining, nightlife, and modern high-rises, the Dubai Marina and Jumeirah Lake Towers (JLT) balance convenience and atmosphere.
  • For a taste of history and souks, Bur Dubai and Deira are older neighborhoods with creekside charm and shorter distances to local markets.

Because positioning matters in Dubai, pick your base based on the activities you anticipate most. If you plan to explore other emirates or travel overland, consider proximity to Sheikh Zayed Road or the metro.

Hotels, apartments, and what to expect

Luxury hotels with full-service amenities are part of Dubai’s identity, but there are also well-located mid-range hotels and serviced apartments that offer great value for longer stays. Expect top-tier concierge services, and if accessibility or family-friendly facilities are important, ask for room layouts, crib/rollaway policies, and lift access before booking.

Short-stay rentals can be a good value for families or longer visits, but verify licensing and guest fees to avoid surprises on checkout.

Getting Around: Transport options, tips, and strategies

Metro, tram, and buses

Dubai’s metro is modern, air-conditioned, and connects major hubs like the airport, Downtown Dubai, Business Bay, and the Marina. The tram services certain coastal and residential areas, and buses cover the wider city. Using public transport can save money and often bypass road congestion, but check last train times and connections for your itinerary.

Taxis, ride-hailing, and apps

Taxis in Dubai are regulated and convenient; meter fares are standard and drivers are generally professional. Popular ride-hail services like Careem operate widely and allow credit-card payment and fare estimates. Downloading a ride-hail app before arrival simplifies movement, especially in off-peak areas.

If you prefer driving yourself, be prepared for fast-moving traffic and pay attention to local driving norms; parking is widely available but can add cost and time during busy periods.

Walking and micro-mobility

While parts of Dubai are walkable — Dubai Marina, Kite Beach, and certain promenade areas — Downtown Dubai and long stretches along Sheikh Zayed Road are less pedestrian-friendly. Use walking for local exploration where comfortable, but plan longer journeys around transit or ride-hail options.

Avoiding rush-hour congestion

Rush hours (weekday mornings and late afternoons) can add significant time to cross-city trips. Factor extra travel time when booking restaurants, tours, or flights; scheduling photo-heavy or long excursions outside peak commute windows will reduce stress.

How to Visit Major Attractions Smarter

Burj Khalifa and Dubai Mall strategies

The Burj Khalifa and Dubai Mall are must-sees for many first-time visitors, but queues and crowds can sap time. Consider early-morning or late-night visits for quieter conditions. For panoramic views without the long At The Top lines, reserve a dining experience at a high-elevation restaurant in the tower complex — you’ll enjoy the sightlines with less crowding and a more relaxed pace.

Book timed-entry tickets online in advance for the tower, aquarium, and other popular sites to minimize waiting. Off-peak and weekday slots are usually calmer.

Palm Jumeirah, Atlantis, and beaches

Visiting Palm Jumeirah and the Atlantis resort offers striking photo opportunities and beachfront time. If a resort day is on the plan, check day-pass options for beach clubs and pools. For public beach time, JBR and Kite Beach are lively and family-friendly, with cafes, rental equipment, and running paths.

Old Dubai, souks, and cultural neighborhoods

Make time to step away from skyscrapers to Old Dubai — Al Fahidi Historical District, the spice and gold souks, and creekside abras. These areas are compact and best explored on foot, at a slower pace, to enjoy architecture, markets, and local cafés. Bargaining at souks is part of the experience for certain goods; do it with good humor and realistic expectations.

Desert experiences and nature escapes

A desert safari is a core Emirati experience. Choose operators that emphasize conservation, respectful wildlife viewing, and safety. For quieter outdoor time, head to Hatta for mountain scenery, kayaking, and heritage villages about an hour from the city center. If you plan regional travel beyond Dubai to places like Abu Dhabi, plan your day trip logistics in advance.

Food, Alcohol, and Dining Culture

Culinary diversity and common expectations

Dubai is a global dining hub. You’ll find top international chefs, regional specialties, and excellent street food. Try Emirati specialties (dates, gahwa coffee, and slow-cooked meats), Lebanese mezze, Indian biryanis, and pan-Asian offerings. Business-lunch menus at fine restaurants often offer excellent value compared to dinner price points.

Alcohol rules and etiquette

Alcohol is served only in licensed venues (hotels, bars, restaurants) and consumption in public is prohibited. Public drunkenness, drunk driving, and rowdy behavior are illegal and strictly enforced. If you want to purchase alcohol for private consumption, check the licensing rules and local regulations before you buy.

Reservations and peak dining times

Book popular restaurants and beach clubs in advance — prime dinner slots and weekend brunches fill up quickly. If you desire a lively evening atmosphere, plan for later dinner times; locals and residents often dine after 9 p.m.

Culture, Laws, and Local Etiquette

Dress code and respectful appearance

Dubai is cosmopolitan and relatively liberal compared to some neighboring places, but respect for local customs is essential. Dress modestly when visiting religious sites and conservative neighborhoods: shoulders and knees covered, and headscarves for women when entering certain mosques. Swimwear is acceptable at beaches and pool areas, but cover up when leaving those spaces.

Public behavior, affection, and photography

Public displays of affection beyond holding hands may attract attention and can be penalized. Avoid kissing and overt intimacy in public, particularly around religious sites and government buildings. Always ask permission before photographing people; photographing government facilities, military installations, and certain public buildings is restricted.

Religious observance and Ramadan considerations

During Ramadan, eating, drinking, and smoking in public during daylight hours is considered disrespectful — non-Muslims are expected to comply in public spaces. Many restaurants operate behind screens or offer takeaway and special hours; check local guidance and be mindful of reduced business hours for some services.

LGBTQ+ travelers and discretion

Laws regarding same-sex relationships are strict. LGBTQ+ travelers should exercise discretion and avoid public displays that could attract attention. If in doubt about local norms, prioritize personal safety and privacy.

Safety, Emergencies, and Accessibility

General safety and low-crime environment

Dubai is widely regarded as safe for travelers, with low rates of violent crime and high standards of public security. Exercise normal travel caution: watch belongings in crowded areas, stay aware of surroundings at night, and avoid isolated spots after hours.

Emergency contacts and medical care

Emergency numbers are available for police, ambulance, and fire services. Keep local emergency numbers on hand and note the nearest hospital to your accommodation. If you have specific health needs, call ahead to hospitals or clinics to confirm language capability and specialty services.

For quick reference, the most commonly used emergency and support numbers include:

  • Police: 999
  • Ambulance: 998
  • Tourist security (non-urgent tourist matters): +971 800 4438

Accessibility and special needs

Dubai has made significant progress in accessibility with ramps, elevators, tactile paving, and accessible public transport in many modern venues. However, older neighborhoods and certain markets may be more challenging to navigate. Contact hotels and attractions in advance to confirm accessible facilities and any additional assistance.

Money-Saving Tactics and Practical Budgeting

When to splurge and where to save

Dubai’s range spans budget-friendly eateries to ultra-luxury experiences. Save by taking advantage of weekday business-lunch menus at high-end restaurants, look for hotel package deals in shoulder seasons, and use public transport for short hops. Consider booking attraction tickets online in advance for early-bird pricing and bundled discounts.

Deals, passes, and apps

Seasonal promotions and city passes can offer savings if you plan to visit many attractions. Local apps aggregate deals on dining and activities — they can deliver real bargains, but read terms and blackouts carefully. Itinerary Blueprints: Practical Day-By-Day Plans

3-day sample: Highlights for first-timers

Day 1: Downtown Dubai — Burj Khalifa observation (book early or choose a dining experience for a relaxed view), Dubai Mall (aquarium and promenade), evening fountain show and walk.

Day 2: Old Dubai and Creek — Al Fahidi Historical District, Dubai Museum/heritage houses, abra ride across the creek, spice and gold souks; late afternoon beach time at Jumeirah.

Day 3: Modern coast — Dubai Marina walk, Palm Jumeirah photo stops, optional Atlantis day pass or Aquaventure, and an evening dhow cruise dinner for a skyline perspective.

5-day sample: Adding depth and a desert excursion

Days 1–3: Follow the 3-day plan but allocate more time to Old Dubai and a food-focused evening in Deira.

Day 4: Desert safari with a reputable operator, focusing on conservation-minded experiences or a private sunset trip for photography.

Day 5: Museum of the Future, City Walk, and shopping or leisure time in a beach club; finish with a rooftop dinner overlooking the skyline.

Week-long: Regional expansion and cultural immersion

Use an extra two days to visit Abu Dhabi (sheikhly architecture and the Louvre Abu Dhabi), and Hatta for mountain scenery and outdoor activities. Plan these as full-day trips with early departures to maximize time.

Practical Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake: Underestimating travel time across the city

Treat Dubai like a long, linear city. Travel times can expand quickly during peak hours — schedule appointments and dinner reservations with buffer time.

Mistake: Failing to book ahead for popular spots

From brimming brunches to timed-entry attractions, Dubai runs on reservations. If a specific experience matters, secure it early.

Mistake: Ignoring local customs

Respectful dress and behavior reduce friction. Small acts — covering shoulders in heritage areas, declining public eating during Ramadan daylight hours — make a big difference to local goodwill.

Mistake: Assuming cash-only or limited card acceptance

Dubai is largely cashless-friendly. Use a contactless card where possible, but carry a small amount of dirhams for small vendors and tips.

How to Travel Responsibly and Respectfully

Dubai’s rapid growth coexists with local traditions and the environment. Travel responsibly by using refillable water bottles (public refill stations are common), avoiding single-use plastics where possible, supporting locally owned businesses, and choosing tour operators that follow ethical wildlife and land-management practices. When you visit heritage sites, follow signage and staff guidance to protect fragile structures.

FAQ

Do I need a visa to travel to Dubai?

Visa requirements depend on your nationality. Many visitors receive a visa on arrival, but some nationalities require pre-approved electronic visas. Check entry requirements well before departure and carry printed proof of approvals if needed.

Is Dubai safe for solo travelers and families?

Yes. Dubai is generally very safe for solo travelers and families. Standard precautions apply: secure valuables, avoid poorly lit or isolated areas late at night, and respect local laws and customs.

Can I drink alcohol in Dubai?

Alcohol is available in licensed venues such as hotels, bars, and specific restaurants, but drinking in public places is prohibited and public intoxication is illegal. Purchase and possession rules for alcohol can be strict — follow local regulations.

What should I wear when visiting religious or heritage sites?

Cover shoulders and knees at cultural sites and mosques. Women may be required to wear headscarves in some places. Carry a lightweight shawl for quick coverage when needed.
