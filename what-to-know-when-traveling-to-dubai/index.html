What to Know When Traveling to Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai's skyline is a global signal of modern ambition, but for first-time visitors the city can be as surprising as it is spectacular. Whether you arrive for business, a family holiday, or a stopover on a longer Middle East itinerary, the practical choices you make before and during your stay shape how smoothly your trip unfolds.

Short answer: Know the basics—visa rules, weather windows, local laws, and transport options—then layer in timing, neighborhood selection, and cultural etiquette to turn a good trip into a confident, enjoyable one. This post will walk you through what matters most, from arrival logistics and getting around to cultural norms, money matters, safety, and how Dubai fits into wider travel plans across the Gulf. You'll finish with actionable checklists and the local-savvy frameworks that let you travel like someone who’s visited before.

Saudi Travel & Leisure exists to give travelers that exact advantage: practical, insider knowledge framed in enjoyable storytelling so you can move beyond the postcard moments and plan an enriched, stress-free visit. Below I’ll provide the experience-driven details and step-by-step planning tools you need to arrive informed and leave ready to explore more of the region.

Why This Matters Before You Fly

Planning for Dubai is as much about logistics as it is about expectations. The right decisions—when to go, where to stay, and how to book popular experiences—save time and money and preserve the energy you want to dedicate to exploration. Dubai is full of micro-decisions that compound quickly: a dinner reservation on the wrong side of town during rush hour, a sold-out sunset ticket at Burj Khalifa, or a misunderstanding about dress code during Ramadan can turn a highlight into a hassle. The remainder of this article gives you practical pathways to avoid those traps.

Practical Arrival Information

Visa and Entry Requirements

Most travelers can obtain a visa on arrival, but rules vary by nationality and purpose of travel. Always verify your specific requirement before departure. If you need a pre-approved visa, apply through the UAE government portal or via your airline. For longer stays or work/travel that involves special permits, follow the host country's official procedures.

Airports, Transfers, and First Steps

Dubai has multiple airports serving different airlines and routes. Dubai International (DXB) is the main hub and the easiest gateway for most travelers. Expect efficient immigration and a good range of arrival services: currency exchange, SIM counters, tourism desks, and taxis. If you land at Dubai World Central (DWC), check transfer times carefully—it's farther from the city center.

On arrival, pick a reputable option for transport: the official airport taxi, a ride-hailing app, or pre-booked transfer from your hotel. Carry a printed or offline map with your hotel address and a local contact number; it’s a small step that prevents confusion if mobile data is slow during the busiest arrival fracas.

Health Screening and Medications

Dubai’s healthcare system is modern and widely accessible. If you rely on prescription medication, bring the prescription and, when possible, a doctor’s letter describing your condition and medications. Some medications that are routine elsewhere are controlled or require special approval in the UAE; verify before you travel. Pack a basic travel health kit for heat-related issues and common ailments.

Best Times to Visit and How Weather Shapes Your Plans

Dubai has two very different travel seasons that affect how you plan days and activities.

Cooler Months (October–April)

This is the peak travel window—temperatures are comfortable for outdoor dining, beach days, and desert outings. Expect higher prices and more crowded attractions, so book major experiences, dinners, and desert safaris in advance.

Hot Season (May–September)

Daytime temperatures can be extreme. Summer travel can still work if you prioritize air-conditioned attractions, early-morning or late-evening outdoor activities, and discounts. If you travel in this window, structure days around indoor cultural visits, mall-based experiences, and evening promenades.

Neighborhoods and Where to Stay

Choosing the right neighborhood is one of the single biggest levers for a better trip. Dubai is long and linear; traffic and speed of life vary from strip to strip.

Downtown, Business Bay, and DIFC

These areas put you close to the Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall, and high-end dining. They’re convenient if your trip centers on iconic attractions or business meetings.

Dubai Marina, JBR, and Palm Jumeirah

If you want beaches and a lively waterfront, consider the Marina and Jumeirah Beach Residence (JBR). Palm Jumeirah is resort-focused and great for a quieter, beach-resort stay, but note it’s farther from Downtown.

Old Dubai—Bur Dubai and Deira

To experience Dubai’s trading history, markets, and cultural streets, stay near the creekside. You’ll be close to souks and museums, and it’s a good counterpoint to the glass-and-steel neighborhoods.

Where to Base Yourself—A Simple Rule

Choose the neighborhood that minimizes travel time to your top three priorities. Because traffic can be heavy, it’s almost always smarter to be centrally located to the things you plan to do every day. If your itinerary is split between beaches and Downtown restaurants late at night, consider a split stay (two nights downtown, two nights on the Marina).

Getting Around: Transport Options and When to Use Them

Dubai’s transport system is efficient if you plan around it. The city was designed for cars, but high-quality public transit and app-based services make transit accessible.

Metro, Tram, and Buses

The Dubai Metro is modern, clean, and efficient for key corridors connecting to Downtown, Marina, and other hubs. It’s great for avoiding rush-hour traffic on popular routes. Trams and buses complement the metro in areas like the Marina and JBR.

Taxis and Ride-Hailing

Taxis are plentiful and affordable by global standards. Ride-hailing apps like Careem and Uber operate in the city and are easy to use for door-to-door travel. During peak times—Friday evenings and event nights—expect longer waits and higher fares.

Driving Yourself

Renting a car gives flexibility but means handling fast-paced local traffic and parking logistics. If your plans involve day trips outside the city (for example, Hatta or desert lodges), renting becomes more convenient. Check local driving laws and ensure your international driving permit (if required) is in order.

Short-Distance Options

For short promenade walks, the Marina and Kite Beach areas are pedestrian-friendly and have dedicated cycling tracks. For quick in-neighborhood runs, electric scooters and bikes are available through local apps.

Download essential local apps early—ticketing apps, ride-hailing, delivery, and map apps. If you prefer a checklist of the most useful apps to install before traveling, see the “Essential Apps” list below.

Money, Costs, and Tipping

Dubai is not always cheap, but costs vary widely depending on choices.

Currency and Cards

The dirham (AED) is stable and widely accepted. Credit and debit cards are accepted almost everywhere. For small purchases and taxi tips, carry some cash. If card machines offer a choice of currency, always choose AED for a better rate.

Typical Prices and Budgeting

Food, transport, and basic entertainment are comparable to Western cities. Luxury experiences—fine dining, premium beach clubs, and exclusive tours—add up quickly. For cost-saving: consider weekday business lunch menus, early-booked attraction bundles, and city passes.

Tipping Culture

Tipping is customary but discretionary. A small tip for taxi drivers and restaurant waitstaff is appreciated. Many high-end venues may include a service charge; check the bill.

Culture, Etiquette, and Legal Considerations

Dubai balances a cosmopolitan lifestyle with local customs and strict legal codes. Respect and situational awareness keep travel simple.

Dress and Public Behavior

Dress modestly in historic neighborhoods and places of worship: shoulders covered and knees concealed. Swimwear is appropriate at the beach and pool but not in malls or family areas. Public displays of affection should be conservative—holding hands is fine, kissing and more intimate gestures are not.

Photography and Privacy

Always ask permission before photographing people, particularly women and families. Photography of some government buildings and certain locations is restricted. Use common sense and respect privacy.

Alcohol and Nightlife

Alcohol is permitted in licensed venues like hotels, bars, and private clubs. Drinking in public or appearing intoxicated is illegal. If you plan to drink, keep it within licensed spaces and arrange private transport.

Drug and Prescription Laws

The UAE enforces strict drug laws—possession of illegal substances carries severe penalties. Some prescription drugs require prior approval or documentation. Always carry prescriptions and check whether your medications are permitted before travel.

Cultural Sensitivity During Ramadan

If you travel during Ramadan, respect local customs: avoid eating, drinking, or smoking in public during daylight hours. Many venues still serve non-fasting tourists discreetly, but you’ll find that timing and etiquette differ.

LGBTQ+ Considerations

Laws in the UAE restrict same-sex relations and public expression. Discretion is advised for LGBTQ+ travelers. Safety practices for all visitors include awareness of local law and low-profile behavior in public.

Major Attractions: Smart Visiting Strategies

Dubai’s headline attractions are spectacular—and very popular. Use timing and booking tricks to see more with less stress.

Burj Khalifa and Dubai Mall

Book timed tickets in advance and consider non-ticket options that still offer views—restaurants or lounges with minimum spends can be a cost-effective alternative to booked observation terraces. If you want the sunset from the tower, book months ahead for peak season.

Desert Safaris and Nature

Choose a reputable operator that prioritizes conservation and welfare for desert animals. Morning and sunset safaris offer cooler temperatures and better photography light. Overnight desert lodges provide a quieter desert experience compared with the same-evening tours.

Museums, Creek, and Old Dubai

Allot time to explore the creekside, take an abra (traditional boat), and wander the souks. These cultural experiences are compact—one half- or full-day can be enough to get a meaningful look at Dubai’s trading past.

Beach Clubs and Pools

Many beach clubs require reservations and have arrival windows; book ahead, especially on weekends. Ladies’ days and weekday deals can deliver excellent value.

Day Trips and Nearby Emirates

Dubai’s location makes it a good base for day trips. For cultural contrasts and more traditional life, consider a day trip to Abu Dhabi and its cultural sites, or head inland for Hatta’s mountain landscape. For planning day-trip logistics, see our piece on practical information for travel in the UAE.

You can also plan a day trip to Abu Dhabi if your schedule allows.

Dining and Food Culture

Dubai’s culinary scene reflects its multicultural population. From Emirati specialties to South Asian and high-end international cuisine, choices are abundant.

Timing and Bookings

Dining late is common; if you want a lively atmosphere, aim for later reservations. For high-demand restaurants, secure bookings well in advance or consider business lunch menus for better pricing and the same signature food.

Traditional and Local Flavors

Try Emirati dishes—majboos, luqaimat, and date-based desserts—and seek out family-run restaurants in older neighborhoods for authentic flavors. Use food trips to connect with culture: coffee rituals, date tastings, and spice markets tell stories about the region.

Safety, Security, and Emergency Preparedness

Dubai is generally safe, but situational awareness and travel insurance are essential.

General Safety

Violent crime is rare. Standard urban precautions—watch your belongings, be cautious with late-night travel in quiet areas—are sufficient. Familiarize yourself with emergency numbers and the location of your embassy or consulate.

Regional Risks and Travel Advisories

Regional tensions can affect travel unpredictably. Monitor reputable government travel advisories in the weeks before you travel, enroll in traveler notification systems if available, and have a contingency plan for schedule changes.

Travel Insurance

Choose a policy that covers medical evacuation, trip interruption, and activities you plan to do (like dune driving or water sports). Keep a digital and printed copy of your policy details.

Accessibility and Travel for People of Determination

Dubai has made important strides in accessibility, and many public spaces are accessible. That said, older quarters and some outdoor sites may still be challenging. If you need specific accommodations—wheelchair access, sensory considerations, or medical support—contact venues ahead of time. Sustainability and Responsible Travel in Dubai

Sustainability matters here. Dubai is investing in water stations to reduce plastic waste and promoting responsible desert tourism. When you visit, choose operators that limit environmental impact, avoid single-use plastics where possible, and respect local wildlife and desert flora.

Comparing Dubai To Other Gulf Destinations

Dubai often serves as an entry point into the Gulf. If you’re considering extending your trip, contrast Dubai’s modern cosmopolitan offerings with the more heritage-focused experiences in neighboring cities. For example, you might compare Dubai’s beachfront vibrancy with the waterfront culture of Jeddah in Saudi Arabia, or pair a Dubai excursion with a quieter desert cultural stay in AlUla for a different pace.

If your trip leans toward combining UAE and Saudi experiences, check the regional travel resources on practical information for travel in the UAE and then plan a follow-up visit to Riyadh or Jeddah depending on your interests.

Money-Saving Tactics and Timing

Dubai can be budget-friendly if you plan. Look for weekday discounts, business lunch menus, and early booking deals for attractions. If you travel during the off-peak months (summer), expect lower rates at many hotels—plan for heat-adjusted activities and enjoy a less crowded city for lower costs.

Packing and Practical Checklists

Packing smart makes time in Dubai simpler. The items below are practical essentials to get you through most situations.

  • Lightweight, breathable clothes for daytime; a light jacket or shawl for heavily air-conditioned interiors and evenings.
  • Swimwear for beaches and pools; cover-ups to use when moving through public spaces.
  • Comfortable walking shoes, sun protection, and a refillable water bottle.
  • Copies of prescriptions and necessary medical documentation.
  • A printed hotel address and emergency contact numbers.
  • An international power adapter and portable charger.

If you want a quick pre-departure checklist you can use at the airport, follow the short list below.

Quick Pre-Departure Checklist (one of two allowed lists)

  • Check visa validity and entry requirements for your nationality.
  • Secure travel insurance covering health and activity risks.
  • Pack prescription documentation and a basic health kit.
  • Pre-download essential apps and offline maps.
  • Book major attraction times and dinner reservations for peak evenings.

Essential Apps and Services

Below is a concise list of the most useful apps to download before departure for efficiency and local convenience.

  • Ride-hailing: Careem or Uber
  • Transit and maps: Google Maps and the Dubai Roads & Transport Authority app
  • Food and grocery delivery: Deliveroo, InstaShop
  • Currency and payments: Your bank’s travel app or Revolut/Monzo-type card
  • Emergency and embassy contact details stored offline

Common Mistakes Travelers Make—and How to Avoid Them

Travelers often assume Dubai is a pedestrian city or that everything is open late with no reservations needed. In reality, many hotspots require advance booking; traffic can be heavy and public transit doesn’t serve every corner. The fix is simple: pick a base near the activities you plan, book top-ticket experiences ahead of time, and build flexible time in your itinerary for transit delays.

Another common error is underestimating the heat or overpacking summer plans with outdoor activities. If you travel in the hot months, prioritize early morning or evening outdoor experiences and lean into air-conditioned indoor options for midday downtime.

Finally, don’t treat customs as optional. Simple acts—covering shoulders in certain neighborhoods, avoiding photographing people without permission, and being discreet during Ramadan—avoid uncomfortable encounters and keep your trip smooth.

How Dubai Fits Into a Broader Middle East Itinerary

Dubai is both a destination and a hub. It pairs naturally with Abu Dhabi and Oman for short extensions and makes a practical gateway for trips north to Qatar or south to Saudi Arabia. If your travel appetite includes visiting Saudi Arabia, whether for Riyadh’s modern cultural sites or AlUla’s desert history, plan logistics in advance: cross-border flights, visa rules, and cultural expectation differences. For travelers considering a multi-country Gulf itinerary, use Dubai as the high-energy leg balanced by quieter, culturally immersive stays elsewhere.

If you’d like inspiration for combining Dubai with Saudi destinations, consider building a follow-up stay in Riyadh for city heritage and modern museums, or a coastal extension to Jeddah for a different waterfront culture.

Final Practical Framework: How To Plan Your Perfect Dubai Trip

  1. Decide your travel window based on weather tolerance and budget. If comfortable with crowds, pick October–April; for lower rates and indoor-focused plans, choose summer.
  2. Book your flight and double-check visa rules for your nationality.
  3. Choose a neighborhood that minimizes commute time to your top three priorities.
  4. Reserve must-do experiences (Burj Khalifa sunset, desert safari, popular restaurants) before arrival.
  5. Download and set up local apps, secure travel insurance, and prepare prescriptions and documents.
  6. On arrival, use official taxis or ride-hailing, and always allow extra time for traffic.

This framework translates curiosity into a clear, repeatable planning process so you travel with confidence, conserve energy, and maximize the experiences you value most.

Conclusion

Dubai rewards travelers who arrive prepared: a little research and a few pre-booked reservations convert big-ticket experiences from stressful to seamless. Plan for weather, traffic, and cultural conventions, choose your base to match your daily priorities, and use local apps and public transit strategically. Above all, make time to balance skyline moments with the quieter cultural corners—Old Dubai, the creek, and Emirati cuisine—so your trip reflects both the city’s modern energy and its roots.

FAQ

Q: Do I need a visa to visit Dubai? A: It depends on your nationality. Many travelers receive a visa on arrival; others require advance authorization. Check official UAE visa information well before you fly and apply through airline or government portals when required.

Q: Is Dubai safe for solo travelers and families? A: Yes. Dubai is generally safe for solo travelers and families. Standard urban caution applies—secure your belongings, be mindful of local laws, and keep travel insurance that covers medical needs.

Q: Are credit cards widely accepted? A: Yes. Cards are accepted at most venues, and contactless payments are common. Carry small amounts of AED for taxis, tips, and smaller purchases.

Q: How should I handle medicines and prescriptions? A: Carry prescriptions and, if possible, a doctor’s letter describing your medication. Some drugs are restricted in the UAE; verify the status of your medications and, if needed, obtain prior approval.
