What's It Like Living in Riyadh

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Introduction

Riyadh is one of the fastest-changing capitals in the world. Once a compact Najdi town, it is now an eight-million-strong metropolis that is reshaping Saudi life under Vision 2030. Many people arrive expecting a single, monolithic experience; the reality is layered: the city is at once conservative and cosmopolitan, intensely modern in infrastructure yet deeply rooted in traditions that shape daily rhythm. If you want to move here, understanding those layers is the fastest path from surprise to confidence.

Short answer: Living in Riyadh feels like living in a city of contrasts where convenience and security meet cultural rules and social structure. Daily life is centered on family, work, and seasons—heat shapes hours, religion shapes routines, and modern development shapes opportunities. With preparation and cultural awareness you can live comfortably and discover a side of Saudi life few visitors see.

This post will walk you through what to expect day-to-day, where people choose to live, how to get started (visas, iqama, banking, driving), the realities of work, education, healthcare and social life, plus practical checklists and blueprints to plan a smooth relocation. I’ll connect cultural context to real logistical steps so you can move with confidence and make choices that fit your priorities.

What Riyadh Feels Like: The Big Picture

A City That Runs on Routine and Rhythm

Riyadh’s daily tempo is shaped by prayer times, the school day, and climate. Mornings and early evenings are the busiest for errands and commuting. In summer, a long midday lull is normal—shops and offices close and life shifts to air-conditioned interiors, then picks up late into the night. Expect a different social calendar: family-oriented gatherings, invitations to coffee and dates, and many activities organized around compound life or private clubs.

Modern Infrastructure, Fast Development

You’ll notice new towers, retail centres, and entertainment projects everywhere. Roads and highways are wide and the city is funneling significant investment into cultural venues, museums, and public spaces. The Riyadh Metro project and improved transit options are gradually changing how people move around, but for most expats a car remains the primary way to travel.

Conservative Society With Rapid Social Change

Riyadh is socially conservative relative to cities like Jeddah or Dubai, but the pace of change is significant. Laws and norms around gender interaction, dress, and public behaviour have evolved; women drive, entertainment venues have opened, and mixed-gender dining is increasingly common. Even so, respecting local customs is essential. The combination of longstanding tradition and visible reform is what gives Riyadh its unique character.

Where People Live: Choosing a Neighbourhood

Compounds, Villas, or Apartments: The Options

Expats often choose between gated compounds, villas in residential districts, or modern apartments in business districts. Each choice comes with trade-offs around social integration, convenience, and cost.

  • Compounds: Provide a “westernised” environment with pools, gyms, play areas and social events. Privacy and convenience make compounds especially attractive to families and newcomers. Expect high security at entry points and a community lifestyle that simplifies many daily chores.
  • Villas: Standalone villas in neighbourhoods like the Diplomatic Quarter offer space and a quieter residential feel. They can be more expensive but provide more immersion into local city life.
  • Apartments: Central locations in Olaya or Al Nakheel put you close to offices and malls. Apartments are practical for singles or couples who prefer urban living.

Where Expats Tend To Settle

Diplomatic Quarter (Al-Safat): Green boulevards, embassy presence, excellent jogging paths, and a calm atmosphere make the DQ the most sought-after area for professionals and diplomats. It’s walkable in places and offers a quieter pace.

Olaya and Sulaymaniyah: These areas are Riyadh’s commercial heart—skyscrapers, shopping and restaurants. Choose here if you want to be in the city’s professional buzz.

Al Nakheel and Al Malaz: Family-friendly with good access to schools and hospitals. Villas and larger apartments are common.

Northern residential districts (e.g., Al Bustan): Close to international schools and often chosen by families who want shorter commutes to Western-style education providers.

If you’re evaluating leases or compounds, plan a reconnaissance trip and visit properties in person. Compound waitlists and availability fluctuate—arriving with a flexible two-week buffer to secure housing pays off.

Getting Started: Visas, Iqama, and Legal Basics

Visas and Entry

Most long-term residents enter on a work visa sponsored by an employer. Tourist e-visas are available for many nationalities, but they do not permit work. Your employer will typically manage the work visa application, but it’s essential to understand timetables and which documents you must provide.

Iqama (Resident Permit)

The iqama is central to life in Saudi Arabia—no bank account, no phone contract, no long-term rental without it. Your employer should sponsor the iqama process. Expect to be fingerprinted, photographed, and to have medical screenings. Keep a digital and hard copy of your iqama at all times.

Driving License and Vehicles

Driving is the default way to move in Riyadh. If you hold a valid foreign driving licence you can usually convert it; requirements differ by nationality. Many expats buy cars soon after arrival—petrol is inexpensive and cars are practical for desert weekend trips. If you prefer not to drive, car services and drivers-for-hire are widely available.

Banking and Utilities

Opening a bank account requires an iqama, passport, employer letter, and proof of address. Local banks offer a range of services and international banks are present in the city. Setting up utilities (water, electricity, internet) is often arranged through your landlord or property management, but be prepared to handle some paperwork and initial deposits.

Housing Logistics: How to Secure a Place

Negotiating Rent and Contracts

Rents are typically quoted annually and some landlords ask for a single cheque for the full year. Negotiation is common—especially if you’re signing for multiple years or paying in full. Understand what the lease covers (maintenance, compound fees) and confirm responsibilities for repairs.

Deposits and Registration

Expect a security deposit and registration with local authorities or the property management company. If you’re living in a compound, many services are included in the community fee—confirm this.

Domestic Help and Employment Rules

Domestic workers are widely available, but they must be sponsored and employed legally. If you are considering hiring help, insist on proper contracts and ensure the worker’s status is legal to avoid steep fines and legal trouble. Compounds often maintain vetted lists of available domestic staff and drivers.

Work and Business Life

Employment Culture and Sectors

Riyadh is the Kingdom’s economic engine. Finance, energy, construction, healthcare, education, technology and government projects are major employers. The Vision 2030 reforms are driving growth in non-oil sectors, which increases demand for international expertise.

Expect a hierarchical work culture where formality and respect for seniority matter. Workplace hours can vary and are adjusted during Ramadan. Contract details—salary components, housing allowance, health insurance, annual leave and repatriation clauses—require careful review.

Work Permits and Sponsorship

Your employer will normally be your sponsor and manage necessary work permit paperwork. Keep copies of your labor contract and understand termination clauses and end-of-service benefits before you sign.

Networking and Professional Integration

Making professional connections is vital; local business councils, international chambers of commerce and industry conferences are excellent places to meet peers. Online expatriate groups and alumni networks also help find mentors and practical tips for settling in.

Education: Schools and Learning for Children

International Schools

Most expat families enroll their children in international schools where English is the primary language. Popular schools follow British, American, or IB curricula. These schools have limited places and waiting lists; secure admission early in the planning phase.

Costs and Considerations

Tuition can be a significant portion of a family’s budget. Check what the school covers (transport, lunches, extracurriculars) and whether employer packages include school allowances. Evaluate location relative to your home and the commute—traffic in Riyadh can make a short distance into a long trip.

Early Childhood and Activities

Nurseries, playgroups and extracurricular programs for music, sports and language are plentiful, often attached to compounds or private centres. For bilingual children, Arabic classes can help integrate into local culture over time.

Healthcare: Quality, Insurance & Practical Steps

Hospitals and Clinics

Riyadh has modern hospitals and private clinics with international-standard care in many specialties. A network of hospitals serves expatriates and Saudi nationals.

Health Insurance

Private health insurance is the norm for expats. Many employers include medical coverage in contracts; if not, arrange comprehensive private insurance that covers hospitalization, emergency evacuation if necessary, and coverage for any chronic conditions.

Pharmacy and Prescription Rules

Keep prescriptions and doctor letters for regular medication. Some drugs that are routine in other countries are restricted, so check customs rules before shipping medicines. Pharmacies are common and well-stocked for standard treatments.

Money Matters: Cost of Living and Salaries

Wages and Benefits

Expat packages often include tax-free salaries, housing allowances, and other perks depending on your role and employer. Compensation varies widely by sector and seniority. Public-sector and major private employers can offer very generous packages that include schooling, annual air tickets, and housing.

Day-to-Day Expenses

Groceries, domestic help and petrol are often cheaper than in many Western countries. Imported goods and luxury items reflect international pricing. Dining out and leisure at upscale venues can be costly but overall you can balance a comfortable life with sensible budgeting.

Banking and Transfers

Major banks facilitate international transfers. For large transfers or buying property abroad consult an advisor to plan taxes and reporting needs in your home country.

Social Life and Making Friends

The Compound Community

Compounds are social hubs. Many expats find their first and strongest friendships here. Social calendars revolve around barbecues, fitness classes, and community events—these are reliable ways to meet other newcomers.

Building Relationships Outside the Expat Bubble

Meeting Saudis and long-term residents requires patience and cultural awareness. Accepting invitations to family gatherings, showing respect for customs, and learning basic Arabic greetings can change how people engage with you. Foreign embassies, cultural institutes, and professional events are also good channels.

Nightlife, Dining, and Cafés

Dining culture in Riyadh is vibrant: local cuisine and international restaurants thrive. Cafés and coffee shops are important social spaces. Alcohol is not available legally, so socialising tends to center on food and coffee. For upscale entertainment, Riyadh’s development includes new cultural venues and restaurants that cater to a range of tastes.

Food, Markets, and Shopping

Supermarkets and Ingredients

You’ll find large hypermarkets and smaller specialty stores with international produce. Spices, rice, and ingredients from across Asia, Africa and the Middle East are readily available—useful if you cook at home frequently.

Souks and Local Markets

Traditional markets sell perfumes, incense, textiles and local crafts. Visiting the Deerah souk or Clock Tower area provides a sensory experience and an opportunity to practice polite bargaining and cultural exchange.

Malls and the Mall Culture

Malls dominate Riyadh’s social life in summer months and offer shopping, dining and family entertainment. They are air-conditioned refuges and convenient meeting spots—expect crowds on weekend evenings.

Entertainment, Sports, and Weekends

Outdoor Activities and the Desert

Desert escapes are classic Riyadh pastimes—camping, dune driving, stargazing, and off-road excursions are common. If you plan desert adventures, go with experienced guides, check weather conditions, and ensure your vehicle is prepared.

Sports and Gyms

Gyms, country clubs and golf courses are plentiful, with many offering family-friendly activities and classes. In recent years, public parks and outdoor spaces have expanded, offering more daytime options for families.

Cultural Venues and Events

Riyadh’s calendar is filling with concerts, art exhibitions and cultural festivals. Keep an eye on local listings for theatre, music, and seasonal events that reflect the city’s growing cultural scene.

Religion, Etiquette and Day-to-Day Conduct

Prayer Times and Business Closures

Five daily prayers structure public life. Many shops and offices briefly pause during prayer; some close for longer around noon. Scheduling meetings outside prayer times is respectful and practical.

Dress and Respectful Behaviour

Dress conservatively in public—loose, modest clothing for both men and women. Women are not legally required to wear an abaya in all contexts, but modest dress remains the norm. Avoid public displays of affection and loud behaviour.

Social Etiquette

Accept invitations and respond to hospitality with gratitude. Avoid sensitive political topics. When visiting private homes, modesty and deference to elders are valued. Learn a few Arabic phrases—simple greetings and words of thanks are always appreciated.

Women Living in Riyadh: Practical Realities

Greater Freedom, But With Cultural Nuance

Recent legal changes have broadened women’s mobility and participation in public life. Women drive, attend sporting events, and work across sectors. However, social expectations remain conservative in many settings—being informed and respectful of local norms yields the best experience.

Professional Opportunities

Many sectors actively recruit female professionals, and companies are increasingly creating inclusive workplaces. Networking with women’s professional groups and embassy events can accelerate integration.

Safety, Security, and Personal Well-Being

Low Crime, High Regulatory Risks

Riyadh has low crime rates and strong public security. However, legal consequences for breaking local laws can be severe—avoid drugs, watch your online posting, and understand local restrictions. Respecting laws and cultural norms is the surest way to stay safe.

Emergency Services and Consular Support

Keep emergency numbers and your embassy’s contact information handy. In a major emergency your consulate is the first line of support for paperwork, advice and repatriation assistance.

Practical Blueprints: How to Move and Settle Fast

Essential Documents Before You Move

  1. Passport (with at least six months validity), visa documents and multiple photocopies.
  2. Employment contract, sponsorship letters and iqama application documents (or company guidance).
  3. Medical records and prescriptions with corresponding doctor’s letters (translated where necessary).
  4. School records and transcripts for children, including vaccination certificates.
  5. Driver’s license, international driving permit (if applicable) and vehicle documents.
  6. Bank letters, proof of funds and any notarised documents required for work or residency.

Quick Two-Week Settling Checklist

  • Register with your employer and collect the iqama.
  • Open a bank account and set up mobile phone service.
  • Secure temporary accommodation if needed, then view longer-term housing.
  • Enrol children in school and collect school transport options.
  • Arrange health insurance and find a local GP/hospital.
  • Hire trustworthy domestic help only through legal channels if needed.

Weekends and Short Trips: Where To Go From Riyadh

Riyadh is an excellent hub for exploring the Kingdom. Plan short getaways that illustrate the variety of Saudi landscapes and experiences.

  • AlUla: For spectacular rock formations, ancient sites and a different cultural pace, book excursions to AlUla and its archaeological precincts—an essential contrast to the desert plains around Riyadh.
  • Jeddah: A short flight takes you to the Red Sea coast for historic coral architecture and seaside culture, more relaxed and cosmopolitan in social terms.
  • Taif: A highland retreat with cooler summer temperatures and seasonal flower farms offers a green break from Riyadh’s heat.
  • Eastern Province (e.g., Al Khobar/Dammam): If your work takes you east, the coastal cities combine business hubs with easy access to beaches and Bahrain by causeway.

Common Misconceptions and What People Worry About

“Is Riyadh Dangerous?”

By most metrics Riyadh is very safe. Violent crime is rare. The reality to manage is legal risk: unfamiliarity with local laws causes the majority of problems for newcomers. Educate yourself before you travel and follow local guidance.

“Will I Be Alone Socially?”

Many expats report an initial feeling of isolation; it diminishes quickly for those who are proactive. Compound life, international schools, professional networks and cultural events create real community. Expect relationship building to take time and reward curiosity.

“Can I Keep My Lifestyle?”

Yes—with adjustments. Home comforts are widely available and the city’s shopping and dining scene supports diverse diets and hobbies. The trade-offs are cultural constraints around behaviour, alcohol, and public displays. Many find the trade-offs acceptable given the security, salaries and ease of family life.

Money-Saving and Budgeting Tips

Housing choices and schooling are the largest line items. Negotiate packages that include housing or school allowances. Use local markets for groceries where possible, and learn which imported items cost more. For longer-term cost control consider compound living for bundled amenities and simpler transport planning.

Technology and Connectivity

Mobile data is fast and widely available. High-quality internet packages are sold through local providers and installed quickly once you have an address and iqama. VPN use is common but check legality and workplace policies before relying on any tool.

Cultural Integration Blueprint: A Simple Four-Step Approach

  1. Observe first: Learn the basic rhythms—dress norms, prayer times, gender-segregated spaces—and adapt your behaviour.
  2. Learn essential language: A few Arabic greetings and phrases open doors.
  3. Participate locally: Accept invitations, attend cultural events, and meet neighbours—small gestures build trust.
  4. Stay curious and respectful: Ask questions politely, show appreciation for hospitality, and avoid judgemental comparisons.

This framework helps you move from visitor to a resident who understands how to navigate cultural nuance.

Conclusion

Riyadh is a city that rewards preparation. If you plan carefully—securing the right documentation, choosing housing that fits your family, and investing time in understanding local customs—you’ll find a secure, comfortable and increasingly dynamic place to live. The city’s combination of modern amenities, investment-led growth, and deep-rooted culture makes it an exceptional base for careers and family life in the Gulf.

FAQ

Q1: How long does it take to get an iqama after arrival? A1: Timelines vary by employer and paperwork completeness. In many cases companies begin the process immediately and an iqama can arrive in a few weeks to a few months. Expect follow-up medical checks and fingerprint appointments in that window.

Q2: Is English widely spoken in Riyadh? A2: Yes—especially in workplaces, clinics and international schools. Many Saudis are bilingual or conversational in English. Nevertheless, learning basic Arabic phrases improves daily interactions and shows respect.

Q3: Can foreigners rent property directly? A3: Yes—foreigners can rent property. Contracts are private arrangements between landlord and tenant. Long-term residency logistics (iqama, bank account, utility setup) usually require employer support and proper documentation.

Q4: What are the best months for outdoor activities? A4: October through March provides cooler, pleasant weather for desert trips, hiking and outdoor social life. Summer months are extremely hot and require planning outdoor activities for early morning or late evening and prioritising air-conditioned venues.
