Where Is Muscat on a Map

Updated October 24, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Short answer: Muscat sits on the northern coast of Oman, hugging the Gulf of Oman at approximately 23.58889°N latitude and 58.40833°E longitude. It occupies a narrow coastal strip below the Hajar Mountains, with its main international gateway at Muscat International Airport (MCT) in Seeb.

This article explains exactly where Muscat is on a map and then builds outward: how to read modern and traditional maps to locate the city, how Muscat fits into the geography of the Arabian Peninsula and the wider Gulf region, practical travel routes from neighboring countries, and the neighborhood-level map markers every visitor should know. I’ll provide clear, repeatable steps for finding Muscat on digital and paper maps, explain the city’s strategic maritime position near the Strait of Hormuz, and give concrete planning advice for travelers coming from Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states.

As the KSA Travel Insider, my goal is to move you beyond a single dot on a map into a confident, map-smart traveler who can navigate logistics, choose the right arrival point, and stitch Muscat into a wider Gulf itinerary with ease.

Muscat’s Position on Different Map Types

On a world map, Muscat appears on the southeast margin of the Arabian Peninsula. Zoomed into a regional map of the Gulf, the city is visible as Oman's coastal hub, facing across the water toward the Strait of Hormuz and Iran to the north-northeast.

On a topographic map the Hajar Mountains form a dramatic backdrop to Muscat—rising just to the south of the urban strip—so the city reads as a linear coastal zone squeezed between mountain slopes and the sea. On satellite imagery you’ll see the pale-white roofs of Muscat’s neighborhoods, the green ribbons of irrigated palms and gardens, and the crescent-shaped harbors of Old Muscat and Muttrah.

Why Those Coordinates Matter for Travelers

Coordinates are practical beyond curiosity. Enter them into a GPS device or mapping app and you’ll get accurate routing to gates, hotels, or specific landmarks. When you’re arranging cross-border travel from the Gulf—whether flying from Dubai or driving from a neighbouring emirate—use the coordinates to confirm arrival points, airport locations, or the exact position of a port or fort.

Muscat in Regional Context: The Arabian Peninsula and the Gulf

Muscat and the Arabian Peninsula

Muscat anchors Oman’s northern coastline and serves as the country’s political and economic heart. The city stands at the eastern end of the Arabian Peninsula, where the coastline curves toward the Arabian Sea. To the south and west, Oman opens into vast interior deserts and mountainous regions; to the north are the waters of the Gulf of Oman and beyond that the strategic Strait of Hormuz.

This geographic location shaped Muscat’s maritime history: it was a key port on trade routes linking Arabia, East Africa, Persia, India, and beyond. On modern maps, that maritime legacy shows up as a concentration of harbors and historic forts clustered around the natural harbor of Muttrah and the fortified headlands of Old Muscat.

Muscat and the Gulf Region

Muscat is one of the major urban centers in the Gulf region and sits alongside other influential Gulf capitals. Look at a regional map and you’ll see Muscat southeast of the major trading hubs of the UAE. Its proximity to the UAE makes it a natural connection point for regional itineraries: travelers frequently combine stays in Muscat with visits to Dubai, Abu Dhabi, or other Gulf states.

For regional planning and comparative routes, consult useful regional travel resources and advice on planning travel within Saudi Arabia and wider regional Gulf travel resources. Those pages help frame Muscat as both a standalone destination and a node in a richer Gulf circuit.

The Strait of Hormuz and Maritime Significance

From any maritime or strategic map, Muscat sits near the mouth of the Persian Gulf’s southern approaches. The Strait of Hormuz—through which a significant portion of the world’s oil shipments pass—lies to the northwest. That proximity has influenced Muscat’s development as a port city and underscores its strategic importance on nautical charts and shipping maps.

Finding Muscat on Different Mapping Platforms

How to Locate Muscat on Google Maps and Apple Maps

  1. Open the app and enter “Muscat” or the exact coordinates 23.58889, 58.40833. The pin will land near Old Muscat and the Muttrah Corniche.
  2. For routing, set the destination as “Muscat International Airport (MCT)” if you are flying in, or a specific landmark like “Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque” for downtown arrivals.
  3. Use satellite view to see the coastal outline, port, and the Hajar ridgeline immediately to the south.

These apps will give you live traffic data, estimated travel times, and public-transport options (where available). If you are planning a cross-border drive from neighboring states, apps also help estimate border crossing times and likely routes.

Offline and Specialty Maps

If you prefer offline navigation, Maps.me and OsmAnd are reliable choices. They accept coordinate input and provide turn-by-turn offline routing—very useful if you expect to travel beyond well-covered urban zones or if you want to preserve mobile data.

For historical or nautical mapping, nautical charts and older colonial-era maps reveal Muscat’s role as a harbor over centuries. These are less useful for day-to-day travel but invaluable for understanding how the city grew along its natural harbor and the location of historic forts.

Paper Maps and Reading Coastal Topography

On a paper map, identify Muscat by locating the long curve of Oman’s northern coastline and then finding the cluster of harbors and jetties that mark Muttrah and Old Muscat. The Hajar Mountains will appear almost immediately south of the shoreline, and main roads run west and northwest along the coast toward Sohar and the interior valleys.

When you read a paper map, let topography guide planning: mountain passes and wadis influence travel times far more than straight-line distances. A short measured distance on a flat map may take far longer to traverse when mountain roads and coastal inlets are involved.

Neighborhood-Level Map Guide: What to Pin in Muscat

Old Muscat and Muttrah: The Historic Harbor

Old Muscat and the adjacent Muttrah Corniche are the most map-dense parts of the city for visitors. On any map, pin these locations first:

  • Al Alam Palace and the twin Portuguese forts (Al Jalali and Al Mirani) in Old Muscat
  • Muttrah Souq and Muttrah Corniche
  • The harbor and dhow moorings that define the coastline

These landmarks cluster tightly on coastal maps, and walking between them is straightforward if you’re staying in the Old Town or nearby hotels.

The Modern City: Ruwi, Al Khuwair, and Qurum

Moving west from Old Muscat you’ll find the modern commercial districts. On a city map, these neighborhoods are laid out with broader roads and newer developments:

  • Ruwi and Al Khuwair are business and commercial hubs with hotels, banks, and shops.
  • Qurum and Shatti al Qurum are coastal neighborhoods with beaches, parks, and larger hotel complexes.

If you plan to stay for convenience and comfort, choose lodging in these zones: they’re well connected and map-friendly for rapid access to restaurants and services.

Seeb and Muscat International Airport

Seeb, northwest of central Muscat, is where Muscat International Airport sits. On maps, the airport’s long runways are an unmistakable feature and help orient travelers arriving by air. Seeb also includes coastal suburbs, industrial zones, and the port of Mina Qaboos.

Express Coastal Markers for Your Map

When you create a personal map for Muscat, mark the following nodes to anchor your itinerary planning: Old Muscat, Muttrah Souq, Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque, Royal Opera House, Qurum Beach, Muscat International Airport, and the major hotel strip along Shatti al Qurum.

Practical Steps: How to Use a Map to Plan Your Muscat Visit

Step-by-Step: From Airport to City Center

  1. Look up Muscat International Airport on your map using coordinates or airport code MCT.
  2. Identify your hotel or landmark destination on the map and check the driving distance and estimated transit time. Central Muscat is typically 25–40 minutes from the airport, depending on traffic and your destination.
  3. Note the coastal highway (generally the Sultan Qaboos Street corridor) that connects the airport to the city center—this is the most reliable route for taxis and private transfers.

These steps apply whether you arrive late at night or early morning; mapping apps update routes in real time and show typical taxi fares or ride-hailing estimates.

How to Map an Efficient 48–72 Hour Stay

When you have limited time, use a simple mapping principle: cluster activities by neighborhood. For example, spend one day in Old Muscat and Muttrah (harbor, forts, souq), a second day across the Qurum and Shatti al Qurum stretch (beach, museums, opera), and a third day for a longer drive south into the Hajar foothills or to the nearby beach towns.

Build your itinerary directly on the map by drawing simple concentric circles or area outlines that reduce transit times and limit backtracking.

Mapping for Day Trips and Mountain Routes

If you plan a day trip into the Hajar Mountains—Wadi Shab or Wadi Bani Khalid, for example—use mapping tools to verify road conditions and driving times. Mountain roads can be narrow and winding; the map’s elevation contour lines and satellite view will help you understand terrain and likely driving speeds.

Cross-Border Travel: Connecting Muscat with Saudi Arabia and the Gulf

Flying to Muscat: Regional Hubs and Flight Times

Muscat is well served by direct flights from major Gulf hubs. If you’re traveling from Riyadh, Jeddah, or Dammam, expect a flight time roughly around two hours from major Saudi airports. From Dubai, the flight takes about an hour; from Abu Dhabi slightly more than an hour. LCC and full-service carriers run multiple daily services, and routes extend across the Gulf and into Europe and Asia.

For practical planning about cross-border itineraries and schedules, review regional travel connections and up-to-date transit advice on travel connections through the UAE and specific pages for flights and drives from Dubai and Abu Dhabi connections. These resources clarify typical flight lengths, recommended transfer times, and how to combine visits across Gulf capitals.

Driving Into Oman: Practical Notes

Driving into Oman from neighboring Gulf states is feasible via designated border crossings. Maps will show the official road links and the easiest driving corridors. If you plan a road trip, study the map for the following:

  • Border crossing locations and official hours (these change; confirm before you drive)
  • Road types and the presence of well-maintained highways versus two-lane rural roads
  • Distances and expected driving durations—mountains and customs checks add time

Always confirm visa requirements for land entry. For every cross-border drive, mark fuel stations and service points along your route on the map—some stretches are long, and services can be sparse.

Overland Alternatives: Ferries and Sea Routes

There are limited ferry connections in the region. For most travelers, the easiest sea-based option is private yacht or dhow charters departing local marinas. If your travel plan involves combining Muscat with ports in the UAE or Iran, consult maritime schedules and port maps in advance.

For planning introductions to travel across the Gulf by sea and air, also look into advice about planning travel from Qatar and transit options via Kuwait, which provide contextual tips for moving between states.

Mapping Safety, Borders, and Practical Concerns

Borders, Visas, and Map Markers for Official Points

When you mark a route that crosses international borders, add the official crossing points and set reminders to check opening hours and visa-processing rules. Maps rarely update visa rules, so consider each border crossing on your map as tentative until you confirm official entry requirements via government sites or embassies.

For Gulf-region travelers, many nationalities can obtain an eVisa for Oman or can receive a visa on arrival depending on passport and residency status. Confirm the latest entry rules before you travel and mark the nearest consulate or embassy on your map for contingency planning.

Weather and Road Conditions: Interpreting Map Clues

Maps with satellite imagery can reveal seasonal risks. Dry riverbeds (wadis) and flat expanses can flood during rare but intense rain events. Pin potential escape routes and alternative roads on your map, and avoid planning single-route itineraries in mountain areas where landslides or sudden weather could isolate you.

Emergency Services and Mapping Vital Locations

On any practical map for travel, mark hospitals, police stations, and emergency numbers. Muscat’s main hospitals and emergency services are concentrated near Ruwi and Seeb; make sure your map highlights these points in a different color for quick reference.

Map Tools and Apps I Recommend

  • Use Google Maps for live transit lines and the best general routing and traffic estimates.
  • Use Maps.me or OsmAnd for offline navigation in remote mountain or desert areas.
  • Use satellite-view layers to preview coastal approaches and parking areas around popular sites.

These app recommendations are pragmatic: pick one online service for arrival day routing and one offline app for mountain drives or areas with poor mobile coverage.

How Muscat Appears on Different Scales of Map: From Global to Street Level

Global Scale: Muscat as a Node in World Trade

On a world map, Muscat’s position is defined by maritime access and proximity to the Indian Ocean shipping lanes. It’s south of the Iranian coastline and east of the United Arab Emirates. For travelers mapping broader routes across Asia, Africa, and Europe, Muscat is often a waypoint between the Arabian Peninsula and South/Southeast Asia.

National Scale: Muscat Within Oman

On maps of Oman, Muscat is the northern population cluster distinct from other Omani governorates. The city forms a dense coastal ribbon from the Old Town eastwards, with Seeb and the airport to the northwest. Major arterial roads connect Muscat to Sohar in the west and coastal towns to the east.

City Scale: Streets, Souqs, and the Coastal Corniche

At street-map scale, the Muttrah Corniche and Old Muscat’s pedestrian streets are easy to identify. Look for the curved harbor and the souq’s laneways leading inland. City-scale maps are indispensable for walking itineraries, taxi pickups, and locating historical sites that don’t stand out on large-scale maps.

Practical Itinerary Maps: Sample Day Plans and How to Layer Them

One-Day Map Plan: Old Muscat and Muttrah Walk

Pin a compact walking circle that begins at the Muttrah Corniche, proceeds through the Muttrah Souq, up to Al Jalali and Al Mirani forts, and finishes at Al Alam Palace and the waterfront. This short loop minimizes taxi travel and maximizes time at mapped attractions.

Two- or Three-Day Map Plan: Add Museums and the Coast

For additional days, add the Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque to a second-day map, along with the Royal Opera House and Qurum Beach. Use the map to plan meals and rest breaks so the day’s walking distance remains comfortable. On a third day, map an excursion into the nearby mountains or to coastal towns east or west of Muscat.

Common Mapping Mistakes Travelers Make and How to Avoid Them

  • Mistake: Relying on straight-line distances. Reality: Roads, mountains, and border formalities add time. Always check route distances using your mapping app's driving mode.
  • Mistake: Assuming all services are available along the route. Reality: Some coastal stretches and mountain roads have limited fuel, food, and phone coverage—pin service stations on your offline map.
  • Mistake: Not confirming coordinates for meeting points. Reality: Give exact coordinates or a widely known landmark when arranging pick-ups or drop-offs—many parts of Muscat have similar-sounding street names.

Map-Based Logistics: Currency, Language, and Service Points

When you map out Muscat, mark practical service points such as ATMs, currency exchange offices, and major grocery stores. English signage is common in tourist zones and commercial districts, but map labels in Arabic may be the official names, so double-check transliterations. Mark the nearest branch of your bank or a reliable international ATM for quick cash access.

For group travel, create a shared Google Map where everyone can add pins for accommodations, meeting points, and points of interest. This shared map approach reduces missed rendezvous and ensures everyone is literally looking at the same plan on their devices.

FAQ

Where exactly is Muscat located in terms of latitude and longitude?

Muscat’s coordinates are approximately 23.58889°N latitude and 58.40833°E longitude. Enter those into any mapping app or GPS device to go straight to the city center.

How long does it take to fly to Muscat from major Gulf hubs?

Flight times are short: roughly one hour from Dubai or Abu Dhabi and around two hours from most major Saudi airports. Always check current schedules, as frequency and carriers change seasonally.

What is the main airport code for Muscat and where is it on the map?

Muscat International Airport is coded MCT and is located in Seeb, northwest of central Muscat. Its long runways are clear on satellite maps and serve as the main gateway for international travelers.

Which map apps should I download before traveling to Muscat?

Use Google Maps for live routing and traffic, and download an offline app like Maps.me or OsmAnd for mountain drives or areas with limited mobile coverage.
