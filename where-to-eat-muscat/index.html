How Muscat Eats: Food Culture and What Makes the City Special

MuscatГўВҖВҷs cuisine evolves from three converging forces: its maritime geography, centuries of trade across the Indian Ocean, and Omani hospitality traditions that center around communal meals. That combination produces food that is at once simple and richly spiced: slow-roasted meats and rice for celebrations, intricate seafood preparations for everyday lunches, and strong, sweet tea culture for social hours.

The maritime influence

MuscatГўВҖВҷs location on the Gulf of Oman means fish and seafood shape local menus. Hamour (grouper), kingfish, prawns and lobster are staples. Fresh catch is often grilled whole, charred lightly and finished with lemon, or simmered in spiced curries that show Indian and East African influence. If you like seafood, Muscat will reward early risers who visit the fish market or book a beachfront restaurant that lets you choose that dayГўВҖВҷs catch.

Trade routes and spice layers

Omani cuisine absorbed flavors from India, East Africa and the Persian Gulf via trade. You will taste cardamom, saffron, cumin and coriander in rice dishes, and dried lime (loomi) is a distinctive acidic note found in many savory plates. The use of coconut milk in some coastal dishes also nods to cross-ocean exchanges, while frankincense appears more often as cultural signifier than ingredient.

Traditional Omani dining is shared. ShuwaГўВҖВ”lamb or goat slow-cooked for 24ГўВҖВ“48 hours in an underground pitГўВҖВ”is served on large trays for groups, often at weddings and national celebrations. Understanding these practices will make your meals more respectful and enjoyable.

Must-Know Omani Dishes and What To Order

Knowing the key local dishes will help you order confidently and create memorable meals.

Shuwa and slow-roasts

Shuwa is the emblematic festive dish: marinated lamb or goat wrapped and buried in an earth oven, emerging intensely tender and smoky. ItГўВҖВҷs not an everyday menu item in all restaurants, but places that serve shuwa do it with pride; ask when itГўВҖВҷs on the menu and consider sharing a tray for the full experience.

Rice-based platters: Makbous, Mashuai, and Zurbian

Makbous (also makbus) is spiced rice with meat and vegetables, related to kabsa and qabuli. Mashuai is a lemony kingfish with rice, often a coastal favorite. Chicken or lamb zurbian (similar to biryani) reflects Mughlai influence and is commonly found in restaurants serving family-style meals.

Harees and thareed

Harees is a porridge-like wheat dish mixed with meat, comforting and often served at breakfast or special occasions. Thareed is bread stewed in a meat or fish sauce until it softens ГўВҖВ” very hearty and best shared.

Mishkak and street skewers

Mishkak are char-grilled skewers, often sold in evening markets or roadside joints. TheyГўВҖВҷre marinated in spices and garlic and served with tamarind or chili sauce. Perfect as a quick taste of local grilling tradition.

Camel and regional specialties

Camel meat appears on menus for adventurous diners and is usually served as curry or grilled cuts; itГўВҖВҷs lean and more textured than beef. DonГўВҖВҷt miss halwa, a fragrant, sticky sweet flavored with rosewater and saffron, commonly bought as a box to take home.

Coffee, tea and dates

Omani coffee tends to be light and aromatic, sometimes scented with rosewater or saffron. Karak tea ГўВҖВ” cardamom, milk and sugar ГўВҖВ” is more robust and ubiquitous in cafГғВ©s. Dates serve as both snack and cultural offering.

Dining Etiquette and Cultural Notes

Respectful behavior enhances local hospitality and smooth service.

Invitations, sharing and receiving food

If sharing a platter, use right-hand customs for passing and eating where appropriate. When offered coffee, a gentle hand gesture signals whether you want a refill; covering the cup politely declines.

Ramadan and religious timings

If your visit overlaps with Ramadan, be mindful that many restaurants operate different schedules; public eating during daylight hours is discouraged out of respect. Hotels often maintain food services for non-fasting guests, but check policies in advance.

For vegetarians and vegans, Indian restaurants and many mezze-style dishes will be your best resource. Vegetarian Omani versions exist but are less common in purely traditional menus.
