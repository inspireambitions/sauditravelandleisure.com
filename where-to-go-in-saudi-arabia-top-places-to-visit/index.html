Where to Go in Saudi Arabia: Top Places to Visit

Updated September 29, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Indexable Saudi guide

Introduction

Saudi Arabia has been quietly remaking itself into one of the most varied travel destinations on earth. From the carved Nabataean facades of Hegra to red-rock canyons, lush mountain terraces, and a Red Sea coastline full of turquoise atolls, the Kingdom now offers both remarkable sights and seamless infrastructure for curious travelers. The pace of tourism growth has been rapid: new sites, curated visitor experiences, and improved domestic connections make planning a trip easier than ever—if you know which places serve your interests and how to structure your time.

Short answer: If you want history and archaeology, head to AlUla and Hegra; for dramatic desert panoramas and hiking, the Edge of the World and Wadi Al Disah deliver; the Red Sea coast (Jeddah, Umluj and the atolls) is best for diving and islands; and the Asir highlands around Abha offer cool mountain scenery and tribal culture. Use an interest-led framework—heritage, nature, coast, mountains—to choose a compact route, then balance travel time with a few overnight stays to experience local life. For up-to-date entry rules and safety considerations, always consult the essential travel guidelines for the Kingdom before you finalize any bookings.

This post will map every decision you’ll face when choosing where to go in Saudi Arabia. I’ll walk you through the major regions, link specific activities to the best places to do them, unpack transport and seasonality, give itinerary blueprints you can adapt, and share practical checklists that ensure your trip is culturally respectful and logistically simple. My goal is to provide the blueprint for an unforgettable Saudi adventure—one that pairs deep cultural context with pragmatic planning so you can travel with confidence.

Why Saudi Arabia Should Be On Your Map Now

Saudi Arabia’s tourism offering is still relatively fresh, which means many remarkable sites remain quieter than comparable attractions elsewhere. The Kingdom is investing heavily in heritage-led tourism and sustainable coastal development, while historic urban centres are being restored and reopened as living cultural quarters. This combination of large-scale infrastructure and visible preservation efforts means you can experience world-class museums and hotels alongside authentic markets and local festivals.

Timing matters: the most comfortable months for travel are late October through early April when temperatures are mild across the country. Summer can be scorchingly hot in lowland and desert areas, though the Asir highlands and some seaside spots are more bearable. Seasonal events—like the AlUla season, Riyadh Season, and multiple local festivals—often coincide with curated exhibitions, concerts and food pop-ups. Aligning your visit with one of these periods can amplify the cultural experience, but it also increases demand for lodging, so book early.

How to Decide Where to Go in Saudi Arabia

Choosing destinations within Saudi Arabia is easiest if you start with one of three planning frameworks: (1) interest-based, (2) time-based, or (3) mobility-based.

  • Interest-based: pick a dominant theme—archaeology and heritage, desert and geology, diving and marine life, or mountain culture—and build around the best region for that theme.
  • Time-based: with 3–5 days prioritize a single region (Riyadh or Jeddah + a nearby day trip); with 7–10 days combine two regions (e.g., AlUla + Red Sea); with 14+ days explore three or more corners of the Kingdom.
  • Mobility-based: if you plan to self-drive, prioritize road-friendly routes like the western corridor from Jeddah to AlUla; if you prefer domestic flights, use Riyadh and Jeddah as hubs to reach far-flung areas quickly.

Below is a fast, high-level map to help you pair themes with the right region.

  • Fast map: Top regions at a glance
    • AlUla / Madinah region — Nabataean tombs, sandstone canyons, curated hospitality experiences
    • Red Sea Coast (Jeddah, Umluj, Farasan Islands) — historic port culture, snorkeling, diving
    • Riyadh & central plateau — museums, Diriyah, the Edge of the World
    • Asir / Abha — terraced farmland, mountain villages, cool climate
    • Eastern province & Al Ahsa — sprawling oases, limestone caves, date-farming culture
    • Northern and desert routes — Wadi Al Disah, Tabuk landscapes, Hail rock art

Regions and Top Destinations (Detailed)

Northwest: AlUla and Hegra (Mada’in Salih)

AlUla sits at the intersection of archaeology, dramatic desert scenery, and carefully managed visitor experiences. Hegra (also known as Mada’in Salih) is the Kingdom’s first UNESCO World Heritage Site and showcases tombs cut into the sandstone cliffs by the Nabataeans—the same culture behind Petra. What sets AlUla apart is the wider landscape: sweeping canyons, sculpted rock formations, oasis settlements and intimate luxury camps.

Visiting: Hegra requires timed entry and you’ll get the most from a guided tour that explains the architecture and funerary inscriptions. Nearby attractions—AlUla Old Town’s mud-brick lanes and Maraya (the mirrored concert hall)—are best visited with a local guide or through curated experiences that often require reservations. If your trip centers on heritage, allocate at least two full days in AlUla to see Hegra, the old town, and a scenic drive through Wadi AlUla.

Practical note: Many of the premier visitor sites around AlUla operate within private or managed zones; some attractions require a hotel or tour reservation for access. Book early, particularly if you plan to stay within the protected visitor areas.

Central Plateau: Riyadh, Diriyah, and the Edge of the World

Riyadh is a city of contrasts: gleaming new museums, skyscrapers, and restored historic precincts. Just outside the capital, Diriyah (the At-Turaif district) has been dramatically restored and offers a window into Najdi architecture and the early history of the Saudi state. For natural spectacle, the Edge of the World (Jebel Fihrayn) provides vertiginous views from sandstone escarpments that fall away to plain and horizon.

Visiting: Combine a few days in Riyadh to visit the National Museum, Masmak Fort, and contemporary galleries, with a half-day or full-day trip to the Edge of the World (best at sunset). Diriyah’s restored Salwa Palace and museums make for a full afternoon. For photo opportunities and a short trek, the Edge requires a 4x4 for much of the access—join an organized excursion if you’re not comfortable with desert navigation.

Red Sea Coast: Jeddah, Umluj, and the Atolls

Jeddah’s historic Al Balad district is a cultural centre built from coral-stone houses, bustling souks and restored mansions such as Nasseef House. The broader Red Sea coast is where Saudi Arabia’s marine wealth is easiest to access: Umluj’s atolls and offshore islands offer white sand, clear water, and world-class snorkeling and diving. Unlike more crowded dive destinations, many Red Sea sites here are quieter and less developed, which benefits divers and marine life alike.

Visiting: In Jeddah, explore Al Balad on foot, visit local art galleries, and enjoy the corniche at sunset. For snorkeling and diving, consider boat-based island-hopping excursions from Umluj or boat charters from Yanbu. Sustainable resort developments are underway on selected islands, but most of the coastal waters remain remarkably untouched—book reputable local operators who follow reef protection practices.

I also recommend browsing activities and attractions across the Kingdom to match the Red Sea’s marine options to your skill level and interests: whether you’re an experienced diver seeking walls and wrecks or a casual snorkeler after calm shallows.

Southwest: Asir Highlands, Abha, Rijal Almaa, and Jabal Sawdah

The Asir region around Abha and Khamis Mushait feels different from the rest of Saudi Arabia: cool, green and terraced. Mountain villages such as Rijal Almaa with their multi-storey block houses and vibrant door paintings give a rare glimpse of traditional highland architecture and tribal cultural expression. Jabal Sawdah, one of the Kingdom’s highest peaks, offers hikes and panoramic valleys.

Visiting: The Asir region is excellent for trekking, market exploration, and experiencing local hospitality. Roads can be narrow and at times rough; a cautious driver and an awareness of seasonal weather are important. Spending two to three nights gives the time needed to explore orchards, villages, and the mountain roadscapes.

Eastern Province: Al Ahsa Oasis and Al Qarah

Al Ahsa is a UNESCO-listed oasis, an expansive palm landscape fed by springs and dotted with historic villages, canals and date farms. Al Qarah’s limestone outcrops and honeycombed caves present an arresting limestone landscape near the palm farms and small settlements where craftwork and local foods reflect the oasis economy.

Visiting: Al Ahsa is an opportunity to slow the pace of travel—watch how irrigation, date cultivation, and village life knit together. Combine a day visiting the oasis and its forts with evenings exploring local cafés and quiet markets.

North and Desert Routes: Wadi Al Disah, Tabuk, and Hail Rock Art

The northwest holds canyon networks and sandstone columns that feel cinematic—Wadi Al Disah’s narrow gorge with high sculpted walls is a compelling place for exploratory hikes and photography. Hail Province offers millennia of rock art panels that track the human presence across millennia—petroglyphs of animals and people remain visible in many sites.

Visiting: Many of these northern and desert routes are best reached by road and can require long drives between fuel stops. Break up travel with overnight stays in Tabuk or small desert camps, and prioritize fuel and water planning.

Volcanic Landscapes and Craters: Al Wahbah

Al Wahbah Crater is a near-perfect circular depression on a volcanic lava field—an evocative stop if you’re routing between Taif and other western cities. While descending into the crater is often discouraged due to difficulty and, in some accounts, toxic gas, the rim offers dramatic photos and a sense of scale that few other geological features provide.

Visiting: Treat the crater as a scenic stop rather than a place for a strenuous descent. Nighttime camping in the surrounding lava fields can be memorable if you come prepared for cooler temperatures and desert winds.

Religious Cities: Makkah and Madinah (Access Considerations)

Makkah and Madinah remain the most visited religious cities in the region but access is restricted: only Muslims are permitted entry to the holy precincts of Makkah and certain areas of Madinah. For non-Muslim travelers, planning revolves around nearby cities and pilgrimage-related markets; Muslim travelers should plan for potentially crowded periods such as Ramadan and Hajj.

Things To Do: Activities and Adventures

Saudi Arabia’s breadth of landscapes unlocks a wide range of activities: archaeological tours, canyon hikes, desert camping, reef diving, cultural workshops, and urban food trails. If you’re building an itinerary, pick two to three activity types and match them to the regions above so each day offers meaningful experiences without frequent, costly transfers.

  • For archaeology and heritage, book guided tours in AlUla that explain Nabataean practices and the archaeology of Hegra.
  • For diving, choose operators along the Red Sea coast with strong reef protection policies and clear safety records.
  • For desert and canyon routes, prioritize morning or late afternoon hikes during cooler hours and use guided 4x4 services for remote roads.
  • For cultural immersion, spend time in restored urban quarters like Diriyah and Al Balad where workshops, galleries and traditional cafés offer direct insight into local crafts and cuisine.

Where to Stay: Accommodation Options and Booking Strategy

The accommodation landscape in Saudi Arabia now ranges from large international hotels to boutique heritage stays and desert camps. In heritage zones such as AlUla, some iconic structures and exclusive camps operate within managed properties and require reservations for both lodging and site access. Coastal areas offer a mix of city hotels and remote island resorts; mountain regions have smaller inns and guesthouses with a local flavour.

Booking strategy:

  • In high-demand seasons or for unique properties (heritage hotels, desert camps), reserve at least 3–6 months ahead.
  • For city stops, choose central hotels to minimize transit time to museums and nightlife.
  • If your plans depend on access to private visitor zones, confirm whether your hotel reservation grants site entry.

Food and Drink: What To Eat and Where To Experience It

Saudi cuisine is regional and generous: in the west you’ll find seafood-rich dishes in Jeddah; the central Najd area features hearty rice-and-meat plates like kabsa and gereesh bil laban; the south offers spiced, mountain-flavoured dishes. Coffee culture is strong and dates are an almost-constant accompaniment. Eating with locals—at a family-run restaurant or a tightly packed café—is one of the fastest ways to learn about social norms and taste regional specialities.

Culinary experiences:

  • Try a Najdi meal in Diriyah to understand historic plates and how they relate to Bedouin hospitality.
  • In Jeddah, sample seafood grills and stroll Al Balad’s markets for spices and coffee blends.
  • Visit date farms in Al Ahsa to learn about cultivation cycles and date-based dishes.

Culture & Etiquette: How to Travel Respectfully

Saudi Arabia is rapidly modernizing, but it is also a society with deep cultural and religious traditions. Respect for local customs is essential and will improve every encounter.

Key points to keep in mind:

  • Dress modestly in public: loose, respectful clothing that covers shoulders and knees is advisable for most public settings. Women are not legally required to wear abayas in all places, but modest attire avoids drawing undue attention.
  • Public behaviour: avoid overt displays of affection and be conscious of gendered social norms in some settings.
  • Photography: always ask permission before photographing people; some heritage sites and private properties may restrict photography.
  • Religious observance: during prayer times, certain facilities close briefly—observe local cues and respect closures.

Practical Travel Tips: Visas, Transport, Seasons, and Safety

Visas: Saudi Arabia has an eVisa system for many nationalities and continues to refine categories for tourists. Processing time and eligibility can vary, so apply well before your planned arrival window. Some special zones and heritage sites may require additional permits or proof of reservations.

Transport: Domestic flights connect major hubs (Riyadh, Jeddah, Dammam, Abha, AlUla, Tabuk) and are often the fastest way to stitch distant regions together. Car rental is widely accessible and driving is a very practical option for routes like Jeddah–AlUla; for off-road desert stretches and mountain tracks a 4x4 is essential. Organized tours are recommended for remote desert and canyon access where navigation demands local knowledge.

Health and safety: Saudi Arabia’s healthcare infrastructure is good in major cities. Bring any regular medications with prescriptions, and take standard precautions for heat and sun exposure. For remote excursions, carry extra water, a physical map or downloaded offline GPS, and share your route plan with your accommodation.

Seasonality and what to expect: October–April is the main tourism season. Summer can be extremely hot except in mountain zones, where the climate is cooler. Rainfall is infrequent but can cause flash flooding in wadis; avoid attempting dry-wadi crossings in rainy weather.

How to Build an Itinerary: Frameworks That Work

An itinerary succeeds when it balances meaningful days with reasonable transit. Here are three proven frameworks, expressed as narrative itineraries you can adapt.

Short Break (4–6 days): Heritage Focus — AlUla + One Day in Medina or Tabuk

Spend two full days in AlUla. Day one: Hegra (book a guided time slot) and AlUla Old Town. Day two: Wadi AlUla scenic drives and Maraya (if accessible by reservation). Use a half-day to travel to or from Medina (for Muslim travelers) or Tabuk if reaching via the north. Keep transfers minimal—fly into AlUla if possible or plan an overnight in a nearby hub.

One-Week Highlights (7–10 days): City, Desert, and Coast

Begin in Jeddah (2 days): explore Al Balad, the corniche and local museums, then take an evening flight north. Move to AlUla (2–3 days) for Hegra and canyons. Finish at a Red Sea resort near Umluj for two days of snorkeling and relaxed seaside time. This mix keeps flights to one or two and offers a balance of urban culture, heritage and marine recreation.

Two Weeks (14 days): Classic Loop — Riyadh, Edge of the World, AlUla, Red Sea Coast, Asir

Start in Riyadh (2 days): museums, Diriyah, a sunset trip to the Edge of the World. Fly to AlUla (2–3 days) for archaeology and desert landscapes. From there return to the Red Sea coast (Jeddah/ Umluj) for marine activities (3 days). Fly south to Abha and the Asir highlands for back-to-nature mountain days (3 days). This loop requires domestic flights and a staged approach to cover distances responsibly.

When building any itinerary, allow for recovery days after long transfers. A slow day—walking a market, sipping coffee in a heritage café—often becomes a favorite memory.

Responsible Travel, Heritage Protection, and Conservation

Saudi Arabia’s rapid transformation includes both development and conservation efforts. Many important archaeological sites and reef systems are fragile. Traveling responsibly means booking with operators who support sustainable practices, obeying site signage, and avoiding damage to architecture and ecological habitats.

Heritage best practices:

  • Avoid touching or climbing on fragile stonework.
  • Use established paths and viewing platforms where provided.
  • Hire licensed guides who support local conservation and have formal training.

Marine best practices:

  • Refrain from stepping on or touching coral.
  • Use reef-safe sunscreen and follow local dive operator instructions.
  • Consider contributing to reef monitoring or conservation initiatives when available.

For the latest visitor expectations and site-specific policies, consult official guidance—these pages will spell out entry restrictions, permitted behaviours, and any seasonal closures that protect sites and species. Following these visitor guidelines helps ensure the Kingdom’s places remain vibrant for future travelers.

Essential Packing & Planning Checklist

  • Valid passport with at least six months’ validity; printed and digital copies.
  • Confirmed accommodation and any site reservations that grant access to managed areas.
  • Lightweight, modest clothing suitable for layered dressing; a sun hat and strong sunscreen.
  • Comfortable walking shoes for uneven trails and a pair of sandals for coastal areas.
  • Basic first-aid kit, any prescription medicines, a refillable water bottle, and electrolyte supplements.
  • Offline maps downloaded and contact numbers for your hotel and emergency services.

Practical Booking and Money Tips

Prices for hotels and tours can rise around festivals and peak season. Where possible, book refundable rates until you confirm all transit and permit arrangements. Credit cards are widely accepted in cities, but carry some cash for smaller towns and market purchases. ATMs are available in major towns; plan cash needs in advance for remote areas.

If you plan to drive, ensure your rental includes roadside assistance and check insurance coverage for off-road driving. For domestic flights, beware of tight connection times—allow a buffer, especially when moving between hubs and heritage sites.

Sample Dayshape: What a Typical Day Looks Like in Each Region

  • AlUla: Sunrise stroll near sculpted rocks, mid-morning guided tour of Hegra, leisurely lunch in the old town, late afternoon canyon walk and sunset photography followed by a locally inspired dinner.
  • Red Sea islands: Early boat departure to spot dolphins, snorkeling around a coral outcrop, lunch on a sandbar, siesta in the shade, late afternoon reef dive or beach relaxation.
  • Asir highlands: Mountain road drives to viewpoints, village visits and a cultural workshop, an evening market walk, and a night in a locally run guesthouse.

These day shapes help you pace your trip so you gain depth without exhaustion.

Conclusion

Saudi Arabia offers more than a checklist of sights: it is a country where powerful landscapes, layered histories, and evolving cultural expression invite thoughtful travel. Choose destinations that align with your interests—heritage in AlUla, coast and reefs along the Red Sea, highland culture in Asir, or dramatic desert vistas near Riyadh—and structure your time so each place earns attention. Pair curiosity with careful planning: check access rules, book well in advance for managed sites, and travel with respect for local customs and fragile environments.

FAQ

Q: How long should I stay to get a true sense of Saudi Arabia? A: For a meaningful first trip, plan 10–14 days. This window allows you to focus on two or three regions (for example, Riyadh plus AlUla plus a coastal stop) without nonstop transfers, letting you experience museums, historic quarters, natural landscapes, and local food in a balanced way.

Q: Is it safe to travel independently in Saudi Arabia? A: Saudi Arabia is generally safe for travelers. Use standard prudence—keep copies of travel documents, inform your hotel of day trips, avoid remote travel alone at night, and follow local advisories for weather and road conditions. For remote desert excursions and off-road routes, hiring experienced local guides or joining organized tours reduces risk.

Q: Do I need a guide for sites like Hegra or the Edge of the World? A: Guided tours add historical context and logistical ease, especially at Hegra where timed access and interpretive information enrich the visit. The Edge of the World benefits from guided 4x4 access for navigation and safety; for remote canyon walks, a guide can add cultural interpretation and environmental awareness.

Q: What should I be mindful of when photographing people and religious sites? A: Always ask permission before photographing individuals, respect signs that prohibit photography, and avoid photographing within holy precincts unless you are certain it is permitted. In markets and urban areas, many vendors are open to being photographed if you ask politely; use this as an opportunity for respectful interaction rather than quick snaps.
