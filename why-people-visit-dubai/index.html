Why People Visit Dubai

Updated October 13, 2025 By Saudi Travel and Leisure Editorial Team Noindex, follow
This guide is still available for reference, but it is no longer part of the site's primary indexed Saudi focus.

Introduction

Dubai has become one of the most photographed, talked-about and visited cities in the world. In 2024 the city welcomed nearly 19 million international visitors — a clear signal that its mix of skyscrapers, beaches, festivals and luxury travel continues to attract people from every continent. Whether you arrive for business, leisure, a weekend stopover or as part of a multi-city Gulf itinerary, Dubai offers a highly curated set of experiences that are engineered to impress.

Short answer: People visit Dubai because the city delivers dramatic contrasts in a single destination — world-class architecture, predictable sun and beaches, high-end hospitality, large-scale entertainment and reliable infrastructure for business and events. Travelers come for the showmanship and stay for the convenience, variety and easy connections to the rest of the Gulf.

This article explains, in practical and cultural detail, exactly what draws people to Dubai and how to plan a trip that matches your priorities — from sightseeing and family holidays to business, sporting events and regional stopovers. I’ll break down the core attractions, the logistics every traveler must know, smart itinerary frameworks, and how Dubai fits alongside regional destinations such as Abu Dhabi and Saudi Arabia for a more rewarding trip. As the lead voice of Saudi Travel & Leisure, I bring local perspective and an actionable blueprint so you can convert curiosity into a perfectly planned visit.

The main message: Dubai is more than a list of headline attractions — it’s a purpose-built destination where ambition meets operational excellence, and understanding how the city is put together will let you visit with confidence and get the most value from your time.

What Makes Dubai So Magnetic

A City Built to Be Seen — Architecture and Urban Drama

Dubai’s skyline is a deliberate performance. The Burj Khalifa, the twisting Cayan Tower, the sail-shaped Burj Al Arab and the man-made Palm Jumeirah were planned to create visual impact and a strong sense of place. The scale and precision of these projects tell a travel story from the moment you descend into the airport: this is a city where engineering, design and spectacle are part of the product.

What matters to the traveler is not only the photo opportunities but how accessible these icons are. Observation decks like the one at the Burj Khalifa are efficient — timed tickets, clear wayfinding and integrated shopping and dining make the experience smooth for visitors. The skyline is not merely for architects; it’s built into the visitor economy.

Predictable Climate and the Beach Offer

Dubai’s climate produces consistent sunshine for most of the year. That predictability powers its beaches, waterfront dining and outdoor programming. For travelers who want a reliable dose of sun, well-maintained public beaches, private hotel beaches and beach clubs on the Palm provide a range of experiences from family-friendly to ultra-luxe.

Even if you’re not a beach person, waterfront promenades such as Dubai Marina and JBR (Jumeirah Beach Residence) convert sun into ambiance: cafés, evening markets and easy boat trips mean the coast is always usable and always animated.

Retail and Entertainment at Mega Scale

Dubai transformed shopping into a full-scale entertainment sector. Malls such as The Dubai Mall pair flagship retail with attractions (aquariums, ice rinks, indoor theme parks), allowing a single visit to serve multiple interests. Annual events — Dubai Shopping Festival and other seasonal promotions — turn retail into a cultural calendar item where prizes, concerts and activations keep the offer fresh.

Beyond malls, the city programs large-scale entertainment: fountain shows, evening light displays, and new attractions like the Ain Dubai ferris wheel create an entertainment density that makes it easy to plan family-friendly or high-energy evenings without long transfers.

Hospitality and Luxury Infrastructure

Dubai’s hotel market emphasizes service, novelty and scale. Luxury properties regularly debut new design concepts and attractions — underwater suites, private island resorts and sky-high restaurants. But the market also includes business-focused hotels and well-priced chain options, which means travelers can select accommodations for experience or efficiency. Room supply and a well-developed airport network keep the city resilient to demand peaks.

Food Variety and Dining Scene

A truly global expatriate population has made Dubai a safe place for any cuisine. Michelin-starred restaurants coexist with robust street food scenes and contemporary Emirati dining. Dining functions as both a social and tourist activity: food festivals, pop-ups and celebrity chef restaurants keep the culinary scene dynamic, and the city’s regulatory framework supports public events and late-night dining.

Events, Sports and Business Travel

Dubai’s strategy to host global events means the city attracts people for conferences, sporting championships, exhibitions and seasonal tournaments. The combination of world-class venues, hospitality capacity and relatively liberal travel policies for many nationalities makes Dubai a reliable choice for meetings, incentives and large-scale events. For sports travelers, major golf events, tennis, horse racing and motorsport create focused windows of interest each year.

Heritage and Cultural Access

Beyond skyscrapers, Dubai retains pockets of traditional life and curated heritage programming. Al Fahidi Historical Neighbourhood, the spice and gold souks and museums around the Creek provide a complementary cultural narrative that shows the city’s trade history and coastal roots. For visitors who want more context, guided cultural experiences and institutions such as cultural understanding centers offer interpretive access to Emirati traditions.

Accessibility and Connectivity

Dubai International Airport is one of the world’s busiest hubs. For many international travelers Dubai functions as a direct destination or an efficient stopover en route to other parts of the Middle East, South Asia and Africa. The city’s visa policy, flight frequency and airport facilities minimize friction for short stays, stopovers and airport-to-city transfers.

Deep Dive: The Top Visitor Motivations

The Visual Spectacle: From Burj Khalifa to Palm Jumeirah

People travel to Dubai to see superlatives in real life. The Burj Khalifa remains a top pull because it translates an abstract idea — the tallest building on earth — into an accessible experience. Observation decks, rooftop restaurants and the surrounding Downtown area are designed so visitors can plan efficient visits. Palm Jumeirah is another engineered experience: a drive across the trunk and a hotel stay on one of the fronds convert the island into a curated island escape.

How to make the most of these attractions: book timed tickets in advance, plan visits for golden hour for the best photographs, and combine attractions in a single area (e.g., Downtown Dubai includes the mall, fountain, and Burj Khalifa) to reduce transit time.

The Promise of Luxury and Novelty

Dubai sells high-quality experiences with predictable standards. For those who want a single stand-out memory — a dinner under the sails of the Burj Al Arab, a pool day at a Palm resort, or a helicopter flight over the city — Dubai provides reliable options. The city’s hospitality sector is adept at packaging novelty in accessible formats.

Practical tip: if you want a luxury experience at a reasonable price, look for daytime access packages, weekday offers and combination tickets which often provide the best value.

Sun and Sand with Year-Round Services

Families and sunseekers choose Dubai because its beach and water offerings are supported by infrastructure: rental facilities, supervised swimming areas and lifeguards on many public beaches. The presence of water parks and family attractions makes Dubai a predictable summer (and winter) family destination.

Best timing: November through March offers the most pleasant temperatures for outdoor activity. Summer offers lower hotel rates and quieter attractions but requires careful planning for heat-managed activities such as indoor malls and evening desert experiences.

Events and Sports as Travel Catalysts

Dubai’s calendar creates specific travel moments: horse racing, golf championships, tennis tournaments and trade shows. Travelers increasingly book trips around these events because the city can deliver a complete experience — from world-class venues to spectator hospitality.

If you travel for an event: secure lodging early, understand local transport plans around major venues, and buy event tickets from official channels.

Culinary Curiosity and World Cuisine

With thousands of restaurants, Dubai becomes a playground for food discovery. From street-level shawarma to high-end tasting menus, the city’s dining depth supports multi-day culinary itineraries. Food festivals and pop-ups provide additional discovery windows.

Smart approach: prioritize neighborhoods (e.g., Al Fahidi for traditional, Alserkal for contemporary scenes, Marina for waterfront dining) and reserve tables for high-demand restaurants.

Family-Friendly Attractions

Dubai has invested heavily in family attractions: theme parks, aquariums, interactive museums and water parks. These attractions are generally organized and safe, making Dubai an easy choice for multigenerational travel.

Planning note: combine indoor and outdoor activities to manage energy levels; schedule water parks earlier in a trip to avoid sun fatigue later.

Shopping and Retail as an Experience

For many visitors, shopping is both a leisure activity and a practical one. Duty-free shopping, global brands and local markets create a retail ecosystem where purchases and entertainment mesh. The city’s sale seasons and promotions add value for international shoppers.

Payment and value tip: tax refunds are available through official channels — confirm requirements at the point of sale and retain receipts.

Accessibility for Business and Transit Passengers

Visitors traveling for business value Dubai’s event infrastructure and hospitality reliability. For transit passengers, the airport’s connectivity and efficient city links make short stopovers appealing for a quick taste of the city.

If you’re on a stopover: plan one anchor activity (e.g., Burj Khalifa or a creek-side abra ride) and keep transfers to midday when traffic is lighter.

Cultural Context: Respectful, Informed Travel

Understanding Local Norms

Dubai is modern and cosmopolitan but remains part of the UAE, an Islamic federation. Familiar customs include modest dress in certain public spaces, conservative behavior during religious observances such as Ramadan and respectful conduct in religious sites. The city is tolerant but also governed by clear laws and regulations that govern public behavior.

Practical cultural advice: dress modestly in government buildings and mosques; avoid public displays of affection in certain areas; and align travel plans with local holiday schedules and prayer times to avoid surprises.

How to Experience Emirati Culture Authentically

To move beyond the surface spectacle, plan a visit to historical quarters, take a guided cultural tour, or attend an Emirati dining experience. Cultural centers provide interpreters and programmatic context that turn attractions into meaningful encounters.

A helpful bridge for travelers from the Kingdom: if you’re coming from Saudi Arabia and want a comparable cultural visit, consider pairing Dubai with curated Saudi heritage sites to contrast urban design and traditional lifestyles. Practicalities: Entry, Transport and Where to Stay

Visa and Entry Essentials

Visa rules vary by nationality and can be updated frequently. The UAE offers visas on arrival for many countries, and electronic visa processes are straightforward for longer stays. If your travel involves multi-destination Gulf plans, check the visa requirements for each emirate and connecting flights.

For travelers from Saudi Arabia and the wider Gulf, short-term entry is often simple; still confirm with your airline or embassy before travel.

Getting Around: Public Transport and Ride-Hailing

Dubai’s metro network connects major nodes (Downtown, Business Bay, Mall of the Emirates, and the airport), and a comprehensive bus network fills in gaps. Ride-hailing apps and taxis are widely available and affordable relative to Western cities. For short itineraries, the metro plus targeted taxis can reduce time lost to transfers.

Insider tip: plan Downtown and Dubai Mall activities to minimize taxi rides; use the metro for airport transfers when luggage is manageable.

Which Neighborhood to Choose

Where you stay should align with your priorities:

  • Downtown/Dubai Mall: best for iconic sightseeing and centralized walking access to major attractions.
  • Dubai Marina/JBR: excellent for beach access, evening dining and a relaxed waterfront vibe.
  • Palm Jumeirah: luxury hotels and resort-style stays for island resort experiences.
  • Al Barsha/Sheikh Zayed Road: value-driven options with easy highway access to attractions.
  • Al Fahidi/Deira: best for cultural immersion and proximity to old Dubai.

Practical Budgeting

Dubai can be expensive, but there are strategies to manage cost: visit outside peak season for better hotel rates, choose combination attraction tickets, eat at local cafes for several meals, and use public transport. Conversely, if your goal is one standout experience, allocate budget to that single memory (a desert dinner, a helicopter tour, or a luxury hotel night).

Day Trips and Extensions: Abu Dhabi and Regional Connections

Why Add Abu Dhabi

Abu Dhabi complements Dubai with landmark cultural institutions such as the Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque, Louvre Abu Dhabi, and island resorts. A day trip is feasible by car (roughly 1.5 to 2 hours each way depending on traffic) and offers a different pace and architectural narrative.

Multi-City Gulf Itineraries

Many travelers combine Dubai with regional stops: Abu Dhabi for cultural institutions, Oman for coastal drives, and Saudi Arabia for deep cultural and historic sites. For those planning extended travel in the Arabian Peninsula, consider starting or ending your trip with a flight to Riyadh or Jeddah to experience Saudi’s heritage and landscapes. Sample Itineraries and Time-Optimized Planning

Short Stopover (24–36 Hours)

For a tight stopover, prioritize one marquee attraction and one cultural experience. Time your visit around a single central hub such as Downtown Dubai (Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall, and the fountain) combined with a late-afternoon visit to Al Fahidi for a quick heritage contrast.

Efficiency payload: prebook Burj Khalifa tickets, use metro connections and reserve a table at a restaurant with views to maximize time.

Classic 3-Day Visit

A three-day trip lets you see the city’s main themes: Day 1 for the skyline and Dubai Mall, Day 2 for beaches and Palm Jumeirah, Day 3 for a desert safari and evening market. Balance indoor and outdoor to manage energy.

Booking note: schedule the desert activity in the late afternoon to avoid midday heat and to enjoy sunset photos.

Week-Long Stay (7 Days)

A week allows deeper exploration: allocate time for Abu Dhabi day trip, an art district morning in Alserkal Avenue, a cultural tour in Al Fahidi, family park days, and relaxed beach time. Add one splurge experience (helicopter, dinner at a landmark hotel or a private island day) and leave a buffer day for unexpected discoveries.

Logistics advantage: longer stays allow slower transitions, fewer rushed transfers and better value hotel rates.

Responsible, Respectful and Safer Travel

Legal and Cultural Boundaries

Dubai enforces laws related to public behavior, alcohol consumption, dress and photography. To avoid problems, familiarize yourself with local laws before travel and follow posted signage in public spaces.

Photography tip: avoid photographing people without permission, especially in conservative contexts, and be cautious around government buildings.

Environment and Sustainable Choices

Dubai’s rapid growth has environmental costs. Travelers can reduce impact by choosing hotels with sustainability programs, limiting single-use plastics, supporting local cultural initiatives and selecting regulated tour operators for desert visits that prioritize wildlife and dune protection.

Health, Safety and Emergency Preparedness

Dubai is generally safe for travelers and has a robust healthcare infrastructure. Travel insurance that covers medical evacuation is advisable for visitors who will undertake desert activities, water sports or remote excursions.

How Dubai Compares to Saudi Destinations — When to Visit Each

Dubai is engineered for spectacle and convenience; Saudi Arabia, in contrast, emphasizes deep cultural history, archaeological landscapes and spiritual pilgrimage sites. The two are complementary for travelers who want both amenity-driven leisure and historically rich experiences.

If your travel priority is curated luxury, retail and hub-based connectivity, Dubai will meet those needs. If you are seeking archaeological sites, cultural immersion with expanding tourist infrastructure, and a quieter, more expansive landscape experience, Saudi destinations such as AlUla and Jeddah provide rich alternatives. Money, Connectivity and Practical Tips

Currency, Payments and Tipping

The UAE dirham is widely supported with card payments accepted in most venues. Tipping is customary but modest. For purchases at souks, bargaining is part of the culture — do it respectfully.

SIM Cards and Connectivity

Buy a local SIM at the airport or city kiosks for reliable data. Free Wi-Fi is common in malls and hotels, but a local SIM provides more flexibility for ride-hailing and navigation.

Packing and What to Expect

Pack layers for air-conditioned interiors and modest clothing for religious sites. Bring comfortable walking shoes for mixed urban surfaces and light clothing for daytime. If your trip includes desert or boat activities, bring sun protection and appropriate footwear.

Mistakes Travelers Make—and How to Avoid Them

Many visitor frustrations occur because of timing and expectations. Common pitfalls include underestimating travel time between attractions during rush hour, booking single high-demand activities the same day without buffer time, and failing to account for local holidays or prayer times. To avoid these issues, leave an extra hour for transfers, prebook popular experiences and consult local calendars before traveling.

A well-balanced two-week itinerary could be structured as follows: three to five nights in Dubai for urban spectacle and events, two nights in Abu Dhabi for museums and cultural institutions, then a flight to Riyadh for history and modern Saudi urban life, and a final three nights in AlUla for archaeological landscapes. Each segment delivers a distinct flavor of the Arabian Peninsula: engineered modernity, national culture and ancient heritage.

Conclusion

Dubai attracts people because it packages rare and large-scale experiences into a highly functional travel product: iconic architecture, year-round sun, world-class hospitality, a huge entertainment calendar and efficient infrastructure. Whether you come for business, family fun, sports, shopping or a taste of modern Gulf luxury, Dubai rewards travelers who plan with intention and prioritize the experiences that matter most to them.

FAQ

What is the best time of year to visit Dubai?

The most comfortable months are generally November through March when daytime temperatures are mild and outdoor activities are pleasant. If you prefer lower hotel prices and fewer crowds, consider visiting in the shoulder months, but plan indoor activities to manage higher temperatures.

Is Dubai family-friendly?

Yes. Dubai has a broad range of family-focused attractions, from theme parks and aquariums to safe public beaches and family dining venues. Plan a mix of indoor and outdoor days to manage energy and sun exposure.

How should I split time between Dubai and Abu Dhabi?

For first-time visitors, allocate at least one full day to Abu Dhabi to visit the Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque and a cultural institution like Louvre Abu Dhabi. If you’re interested in more in-depth cultural programming, add an overnight stay.

Can I combine Dubai with Saudi Arabia on the same trip?

Absolutely. Dubai pairs well with Saudi destinations for travelers who want a contrast between modern urban spectacle and deep cultural heritage. For pairing frameworks, start with our travel to Saudi Arabia hub to build complementary itineraries.
